# PMP Exam Master Brief: Project Planning and Integration
### Weak Spots Targeted: Escalation · Communication

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Project planning and integration is the discipline of weaving every knowledge area, every stakeholder need, and every constraint into one coherent plan — and then keeping that plan alive as conditions change. Integration is the PM's exclusive domain; no one else on the project does this work. It starts with the charter (the authorization to spend money), produces the project management plan and baselines, governs every change through Integrated Change Control (ICC), captures lessons throughout, and closes work properly. On the exam, integration is roughly 8–12% of questions but it bleeds into every other domain — almost every "what does the PM do FIRST" question is testing integration. In real projects, weak integration shows up as siloed plans, surprise change-control failures, and lessons that never get captured. On the exam, misreading "is this change inside the baseline" and "did this change go through ICC" kills your score. PMI expects you to be the conductor — not the loudest instrument.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about owning the whole plan, controlling every change through governance, and capturing knowledge so nothing is built or learned twice.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Planning and Integration Look Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Charter → PM plan with subsidiary plans and baselines → directed work → ICC for every change → closure. Plans heavy, change formal. |
| **Agile** | Lightweight charter/vision → product backlog + release plan → continuous iterative planning → change embraced at backlog level → retrospectives as the integration engine. Plans light, change frequent. |
| **Hybrid** | Charter + PM plan retained for governance; iteration-level planning agile; ICC used for cross-program impacts; release plan is the integration backbone. |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **No charter = no project.** PMI never starts work without a signed charter. Verbal sponsor approval is not a charter.
2. **Every change goes through ICC.** "Small" change, "obvious" change, "sponsor-approved verbally" change — all wrong without an approved change request.
3. **Progressive elaboration is the default, not an exception.** Plans get more detailed as more is known. Not "we didn't finish planning" — it is the planning method.
4. **Lessons learned is continuous, not a closure step.** PMBOK 7 explicitly treats knowledge management as a live process.
5. **The PM owns integration. Period.** A team lead, sponsor, or PMO can support, but integration accountability cannot be delegated.

### Ethics Angle
- **Responsibility:** Bypassing ICC to "move faster" trades short-term ease for traceability — a Responsibility breach.
- **Honesty:** Baselines must reflect approved state; quietly re-baselining to hide variance is dishonest.
- **Respect:** Inputs to the plan from team members, vendors, and stakeholders deserve genuine integration, not lip service.
- **Fairness:** Change requests must be evaluated on impact, not on who submitted them.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Project Charter** | Document that authorizes the project, names the PM, and grants spend authority | Trap: confusing with business case (business case justifies; charter authorizes) |
| **Project Management Plan** | Integrated set of subsidiary plans + three baselines (scope, schedule, cost) | Trap: treating as single document; it is a portfolio of plans |
| **Baselines** | Scope baseline, Schedule baseline, Cost baseline — approved versions used to measure variance | Trap: changing without ICC; baselines change only through approved change requests |
| **Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB)** | Integrated scope + schedule + cost baseline used for EVM | Trap: confusing with cost baseline alone |
| **Subsidiary Plans** | Scope, schedule, cost, quality, resource, communications, risk, procurement, stakeholder management plans | Trap: forgetting one — 10 subsidiary plans + 3 baselines = full PM plan |
| **Configuration Management Plan** | Defines how project artifacts and product versions are controlled | Trap: confusing with change management plan; config = WHAT, change = HOW |
| **Change Management Plan** | Defines how change requests are submitted, evaluated, approved | Trap: assuming ICC is a tool, not a plan-driven process |
| **Integrated Change Control (ICC)** | Process to review, approve, and manage changes to deliverables, baselines, and PM plan | Trap: thinking ICC is optional for "small" changes |
| **Change Control Board (CCB)** | Formal body authorized to approve/reject changes per change management plan | Trap: assuming CCB always exists; not every project has one; PM authority can be defined instead |
| **Progressive Elaboration** | Iteratively refining plan as more detail becomes known | Trap: confusing with scope creep; progressive elaboration is sanctioned, scope creep is not |
| **Rolling Wave Planning** | Near-term work planned in detail; far-term work at higher level | Trap: thinking it is incomplete planning; it is the technique |
| **Project Kickoff** | Formal start meeting after charter approval; aligns team and stakeholders | Trap: confusing with sponsor signature; kickoff happens after charter, not instead of it |
| **Direct and Manage Project Work** | Executing process that produces deliverables and work performance data | Trap: confusing with Monitor and Control |
| **Manage Project Knowledge** | Process that uses existing knowledge and creates new knowledge to achieve objectives | Trap: assuming lessons are captured only at closure |
| **Monitor and Control Project Work** | Process of tracking and reporting overall progress against PM plan | Trap: confusing with Control Quality or Control Schedule; this is the integration-level oversight |
| **Close Project or Phase** | Finalize all activities, formally close the project, transfer deliverables, archive | Trap: skipping lessons learned and final acceptance |
| **Lessons Learned Register** | Living document of lessons identified throughout project | Trap: treating as closure-only artifact; PMBOK 7 wants continuous |
| **Issue Log** | Document tracking issues (not risks), owner, status, resolution | Trap: confusing with risk register |
| **Work Performance Data → Information → Reports** | Raw data → analyzed information → distributed reports | Trap: confusing data with information; PMI tests vocabulary |
| **Project Vision (Agile)** | Lightweight version of charter; sets product purpose | Trap: assuming no charter is needed in agile; PMI still expects authorization |
| **Release Plan (Agile)** | Higher-level plan covering multiple sprints to a release | Trap: confusing with sprint plan |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Sponsor verbally approves a scope change | Submit a formal change request through ICC; do not act on verbal approval | Verbal approval does not update baselines or the PM plan |
| Charter is missing/incomplete and work has started | Pause discretionary work; complete and obtain sponsor sign-off on the charter | No charter = no authorization; spending unauthorized money is a control breach |
| A change is "obvious" and "small" | Still submit it through ICC; impact assessment may surprise | Every change touches scope/schedule/cost/risk — ICC is the integration step |
| Lessons learned have not been updated for three months | Capture them now; add lessons-learned reviews to the integration cadence | Knowledge management is continuous, not deferred to closure |
| Team member finds a defect during build | Log issue, assess impact, route through Control Quality + ICC if rework affects baseline | Defect ≠ change request directly; impact assessment first |
| PMO asks why integration management plan looks light | Show the integrated PM plan and explain tailoring rationale | Tailoring is allowed; lack of explanation is not |
| Sponsor asks for the "current plan" | Provide the latest approved PM plan with baselines, not the working draft | Approved baselines are the source of truth for sponsor communication |
| Two subsidiary plans conflict (e.g., schedule says fast, quality says heavy testing) | Facilitate resolution between owners; update conflicting plan via ICC | Subsidiary plans must remain internally consistent — this is integration |
| Closeout is requested but deliverables are not formally accepted | Obtain formal acceptance first; do not close before validation | Closure without acceptance leaves liability and traceability gaps |
| Agile team asks to skip release planning because "we plan each sprint" | Hold release planning to set product goal and integrate dependencies | Sprint planning does not replace release-level integration |

**Two Escalation-Specific Scenarios (your weak spot):**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| A change request exceeds the PM's documented authority | Escalate to the CCB or sponsor per the change management plan; do not approve unilaterally even if "obvious" |
| A baseline change is being requested informally outside ICC (e.g., over chat, in a side meeting) | Redirect to the formal change request; escalate to sponsor only if redirection is refused, with the integrity breach as the point |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

### The Integration Decision Tree (PMI Style)

```
SOMETHING CHANGES (scope, schedule, cost, risk, stakeholder, resource)
        │
        ▼
Is it a change to an approved baseline or PM plan?
    NO  → Tracked as work performance; no ICC needed
    YES ↓
        ▼
Submit FORMAL CHANGE REQUEST
        │
        ▼
Impact assessment (scope, schedule, cost, risk, quality, resources, stakeholders)
        │
        ▼
Within PM authority per change mgmt plan?
    YES → PM approves; update baselines + PM plan + comms
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Route to CCB / Sponsor for decision
        │
        ▼
Approved → update baselines + PM plan + lessons learned + comms
Rejected → log decision; update lessons learned; notify stakeholders
```

### Integration Mnemonic: **C-P-D-M-C-C** (PMBOK 6 process names, still tested)

> **C**harter → **P**M Plan → **D**irect Work → **M**anage Knowledge → **C**ontrol Work + ICC → **C**lose

Memory hook: **"Charter Plans Direct Managed Controlled Closure."**
- Charter authorizes; PM Plan integrates; Direct executes; Manage Knowledge learns continuously; Control Work + ICC governs; Close transfers and archives.

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Full PM plan with all 10 subsidiary plans + 3 baselines; CCB usually present
- Change control formal; ICC produces a clear audit trail
- Rolling wave planning common — near-term in detail, later phases higher level
- Closure is a defined phase with formal acceptance and lessons archival

### Agile / Hybrid
- Lightweight charter / project vision; iteration zero may produce the equivalent
- Release plan + product backlog replace a heavy PM plan; sprint planning replaces detailed work scheduling
- Change is welcomed via backlog refinement; in-sprint change discouraged but not banned
- Retrospectives are the continuous knowledge-management mechanism; lessons logged each iteration
- Hybrid: predictive governance retained for procurement/contracts; agile planning at delivery layer

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** BSEC and Bangladesh Bank often mandate documented change control with audit trails — ICC artifacts must be retained for years
- Configuration management is critical in regulated environments — versioning of artifacts/code must align with change records
- **Government / public-sector:** charter may take the form of a project approval letter or Cabinet directive; PM authority is constrained by procurement rules
- **Vendor-heavy programs:** integration of vendor plans into PM plan is a recurring failure mode; ensure subsidiary plans reflect vendor commitments

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Implement the change immediately since the sponsor approved verbally" | Sponsor authority is enough; we trust the sponsor | Verbal ≠ ICC; baselines and audit trail require a formal change request |
| "Skip ICC for cosmetic changes to save time" | Tiny changes do not warrant overhead | "Cosmetic" is judged by ICC, not by the PM; small changes can have surprising impact |
| "Update lessons learned only at project closure" | Closure is the natural moment | PMBOK 7 explicitly treats Manage Project Knowledge as continuous |
| "Have the team lead handle integration since the PM is busy" | Delegation is a PM skill | Integration is the PM's accountability; cannot be delegated wholesale |
| "Re-baseline to reset the cost variance" | A new baseline simplifies reporting | Re-baselining without authorization hides variance and breaks integrity |
| "Skip the charter — the sponsor has already approved the budget" | Budget approval is more important than paperwork | Charter authorizes the PM and the project; budget alone does not |
| "Close the project once the deliverables are produced" | Production = done | Closure requires formal acceptance + lessons + archival + administrative closure |
| "Combine multiple change requests into one ticket to streamline review" | Less paperwork is better | Combined CRs hide individual impact; PMI wants per-change traceability |

## 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "Sponsor **verbally approved** a change" | Submit formal change request; verbal is not ICC |
| "**Small change** to scope/schedule/cost" | Still goes through ICC; "small" is determined by impact assessment |
| "**Re-baseline**" mentioned | Only after approved change; never to hide variance |
| "Charter is **missing**" | Pause discretionary work; obtain authorization first |
| "**Lessons learned** at closure" | Wrong reflex; continuous capture is the PMI expectation |
| "**Issue log** vs risk register" | Issue = occurred; risk = potential |
| "**Configuration management**" | Versioning of artifacts/product, not the change process itself |
| "**Manage Project Knowledge**" | Continuous; treats tacit + explicit knowledge |
| "**Direct and Manage Project Work**" | Executing process; produces deliverables and WPD |
| "**Monitor and Control Project Work**" | Integration-level oversight; compares actuals to PM plan |
| "**Close Project or Phase**" | Formal acceptance + lessons + archive + admin closure |
| "**Progressive elaboration**" | Sanctioned refinement; not scope creep |
| "**Rolling wave planning**" | Near-term detailed; far-term high-level |
| "**Project Vision / Release Plan**" (agile) | Agile integration backbones — not absence of integration |
| "Sponsor **bypassed ICC**" | Re-engage; restore process; escalate ethics if needed |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are managing a USD 9M core-banking implementation. The sponsor has approved a "minor" scope addition over a phone call to keep a key business stakeholder happy. The addition will consume 4% of contingency reserve and shift the critical path by five days. The sponsor asks you to "just slip it in." The next steering committee is in three weeks. What should you do FIRST?

A. Implement the change immediately since the sponsor has authority and the addition is minor.
B. Submit a formal change request through ICC, conduct impact assessment, and present the result to the sponsor before steering.
C. Refuse the change and inform the business stakeholder it requires re-prioritization.
D. Implement the change but add a note about it in the next steering report.

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

You are coaching an agile product team building a regulatory-reporting platform. The team has skipped release planning for the last two release cycles, planning only at sprint level. Dependencies with two upstream teams are now slipping repeatedly. The product owner says "sprint planning is enough — release planning is waterfall." What is the BEST action?

A. Cancel release planning permanently since the team prefers sprint-only planning.
B. Facilitate a conversation explaining that release planning sets the product goal and integrates cross-team dependencies, then schedule a lightweight release-planning session.
C. Escalate the dependency slippage to senior management and request resource alignment.
D. Add cross-team coordination as a recurring sprint planning topic instead of holding release planning.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are leading a hybrid digital-onboarding project. The procurement subsidiary plan requires a 4-week vendor selection cycle. The scope subsidiary plan assumes vendor onboarding completes within 2 weeks. Both plans are approved. The conflict has now blocked sprint planning. The vendor manager and product owner each point to their plan. What should you do FIRST?

A. Decide in favor of procurement since vendor cycle is contractually binding.
B. Decide in favor of product since delivery timeline matters more.
C. Facilitate a working session between procurement and product to reconcile the conflict, then route the impact through ICC to update the affected plan.
D. Escalate to the sponsor for a decision.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

You are taking over a USD 6M infrastructure project mid-execution. The prior PM left without formal handover. You find no signed charter, an outdated PM plan, and an issue log that has not been touched for two months. Sponsor expects status next week. Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Obtain or recreate the project charter, signed by the sponsor.
B. Submit a formal change request for the missing baselines.
C. Reconstruct the current PM plan state and refresh issue/risk registers.
D. Recommend project termination given the governance gaps.
E. Skip the charter step since the project is already running.

---

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Predictive)**

A new project has just been authorized at a bank. The PM has been assigned. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Develop the project management plan including subsidiary plans and baselines.
2. Conduct the project kickoff meeting with the team and key stakeholders.
3. Sign and publish the project charter authorizing the project and the PM.
4. Identify stakeholders and create the stakeholder register.

A. 3 → 4 → 1 → 2
B. 4 → 3 → 1 → 2
C. 3 → 1 → 4 → 2
D. 1 → 3 → 4 → 2

**Q6 — EXCEPT Question (Predictive)**

Which of the following is NOT a valid input to the Develop Project Management Plan process?

A. Project charter
B. Outputs from other planning processes
C. Enterprise environmental factors
D. Organizational process assets

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

Three months into a 12-month project, you discover that the actual cost performance index (CPI) is 0.78 and the schedule performance index (SPI) is 0.85. The sponsor has a board update next week. Your sponsor asks you to "re-baseline the project so we reset the variance and start fresh." There has been no approved change in scope. What do you do?

A. Re-baseline as requested since the sponsor has authority.
B. Refuse to re-baseline without approved scope changes; present actual variance, root-cause analysis, and a recovery plan to the sponsor.
C. Re-baseline only the cost baseline since schedule pressure remains.
D. Wait until after the board update to decide.

---

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid capital-markets program. The PM plan is large and formal; the team delivers in two-week sprints. Regulatory stakeholders demand monthly written status against the PM plan baselines. Business stakeholders want real-time sprint-level visibility. Executive sponsors want a one-page strategic view. What is the BEST approach?

A. Produce a single integrated monthly status report that satisfies all audiences.
B. Tailor: monthly written status against baselines for regulators; live sprint board for business; one-page executive dashboard for sponsors.
C. Discontinue the PM plan baselines since sprints provide better visibility.
D. Replace all reports with the live sprint board.

---

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project. A vendor has submitted a change request that exceeds your documented PM financial authority by USD 200K. Your change management plan requires CCB approval above your threshold. The vendor insists on a decision in 48 hours or they will withdraw the proposal. You have already obtained impact assessments from scope, schedule, and risk owners. What is the BEST next step?

A. Approve the change to keep the vendor engaged and document the override.
B. Convene an emergency CCB session within the 48-hour window, present the impact assessments, and obtain formal decision.
C. Reject the change and proceed without the vendor.
D. Defer the decision until the next scheduled CCB meeting and accept the vendor withdrawal risk.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

A program manager finds that lessons learned are being captured per project but never aggregated, searched, or reused across the program. Multiple projects have repeated the same procurement misstep over six months. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Investigate why aggregated lessons are not being consulted before action items recur.
B. Build a searchable, centralized knowledge base accessible to all program PMs.
C. Make a lessons-review the first agenda item of each project kickoff for the program.
D. Mandate that PMs write longer lessons-learned reports.
E. Stop the program until lessons-learned discipline is fixed.
F. Add a lessons-quality KPI to PM performance evaluations.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** ICC is non-negotiable for any change touching scope, schedule, or cost — even small ones with sponsor support. Submitting the change request, assessing impact, and presenting findings to the sponsor protects baselines, audit trail, and the sponsor's own decision quality. Option A bypasses ICC. Option C refuses without analysis. Option D logs the bypass after the fact, which does not retroactively meet integration requirements.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor authority does not waive ICC — verbal approval is input, not output.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Release planning is the integration mechanism that sets product goal and surfaces cross-team dependencies — it is not waterfall, it is essential agile integration at scale. Option A removes the mechanism entirely. Option C escalates without first restoring the planning practice. Option D crams cross-team work into sprint planning, which is the wrong altitude.

**Mindset Lesson:** Release planning is integration at the right altitude — sprint planning cannot replace it for multi-team dependencies.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Subsidiary plan conflicts are the PM's integration accountability. Facilitating between owners is the first move; the conflict resolution then routes through ICC to update one or both plans. Option A and B pick sides without analysis. Option D escalates without first attempting reconciliation.

**Mindset Lesson:** Subsidiary plan conflicts are resolved by the PM through facilitation + ICC — not by picking a side or escalating reflexively.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** No charter = no authorization; recreating and signing the charter (A) restores PM and project authority. Reconstructing the PM plan state and refreshing registers (C) gives the PM a real picture before status reporting. Option B (change request for missing baselines) misuses ICC — baselines that never existed are not changes. Option D (terminate) is dramatic without analysis. Option E ignores the foundational governance gap.

**Mindset Lesson:** Take-over due-diligence is charter first, plan state second — every other action depends on those.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A (3 → 4 → 1 → 2)**

**PMI Justification:** Charter is signed first (3) to authorize the project and PM. Stakeholder identification (4) is required input to planning. PM plan including subsidiary plans and baselines is then developed (1). Kickoff (2) happens after the plan is ready, to align team and stakeholders against an actual plan. Kickoff before plan ready produces alignment to a vague target.

**Mindset Lesson:** Charter → stakeholders → plan → kickoff — sequence prevents alignment to a non-existent target.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: E**

**PMI Justification:** Work performance reports are an output of Monitor and Control Project Work, not an input to Develop PM Plan. The PM plan is developed at planning; work performance reports emerge during monitoring/controlling. Inputs to Develop PM Plan include charter, outputs of other planning processes, EEFs, and OPAs.

**Mindset Lesson:** Process inputs vs outputs is a recurring PMI exam mechanic — memorize the direction of flow.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Re-baselining without approved scope change hides variance and breaks the Honesty principle in the PMI Code of Ethics. Presenting actual variance with root-cause analysis and recovery plan is what PMI expects. Option A is dishonest. Option C selectively hides cost variance. Option D delays the decision past the inflection point.

**Mindset Lesson:** Re-baseline is a consequence of approved change, never a cause to hide variance.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 endorses tailoring communication to audience. Regulators need baseline-conformant reporting; business stakeholders need real-time visibility; sponsors need strategic summary. All three can coexist. Option A flattens audiences. Option C abandons baselines that regulators require. Option D ignores regulatory and sponsor needs.

**Mindset Lesson:** Hybrid communication tailors to audience without abandoning required baselines.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PM authority is bounded by the change management plan. Above threshold requires CCB. The change management plan also exists to handle urgent decisions — convening an emergency CCB session is the correct exercise of governance. Option A overrides authority. Option C rejects without governance. Option D defers past the urgency window and accepts a foreseeable loss.

**Mindset Lesson:** Time pressure does not waive ICC — it accelerates ICC.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, B, C**

**PMI Justification:**
- **A:** Diagnose before intervention — PMBOK 7 Knowledge Management thread.
- **B:** Searchable centralized knowledge base enables reuse — the core deficiency.
- **C:** Lessons-review at kickoff puts learning in the path of action.
- D is wrong: longer reports do not produce reuse.
- E is wrong: stopping the program is disproportionate.
- F is wrong: KPI-incentivizing lessons quality often produces theater, not learning.

**Mindset Lesson:** Knowledge reuse is enabled by accessibility + ritualized consultation — not by longer reports or coercion.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**PROJECT PLANNING AND INTEGRATION — Exam Morning Card**

- Charter authorizes; PM plan integrates; baselines measure; ICC governs change; closure transfers + archives.
- Subsidiary plans + 3 baselines = full PM plan. Conflicts are PM's to integrate.
- Every change → formal change request → impact assessment → ICC decision. No exceptions for "small" or "verbal."
- Re-baseline only after approved change. Never to hide variance.
- Manage Project Knowledge is continuous. Lessons captured throughout, not just at closure.
- Progressive elaboration ≠ scope creep. Rolling wave planning is the technique.
- Agile equivalents: project vision, release plan, product backlog, retrospectives.
- Hybrid: keep ICC and baselines for governance; iterate planning at delivery layer.
- Configuration management = versions of artifacts/product. Change management = process for change.
- Take-over checklist: charter first, plan state second, registers third.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *PMI escalates a change through ICC when impact exceeds PM authority — always with impact assessment, always with audit trail, never on a verbal nod.*
