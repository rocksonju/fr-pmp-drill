# PMP Exam Master Brief: Agile Principles
### Weak Spots Targeted: Escalation · Communication

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Agile principles are the 4 values and 12 guiding statements from the Agile Manifesto that explain WHY agile teams work the way they do — not the ceremonies, not the artifacts, but the underlying logic. PMI treats them as the philosophical bedrock for everything in the Agile Practice Guide and roughly half the situational behavior expected on the current ECO-aligned exam. They drive choices around customer collaboration over contract negotiation, working software over documentation, individuals over process, and responding to change over following a plan. On the exam, ~25–35% of all questions are agile or hybrid; almost every one of them is decidable by mapping the stem back to a principle (sustainable pace, simplicity, reflection, self-organization). In real projects, teams adopt mechanics (standups, sprints, kanban) without internalizing principles — PMI tests whether you do. Misreading "we have all the ceremonies, so we're agile" is a top-five trap on the current exam.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about the values and 12 principles that explain why agile teams behave as they do — every situational answer flows from them.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Agile Principles Look Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Principles influence governance — early stakeholder engagement, lessons learned, change control discipline; ceremonies are absent but the spirit of inspect-and-adapt can be borrowed at phase gates |
| **Agile** | Principles are the operating system; team agreements, ceremonies, and tooling are expressions of them; deviating from the principles to "go faster" is a smell |
| **Hybrid** | Principles guide team-level behavior; predictive governance retained where contracts, regulators, or external dependencies demand it; tailoring is principle-driven, not ceremony-driven |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Ceremonies without principles are theater.** A team running standups, sprints, and reviews while management forces overtime and forbids change is not agile. PMI will mark "doing scrum" as not enough.
2. **Working software is the primary measure of progress — not velocity, not story points, not burndown completeness.** PMI rejects vanity metrics.
3. **Customer collaboration is continuous, not contractual.** Real world treats the contract as the truth; PMI treats the conversation as the truth.
4. **Self-organizing teams choose HOW; the product owner decides WHAT and the customer decides WHY.** PMs/Scrum Masters who direct task assignment violate the principle.
5. **Simplicity — the art of maximizing the amount of work not done — is essential.** Real world rewards busy. PMI rewards eliminated work.

### Ethics Angle
- **Respect:** Self-organization requires respect for the team's capacity to decide. Override = ethics violation, not just process drift.
- **Honesty:** Information radiators must show real status; hiding red on a board to protect stakeholders breaches honesty.
- **Responsibility:** Sustainable pace is a PM responsibility — burning the team to hit a date violates this principle even if delivery succeeds.
- **Fairness:** All voices in retrospectives carry equal weight. PMs/Scrum Masters who suppress dissent fail Fairness.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Agile Manifesto — 4 Values** | Individuals & interactions > processes & tools; Working software > comprehensive documentation; Customer collaboration > contract negotiation; Responding to change > following a plan | Trap: right side is NOT dismissed — "while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more" |
| **12 Agile Principles** | Twelve statements from the Manifesto guiding agile behavior | Trap: memorizing numbers; PMI tests by behavior not principle number |
| **Empiricism** | Inspect and adapt based on transparent reality | Trap: planning more to feel certain; empiricism says shorten the loop instead |
| **Servant Leadership** | Leader removes impediments and serves the team; team self-organizes | Trap: confusing servant leadership with passive leadership — servant leaders still hold the team accountable to principles |
| **Sustainable Pace** | Constant velocity teams can maintain indefinitely | Trap: heroic sprints to recover scope; sustainable pace is a principle, not a preference |
| **Self-Organizing Team** | Team decides HOW work is done; chooses task allocation | Trap: PM assigning tasks in a sprint; even with good intent, it breaks the principle |
| **Cross-Functional Team** | All skills needed to deliver value present within the team | Trap: confusing cross-functional with multi-skilled per person; team has all skills, individuals do not need all |
| **Working Software / Working Product** | Primary measure of progress per Principle 7 | Trap: replacing with velocity, story points completed, or % requirements documented |
| **Information Radiator** | Highly visible, low-friction display of project state | Trap: assuming a tool dashboard satisfies this; if it requires clicks/logins, it is not radiating |
| **Definition of Done (DoD)** | Team's shared criteria for a story to be considered complete | Trap: DoD is owned by the team, not the PM; changes require team agreement |
| **Definition of Ready (DoR)** | Criteria a backlog item meets before being pulled into a sprint | Trap: confusing with DoD; DoR is upstream, DoD downstream |
| **Inspect & Adapt** | Continuous improvement cycle in retrospectives and reviews | Trap: only inspecting; PMI requires the adapt step to count |
| **Simplicity (Principle 10)** | Maximizing work NOT done | Trap: equating simplicity with low effort; it means deliberate scope minimization |
| **Reflection (Principle 12)** | Team regularly reflects on how to become more effective and tunes behavior | Trap: holding the ceremony but not changing anything — fails the principle |
| **Customer Collaboration (Value 3)** | Continuous customer involvement throughout delivery | Trap: customer involvement only at start and end; principle requires continuous |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Team consistently misses sprint goals due to overcommitment | Facilitate a retrospective conversation about capacity and forecasting; let team adjust commitment | Self-organizing teams own their forecast; PM does not unilaterally reduce it |
| Stakeholder demands a fixed-scope, fixed-date contract on agile work | Facilitate a conversation about iterative delivery; propose fixed-time with prioritized scope | Responding to change > following a plan; contract negotiation > collaboration is the wrong order |
| Team wants to skip retrospective because "nothing changes anyway" | Address the underlying issue — adapt is the missing half; do not eliminate the ceremony | Reflection without adaptation fails Principle 12; removing reflection breaks the loop entirely |
| Product owner is unavailable for sprint planning | Reschedule planning or escalate impediment; do not proceed without PO | Customer collaboration is the principle; proceeding without the PO violates it |
| Team is working weekends to recover from scope growth | Surface sustainable-pace breach; renegotiate scope or timeline with PO | Sustainable pace is a principle, not a stretch goal |
| New manager requires daily individual status updates from team members | Educate on agile principles; redirect to standup and information radiators | Pulling individuals out of team rhythm breaks self-organization and transparency |
| Two team members disagree on technical approach | Let the team resolve through discussion; intervene only if they cannot | Self-organization includes technical conflict resolution |
| Stakeholder asks for a comprehensive requirements document upfront | Offer just-enough documentation and continuous review touchpoints | Working product > comprehensive documentation; documentation is NOT eliminated, just not primary |
| Sprint review attendance drops | Investigate why; restructure to deliver more value to stakeholders | Working software/feedback is the principle — boring reviews are a PM failure |
| Team commits to more than they can finish to please the PO | Coach the PO on the principle of sustainable pace and the team on honest forecasting | Pleasing the PO at capacity cost breaches multiple principles |

**Two Escalation-Specific Scenarios (your weak spot):**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| A principle is being violated by a stakeholder outside the team (e.g., executive mandates overtime, fixed scope) | Facilitate first; if unresolved, escalate to sponsor with the specific principle in conflict and the cost of violation. Do not silently absorb. |
| A team member escalates directly to the sponsor about an impediment without raising it in the team first | Re-engage the team member, restore the impediment flow (team → Scrum Master/PM → sponsor), and examine why the trust to use the normal channel was missing |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

### The Agile Principle Decision Flow (PMI Style)

```
SITUATION ARISES
        │
        ▼
Does it violate a principle? (sustainable pace, self-org, working product, customer collab, simplicity, etc.)
    NO  → Use normal team process (backlog, ceremony, agreement)
    YES ↓
        ▼
Is the violation inside the team's control?
    YES → Coach team; adjust agreement in retrospective
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Can the PM/Scrum Master remove the impediment directly?
    YES → Act as servant leader; remove impediment; document
    NO  ↓
        ▼
ESCALATE to sponsor / leadership
   with the principle in conflict + impact + recommended action
        │
        ▼
Restore principle alignment; update team agreement if needed
```

### The 4 Values Mnemonic: **I-W-C-R**

> **I**ndividuals & interactions > processes & tools
> **W**orking product > comprehensive documentation
> **C**ustomer collaboration > contract negotiation
> **R**esponding to change > following a plan

Memory hook: **"I Will Choose Reality"** — when stem asks what to prefer, choose the left side; the right side still has value but is not primary.

### The 12 Principles — Cluster Memory

- **Customer & Value (1, 3, 7):** Early/continuous delivery · Working product is the measure · Deliver frequently
- **People & Team (4, 5, 6, 11):** Business + developers daily · Motivated individuals + trust · Face-to-face conversation · Self-organizing teams
- **Pace & Sustainability (8, 9):** Sustainable constant pace · Technical excellence + good design
- **Simplicity & Adaptation (2, 10, 12):** Welcome changing requirements · Simplicity (maximize work NOT done) · Reflect and adjust

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Principles still apply at governance level: continuous stakeholder engagement (collab over contract), iterative phase reviews (inspect-adapt), lessons learned (reflection)
- Documentation primary by nature, but discipline of "just enough" still applies — avoid documentation theater
- Change control rigid; the principle of "responding to change" expressed through structured change-request agility, not free-form rework

### Agile / Hybrid
- Ceremonies are the visible expression of principles — judge ceremonies by whether they uphold principles, not whether they happen
- Hybrid teams keep agile principles for delivery cadence and predictive governance for contracts/regulators; tailor at the boundary, not inside the team
- Scaled agile contexts (multiple teams) require explicit alignment on principle interpretation — e.g., what "customer collaboration" means when the customer is internal vs external

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** BSEC and Bangladesh Bank may require documentation outputs that look heavy; satisfy them with just-enough-traceability artifacts generated from working code/tests, not parallel write-ups
- Audit-heavy environments need DoD to include compliance evidence; this does not breach the principle, it embeds it
- Regulated environments often misinterpret "responding to change" — the principle is about welcoming change to maximize value, not bypassing change control
- **Public-sector / RFP-driven projects:** "contract negotiation over customer collaboration" is the dominant cultural reflex; counter by building continuous collaboration into the contract itself (T&M with sprints, ranged scope, governance check-ins)

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Assign tasks to team members based on their skills to optimize throughput" | Skill-matched assignment is efficient and reduces idle time | Violates self-organization; teams choose HOW. PMs/Scrum Masters who assign break Principle 11 |
| "Eliminate documentation to be truly agile" | Manifesto values working product over documentation | The right side is not zero; "just enough" documentation is the principle, not none |
| "Lock the scope at the start of the sprint and reject any changes" | Sprint commitment must be protected | Within-sprint change is discouraged but not forbidden; "responding to change" is a value; rigid lock against valuable change breaks the principle |
| "Adopt the full Scrum framework before discussing principles" | Frameworks are concrete; principles are abstract | Frameworks without principles produce cargo-cult agile; PMI explicitly tests whether you start with principles |
| "Hold a retrospective every sprint regardless of whether anything changes" | Ceremony discipline matters | Ceremony without adapt step fails Principle 12; PMI prefers a meaningful change less often than ritual without change |
| "Have the PM resolve technical disagreements between team members quickly" | Quick conflict resolution is good leadership | Self-organizing teams resolve technical disputes themselves; PM intervention should be last resort |
| "Increase the number of standups to improve team communication" | More communication = better | Standups are time-boxed for a reason; more meetings violates simplicity (Principle 10) |
| "Ask the team to work extra hours to meet the upcoming release" | Senior PMs protect commitments | Sustainable pace is a principle; trading it for one release is a breach with downstream cost |
| "Convert all stakeholder feedback into formal change requests before discussion" | Change control discipline is core PM | Customer collaboration is continuous; gating every conversation through a change request kills the principle |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are managing a predictive banking compliance project that has adopted "agile-style" status reporting at the sponsor's request. The sponsor instructs you to colour the dashboard green because "amber will spook the board even though we'll catch up next phase." The current burndown shows the team will miss the milestone by three weeks based on actuals. The project itself is not agile; only the reporting style is borrowed. What should you do FIRST?

A. Colour the dashboard green since the project is not actually agile and the sponsor owns external communications.
B. Refuse to alter the colour, present the actual status, and meet privately with the sponsor to understand and address their underlying concern about board perception.
C. Escalate the sponsor's request to the project steering committee as a transparency violation.
D. Show the milestone in green but add a footnote about the schedule risk so the data is technically present.

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

You are coaching an agile team building a securities-trading platform. Over the last four sprints, the team has run retrospectives, but no team-agreement changes or process adjustments have been made. Team members say "nothing ever changes, but we still go." Sprint goals are being met. What is the BEST action?

A. Cancel future retrospectives since the team is delivering successfully without changes.
B. Facilitate a conversation about why the team is not surfacing adjustments, and work with the team to commit to one experiment per retrospective.
C. Mandate that each retrospective produce at least three action items going forward.
D. Replace retrospectives with a quarterly improvement workshop run by the PM.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid digital-wallet project. A senior business stakeholder demands a comprehensive 80-page requirements specification before any development begins, citing "agile is fine for software but not for our business case." The product owner agrees just enough documentation has already been produced. The stakeholder threatens to block release approval. What should you do FIRST?

A. Direct the team to produce the 80-page specification to preserve the relationship and avoid release-approval risk.
B. Meet with the stakeholder to understand the specific information needs behind the request and explore whether tailored, lighter artifacts can address those needs.
C. Escalate the stakeholder's threat to the sponsor as a project risk.
D. Refuse the request and proceed with the planned delivery approach since the PO is satisfied.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A predictive infrastructure project is showing signs the team would benefit from agile principles even though the contractual approach is fixed. Stakeholder engagement is weak, change requests are processed slowly, and morale is low. The sponsor has asked you to "introduce some agility." Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Introduce iterative stakeholder review checkpoints between phase gates.
B. Convert the contract to a time-and-materials arrangement immediately.
C. Establish a team retrospective at the end of each phase to apply inspect-and-adapt without changing delivery approach.
D. Adopt full Scrum ceremonies on top of the existing predictive plan.
E. Replace the project plan with a backlog and abandon phase gates.

---

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A new agile team is forming on a payments product. The PM wants to ensure principle alignment from day one. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Facilitate creation of the team agreement covering working hours, ceremonies, and conflict resolution.
2. Co-create the team's Definition of Done with the product owner and team.
3. Hold a kickoff conversation on the 4 values and 12 principles to align expectations.
4. Run the first sprint planning and commit to a goal.

A. 4 → 1 → 2 → 3
B. 3 → 1 → 2 → 4
C. 1 → 3 → 2 → 4
D. 2 → 1 → 3 → 4

**Q6 — EXCEPT Question (Predictive)**

Which of the following is NOT one of the four values in the Agile Manifesto?

A. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
B. Working software over comprehensive documentation
C. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
D. Responding to change over following a plan
E. Simplicity over complexity

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are the Scrum Master on a regulatory-reporting platform for a bank. During the sprint review, a stakeholder asks whether the new module passes the BSEC compliance check. The team has not yet completed that part of the Definition of Done; the lead developer signals you privately that "we'll close it tomorrow." Saying so in the review would create awkwardness with the regulator stakeholder present. What do you do?

A. State that the module is "substantially complete" and follow up after the review.
B. Confirm honestly that the compliance check is not yet complete, that it is in the Definition of Done, and that the team will close it before considering the story done.
C. Defer the question and offer to brief the stakeholder offline so the review can stay positive.
D. Mark the story as done now and treat the compliance closure as a separate task to be picked up next sprint.

---

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid trading-platform project. Business stakeholders want daily progress emails. The team finds the emails distract from delivery and resent the time it takes to compose them. The team uses a live kanban board and burnup chart accessible to all parties. Regulatory stakeholders require formal monthly status reports. What is the BEST approach?

A. Continue daily emails since stakeholder preference outweighs team preference.
B. Replace daily emails with the live information radiator for business stakeholders; produce the formal monthly report for regulators; coach business stakeholders on accessing radiators for real-time view.
C. Discontinue all status communication and rely on the kanban board alone.
D. Send daily emails only to the sponsor and update everyone else weekly.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project. A senior executive has mandated that the team work weekends for the next month to catch up on a slipped milestone. Two of your developers have told you privately they are close to burnout. The team retrospective has flagged unsustainable pace twice in three sprints. You have spoken to the executive once with no result. What is the BEST next step?

A. Continue the weekend work since the executive has authority and the milestone matters.
B. Proactively brief the sponsor with the principle in conflict (sustainable pace), the retrospective evidence, the burnout risk, and a recommended renegotiation of scope or date.
C. Allow each team member to opt out individually so volunteers carry the load.
D. Push the team for one more weekend, then escalate if the milestone is still at risk.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

You are coaching an organization that has adopted Scrum ceremonies across all teams but is not seeing the productivity and engagement gains it expected. Leadership still mandates fixed scope, fixed deadlines, and individual performance evaluation by story points completed. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Engage leadership to surface which agile principles are in conflict with current mandates and quantify the cost of misalignment.
B. Roll back the Scrum ceremonies until leadership is ready to support the principles.
C. Coach leadership on alternative measures of progress (working product, customer satisfaction) instead of story points.
D. Increase the number of Scrum ceremonies to reinforce agile behavior.
E. Work with leadership to redesign performance evaluation to align with team-level outcomes.
F. Escalate the leadership mandates to a higher executive committee for override.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty) and the principle behind information radiators both forbid altering status data to suit perception. Even on a non-agile project, the borrowed reporting style must reflect actual progress. The PM's first move is to refuse the alteration AND engage the sponsor on the underlying concern, which is what protects the relationship without compromising integrity. Option A is dishonest. Option C escalates to ethics committee before engaging the sponsor. Option D is selective disclosure dressed up as transparency.

**Mindset Lesson:** Honesty does not require ceremony — even borrowed agile-style reports must show real status.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Principle 12 requires both reflection AND adjustment — reflection alone fails the principle. The team is running the ceremony but skipping the adapt step. The Scrum Master/PM facilitates a conversation to surface why, and the team commits to a concrete experiment. Cancelling (A) eliminates the loop. Mandating action items (C) is coercive and breaks self-organization. Replacing with a PM-run workshop (D) centralizes work the team should own.

**Mindset Lesson:** A ceremony without behavioral change fails the principle — fix the adapt step, do not delete the ceremony.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder Performance Domain and Agile Practice Guide both expect the PM to engage and understand before acting or escalating. The principle is "working product over comprehensive documentation" — the right side has value. Lighter, tailored artifacts may satisfy the stakeholder's actual information need. Option A produces theater documentation that wastes team capacity. Option C escalates without engaging the stakeholder. Option D refuses without dialogue, which the principle does not require.

**Mindset Lesson:** "Working product over comprehensive documentation" does NOT mean refusing documentation — it means matching the artifact to the actual need.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** A (iterative review checkpoints) and C (phase-end retrospectives) introduce inspect-and-adapt and continuous engagement without breaking the contractual approach — they apply principles within the predictive shell. Option B (contract conversion) is unilateral and ignores governance/legal implications. Option D (full Scrum on top of a predictive plan) creates ceremony theater. Option E (replace plan with backlog) abandons the committed delivery approach without authorization.

**Mindset Lesson:** Principles can be introduced inside predictive projects through tailored touchpoints — full framework adoption is not required to gain principle benefit.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: B (3 → 1 → 2 → 4)**

**PMI Justification:** Per the Agile Practice Guide, a new team aligns on values and principles first (3), then establishes its working agreement (1), then co-creates the DoD with the PO (2), and only then commits to a first sprint (4). Skipping principle alignment risks ceremony-without-principle. Defining DoD before the agreement skips conflict-resolution rules. Sprint planning before alignment commits the team to scope they do not yet have shared rules for.

**Mindset Lesson:** Principles → agreements → DoD → commitment — sequence builds the foundation before delivery.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: E**

**PMI Justification:** "Simplicity over complexity" is NOT one of the four Manifesto values. The four values are listed in A–D. Simplicity IS a principle (Principle 10) but appears as a guiding principle, not a value pair. Knowing the difference between values (4) and principles (12) is recurring PMI exam content.

**Mindset Lesson:** Four values; twelve principles — simplicity is a principle, not a value.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty) requires accurate communication regardless of audience comfort, especially with regulator stakeholders present. The Definition of Done is the team's transparency contract — incomplete DoD = story not done. Option A ("substantially complete") is dishonest hedging. Option C defers the answer to avoid awkwardness. Option D bends the DoD to fit the calendar — a breach of team agreement and integrity.

**Mindset Lesson:** DoD is the line — when asked if work is done, the answer is "DoD met" or "DoD not met," never "almost."

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 and the Agile Practice Guide both endorse tailoring communication to audience and using information radiators to satisfy real-time visibility needs without taxing the team. Daily emails are push communication that competes with delivery time; the radiator is pull, available on demand. Regulatory monthly reports remain because compliance requires them. Option A privileges preference over principle. Option C ignores regulator obligation. Option D creates information asymmetry between stakeholders.

**Mindset Lesson:** Push communication must justify itself; if a pull radiator can serve the same information need, prefer it.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Sustainable pace (Principle 8) is being breached, retrospectives have flagged it twice, and one direct conversation with the executive has failed. PMBOK 7 Leadership and Team Performance Domains expect the PM to escalate with evidence and a recommendation, not to absorb the breach. Option A absorbs the breach and risks team health. Option C creates voluntary martyrdom — the principle is team-level. Option D risks burnout before acting on evidence already present.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sustainable pace is escalable when leadership mandates conflict with it — with principle, evidence, and recommendation.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:**
- **A:** Surfacing the principle conflict with quantified cost is the PM's job per PMBOK 7 Leadership Domain.
- **C:** Working product and customer satisfaction are the principle-aligned measures of progress; story points are a planning tool.
- **E:** Performance evaluation aligned to team outcomes supports self-organization and motivated-individuals principles.
- B is wrong: rolling back ceremonies abandons the loop entirely; coaching is the correct intervention.
- D is wrong: more ceremonies do not solve a principle problem.
- F is wrong: bypassing leadership to a higher committee skips engagement with the very people who must change behavior.

**Mindset Lesson:** Cargo-cult agile is fixed by aligning principles and incentives — not by adding ceremonies, not by escalating around the people who hold the mandate.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**AGILE PRINCIPLES — Exam Morning Card**

- 4 values + 12 principles. Right side of each value still has value — left side is preferred.
- **I-W-C-R:** Individuals · Working product · Customer collab · Responding to change.
- Ceremonies without principles = theater. PMI tests behavior, not ceremony adherence.
- Working product = the primary measure of progress. Not velocity, not story points, not % docs done.
- Self-organizing teams choose HOW; PO decides WHAT; customer decides WHY. PM facilitates and removes impediments.
- Sustainable pace is a principle, not a preference — escalable when violated.
- Simplicity = maximizing the work NOT done. Add a ceremony only after considering removal.
- Reflection without adaptation fails Principle 12. The adapt step is what counts.
- Customer collaboration is continuous; contract is the floor, not the truth.
- Tailor for regulation/audit by embedding compliance in DoD — do not bypass it, do not duplicate it.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *Agile principles are escalable — when leadership mandates breach a principle, escalate with the principle named, evidence attached, and recommendation made.*
