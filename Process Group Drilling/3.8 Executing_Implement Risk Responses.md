# PMP Exam Master Brief: Implement Risk Responses

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Implement Risk Responses is the Executing process where approved risk response actions from the risk register are actually carried out by risk owners and action owners. It is the execution bridge between Plan Risk Responses (where strategies are designed) and Monitor Risks (where effectiveness is evaluated) — making it the process that converts risk plans into actual risk controls. Exam weight sits within the Risk domain at roughly 8–10% of the PMP exam, with significant cross-domain bleed into Resource Management (because risk owners are often functional team members the PM does not directly control), Stakeholder Engagement (because resistant stakeholders can block response execution), and Procurement (because risk transfer operates through contracts). The most important exam distinction is between planning a response and executing one: a response that exists only in the register provides zero protection. The PM facilitates response execution but does not personally implement it — the risk owner is accountable for the response strategy, while action owners may carry out specific tasks within it. Secondary risks (created by implementing a response) and residual risks (remaining after implementation) both require explicit treatment and ownership. Risk responses that affect project baselines require formal change requests through integrated change control, regardless of urgency. In agile contexts, impediments are treated as actualized risks, and the scrum master's impediment-removal role is the agile expression of this process.

**In one sentence:** Implement Risk Responses is the executing discipline of ensuring every approved risk response is actively carried out by the accountable owner at the right time, with secondary and residual risks captured and owned throughout.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Risk register drives formal response assignments with named risk owners, trigger conditions, and due dates; periodic risk reviews confirm implementation status; change requests triggered when responses require baseline adjustments; risk audits evaluate process compliance |
| Agile | Impediments in the impediment log are actualized risks requiring immediate response; the scrum master removes impediments, functioning as the primary risk response implementer; retrospectives surface new risks and evaluate response effectiveness; risk-adjusted backlog includes response tasks as explicit work items |
| Hybrid | Formal risk register with named owners for strategic and high-impact risks; agile impediment board for delivery-level tactical risks; governance layer receives formal risk status reports; delivery team uses retrospectives for continuous risk response evaluation |

**Top 5 PMI-isms:**

1. The PM facilitates risk response execution; the risk owner implements it — real-world PMs absorb all risk ownership personally, creating single points of failure and obscuring accountability.
2. A planned but unimplemented risk response provides no protection — real-world PMs equate "planned" with "managed," which PMI treats as a critical and exam-testable gap.
3. Every risk response may create new risks (secondary risks) that require the same full treatment as primary risks — real-world PMs focus only on the primary risk being addressed and neglect what the response introduces.
4. Residual risks must be explicitly accepted and owned after response implementation — real-world PMs assume mitigation eliminates the risk entirely; PMI requires a conscious acceptance decision for whatever remains.
5. When a risk owner fails to implement a response, the PM must first diagnose the obstacle before escalating — real-world PMs escalate immediately when owners miss action; PMI requires understanding whether the barrier is a resource, authority, or capability gap the PM can remove.

**Ethics Angle:**

- **Responsibility:** The PM is responsible for ensuring all responses are assigned and tracked; the risk owner is accountable for executing their assigned response — both roles carry defined obligations.
- **Honesty:** If a risk response is failing or has not been implemented, the PM must report this accurately in the risk register and status reporting; understating implementation gaps is a form of misrepresentation.
- **Fairness:** Risk response ownership must be assigned to the person with genuine capability and authority to execute it, not based on convenience or organizational politics; assigning ownership to someone lacking resources is an unfair and ineffective delegation.
- **Respect:** Risk owners must be given adequate time, resources, and authority to implement their assigned responses; assigning ownership without enabling execution disrespects the role.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Risk Owner | Person assigned responsibility for monitoring a specific risk and ensuring its response strategy is implemented | Do not confuse with Action Owner — risk owner owns the strategy and outcome; action owner executes a specific task within it |
| Action Owner | Person responsible for executing one or more specific risk response tasks within the broader response strategy | An action owner may be different from the risk owner; both must be explicitly named in the risk register |
| Risk Register | Central document containing identified risks, probability/impact assessments, response strategies, owners, triggers, and implementation status | Updated as an output of this process to reflect implementation status changes; not a static planning artifact |
| Risk Report | Summary-level document reporting overall project risk exposure; updated to reflect execution of risk responses | More executive-facing than the risk register; also updated as an output of Implement Risk Responses |
| Secondary Risk | A new risk created as a direct result of implementing a risk response | Always plan for secondary risks when selecting and implementing responses; the exam consistently tests whether candidates identify them |
| Residual Risk | Risk that remains after response implementation; must be explicitly accepted, not ignored | Residual risk is not a process failure — it is an accepted remainder; PMI requires a conscious ownership decision, not silence |
| Workaround | Unplanned response to an unidentified risk that has occurred, or a response invoked when the planned approach has failed | Workarounds are reactive and unplanned; they are not interchangeable with contingency plans, which are pre-planned and pre-approved |
| Contingency Plan | Pre-planned response triggered by a specific risk trigger condition or warning sign | Activated when the defined trigger is met — not before as a precaution; premature activation wastes contingency resources |
| Fallback Plan | Response activated when the primary contingency plan proves insufficient or ineffective | Fallback = plan B after plan A fails; distinct from contingency, which is the first planned response |
| Risk Trigger (Warning Sign) | A pre-defined event or condition indicating a risk is about to materialize or has occurred | Triggers activate contingency plans; failure to monitor for and act on triggers is a response execution failure |
| Threat Responses | Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept — strategies for negative risks | Accept does not mean ignore — active acceptance includes a contingency plan or reserve; passive acceptance means monitoring only |
| Opportunity Responses | Exploit, Share, Enhance, Accept — strategies for positive risks | Exploit eliminates uncertainty to ensure the opportunity occurs; Enhance increases probability or impact; Exploit is stronger than Enhance |
| Change Request | Required when a risk response affects scope, schedule, cost, or quality baselines | Risk responses that require baseline changes must go through Perform Integrated Change Control; no self-authorization by the PM |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A risk owner has not implemented the assigned response by its due date" | Meet with the risk owner to understand the obstacle preventing implementation | PMI requires diagnosing barriers before escalating; the owner may face resource, authority, or information gaps the PM can remove |
| "A risk response was implemented but the risk level has not decreased" | Update the risk register and evaluate whether an alternative response strategy is needed | An ineffective response is a monitoring finding that requires response revision, not simply more implementation effort |
| "A new risk is identified during response implementation" | Add the new risk to the risk register and follow the identification/assessment process | New risks discovered during execution are treated identically to any other risk — register, assess, plan, assign owner |
| "The team implements a workaround for an unplanned risk event" | Document the workaround in the risk register and issue log; update the project management plan as needed | Workarounds must be documented to preserve organizational knowledge; undocumented workarounds disappear from the record |
| "A risk response will require additional budget not in the current baseline" | Submit a change request through integrated change control before implementing the response | Any response affecting a cost baseline requires formal authorization; the PM cannot self-approve a baseline change |
| "A secondary risk is identified after implementing a mitigation strategy" | Assess the secondary risk and add it to the risk register with an assigned owner | Secondary risks are as real as primary risks and require the same full treatment immediately upon identification |
| "The stem describes a risk response that is planned and documented but not yet started" | Recognize that an unimplemented response provides no protection — treat as a control gap | PMI consistently distinguishes between planning a response and executing it; the register entry alone does not manage the risk |
| "A contingency plan trigger condition has been reached but the response has not been activated" | Activate the contingency plan immediately per the risk management plan | Trigger conditions exist to prompt timely action; delay after trigger activation increases the potential impact of the risk event |

**Escalation and Weak-Spot Sub-Table:**

| Escalation Scenario | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Risk owner repeatedly fails to act and cannot explain why" | Escalate to the risk owner's functional manager after documenting the failure and the PM's attempted resolution steps | The PM must demonstrate due diligence before involving functional management; undocumented escalation is procedurally weak |
| "A critical risk response requires vendor action outside the PM's contractual authority" | Escalate to the sponsor and procurement team; this may require formal contract action | Vendor obligations are contractual matters; the PM cannot compel vendor action without procurement involvement |

### 6. Visual Memory Aid

```
IMPLEMENT RISK RESPONSES DECISION FLOW
========================================

Risk response assigned in risk register?
        |
       YES --> Has the trigger condition been met?
                    |
                   YES --> Is the risk owner executing the response?
                                |
                               YES --> Monitor effectiveness --> Update risk register
                                |
                                NO --> Diagnose obstacle --> Remove barrier or escalate
                    |
                    NO --> Continue monitoring for trigger; no activation yet
        |
        NO --> Planning gap; return to Plan Risk Responses

AFTER IMPLEMENTATION:
  Did the response create a new risk?   --> Secondary risk: register, assess, assign owner
  Does risk remain at any level?        --> Residual risk: explicitly accept and document
  Did the response affect any baseline? --> Change request required before/concurrent with action
```

**Mnemonic:** ROAST — Register, Owner, Activate, Secondary, Track

**Memory Hook:** "If you don't ROAST the risk, it will cook the project."

**Mnemonic Unpack:**
- **R** = Register — every risk and its response must be documented in the risk register
- **O** = Owner — every risk requires an accountable, named risk owner
- **A** = Activate — responses activate at the trigger condition, not before and not after
- **S** = Secondary — every implemented response may create a new secondary risk requiring its own treatment
- **T** = Track — implementation status must be continuously tracked, not assumed complete once assigned

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project:**
- Formal risk register with named owners, trigger dates, implementation milestones, and status tracking.
- Change requests required when responses affect cost, schedule, scope, or quality baselines.
- Periodic risk reviews (weekly or monthly depending on project phase) confirm implementation status against the plan.
- Risk audits evaluate whether risk management processes are being followed correctly — distinct from risk reviews.
- Contingency reserves are formally managed; access requires documented trigger activation.

**Agile/Hybrid:**
- Impediments in the agile impediment log are actualized risks; the scrum master's removal of impediments is the agile expression of Implement Risk Responses.
- Risk-adjusted backlogs explicitly include risk response tasks as backlog items with story points or effort estimates.
- Retrospectives serve as the primary mechanism for evaluating response effectiveness and identifying new or evolving risks.
- Hybrid projects maintain a formal risk register for strategic and cross-project risks while using impediment boards for sprint-level operational risks.
- The PM's role in agile shifts from assigning responses to removing blockers that prevent the team from responding.

**Industry/Regulatory Factors:**
- **Banking/Capital Markets/BSEC:** Regulatory risks (compliance breaches, audit findings, capital adequacy threats) require formal documented responses with named owners, implementation evidence, and audit trail records; informal or verbal responses are insufficient.
- Financial sector risk responses involving legal or regulatory exposure typically require legal and compliance sign-off before the response is implemented.
- Industries with safety-critical risks (construction, pharmaceuticals, aviation) face mandatory response implementation timelines; a delayed safety-related risk response is not a project management gap — it is a regulatory violation.
- Cross-border projects face jurisdiction-specific risk transfer mechanisms; insurance requirements, indemnification clauses, and liability caps vary by country and require local legal review.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "The PM should implement the risk response themselves when the risk owner is unavailable" | Experienced PMs step in to keep things moving; they feel personally accountable for every project outcome | PMI requires the PM to resolve the ownership gap by finding a replacement owner or escalating; absorbing all response ownership creates accountability confusion and is not scalable |
| "Activate the contingency plan as a precaution even before the trigger condition is met" | Experienced PMs use contingency resources proactively when they sense a risk is about to materialize | Contingency plans are triggered by specific pre-defined conditions; premature activation consumes reserves intended for actual trigger events and bypasses the risk management process |
| "A workaround is the same as a contingency plan and can be used interchangeably" | Both address risk events; experienced PMs use these terms interchangeably in practice | Workarounds are unplanned responses to unexpected events; contingency plans are pre-planned and pre-approved — they enter the process through entirely different channels |
| "Ignore residual risk if it is minor or unlikely" | Minor risks feel like noise; experienced PMs focus their attention on high-impact items | PMI requires all residual risks to be explicitly accepted and owned regardless of magnitude; ignoring any risk — even a minor one — is a process failure |
| "Transfer a risk by verbally agreeing with a vendor to take it on" | Verbal agreements feel efficient and sufficient in established relationships | Risk transfer must be contractually documented to be legally binding and auditable; a verbal agreement transfers nothing in a PMI context |
| "Once a risk response is implemented, close the risk in the register" | Closing the risk signals task completion and good register hygiene | Risks are closed only after monitoring confirms the threat or opportunity has fully passed; early closure creates blind spots for residual and secondary risks |
| "Secondary risks are less important than primary risks and can be addressed in the next planning cycle" | Experienced PMs treat secondary risks as follow-on items; primary risks feel more urgent | Secondary risks are as real and potentially as severe as primary risks; they require immediate registration, assessment, and ownership upon identification |
| "The PM is the appropriate risk owner for all project risks" | PMs feel total accountability and ownership for project outcomes; assigning themselves all risk ownership feels responsible | Risk owners must have the authority and domain capability to execute their assigned response; the PM typically cannot execute responses across all functional domains |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM is managing a construction project in a jurisdiction with mandatory daily safety inspections. The risk register includes a high-probability structural risk with an approved mitigation response: daily inspections by a certified safety engineer. The project is two weeks behind schedule due to supply chain issues. The sponsor calls the PM and instructs them to suspend the daily inspections for two weeks to accelerate construction activity, arguing the structural risk has been "substantially mitigated already" and that inspections are slowing progress. The safety regulation requires daily inspections without exception. Workers will be on-site throughout the suspension period. What should the PM do?

A. Follow the sponsor's instruction since schedule recovery serves the project's overall objectives and the risk is assessed as partially mitigated.
B. Consult the project's legal advisor about the sponsor's instruction before deciding how to proceed.
C. Continue implementing the approved safety response and communicate to the sponsor that regulatory requirements cannot be waived by project preference or schedule pressure.
D. Negotiate with the sponsor to reduce inspection frequency to every other day as a compromise between schedule and safety.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony/Engagement**

During a sprint retrospective on a software development project, the team identifies that two impediments logged at the start of the sprint — one involving a blocked API dependency from an external team and one involving unresolved acceptance criteria from the product owner — were never resolved during the sprint. The scrum master confirms both were escalated to the product owner but no action followed. The product owner did not attend the retrospective. Which action BEST reflects the agile approach to addressing these unresolved risk responses?

A. Log both impediments as product defects and place them in the next sprint backlog for resolution.
B. Document the unresolved impediments in retrospective action items with specific owners and due dates, and escalate the product owner's absence as a separate impediment requiring attention.
C. Remove both impediments from the log since the sprint is closed and the items are now historical rather than active.
D. Assign the scrum master to resolve both impediments directly before the next sprint planning session begins.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project PM is managing a financial services system migration. The risk register includes a critical data integrity risk with an approved mitigation response: a mandatory data validation checkpoint before production cutover. Three days before the scheduled cutover, the technical lead informs the PM that executing the validation checkpoint fully will require an additional 10 hours of unplanned work, pushing the cutover date back by two days. The business sponsor has communicated that any delay will trigger a contract penalty clause worth $50,000. The PM is being pressured to skip the checkpoint to preserve the original date. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Instruct the technical lead to compress the validation work to fit within the original schedule without the full checkpoint.
B. Notify the sponsor of the delay and immediately request a penalty waiver from the client.
C. Assess whether skipping the validation checkpoint is a viable risk trade-off given the financial penalty.
D. Document the situation, assess the impact of both options against the risk register, and submit a change request for the schedule impact.

**Q4 — Two-Correct Pick-Best (Predictive)**

A PM conducting a risk register review discovers that three approved risk responses have passed their assigned due dates without implementation. Two risk owners report they lack the budget authority to execute their responses. The third risk owner left the organization three weeks ago and was never replaced. The PM must address all three gaps. Which single action BEST addresses all three situations comprehensively?

A. Request emergency supplemental funding for the two under-resourced responses and find a volunteer replacement owner for the third immediately.
B. Submit change requests for the two budget-constrained responses and escalate the ownership vacancy to the functional manager.
C. Conduct a comprehensive risk review session documenting all implementation gaps, prepare change requests for the budget-constrained responses with updated plans, and formally reassign the vacant ownership role.
D. Escalate all three gaps to the sponsor as a combined risk management failure report requiring executive intervention.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile PM identifies a new risk during sprint execution that was not previously logged. Place the following actions in the CORRECT PMI-aligned sequence:

1. Assign a named risk owner to the newly identified risk.
2. Identify the risk and add it to the risk-adjusted backlog and risk register.
3. Determine and document the appropriate risk response strategy.
4. Assess the risk's probability and potential impact.

A. 2, 4, 3, 1
B. 2, 1, 4, 3
C. 4, 2, 3, 1
D. 1, 2, 4, 3

**Q6 — EXCEPT/NOT**

All of the following are outputs of the Implement Risk Responses process EXCEPT:

A. Change requests
B. Project management plan updates
C. Risk register updates
D. Risk response strategies

**Q7 — Ethics/Honesty Hardline**

A PM discovers that a team member implemented a risk response that was not approved or documented in the risk register. The unauthorized response was effective — the risk was avoided. However, the response consumed $15,000 from the project's contingency reserve without a change request or sponsor authorization. The team member argues that since the outcome was positive and the project benefited, formal documentation is unnecessary. How should the PM handle this situation?

A. Accept the outcome since the risk was avoided and no harm resulted from the project's perspective.
B. Recognize the team member's initiative informally and document the outcome in lessons learned for future reference.
C. Require the team member to submit a retroactive change request documenting the unauthorized expenditure and update the issue log accordingly.
D. Report the unauthorized use of contingency reserves to the sponsor as an immediate budget deviation requiring reconciliation.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Agile or Hybrid)**

A distributed agile team is managing a product development project. A high-impact technical risk — potential failure of a third-party integration — has been assigned to a senior developer as risk owner. During the sprint review, a key stakeholder asks about the status of the risk response. The assigned risk owner is not present at the review, and risk status is not reflected anywhere on the team's visible risk board. The stakeholder is visibly concerned. What communication and process approach BEST ensures this situation does not recur?

A. Require the risk owner to attend all future sprint reviews as a mandatory attendance condition.
B. Add risk response status as a standing agenda item in sprint reviews and establish a team norm that the risk board is updated before each review.
C. Assign a dedicated backup communicator for each risk owner whose role is to provide status when the primary owner is unavailable.
D. Distribute a written risk status report to all stakeholders 24 hours before each sprint review as a precautionary measure.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project PM is managing a regulatory compliance initiative at a banking institution. A high-priority risk response — an independent compliance audit required as part of the risk management plan — was assigned to a specialist vendor as a contractual deliverable. The vendor has now informed the PM that due to unexpected internal resource constraints, they cannot complete the audit within the agreed timeline. The regulatory filing deadline for the bank is fixed and non-negotiable. Failure to submit the audit with the filing means the bank cannot meet its regulatory obligation. What is the PM's BEST course of action?

A. Grant the vendor additional time to complete the audit and increase monitoring frequency to manage the schedule risk.
B. Escalate immediately to procurement and legal to assess the contract breach situation and simultaneously explore alternative audit providers to protect the filing deadline.
C. Direct the bank's internal compliance team to perform the audit as a workaround, bypassing the vendor entirely.
D. Notify the regulator of the anticipated delay and request a deadline extension before pursuing any other action.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A PM reviews the outcomes of three recently implemented risk responses on a complex predictive project. Response A successfully and completely eliminated the identified threat. Response B reduced the probability of the risk event from High to Low, but the threat still exists at the lower probability level. Response C partially mitigated its target risk but in doing so introduced an unexpected new risk not previously identified. Which THREE actions should the PM take?

A. Close Response A's risk entry in the register since the threat has been fully and verifiably eliminated.
B. Close Response B's risk entry in the register since the response was successfully implemented and the probability was reduced.
C. Update the risk register to reflect Response B's reduced probability, document the remaining exposure as residual risk, and record the formal acceptance of that residual.
D. Register the new risk created by Response C as a secondary risk, assess its probability and impact, and assign an owner.
E. Discontinue Response C's mitigation strategy entirely and revert to the original unmitigated risk state.
F. Conduct a full risk audit across all three responses to assess process compliance before taking any register updates.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Responsibility): PMs must comply with applicable laws and regulations. PMBOK 7 Stewardship domain requires protecting the well-being of workers and adhering to legal obligations. A regulatory requirement for daily inspections cannot be waived by sponsor preference or schedule pressure — it is a legal constraint, not a project preference. The approved risk response must continue.
- A fails: following a sponsor's instruction to violate a safety regulation is an ethics violation; sponsor authority is bounded by legal obligations.
- B fails: consulting legal counsel is a reasonable secondary step but is not the primary action; the PM's first obligation is to continue the approved safety response.
- D fails: negotiating a compromise that still violates the regulatory minimum is not a valid option; frequency reduction below the legal requirement creates legal and safety exposure regardless of sponsor approval.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor authority ends where legal and regulatory safety obligations begin; the PM's ethical accountability to workers and the law is not subject to negotiation.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide: retrospectives are the vehicle for identifying process improvements and unresolved impediments; unresolved impediments carry forward with explicit ownership and due dates. The product owner's absence is itself an impediment to risk response execution and must be escalated as such.
- A fails: classifying risk impediments as product defects misrepresents their nature and routes them through the wrong process; defects are quality items, not risk responses.
- C fails: closing impediments because the sprint ended is a process failure; unresolved impediments are active risks that persist regardless of sprint boundaries.
- D fails: the scrum master resolving all impediments directly violates team self-organization principles and creates a dependency bottleneck.

**Mindset Lesson:** Unresolved risk impediments from a closed sprint remain active risks; they carry forward with named owners and dates, and the retrospective is the mechanism to formalize this.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Integration Management: any action affecting a baseline requires a change request through integrated change control. The PM must assess both options against the risk register (data integrity risk vs. contract penalty risk) and submit a change request rather than making a unilateral decision. Skipping an approved risk response without formal authorization is itself a risk management process failure.
- A fails: compressing the validation work unilaterally may still fail to meet the quality threshold and bypasses the change request process.
- B fails: notifying the sponsor and requesting a waiver is a subsequent action; the PM must first assess impact and prepare a change request to present with the notification.
- C fails: assessing whether to skip an approved safety/quality risk response without going through change control is unauthorized; the PM cannot self-authorize the removal of an approved control.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a risk response affects a baseline or when implementing it conflicts with another constraint, the change request process governs — PM judgment alone is not sufficient authorization.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk and Integration domains: a comprehensive response addresses all identified gaps systematically. C documents all failures, raises appropriate change requests for the budget-constrained responses, and formally resolves the ownership vacancy — addressing all three situations in a single coordinated action.
- A fails: requesting emergency funding without a change request bypasses integrated change control; finding a volunteer without formal reassignment lacks accountability.
- B fails: correct in direction but incomplete — it addresses two situations without a comprehensive review that captures the full scope of the implementation failure.
- D fails: escalating to the sponsor before the PM has assessed, documented, and prepared solutions abdicates PM responsibility and provides the sponsor with a problem without options.

**Mindset Lesson:** Multiple simultaneous implementation failures require a comprehensive, documented response that addresses root causes and formalizes corrective actions — piecemeal fixes leave gaps.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A (2, 4, 3, 1)
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk Performance Domain: the standard sequence is identify → assess → plan response → assign owner. A risk cannot be properly planned without assessment, and ownership assignment is most meaningful after the response strategy is defined. The sequence ensures each step builds on accurate information from the prior step.
- B fails: assigning an owner before assessment and response planning means the owner has no defined responsibility to take on.
- C fails: assessing before identifying/registering the risk is not procedurally sound; the risk must be captured before assessment can be formally conducted.
- D fails: assigning ownership first puts accountability before accountability is defined by a response strategy.

**Mindset Lesson:** Risk management always follows identify → assess → plan → assign, even in agile contexts; skipping or reordering steps undermines the integrity of the response.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** Risk response strategies (Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept for threats; Exploit, Share, Enhance, Accept for opportunities) are outputs of Plan Risk Responses, not Implement Risk Responses. Implement Risk Responses executes pre-defined strategies; it does not create or select them. The outputs of this process include change requests, project management plan updates, and risk register updates.
- A is a valid output (change requests when responses affect baselines).
- B is a valid output (project management plan updates).
- C is a valid output (risk register updates reflecting implementation status).

**Mindset Lesson:** Risk response strategies are planned in Plan Risk Responses and executed in Implement Risk Responses — these are distinct processes with distinct outputs; confusing them is the most common exam error on this topic.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Responsibility and Honesty): unauthorized expenditure of contingency reserves — even for a positive outcome — violates the PM's obligation to follow approved processes. A retroactive change request maintains budget integrity and the audit trail. A good outcome does not legitimize an unauthorized process deviation.
- A fails: accepting unauthorized expenditure normalizes process violations and undermines budget governance.
- B fails: recognizing the initiative without formalizing the expenditure leaves the budget record inaccurate and the process violation unresolved.
- D fails: escalating to the sponsor before completing the change request process is premature; the PM should initiate the retroactive formalization first, then inform the sponsor as part of the change request review.

**Mindset Lesson:** Positive outcomes do not retroactively legitimize unauthorized process deviations; document and formalize immediately regardless of the result.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk and Communications domains: the root problem is a systemic process gap — risk status is not integrated into the regular sprint review communication cadence. Adding risk status as a standing agenda item with a pre-review update norm addresses the structural cause rather than managing individual behavior or adding new communication layers.
- A fails: mandating individual attendance solves the person-dependency but not the structural information gap; the information would still not be visible if the owner attended but the board was not updated.
- C fails: adding a backup communicator creates an information relay layer that introduces latency and potential distortion without fixing the visibility gap.
- D fails: a separate pre-review written report duplicates effort and creates a parallel information stream rather than integrating risk status into the existing review structure.

**Mindset Lesson:** Systemic communication gaps in risk response status require structural fixes — standing agenda items and team norms — not individual behavioral mandates or new parallel reports.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk, Procurement, and Integration domains: a vendor failure to deliver a contractual risk response deliverable is simultaneously a contract breach and a project risk event with regulatory consequences. Procurement and legal must be engaged immediately to assess remedies, and alternative providers must be explored in parallel given the non-negotiable deadline. Time-sensitive regulatory obligations require multi-track action, not sequential decision-making.
- A fails: granting additional time without contractual remedy exploration ignores the fixed regulatory deadline and rewards non-performance.
- C fails: directing internal compliance to perform the audit may be a viable option but requires procurement and legal guidance to assess whether it voids the vendor contract or creates scope conflicts; it cannot be decided unilaterally by the PM.
- D fails: notifying the regulator of a delay before exhausting internal remedies prematurely accepts failure and may not be necessary if alternative providers can be engaged.

**Mindset Lesson:** Vendor contract breaches with regulatory consequences require immediate multi-track escalation to procurement and legal — not sequential workaround attempts.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, C, D
**PMI Justification:**
- A: Response A fully eliminated the threat; this is the correct and only appropriate time to close a risk entry — when the threat is verifiably gone.
- C: Response B produced partial mitigation; the remaining exposure is residual risk requiring documented acceptance and ongoing ownership, not register closure.
- D: The new risk from Response C is a secondary risk by definition; it requires full registration, assessment, and ownership assignment immediately.
- B fails: closing Response B's risk entry is incorrect because the threat still exists at a reduced probability; early closure creates a monitoring blind spot for the residual exposure.
- E fails: reverting to the unmitigated state would increase risk exposure back to the original level; the partial mitigation from Response C retains value even with the secondary risk.
- F fails: a risk audit is a monitoring and governance tool that evaluates process compliance over time; it is not an immediate action that precedes updating the risk register based on known implementation outcomes.

**Mindset Lesson:** Close risks only when threats are fully and verifiably eliminated; partial mitigation produces residual risk that must be owned; secondary risks require immediate and complete treatment equal to any primary risk.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Implement Risk Responses executes what Plan Risk Responses designed; a response in the register with no implementation is not a control.
- The PM facilitates; the risk owner executes — assigning ownership is the PM's job; implementing is the owner's.
- An unimplemented risk response provides exactly zero protection, regardless of how well it was planned.
- Contingency plans activate at the defined trigger condition — not before as precaution, not after the damage is done.
- Workarounds are unplanned; contingency plans are pre-planned — these are not interchangeable terms.
- Every risk response may create a secondary risk; always ask what the response introduces, not just what it addresses.
- Residual risk must be explicitly accepted and owned — it does not disappear after implementation and it is not a failure.
- Responses affecting baselines always require a formal change request; no self-authorization by the PM or sponsor.
- Risk transfer requires contractual documentation; verbal agreements transfer nothing in PMI's framework.
- When risk owners fail to act, diagnose the obstacle before escalating — barriers may be resource or authority gaps the PM can remove.
- **Reflex burn-in: An approved but unimplemented risk response is as dangerous as having no response — execute, remove the barrier, or escalate immediately.**
