# PMP Exam Master Brief: Plan Procurement Management

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Plan Procurement Management is the process of documenting project procurement decisions, specifying the approach, and identifying potential sellers. It sits within the Planning Process Group under the Procurement Management knowledge area and typically carries 9–10% exam weight, bleeding heavily into Risk Management (contract risk allocation), Scope Management (SOW precision), Cost Management (contract pricing structures), and Legal/Compliance dimensions critical in regulated industries. The process produces the Procurement Management Plan, Procurement Strategy, Bid Documents (RFP, RFQ, IFB), Source Selection Criteria, Make-or-Buy Decisions, Independent Cost Estimates, and Change Requests as primary outputs. The PM's core role here is architecting risk transfer — choosing contract types that align incentives between buyer and seller based on how well scope can be defined. Fixed-price contracts transfer risk to the seller; cost-reimbursable contracts transfer risk to the buyer; T&M contracts sit in between and require the tightest oversight. PMI treats procurement planning as a strategic, not administrative, activity: the contract type choice, SOW quality, and source selection criteria directly determine project success long before a vendor is selected. In regulated industries such as banking, capital markets, or BSEC-governed environments, procurement planning must also integrate mandatory compliance clauses, audit rights, data sovereignty requirements, and regulatory approval gates into contract structures. Procurement planning is inseparable from stakeholder alignment — procurement decisions require sponsor authority, legal concurrence, and finance validation before bid documents are released. **In one sentence:** Plan Procurement Management is the deliberate, risk-informed design of how the project will acquire goods and services from outside the performing organization, encoding that design into binding contractual structures before a single seller is contacted.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Full procurement plan created upfront; SOW fully defined; contract type selected based on scope certainty; formal RFP/RFQ/IFB process; legal and finance sign-off before bid documents are released |
| Agile | Procurement may use time-and-material contracts to preserve flexibility; SOW replaced by high-level product vision; preferred-vendor lists or master service agreements used; procurement plan evolves with product backlog |
| Hybrid | Core infrastructure procured predictively with fixed-price contracts; iterative software or service components procured via T&M or blanket POs; procurement plan has a tiered structure matching delivery mode to contract type |

**Top 5 PMI-isms:**

1. PMI requires the PM to define source selection criteria before soliciting bids — real-world PMs often let procurement departments run selection independently without PM-defined weighted criteria.
2. PMI treats the make-or-buy analysis as a formal, documented decision requiring both quantitative cost analysis and strategic risk assessment — not a quick executive directive the PM simply implements.
3. PMI mandates that the procurement SOW be complete enough that sellers can price accurately before any bid is released — real-world PMs frequently issue RFPs with vague scope expecting bidders to clarify.
4. PMI views risk allocation through contract type selection as a project management responsibility, not a legal or procurement department responsibility — the PM must actively drive this decision.
5. PMI expects independent cost estimates to be prepared before evaluating seller bids, so the PM can assess bid realism — most real-world PMs skip this step and rely entirely on competitive pricing to validate cost.

**Ethics Angle:**

- **Fairness:** Source selection criteria must be defined and documented before bids are received, ensuring all sellers compete on the same transparent basis; adjusting criteria post-bid to favor a preferred vendor violates fairness.
- **Honesty:** The SOW must accurately represent scope; overstating or understating requirements to manipulate pricing distorts the procurement process and misrepresents the project to sellers.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is accountable for ensuring make-or-buy analysis is rigorous and documented, not delegated entirely to finance without PM professional judgment.
- **Respect:** Sellers' responses to RFPs represent significant investment; PMI expects the PM to ensure the process is genuine and that no bid is solicited with a predetermined winner.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| FFP (Firm Fixed Price) | Seller delivers defined scope for a fixed price regardless of actual cost; maximum risk on seller | Do not confuse with FPIF — FFP has no incentive fee and no cost sharing |
| FPIF (Fixed Price Incentive Fee) | Fixed price with an incentive fee tied to performance target; buyer shares in cost savings or overruns per the share ratio | The Point of Total Assumption (PTA) is a key calculation: PTA = ((Ceiling Price − Target Price) / Buyer's Share Ratio) + Target Cost |
| FPEPA (Fixed Price with Economic Price Adjustment) | Fixed price contract with provisions for pre-defined price adjustments due to inflation or commodity index changes; used in long-duration contracts | Do not treat as a cost-reimbursable contract; it is still a fixed-price variant |
| CPFF (Cost Plus Fixed Fee) | Buyer reimburses all allowable costs plus a fixed fee regardless of performance; maximum risk on buyer | Fixed fee does NOT change with cost overruns — it is fixed, not percentage-based |
| CPIF (Cost Plus Incentive Fee) | Buyer reimburses costs plus an incentive fee based on cost performance against a target; buyer and seller share cost overruns/underruns per share ratio | Incentive fee can go to zero but not negative; do not say the seller "pays back" fees |
| CPAF (Cost Plus Award Fee) | Buyer reimburses costs plus an award fee determined by buyer's subjective assessment of performance; minimal seller recourse on fee decisions | Award fee is at buyer's discretion; seller cannot dispute it like an incentive fee |
| T&M (Time and Material) | Hybrid contract: buyer pays a fixed rate per unit of time/material; no defined ceiling unless a "not to exceed" clause is added | Without a NTE clause, T&M has no cost cap — highest cost risk to buyer in practice |
| SOW (Statement of Work) | A narrative description of the products, services, or results to be supplied by the seller; must be precise enough for accurate pricing | Do not confuse with the project scope statement — the procurement SOW is seller-facing and contract-specific |
| RFP (Request for Proposal) | Formal solicitation document requesting a detailed proposal including technical approach, management plan, and pricing from prospective sellers | RFP invites full proposals; RFQ invites price quotes only; IFB invites sealed bids for well-defined work |
| Make-or-Buy Analysis | Quantitative and strategic evaluation of whether to produce goods/services internally or procure externally; outputs feed the procurement strategy and management plan | This is a PM-led analytical decision, not purely a finance or executive directive; the PM must document it |
| Source Selection Criteria | Weighted evaluation factors used to rate seller proposals; must be defined BEFORE bids are received | Defining or adjusting criteria after receiving bids invalidates procurement fairness per PMI Code of Ethics |
| Independent Cost Estimate | Internal or third-party cost estimate prepared by the buyer to benchmark seller bid pricing; identifies unrealistic bids | Failing to prepare this before bid evaluation is a common exam trap; PMI treats it as mandatory |
| Procurement Strategy | Documented approach for the delivery method, contract payment type, and procurement phases; formal output of this process | Do not treat as the same as the Procurement Management Plan — strategy is one component within it |
| Bid Documents | Formal documents (RFP/RFQ/IFB) sent to prospective sellers to solicit proposals; quality of bid documents directly affects proposal quality | Vague bid documents are a PM failure, not a seller failure — the PM is responsible for document quality |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "The PM is preparing to solicit vendors for a major system" | Complete the make-or-buy analysis and define source selection criteria before releasing bid documents | PMI requires procurement strategy and selection criteria documented before any external contact |
| "Scope is highly uncertain and requirements will evolve during delivery" | Choose a T&M or CPIF contract type to preserve flexibility | Locking in an FFP contract when scope is undefined transfers unacceptable risk to the seller and incentivizes scope games |
| "The sponsor wants to sole-source a vendor the PM has worked with before" | Document the sole-source justification formally and confirm it meets organizational procurement policy | PMI requires written justification for sole-source; PM preference or sponsor preference alone is insufficient |
| "Two bids are received but both exceed the independent cost estimate by 30%" | Issue an amendment to the bid documents or re-evaluate the SOW, not simply accept the lowest bid | A 30% gap signals the SOW may be unclear or scope is under-estimated; the independent cost estimate is the validity benchmark |
| "The PM receives a proposal that technically meets all criteria but is 40% cheaper than others" | Evaluate the proposal against all source selection criteria, including technical and management approach, not just price | PMI source selection is multi-criteria weighted; abnormally low pricing is a risk indicator, not an automatic win |
| "The legal team wants to add non-standard clauses to the contract" | Facilitate resolution between legal, the seller, and the project team before contract execution | Contract terms are part of procurement planning; the PM must coordinate, not simply defer to legal and accept delays |
| "The PM is told the project will use an existing master service agreement" | Review the MSA terms and assess whether they cover the specific project scope and risk allocation needed | MSAs set general terms; project-specific SOWs and amendments must still be tailored and verified |
| "The project operates in a BSEC-regulated environment and requires third-party data processing" | Ensure the SOW and contract include data sovereignty, audit rights, and regulatory compliance clauses before finalizing bid documents | Regulatory requirements are non-negotiable inputs to procurement planning; omitting them exposes the organization to legal and compliance risk |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table:**

| Scenario | PM Should Do | PMI Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Sponsor insists on skipping competitive bidding to award to a preferred vendor | Document the decision formally, obtain written sponsor and legal authorization, and note the procurement exception in the project log | PMI does not prohibit sole-source but requires transparent documentation; silent compliance without documentation violates responsibility |
| PM discovers after bids are received that source selection criteria were never formally documented | Halt evaluation, formalize the criteria with stakeholder input, and restart the evaluation transparently | Retroactive criteria definition compromises fairness per PMI Code of Ethics; the discomfort of restarting is less severe than an invalid award |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project:**
- Full procurement management plan required before any solicitation activity
- Formal make-or-buy analysis with documented cost comparisons and risk assessments
- Contract type selection driven by scope certainty at the time of award
- Source selection criteria weighted and approved by governance before bids open
- Independent cost estimates mandatory before bid evaluation

**Agile/Hybrid:**
- Procurement planning may be iterative; contract type may shift across release boundaries
- Master service agreements with statement-of-work amendments preferred to reduce overhead
- T&M with NTE clauses commonly used to preserve backlog flexibility while controlling cost exposure
- Agile delivery vendors may be evaluated on team capability and methodology fit, not just price
- Rolling wave procurement planning aligned to sprint or release planning cycles

**Industry/Regulatory Factors:**
- **Banking/Capital Markets:** Third-party vendor risk management frameworks (e.g., OCC Bulletin 2013-29, Basel III third-party requirements) impose mandatory due diligence, ongoing monitoring, and exit strategy documentation as procurement planning inputs
- **BSEC-Regulated Environments:** Contracts must include data localization clauses, audit access rights, incident notification timelines, and regulator disclosure obligations — these are non-negotiable SOW inputs
- **Government/Public Sector:** Procurement must comply with formal competitive bidding regulations; sole-source justifications require statutory grounds; debarment checks are mandatory
- **Healthcare/Pharma:** Vendor qualification and GxP compliance documentation must be embedded in SOW and source selection criteria before procurement begins

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Award to the lowest-cost bidder who meets minimum technical requirements" | Experienced PMs know budget pressure is real and cost is always a primary factor; minimum technical compliance feels sufficient | PMI requires evaluation against all weighted source selection criteria; lowest cost is one factor, not a trump card; ignoring management approach and risk factors is incomplete evaluation |
| "Use an FFP contract because it protects the project budget" | FFP does cap buyer cost exposure and PMs are often under budget pressure | When scope is poorly defined, FFP incentivizes the seller to cut scope or quality; PMI teaches that forcing FFP on undefined scope transfers scope-definition risk to the buyer in disguise |
| "Let the legal department finalize the contract type and terms" | Legal expertise in contracts is real; many organizations have legal departments that handle procurement | PMI holds the PM accountable for risk allocation decisions in procurement; delegating contract type selection entirely to legal is an abdication of the PM's responsibility |
| "Skip the independent cost estimate since competitive bidding will validate pricing" | Multiple competing bids feel like a natural market check; preparing a separate estimate seems redundant | PMI treats the independent cost estimate as a mandatory benchmark; without it, the PM cannot detect whether all bids are collectively overpriced due to a vague SOW |
| "Release the RFP quickly while the SOW is still being refined, then clarify via addenda" | Getting bids early accelerates the schedule; addenda are standard procurement tools | PMI requires the SOW to be complete before bid documents are released; an incomplete SOW generates non-comparable proposals and exposes the organization to contract disputes |
| "Add the preferred vendor to the source selection criteria as a 'preferred partner' bonus" | Existing relationships reduce onboarding risk and ramp-up time; experienced PMs value vendor familiarity | Embedding preference for a specific vendor in selection criteria violates the fairness principle of PMI Code of Ethics; criteria must be performance-based and applied equally |
| "Change the source selection criteria after reviewing bids to better reflect what was received" | Experienced PMs adapt to information; adjusting criteria to surface the best proposal seems pragmatic | Retroactive criteria changes are a fundamental fairness violation; PMI Code of Ethics requires criteria to be locked before bids are received to ensure a level playing field |
| "Choose a T&M contract because it is the most flexible option for all scenarios" | T&M is genuinely flexible and preferred in agile contexts | T&M with no NTE clause is the highest cost-risk contract for the buyer; PMI expects contract type to match scope certainty, not default to flexibility |

### 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "Scope is not yet fully defined" | Do not select FFP; select T&M or CPIF; risk is on the buyer and contract type must reflect scope uncertainty |
| "Protect the buyer from cost overruns" | FFP when scope is defined; FPIF if incentivizing cost performance; T&M with NTE if agile context |
| "Seller takes all the cost risk" | FFP family — seller absorbs all cost overrun risk |
| "Project is in a regulated industry with third-party data" | SOW must include compliance clauses, audit rights, data sovereignty before bid release |
| "Sponsor wants to skip competitive bidding" | Document sole-source justification, obtain written authorization, comply with policy before proceeding |
| "PM needs to evaluate bids fairly" | Source selection criteria must have been defined and documented BEFORE bids were received |
| "Bids are significantly higher than expected" | Compare against independent cost estimate; investigate SOW clarity before accepting or rejecting bids |
| "Vendor selection is based only on lowest price" | Wrong PMI approach unless the contract is a sealed-bid IFB for commoditized work; use weighted multi-criteria evaluation |
| "Project needs flexibility for evolving requirements" | T&M contract with NTE clause or MSA with SOW amendments; avoid FFP |
| "Long-duration contract subject to inflation" | FPEPA (Fixed Price with Economic Price Adjustment) — not a standard FFP, not cost-reimbursable |
| "PM wants to incentivize the seller to finish under budget" | FPIF or CPIF — incentive fee structures tied to cost performance target |
| "The PM is asked what to do FIRST before soliciting vendors" | Complete make-or-buy analysis, define procurement strategy, finalize SOW, and prepare source selection criteria — all before releasing bid documents |
| "The contract allows the buyer to direct additional work beyond the original scope" | T&M — the only contract type where scope can be directed incrementally by the buyer without formal change orders for each unit |
| "PM discovers the SOW was ambiguous after receiving non-comparable bids" | Issue a clarifying addendum and allow sellers to revise proposals; do not evaluate non-comparable bids as-is |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM at a financial institution is leading a core banking system replacement project. The CFO has indicated a strong preference for a specific vendor who provided consulting services to the organization two years ago. During procurement planning, the PM is preparing the source selection criteria. The CFO asks the PM to include a "prior organizational familiarity" criterion weighted at 30% to reflect the value of institutional knowledge. The PM knows the preferred vendor has a strong track record but is aware that two other vendors may be technically superior. No formal evaluation has yet occurred. What should the PM do?

A. Include the "prior organizational familiarity" criterion as requested; the CFO's judgment reflects legitimate organizational priorities.
B. Decline to include subjective preference criteria; define performance-based source selection criteria and present them to the CFO for approval before bid release.
C. Include the criterion at a lower weight, such as 10%, as a compromise that balances the CFO's preference with fairness.
D. Escalate to the PMO governance board without informing the CFO to protect the procurement process.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Fairness principle requires that source selection criteria be objective, performance-based, and finalized before bids are received. Including a criterion designed to favor a specific known vendor violates the fairness standard regardless of executive sponsorship. PMBOK 7 Delivery domain and Stewardship domain reinforce that the PM must maintain process integrity even under organizational pressure. Option A simply complies with an ethically problematic directive. Option C is a partial compromise that still embeds bias. Option D is escalation without communication — PMI expects the PM to address the concern directly with the involved party first before escalating.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a senior stakeholder requests a procurement process adjustment that advantages a predetermined winner, the PM's first action is to redirect toward objective criteria, not negotiate the degree of bias.

---

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide supports T&M contracts in agile procurement contexts where scope is defined iteratively. A T&M contract with a NTE clause preserves the collaborative scope development the team needs while capping buyer cost risk — the primary objection to pure T&M. PMBOK 7 and Agile Practice Guide both identify that forcing FFP on undefined scope creates perverse incentives and scope disputes. CPFF (B) is cost-reimbursable with no incentive structure and exposes the buyer to unlimited cost. FPIF (D) requires a defined target cost, which does not exist here.
**Mindset Lesson:** Contract type must match scope certainty at the time of award; agile scope uncertainty is best managed with T&M plus a cost ceiling, not forced into a fixed-price structure.

---

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 Planning Process Group sequence requires Plan Procurement Management outputs — make-or-buy decision, procurement strategy, SOW, and source selection criteria — to be complete before entering the Conduct Procurements process. Releasing bid documents before planning is complete produces non-comparable bids and exposes the project to contractual risk. Option A ignores process sequence under schedule pressure. Option C delegates a PM responsibility to the procurement department. Option D (RFI) is a market research tool that precedes formal solicitation but does not substitute for completing procurement planning first.
**Mindset Lesson:** Schedule pressure never justifies releasing bid documents before procurement planning outputs are complete; incomplete planning at this stage multiplies rework and risk.

---

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK procurement principles establish that FFP is the appropriate contract type when scope is fully defined, stable, and unlikely to change — it maximizes cost certainty for the buyer and transfers cost risk to the seller. CPIF (A) is used when cost incentives are needed and scope is defined but performance is variable — unnecessary here. T&M (C) is used for undefined or evolving scope — contradicted by the stable scope described. FPEPA (D) is correct for long-duration contracts with inflation risk, making it plausible, but the question specifies "stable material costs," eliminating the primary justification for FPEPA.
**Mindset Lesson:** Match contract type to scope certainty first; when scope is fully defined and stable, FFP is the default PMI answer regardless of contract duration.

---

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK and Agile Practice Guide support market sounding via RFI as a precursor to formal solicitation, but the core sequence within Plan Procurement Management is: make-or-buy → strategy → SOW/vision → source selection criteria → bid documents. Option A places criteria definition after proposal receipt — a fairness violation. Option B omits the make-or-buy analysis. Option D reverse-engineers criteria to support a predetermined selection — an ethics violation. Option C correctly begins with make-or-buy, uses RFI for market sounding (valid pre-planning step), then follows correct sequence.
**Mindset Lesson:** Source selection criteria must always be defined and documented before bids are received; this sequence is non-negotiable under PMI ethics standards.

---

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** "Selected Sellers" is an output of the Conduct Procurements process, not Plan Procurement Management. Plan Procurement Management produces: Procurement Management Plan, Procurement Strategy, Bid Documents, Source Selection Criteria, Source Selection Analysis, Make-or-Buy Decisions, Independent Cost Estimates, and Change Requests. Options A, B, and D are all direct outputs of Plan Procurement Management.
**Mindset Lesson:** Vendor selection happens in Conduct Procurements; Plan Procurement Management designs the process for selection but does not execute it.

---

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Fairness principles require the PM to conduct a genuine procurement process. Designing criteria to predetermine an outcome is fraudulent process engineering regardless of executive direction. The PM must escalate the ethics concern to the appropriate authority (PMO, ethics office, legal, or board). Option B is complicity in a fraudulent process. Option C (pre-briefing the vendor) is bid rigging — a serious ethics and legal violation. Option D documents personal protection but does not address the ethical obligation to act.
**Mindset Lesson:** When directed to corrupt a procurement process, the PM's obligation is to escalate to an appropriate authority, not find a softer form of compliance.

---

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** In regulated industries, compliance and data security requirements are non-negotiable inputs to the SOW and must be included in bid documents before release. PMBOK 7 Stewardship domain and regulatory frameworks (e.g., BSEC requirements) treat compliance obligations as fixed constraints, not post-selection negotiation points. Omitting compliance requirements from the RFP means evaluating proposals that are not comparable on a material dimension and selecting a vendor whose compliance capability is unknown. Options A, C, and D all defer compliance assessment to post-selection stages, creating legal and regulatory exposure.
**Mindset Lesson:** Regulatory and compliance requirements are procurement planning inputs, not post-award negotiation items — they belong in the SOW and bid documents.

---

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Decision domain and Stakeholder domain establish that the PM's role is to ensure decision-makers have complete, accurate information — not to unilaterally override stakeholder authority or silently comply with potentially suboptimal decisions. The PM should document the cost-risk trade-off analysis transparently and present it to the governance structure, then allow authorized decision-makers to choose. Option A capitulates without ensuring the CFO has risk information. Option C accepts the decision but does not fulfill the PM's responsibility to inform. Option D reopening bidding is inappropriate and would be a fairness violation against Vendor A.
**Mindset Lesson:** The PM's job in stakeholder-driven procurement decisions is to ensure the decision is informed, documented, and made at the appropriate authority level — not to make the decision unilaterally or accept it silently.

---

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, C, E
**PMI Justification:** Plan Procurement Management outputs include the Procurement Management Plan (A), Source Selection Criteria (C), and Bid Documents including RFP/RFQ/IFB (E). The Project Charter (B) is an output of Develop Project Charter in the Initiating Process Group. Selected Sellers (D) is an output of Conduct Procurements. The Procurement Statement of Work (F) is an output of Plan Procurement Management in PMBOK 6 framing — in PMBOK 7 it is embedded within the procurement strategy and bid documents; however, on the exam it is typically listed separately and is a valid output. If F is included in the answer set and the question selects 3, the distinction between E and F as separate outputs is the key differentiator. Under strict PMBOK 6 listing, A, C, and F are outputs; E (bid documents) contains the SOW. Under PMBOK 7, A, C, and E are the primary named outputs. The exam most commonly tests A, C, and E as the three-selection answer.
**Mindset Lesson:** Know the difference between what is produced in Plan Procurement Management (plan, criteria, bid documents) versus what happens in Conduct Procurements (selected sellers, agreements).

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Plan Procurement Management = designing how and what to buy before talking to any seller.
- Make-or-buy analysis is a formal, PM-led decision — not an executive directive to implement silently.
- Contract type choice is a risk allocation decision: defined scope = FFP; evolving scope = T&M or cost-reimbursable.
- SOW must be complete and precise before any bid document is released.
- Source selection criteria must be finalized and documented BEFORE bids are received — no exceptions.
- Independent cost estimate is mandatory; it benchmarks whether bids are realistic.
- Regulated environments (banking, BSEC, capital markets) require compliance, audit, and data clauses in the SOW as non-negotiable planning inputs.
- Sole-source awards require documented justification and proper authorization — not just sponsor preference.
- "Selected Sellers" is an output of Conduct Procurements, not Plan Procurement Management.
- T&M without a NTE clause carries the highest cost risk for the buyer — always check for cost ceiling.
- FPEPA is still a fixed-price contract — not cost-reimbursable — used for long-duration inflation risk.
- **When scope is defined, use FFP; when scope is unclear, use T&M or CPIF; when a stakeholder asks you to bias selection criteria, escalate to an ethics authority — these three reflexes answer 80% of procurement questions.**
