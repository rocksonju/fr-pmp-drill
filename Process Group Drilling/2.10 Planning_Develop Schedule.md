# PMP Exam Master Brief: Develop Schedule

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Develop Schedule is the process of analyzing activity sequences, durations, resource requirements, and schedule constraints to create a schedule model for project execution, monitoring, and control. It is the culminating planning process in schedule management — it takes all prior outputs (activity list, sequences, resource assignments, duration estimates) and assembles them into a schedule model from which the schedule baseline is extracted. The critical path method, resource optimization, schedule compression, and data analysis techniques are all applied within this process to produce a schedule that is both technically valid and achievable. This is the most heavily tested schedule management process because it requires the largest number of integrated calculation skills: CPM, float, PERT ranges, leads and lags, resource leveling, crashing, and fast-tracking. Cross-domain bleed is pervasive: scope (what is in the schedule), resources (who and how much), cost (crashing adds cost), and risk (schedule reserve) all intersect here. In capital markets and banking, the schedule baseline must align with fiscal year boundaries, regulatory reporting cycles, and BSEC submission windows, making network diagram optimization and constraint management especially consequential. In one sentence: Develop Schedule synthesizes all scheduling inputs into an approved, resource-leveled, risk-informed schedule model from which the schedule baseline is created and project execution is authorized.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | A full network diagram is built, CPM analysis identifies the critical path and float for all activities, resource optimization adjusts the schedule for availability, and compression techniques (crashing/fast-tracking) resolve any overruns — the output is a formally approved schedule baseline. |
| Agile | The release roadmap and sprint calendars serve as the schedule; iteration planning commits to sprint-level delivery increments; no Gantt baseline exists; the team forecasts using velocity and remaining backlog rather than a fixed network model. |
| Hybrid | A high-level milestone schedule is baselined predictively for regulatory and contractual commitments; the delivery work within milestones is scheduled iteratively using sprint planning and velocity; the two tracks are reconciled at milestone boundaries. |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. The critical path is the longest path through the network, not necessarily the most risky path — most experienced PMs equate critical path with the highest-risk path, but a near-critical path with high uncertainty may pose greater schedule risk.
2. Resource leveling extends the schedule if necessary to resolve over-allocations — real-world PMs often resolve over-allocation by adding overtime, which PMI treats as crashing (a cost trade-off), not leveling.
3. Fast-tracking increases risk (activities done in parallel may produce rework) — practitioners prefer fast-tracking because it appears free, but PMI expects awareness of the quality and rework risk it introduces.
4. Crashing always increases cost — there are no exceptions; any answer suggesting crashing is free or cost-neutral is wrong.
5. The schedule baseline is the approved version of the schedule model and can only be changed through formal change control — real-world PMs informally adjust dates and call it "updating," but PMI treats any change to the baseline as requiring a change request.

**Ethics Angle**

- Honesty: Presenting a schedule baseline that the PM knows is only achievable under optimistic assumptions without disclosing the confidence level is an ethical failure.
- Responsibility: The PM is accountable for the integrity of the schedule model; allowing incorrect logic or unrealistic constraints to persist without surfacing them violates professional responsibility.
- Fairness: Resource optimization decisions that consistently overload junior team members while protecting senior ones create inequitable working conditions.
- Respect: Involving the team in schedule development (bottom-up, team estimation, consensus on sequencing) respects their expertise and produces more realistic and owned commitments.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Critical Path Method (CPM) | Determines the longest path(s) through the network; activities on the critical path have zero total float. | The critical path is the LONGEST path with zero float — it is not always the most complex or most risky path. |
| Forward Pass (ES/EF) | Early Start (ES) and Early Finish (EF) calculated left-to-right through the network. EF = ES + Duration − 1. | Watch for "finish-to-start" dependencies; each successor's ES = predecessor's EF + 1 (or + lag). |
| Backward Pass (LS/LF) | Late Start (LS) and Late Finish (LF) calculated right-to-left. LS = LF − Duration + 1. | When the backward pass is done, critical path activities will have LS = ES and LF = EF. |
| Total Float | TF = LS − ES = LF − EF. Amount an activity can be delayed without delaying the project end date. | Critical path activities have TF = 0. Negative float means the project is behind schedule from the baseline date. |
| Free Float | Amount an activity can be delayed without delaying its immediate successor. FF ≤ TF always. | Free float is activity-specific; total float is path-specific. Do not swap them on float interpretation questions. |
| Schedule Compression — Crashing | Adding resources to critical path activities to shorten duration. Always increases cost. | Crashing only works on critical path activities; crashing non-critical activities has no effect on project duration. |
| Schedule Compression — Fast-Tracking | Performing activities in parallel that were originally planned sequentially. Increases risk of rework. | Fast-tracking does not add cost directly but introduces quality and rework risk; it is not a "free" compression option. |
| Resource Leveling | Adjusting start and finish dates to resolve resource over-allocations. May extend the critical path. | Resource leveling resolves over-allocation; it does NOT add resources — it redistributes or delays work. |
| Resource Smoothing | Adjusts activities within their float to optimize resource usage without extending the schedule end date. | Resource smoothing cannot extend the schedule end date; resource leveling can. These are tested as contrast pairs. |
| Lead | Acceleration of a successor activity; negative lag. Represented as a negative value in some tools. | Leads reduce schedule duration but add coordination risk; they are not free acceleration. |
| Lag | Delay of a successor activity. A waiting period required between predecessor finish and successor start. | Lags are legitimate constraints, not padding; they represent real required waiting (cure time, regulatory review). |
| Schedule Baseline | The approved version of the schedule model; changes require formal change control. | The baseline is not the same as the current schedule; the current schedule is updated; the baseline requires a change request to update. |
| Schedule Model | The schedule data and tool-based representation of all activities, dependencies, dates, and resources. | The schedule model is the full representation in the scheduling tool; the schedule baseline is the approved snapshot extracted from it. |
| Network Diagram | Graphical representation of activity sequences and dependencies; used as input to CPM analysis. | Network diagrams can be drawn in Precedence Diagramming Method (PDM) using FS, FF, SS, SF dependency types. |
| PERT Range | Using three-point estimates: Project duration range = sum of activity tE values ± combined σ values. | For a path: combined σ = √(Σσ²) for activities on the path. Do not simply add standard deviations — add variances, then take the square root. |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "The schedule shows the project will finish 6 weeks late" | Perform schedule compression analysis (crashing cost-benefit, fast-tracking feasibility) before recommending a path to the sponsor | The PM analyzes options and presents alternatives — not a unilateral decision or immediate escalation without data. |
| "A resource is allocated 150% in Month 3" | Apply resource leveling or resource smoothing to resolve the over-allocation | Over-allocation is addressed through optimization techniques; assigning overtime without authorization is not the first PMI answer. |
| "The sponsor approves an acceleration that requires two activities to run in parallel" | Assess the rework risk of fast-tracking and update the risk register | Fast-tracking approval does not eliminate the need to formally document and manage the introduced risk. |
| "A team member requests to start an activity 3 days early to help a colleague" | Assess the impact on the schedule model and network logic before approving | Starting early may create dependency conflicts or resource contention; schedule changes require analysis. |
| "The network diagram has a path with negative float" | Investigate the cause — the project end date constraint is tighter than the calculated finish | Negative float means the calculated schedule cannot meet the imposed date; the PM must address the constraint, not ignore it. |
| "The schedule is complete but critical path activities show no float" | This is expected and correct; communicate the implication that any delay to critical path activities will delay the project | Zero float on the critical path is normal, not an error. |
| "Management wants to approve the schedule before resource leveling is done" | Explain that resource-unleveled schedules are unrealistic and will produce false float calculations | A schedule approved without resource leveling presents an unachievable baseline. |
| "After applying resource leveling, the project end date extends 4 weeks beyond the contractual date" | Perform crashing analysis to recover the schedule within the contractual constraint | Leveling resolves resource issues; crashing is the tool to recover date slippage at a cost. |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "The project schedule shows the contractual penalty date will be missed by 2 weeks even with compression" | Document the analysis, present options with cost-time trade-offs to the sponsor, and formally escalate through the change control process | The PM's role is to present options with full analysis; the sponsor or change control board makes the binding decision. |
| "A BSEC reporting deadline is inside the project schedule but no lag was added for the regulatory review period" | Update the schedule model to include the regulatory review lag before baselining | Fixed external durations (regulatory reviews, approval periods) must be modeled as lags or activities, not absorbed into float assumptions. |

### 6. Visual Memory Aid

```
DEVELOP SCHEDULE — Critical Path & Compression Flow

INPUTS ASSEMBLED:
  Activity List + Sequences + Duration Estimates
  + Resource Assignments + Resource Calendars
  + Schedule Management Plan
        |
        v
STEP 1: BUILD NETWORK DIAGRAM (PDM)
  (FS, FF, SS, SF dependencies; leads and lags)
        |
        v
STEP 2: FORWARD PASS
  Calculate ES and EF for every activity
  (left to right through the network)
        |
        v
STEP 3: BACKWARD PASS
  Calculate LS and LF for every activity
  (right to left from the project end date)
        |
        v
STEP 4: CALCULATE FLOAT
  TF = LS − ES  |  FF = ES(successor) − EF(activity)
  Critical Path = all activities with TF = 0
        |
        v
STEP 5: RESOURCE OPTIMIZATION
  Over-allocated? --> RESOURCE LEVELING (may extend end date)
                 OR RESOURCE SMOOTHING (within float, no extension)
        |
        v
STEP 6: SCHEDULE FEASIBLE?
  End date meets constraint?
  |-- YES --> Proceed to baseline
  |-- NO  --> COMPRESSION
               |-- Cost not a major concern? --> FAST-TRACK
               |   (parallel activities, increases rework risk)
               |-- Cost is available? --> CRASH
                   (add resources to critical path, increases cost)
        |
        v
STEP 7: APPROVE SCHEDULE BASELINE
  Baseline = approved version of schedule model
  Changes require formal change control
        |
        v
OUTPUT: Schedule Baseline + Project Schedule
  (milestone charts, bar charts, network diagrams)
  --> feeds Control Schedule
```

**Mnemonic: FBF-ROC-B**
F — Forward pass (ES, EF)
B — Backward pass (LS, LF)
F — Float calculation (TF, FF, Critical Path)
R — Resource optimization (Leveling / Smoothing)
O — Options analysis (Compress if needed)
C — Crash or Fast-track decision
B — Baseline approval

Memory hook: "Find Backward Floaters, Resolve Over-allocations, Choose Between crash and fast-track, then Baseline."

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Full network diagram (PDM) is built and CPM analysis is performed.
- Resource leveling and smoothing are applied before baselining.
- Schedule baseline requires formal PMO and sponsor approval.
- Variance thresholds from the Schedule Management Plan govern when the baseline must be updated via change control.
- Monte Carlo simulation may be applied for complex projects to determine schedule probability distributions and reserve needs.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- In agile: release roadmaps, iteration calendars, and sprint backlogs replace a Gantt schedule baseline; the "schedule" is a living forecast updated every iteration.
- Velocity-based forecasting replaces CPM as the primary schedule projection tool.
- In hybrid: a milestone schedule baseline is maintained predictively; sprint-level scheduling is iterative and does not require formal baselining.
- The interface between the agile and predictive tracks must be explicitly managed: the iterative team's velocity must be reconciled with the fixed milestone dates.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC: Schedule baselines must incorporate regulatory submission windows, mandatory review periods, and audit cycles as hard constraints — not soft milestones. Float on activities immediately preceding regulatory deadlines is a risk indicator, not an opportunity.
- Construction: Critical chain method (buffer management) is commonly used alongside CPM to account for resource uncertainty.
- Pharmaceutical: Regulatory approval timelines (FDA, EMA) are fixed external lags; the schedule model must include them as explicit activities with documented durations.
- IT: Technical dependencies (API availability, cloud provisioning lead times) must be modeled as leads and lags, not informally managed.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Fast-track the schedule since it does not add cost" | Fast-tracking looks like the preferred choice because it does not require budget — experienced PMs prefer it for that reason. | Fast-tracking increases rework risk and requires explicit risk management; PMI never characterizes it as cost-free or risk-free. |
| "Apply resource leveling without assessing the impact on the end date" | Resolving over-allocation feels urgent and right. | Resource leveling may extend the critical path and push the end date past constraints; impact analysis must precede implementation. |
| "Crash a non-critical path activity to shorten the schedule" | Adding resources to busy activities feels productive. | Crashing non-critical activities wastes money without shortening the project; only critical path activities yield schedule compression from crashing. |
| "Update the schedule dates directly without going through change control when the baseline slips" | Real-world PMs adjust dates routinely to reflect reality and consider it good project management. | Any change to the schedule baseline requires formal change control; informal updates corrupt the baseline's integrity. |
| "The critical path always contains the highest-risk activities" | Risk management experience trains PMs to associate critical path with maximum risk. | The critical path is defined by zero float, not by risk level; a near-critical path with high uncertainty may represent greater schedule risk than the critical path. |
| "Resource smoothing can extend the project end date if needed" | Smoothing sounds like a more powerful version of leveling. | Resource smoothing only works within available float; it cannot extend the end date — that is resource leveling. |
| "Negative float means the project has buffer time" | "Negative" sounds like extra capacity to some practitioners. | Negative float means the calculated project finish is later than the imposed deadline; it signals a schedule problem, not a buffer. |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM has developed a schedule model for a compliance system upgrade at a regulated financial institution. The CPM analysis shows the project will complete 5 weeks after the BSEC-imposed regulatory deadline. The only way to meet the deadline is to crash three critical path activities at a combined cost of $180,000 and fast-track two others, accepting a 30% probability of rework. The sponsor asks the PM to present a schedule that shows on-time completion without mentioning the cost or rework risk, arguing that "we can deal with it later." The PM knows the steering committee approves budget.

What should the PM do?

A. Present the on-time schedule as requested since the sponsor has authority over the project.
B. Present both the constrained schedule (with crashing and fast-tracking costs and risks documented) and the unconstrained schedule, letting the steering committee make an informed decision.
C. Refuse to present any schedule until the sponsor agrees to disclose all costs and risks.
D. Present the on-time schedule but attach a private risk memo to the project files.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

An agile team has completed 6 sprints of a 12-sprint release. Velocity has averaged 42 story points per sprint (range: 36–48). The product backlog has 250 story points remaining. The product owner asks whether the team will complete the release on time. The release deadline is in 6 more sprints.

What should the PM tell the product owner?

A. Yes, the team is on track: 6 sprints × 42 = 252 points, which covers the 250 remaining.
B. The team is marginally on track at average velocity, but the range of 36–48 creates uncertainty; at minimum velocity (36 × 6 = 216), the team would be short by 34 points; the PM recommends reviewing backlog prioritization to ensure highest-value items are completed first regardless of outcome.
C. No, the team will not finish on time because the range indicates the team might not achieve average velocity.
D. The team cannot forecast release completion from velocity alone and should stop estimating.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project PM receives a network diagram from the engineering team showing that two activities originally sequenced Finish-to-Start have been changed to Start-to-Start with a 5-day lag in the schedule model — a change that shortens the critical path by 8 days. The engineering team made this change independently to help meet a milestone date. No change request was submitted and the schedule baseline has not been updated formally.

What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Accept the change since it helps the project and the engineering team is technically qualified.
B. Revert the change in the schedule model and direct the team to submit a change request so the logic change can be assessed for downstream dependencies and rework risk.
C. Update the schedule baseline to reflect the new dates without filing a change request since it is a technical, not scope, change.
D. Ask the engineering team to document the change and monitor for rework.

**Q4 — Two Correct Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM has completed CPM analysis and found two paths through the network: Path A has a duration of 45 days with zero float (the critical path); Path B has a duration of 42 days with 3 days of float. An activity on Path B has a PERT standard deviation of 4 days. The project manager is assessing schedule risk.

Which statement BEST reflects the PMI perspective on this schedule?

A. Path A is the only schedule risk concern because it has zero float.
B. Path B is also a significant schedule risk because its low float combined with the high standard deviation of one activity (4 days σ) could make it critical; both paths require active monitoring.
C. Path B is safe because it has 3 days of float.
D. The critical path is all that matters; Path B only needs attention when float is consumed.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A hybrid delivery team is developing its schedule for the next program increment. Place the following activities in the correct sequence for developing the hybrid schedule:

1. Build the network diagram for regulatory milestone activities using CPM
2. Confirm team velocity from prior sprints for iterative delivery components
3. Reconcile the iterative forecast with the regulatory milestone dates
4. Estimate durations for regulatory deliverables using three-point estimates
5. Develop the sprint calendar for iterative delivery work

Which sequence is CORRECT?

A. 2, 4, 1, 5, 3
B. 4, 1, 2, 5, 3
C. 1, 4, 2, 3, 5
D. 2, 5, 4, 1, 3

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT a technique used in the Develop Schedule process?

A. Critical Path Method
B. Resource Leveling
C. Monte Carlo Simulation
D. Decomposition

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A PM knows that the approved schedule baseline for a banking system migration was developed without including a mandatory 10-business-day regulatory notification period required before go-live. The baseline was approved by the sponsor and the PMO. Three months into the project, the PM discovers the omission when reviewing compliance documentation. The go-live date is 6 weeks away and including the notification period would push go-live 2 weeks past the approved date.

What is the PM's FIRST ethical and professional obligation?

A. Proceed with the go-live as scheduled and handle the notification period violation as an issue.
B. Immediately raise a change request, disclose the baseline error to the sponsor and PMO, and quantify the impact on the go-live date.
C. Quietly add the notification period to the schedule as a buffer within existing float.
D. Ask the compliance officer to waive the notification period given the approved schedule.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A portfolio steering committee meeting is scheduled for tomorrow. The PM leads a hybrid project with a predictive milestone track and an agile delivery track. The steering committee includes the CFO (cost-focused), the Chief Compliance Officer (risk and regulatory focused), and the CTO (delivery and technical focused). The PM has 15 minutes to present schedule status.

What is the BEST communication approach?

A. Present the full network diagram with all CPM calculations to give the committee complete technical visibility.
B. Present a tiered summary: milestone adherence for the CCO (with regulatory dates highlighted), sprint velocity trend and release forecast for the CTO, and schedule cost trade-offs (crashing options) for the CFO — all on one integrated status dashboard.
C. Focus only on the critical path since it is the most important schedule element.
D. Provide only a Gantt chart covering the next 4 weeks of activities.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM discovers that a 3-day lag representing a vendor approval period was removed from the schedule model by a team member who thought it was a mistake. This lag was on a near-critical path with only 2 days of float. Removing it made the path appear to have 5 days of float, masking a near-critical risk. The schedule baseline has not been changed, but the working schedule model is now incorrect. The program director reviews the schedule tomorrow.

What should the PM do?

A. Restore the lag in the schedule model, document the unauthorized change, and inform the program director of the corrected status before the review.
B. Leave the model as is since the lag change was minor and the baseline is unchanged.
C. Ask the team member to restore the lag quietly and say nothing since no harm was done.
D. Escalate to the PMO before restoring the lag to get formal authorization.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A project manager needs to shorten the critical path by 10 days. Which THREE of the following actions are valid schedule compression techniques or legitimate responses?

A. Add resources to critical path activities (crashing) — costs will increase but duration decreases.
B. Remove quality assurance activities from the schedule entirely to save time.
C. Overlap two critical path activities that were originally planned sequentially (fast-tracking) — rework risk increases.
D. Reduce the scope of a critical path deliverable through formal change control and re-estimate its duration.
E. Move float from a non-critical path activity to a critical path activity to create buffer.
F. Negotiate a revised contract milestone date with the client through formal change control if the compression analysis shows the target is unachievable.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility: stakeholders making budget and schedule decisions require full information. PMBOK 7 Delivery and Stakeholder Performance Domains: the PM's role is to enable informed decision-making, not to curate information to match sponsor preferences. Option A complies with authority but violates honesty. Option C is confrontational and obstructs the process. Option D creates private documentation without surfacing the issue, which fails the honesty standard.
**Mindset Lesson:** The PM's role is to present options with complete cost-risk data; the sponsor and governance body make the decision — not the PM, and never without full information.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — velocity-based forecasting requires presenting the range, not just the mean, to give the product owner an honest probability picture. At minimum velocity, the team cannot complete the backlog; backlog prioritization is the agile PM's lever to maximize value delivery within uncertainty. Option A presents false confidence from average-only data. Option C overstates the risk from range variability alone. Option D abandons a valid and useful forecasting tool.
**Mindset Lesson:** Agile forecasts must acknowledge velocity range uncertainty and direct the team to prioritize value delivery within the uncertainty band.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 and integrated change control: unauthorized changes to the schedule model logic, even beneficial ones, undermine schedule integrity and may mask downstream dependency risks. The reversal and change request process ensures the logic change is analyzed for all impacts. Option A bypasses governance. Option C updates the baseline without a change request, which is a direct violation. Option D accepts the change without formal review.
**Mindset Lesson:** Logic changes to the schedule model require change control regardless of whether they improve or worsen the schedule.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Uncertainty Domain and schedule risk analysis: near-critical paths with high variance are a recognized schedule risk. A standard deviation of 4 days on a path with only 3 days of float means there is a significant probability that Path B becomes critical. PMI expects PMs to monitor near-critical paths actively, not just the nominal critical path. Options A, C, and D reflect incomplete schedule risk awareness.
**Mindset Lesson:** Near-critical paths with high variance require the same active monitoring as the critical path; float alone does not measure schedule risk.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** In hybrid schedule development, the predictive components (regulatory milestones) are estimated and network-analyzed first to establish the fixed framework. Then iterative delivery forecasts are developed using velocity and sprint cadence. The two tracks are then reconciled to ensure the iterative work lands within milestone windows. Sequence: 4 (estimate), 1 (CPM), 2 (velocity), 5 (sprint calendar), 3 (reconcile).
**Mindset Lesson:** In hybrid scheduling, establish fixed milestone constraints first, then build the iterative delivery plan to fit within them.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** Decomposition is a technique used in Define Scope and Create WBS to break down scope into work packages and activities — it is not a Develop Schedule technique. CPM (A), Resource Leveling (B), and Monte Carlo Simulation (C) are all explicitly listed Develop Schedule techniques in PMBOK 6.
**Mindset Lesson:** Decomposition belongs to scope management, not schedule development; confusing the two is a knowledge-area boundary error.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility: discovering a baseline error that will cause a regulatory violation creates an immediate professional obligation to disclose and correct, regardless of how the error arose. Option A proceeds knowing a compliance violation is likely. Option C conceals the error and hopes float absorbs it — this is dishonest. Option D asks the compliance officer to facilitate a violation.
**Mindset Lesson:** Known baseline errors with compliance implications must be disclosed and corrected through change control; concealing them is an ethical violation regardless of how late the discovery occurs.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder Performance Domain: communication must be tailored to each stakeholder's information needs and decision-making context. A tiered integrated dashboard addresses the CFO's cost trade-off visibility, the CCO's regulatory milestone focus, and the CTO's technical delivery interest — all within the 15-minute window. Option A provides more detail than the committee can use. Option C omits information relevant to two of three decision-makers. Option D provides only near-term operational data without strategic context.
**Mindset Lesson:** Executive schedule communication is always tiered by role and decision-making need; never present the same level of technical detail to all stakeholders simultaneously.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — schedule model integrity requires that the working schedule accurately represents the plan. An unauthorized change that masks a near-critical path risk is a data integrity issue requiring correction and disclosure before the director's review. Option B accepts a known inaccuracy. Option C avoids accountability for the change. Option D over-escalates — restoring a factual error does not require PMO authorization, but it does require disclosure to affected stakeholders.
**Mindset Lesson:** Unauthorized schedule model changes must be corrected and disclosed before stakeholder reviews; transparency about schedule model integrity is a PM responsibility.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, F
**PMI Justification:** Crashing (A) is a valid compression technique — costs increase but duration decreases on the critical path. Fast-tracking (C) is a valid compression technique — rework risk increases but no direct cost is added. Negotiating a revised milestone through change control (F) is a valid response when analysis shows the target cannot be achieved — it is honest, governance-compliant, and protects the project. Removing QA activities (B) changes scope without authorization and introduces quality risk — PMI never endorses this. Float transfer (E) is not a real schedule management concept; float is a property of network logic, not a transferable resource. Reducing scope through formal change control (D) is technically valid but only if scope reduction is on the critical path and authorized — it is the least clean of the three, making A, C, F the strongest set.
**Mindset Lesson:** Schedule compression options are crashing (cost) and fast-tracking (risk); when neither achieves the target, formal milestone renegotiation is the ethical and governance-compliant path.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Develop Schedule is the capstone schedule planning process; it produces the Schedule Baseline — the approved version used for all performance measurement.
- CPM identifies the critical path (longest path, zero total float); any delay to a critical path activity delays the project end date.
- Forward pass calculates ES/EF left-to-right; backward pass calculates LS/LF right-to-left; float = LS − ES or LF − EF.
- Negative float = the project is already past its constraint date; it is not buffer.
- Compression options: Fast-Track (parallel activities — adds rework risk, no direct cost) or Crash (add resources to critical path — always adds cost).
- Resource Leveling resolves over-allocations and may extend the end date; Resource Smoothing works within float and cannot extend the end date.
- Near-critical paths with high variance are schedule risks even with positive float — monitor them as actively as the critical path.
- The Schedule Baseline can only be changed through formal change control; working schedule updates do not require change requests.
- In hybrid projects: predictive CPM governs milestone tracks; velocity governs iterative delivery; the two must be reconciled at milestone boundaries.
- Regulatory deadlines, external review periods, and mandatory notification lags must be modeled in the schedule, not absorbed as float assumptions.
- **Reflex burn-in: When the stem says "shorten the schedule," crash if cost is available and the critical path is clear; fast-track if cost is not available but acknowledge rework risk — and never apply either technique to non-critical path activities expecting a schedule gain.**
