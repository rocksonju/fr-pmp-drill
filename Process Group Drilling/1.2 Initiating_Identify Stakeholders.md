# PMP Exam Master Brief: Identify Stakeholders

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Identify Stakeholders is the process of systematically discovering, documenting, and analyzing all individuals, groups, and organizations that have an interest in — or can be affected by — the project, its processes, or its outcomes. It sits in the Initiating Process Group under Stakeholder Management, and it runs in parallel with or immediately after Develop Project Charter. Critically, it is not a one-time event: PMI requires re-executing this process whenever the project enters a new phase, when project complexity changes, or when stakeholders shift. The deliverable is the stakeholder register, which seeds every downstream stakeholder engagement and communication planning activity. In practice, PMs skip or minimize this process under schedule pressure and pay for it in execution with change requests, sponsor disengagement, and political interference. In regulated environments like Bangladesh Bank-supervised banks, BSEC-regulated capital market firms, or DSE-listed companies, failing to identify regulatory bodies, compliance officers, and external auditors as early stakeholders creates governance gaps that are expensive to close later. Exam weight is moderate as a standalone topic but stakeholder identification concepts appear across 15–25% of all scenario questions — embedded in communication, risk, procurement, and integration stems. **In one sentence:** Identify Stakeholders is the process of discovering everyone who matters to the project before they discover that the project forgot about them.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Stakeholder register created during Initiating; identification led by PM using structured analysis tools; updated at phase gates; formal stakeholder engagement assessment matrix maintained throughout |
| Agile | Stakeholder identification is continuous and collaborative; product owner represents many stakeholder interests; sprint reviews are stakeholder identification and engagement events combined; team members actively elicit emerging stakeholder groups |
| Hybrid | Formal stakeholder register created at project level for governance; delivery team maintains a living stakeholder map updated each sprint or delivery cycle; external/regulatory stakeholders identified formally, internal ones identified iteratively |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Identify Stakeholders is Initiating work, not Planning work.** Real-world instinct is to defer stakeholder identification until there is enough scope to know who matters. PMI requires identification to begin at chartering — the charter itself lists key stakeholders. Waiting until planning begins is already late by PMI standards.
2. **Missed stakeholders discovered later are a process failure, not a normal discovery.** In practice, discovering new stakeholders in execution is treated as routine. PMI views stakeholders identified after Initiating as a gap in the identification process — triggering re-execution of this process, not just adding a name to a list.
3. **The stakeholder register is not a contact list.** PMs create stakeholder registers that look like org charts or email directories. PMI's register includes power, interest, influence, and engagement assessments — it is an analytical tool for engagement planning, not a roster.
4. **Negative stakeholders and opponents must be explicitly identified.** Experienced PMs focus their stakeholder work on supporters and neutrals. PMI requires explicit identification of stakeholders who may oppose or resist — these represent risks and must be assessed and managed.
5. **The PM does not decide who is a stakeholder — impact and interest do.** In practice, PMs curate stakeholder lists based on who they want to engage. PMI says anyone affected by project decisions or outcomes is a stakeholder, regardless of whether the PM wants them engaged or finds them convenient.

### Ethics Angle

- **Fairness:** Deliberately excluding stakeholders who are politically inconvenient or who might challenge the project is a fairness violation — all affected parties have a right to be recognized.
- **Responsibility:** Failing to identify compliance officers, regulators, or affected community members in a Bangladesh Bank regulatory project violates the PM's responsibility to protect the organization from foreseeable harm.
- **Honesty:** If a key stakeholder group is identified but omitted from the register to simplify politics, this is dishonest practice — the register must reflect reality.
- **Respect:** Engaging stakeholders without first understanding their interests (skipping analysis) treats them as objects rather than people with legitimate concerns.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Stakeholder Register | Primary output of Identify Stakeholders; contains identification, assessment, and classification data for each stakeholder | This is NOT the stakeholder engagement plan — the register identifies; the plan prescribes engagement strategy (produced during Planning) |
| Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix | Tool comparing current vs desired engagement levels: Unaware / Resistant / Neutral / Supportive / Leading | This is a TOOL used during both identification and planning — it first appears in Initiating to capture current state |
| Power/Interest Grid | 2x2 matrix classifying stakeholders by power (authority) and interest (concern); quadrants guide engagement level | Candidates confuse this with the stakeholder register — the grid is a tool/technique, not the output |
| Salience Model | Stakeholder prioritization model using three dimensions: Power, Legitimacy, Urgency | Used when stakeholders have overlapping or competing claims — more nuanced than the Power/Interest Grid; used in complex regulatory or multi-government projects |
| Influence/Impact Grid | Matrix assessing stakeholder ability to affect project decisions (influence) vs degree of project effect on them (impact) | Distinct from Power/Interest — influence is organizational/behavioral; power is formal authority |
| Stakeholder Cube | 3D extension of 2×2 grids adding a third dimension (attitude, urgency); rarely tested but signals analytic depth | Do not confuse with the salience model — they are different tools for multi-dimensional stakeholder analysis |
| Change Log | Running record of change requests and decisions; Identify Stakeholders may produce updates to the change log if stakeholder identification reveals scope changes | The change log is an output of identification only when discovery alters project parameters |
| Agreements | Contracts, MOUs, service agreements that define formal stakeholder obligations; also an input to Identify Stakeholders | Agreements can reveal stakeholders not obvious from the charter — vendor contracts may surface external regulators as affected parties |
| OPA Stakeholder Templates | Historical stakeholder registers, lessons learned about stakeholder engagement failures, and identification checklists | Using prior project's stakeholder register from a similar banking migration as an OPA template is correct and efficient |
| EEF Organizational Culture | The cultural environment — formal vs informal authority, communication norms, political landscape — that shapes who is a real stakeholder vs nominal one | Bangladesh Bank's hierarchical culture means informal influencers (senior advisors, department heads) may hold more real power than their org chart suggests |
| Stakeholder Identification Timing | PMI: at Initiating AND whenever significant changes occur (new phase, major scope change, new regulatory requirement) | The biggest trap: treating this as a one-time event rather than an iterative process throughout the project lifecycle |
| Expert Judgment | Input from experienced individuals (legal counsel, compliance officers, domain experts, former PMs) who can identify non-obvious stakeholders | Do not assume the PM alone can identify all stakeholders — expert judgment from compliance, legal, and operations is essential in regulated industries |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Project charter has just been signed" | Begin Identify Stakeholders process immediately | Stakeholder identification is an Initiating process — it starts at or right after chartering, not during planning |
| "A stakeholder group was missed during initiation and discovered in execution" | Re-execute the Identify Stakeholders process and update the stakeholder register | PMI requires re-execution of identification when new stakeholders are discovered, not just adding a name informally |
| "Two stakeholders have conflicting interests" | Document in the stakeholder register, analyze positions, and develop engagement strategies — escalate if conflicts threaten project objectives | Conflict between stakeholders is a management issue surfaced through analysis; escalation is a last resort, not first action |
| "A regulatory body (Bangladesh Bank) has not been listed as a stakeholder" | Add them to the stakeholder register immediately and assess their engagement requirements | External regulators are always stakeholders in regulated-sector projects; their omission is a compliance and project risk |
| "A stakeholder has moved from Supportive to Resistant during execution" | Update the engagement assessment matrix and adjust the stakeholder engagement plan | Changes in engagement level trigger plan updates — this is expected and should be managed proactively |
| "The project is entering a new phase" | Re-execute Identify Stakeholders to identify any new or changed stakeholders for the new phase | Phase gates are mandatory re-identification trigger points per PMI guidance |
| "Sponsor asks PM to exclude a dissenting department from the stakeholder register" | Refuse and explain that all affected parties must be included; discuss engagement strategy for managing resistance | Excluding stakeholders for political reasons violates fairness and creates unmanaged risk |
| "The stakeholder register has not been updated in months during a long project" | Update the register as part of regular project reviews; treat it as a living document | Stakeholder circumstances change; a static register creates outdated engagement plans and missed risks |
| "PM is unsure who the key stakeholders are for a new regulatory system" | Conduct stakeholder identification using expert judgment from compliance, legal, and operations teams | Expert judgment from domain experts is the correct tool when the PM lacks full organizational visibility |

### Escalation Edge Cases

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A senior executive demands that a vendor partner not be listed as a stakeholder, despite having contractual obligations" | Document the concern, explain the stakeholder definition to the executive, and escalate to the sponsor if pressure continues | Stakeholder definition is objective — anyone with a stake in the project is a stakeholder; excluding them creates unmanaged risk and violates honesty |
| "A previously identified stakeholder is actively working to undermine the project through back channels" | Keep them in the register as resistant/opponent, update engagement strategy, inform the sponsor of the threat | Removing a known opponent from the register does not neutralize them — it blinds the PM to the risk; sponsor awareness is required |

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Produce a formal stakeholder register at project start with full power/interest/engagement assessments
- Identification is PM-led but must draw on expert judgment from compliance, legal, operations
- Update the register formally at each phase gate or major milestone review
- In capital markets projects (DSE/CSE system integrations), stakeholders include exchange technical teams, clearing house staff, and BSEC oversight personnel — all must be formally identified

### Agile / Hybrid

- Identification is continuous and collaborative — the team participates in identifying stakeholders, not just the PM
- Sprint reviews double as stakeholder identification events — new faces in the review are potential stakeholders to formalize
- Product owner acts as proxy for many end-user stakeholders but should not be the sole identifier of business stakeholders
- In hybrid environments, use a formal register for external/governance stakeholders and an informal stakeholder map for iterative internal engagement

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Bangladesh Bank projects:** Always identify Bangladesh Bank examiners, internal compliance officers, the CTO, and the Board Risk Committee as stakeholders — even for "internal" IT projects that touch customer data or financial reporting
- **BSEC-regulated entities:** Listed company projects with investor impact must identify institutional investors, the BSEC surveillance team, and independent directors as stakeholders from Day 1
- **DSE / CSE integration projects:** Exchange member firms, settlement banks, and the CDBL (Central Depository) are external stakeholders that must be identified before planning begins
- **Multinational or donor-funded projects:** Donor agencies (World Bank, ADB, IFC) may have specific stakeholder identification requirements embedded in the grant or loan agreement — check agreements as inputs

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Identify stakeholders during planning when scope is clearer" | More scope information means better stakeholder identification — waiting seems logical | Identify Stakeholders is an Initiating process; deferring it creates planning assumptions based on incomplete stakeholder input, leading to change requests later |
| "The stakeholder register is a contact list with names, titles, and emails" | In practice, stakeholder registers are often just contact databases | PMI's stakeholder register includes identification data AND assessment data (power, interest, influence, engagement level) — without analysis, it is not a PMI-compliant register |
| "Only positive/supportive stakeholders need to be in the register" | Documenting opponents feels uncomfortable and may create political friction | All stakeholders — including resistant and unaware ones — must be in the register; opponents represent risks that must be managed, not ignored |
| "Identify Stakeholders is done once at project start" | One-time identification is efficient and seems sufficient for most projects | PMI requires re-execution at phase gates and when significant changes occur — treating it as one-time misses emerging stakeholders who derail execution |
| "The sponsor is responsible for identifying all stakeholders" | Sponsors have organizational authority and visibility — letting them identify stakeholders seems efficient | The PM is responsible for facilitating stakeholder identification; relying solely on the sponsor introduces bias toward their organizational view |
| "New stakeholders found in execution are just added informally to the list" | Ad hoc additions seem practical and lightweight | PMI requires re-executing the Identify Stakeholders process — not informal additions — to ensure proper analysis and engagement planning for new stakeholders |
| "Exclude regulatory bodies from the stakeholder register if they are not directly involved in delivery" | Regulators seem like external constraints, not stakeholders in the project | Anyone who can affect or be affected by the project is a stakeholder — regulators like Bangladesh Bank can approve, delay, or derail regulatory projects; they must be included |
| "Power/Interest Grid is the only stakeholder classification tool needed" | It is the most well-known and commonly tested tool | PMI offers multiple models (Salience, Influence/Impact, Stakeholder Cube); complex or regulated projects need richer analysis than a single 2x2 matrix provides |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager at a BSEC-licensed asset management company is leading a regulatory compliance project mandated by a new BSEC circular. During Identify Stakeholders, the PM discovers that a senior director — who is the sponsor's personal ally — has a significant financial interest in one of the vendor options being evaluated. The sponsor instructs the PM not to list this director as a stakeholder to "keep things clean." What should the PM do?

A. Follow the sponsor's instruction and omit the director, but document the conversation privately.
B. Include the director in the stakeholder register with the conflict of interest noted, and raise the matter with the sponsor and, if needed, compliance/ethics officer.
C. Escalate directly to the BSEC before consulting the sponsor, to protect the organization.
D. Include the director but do not note the conflict of interest in the register to avoid political sensitivity.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

During sprint review, two new participants attend for the first time: a representative from the settlement bank and one from CDBL. These individuals raise concerns about the system's data format that, if unaddressed, would require significant rework. What is the BEST action for the PM/Scrum Master?

A. Thank them for attending and address their concerns in the product backlog during the next sprint.

C. Inform them that the sprint review is not the appropriate forum for raising technical concerns and direct them to submit a formal change request.
D. Escalate their concerns to the product owner and defer stakeholder registration until the end of the project phase.

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project at a commercial bank is transitioning from initiation to planning. The IT department head insists that his team's database architect must be listed as the primary technical stakeholder, while the Chief Risk Officer claims her team's risk analysts are the true technical stakeholders for this regulatory project. Both have gone to the PM seeking resolution. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. List both departments as stakeholders and classify them using a Power/Interest Grid to determine engagement priority.
B. Ask the sponsor to decide which department is the primary technical stakeholder.
C. List the IT architect as primary since IT owns delivery, and note the CRO's team as secondary.
D. Delay stakeholder register completion until the conflict is resolved by the departments themselves.

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Fairness and Honesty values; PMBOK 7 — Stewardship. A conflict of interest in a procurement decision is a material fact that must be documented and reported. Option A follows the sponsor's instruction to exclude — this is a fairness and honesty violation that exposes the organization to regulatory and legal risk. Option C bypasses all organizational channels and escalates to the regulator as a first step — disproportionate and inappropriate before internal channels are exhausted. Option D includes the stakeholder but suppresses material information — this is dishonest and protects the conflict of interest rather than managing it.

**Mindset Lesson:** Including a stakeholder in the register with documented concerns is always better than excluding them — the register is a management tool, not a political document.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder engagement principle; Agile Practice Guide — iterative stakeholder engagement. In agile environments, new stakeholders discovered at ceremonies must be formally identified through re-execution of the process, not just added informally. Option A acknowledges the concern but skips the stakeholder identification process re-execution. Option C treats the sprint review as the wrong venue — PMI and Agile Practice Guide both position reviews as engagement and feedback events where new stakeholders naturally surface. Option D defers formalization inappropriately — stakeholders with active concerns must be registered and engaged now.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sprint reviews are not just delivery demonstrations — they are live stakeholder discovery events; treat new faces as newly identified stakeholders requiring process-level recognition.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Integration and Stakeholder Management; Power/Interest Grid as classification tool. Both departments have legitimate stakes in a regulatory project — excluding or deprioritizing either without analysis creates engagement risk. Using the Power/Interest Grid to classify both allows objective priority determination. Option B avoids PM responsibility by delegating classification to the sponsor without first conducting analysis. Option C prejudges the outcome without analysis — IT ownership of delivery does not make risk analysts secondary stakeholders in a regulatory project. Option D stalls the identification process indefinitely.

**Mindset Lesson:** Stakeholder classification tools exist precisely for situations with competing claims — use them to remove politics from prioritization.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder Management; regulatory EEF identification at initiation. Option A correctly includes regulatory and governance bodies that have direct authority over a Bangladesh Bank compliance project. Option C correctly uses expert judgment — a primary tool for identifying stakeholders that the PM cannot discover through their own organizational view alone. Option B wrongly limits the register to internal team members — this is the most common real-world failure that PMI specifically warns against. Option D defers external stakeholder identification to planning — external regulators must be identified in Initiating.

**Mindset Lesson:** In regulated-sector projects, the regulator is always your most powerful stakeholder — they belong in the register on Day 1.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder Management process flow; Agile Practice Guide — iterative engagement sequence. Identification must precede analysis; analysis must precede creating the register (which is the output of combined identification and analysis); engagement strategy is planned after the register exists. Option B begins with planning before identification — this is backwards. Option C creates the register before identifying stakeholders — logically impossible. Option D starts with analysis before identification — analysis requires something to analyze.

**Mindset Lesson:** Identify → Analyze → Register → Plan is the sequence; any reordering inverts cause and effect.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Identify Stakeholders ITTOs. Resource leveling is a Schedule Management tool used to resolve resource conflicts during planning and execution — it has no role in stakeholder identification. Options A, B, and C are all legitimate tools: expert judgment is a primary technique; the stakeholder engagement assessment matrix is a data representation tool used during identification to capture current engagement state; data gathering techniques like questionnaires and brainstorming are used to surface stakeholder information.

**Mindset Lesson:** When asked about Identify Stakeholders tools, anything that smells like scheduling, resource management, or budgeting does not belong.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Fairness and Responsibility values. Minority shareholders with formal rights under the company's articles are legally-defined stakeholders — their omission is both an ethical and legal compliance failure. Option A perpetuates the prior PM's error and violates the current PM's independent ethical obligations. Option C documents a risk without correcting the underlying problem — the register must be accurate. Option D avoids direct accountability and is an inappropriate first step when direct action is available.

**Mindset Lesson:** Inheriting someone else's omission does not absolve you — the PM's ethical obligation is to correct inaccurate project documents, not maintain them for continuity's sake.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Tailoring; Agile Practice Guide — stakeholder engagement in hybrid environments. Different stakeholder groups require different identification and engagement approaches based on their role, authority, and interaction style. Option A ignores the need for tailoring — applying one approach to governance executives and agile delivery users is neither efficient nor effective. Option C defers stakeholder identification to planning, violating Initiating process sequence. Option D concentrates stakeholder ownership with the product owner, removing the PM's identification responsibility and creating a single point of failure.

**Mindset Lesson:** In hybrid environments, stakeholder identification and engagement are not one-size-fits-all — match the approach to the stakeholder tier.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Risk and Stakeholder Management; escalation sequencing. A resistant stakeholder in a compliance project is a documented project risk — dismissing it is not acceptable governance. The PM's obligation is to ensure the risk is properly documented and that the sponsor makes an informed decision. Option A accepts the sponsor's dismissal without creating a risk record — this violates the PM's stewardship responsibility. Option C bypasses the sponsor for a Board escalation without exhausting sponsor-level resolution — disproportionate and premature. Option D removes the stakeholder from the register because of resistance — this blinds risk management to an active threat.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor dismissal of a stakeholder risk is itself a risk — document it, escalate it formally, and ensure the sponsor owns the decision in writing.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: B, C, and D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Identify Stakeholders ITTOs. Option B (expert judgment) is a primary tool for identifying non-obvious stakeholders in complex regulated environments. Option C (review agreements/contracts) is an input technique — agreements reveal external parties with stakes. Option D (include regulatory bodies and governance committees) correctly recognizes mandatory stakeholders in a Bangladesh Bank-supervised environment. Option A (project schedule) is a Planning output — not part of Identify Stakeholders. Option E (communications management plan) is a Planning process output — developing it requires the stakeholder register to exist first.

**Mindset Lesson:** Identify Stakeholders is about discovering and classifying — not yet communicating or scheduling; plan artifacts come after the register is built.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Identify Stakeholders is an Initiating process — it starts with or immediately after the project charter, not during planning.
- The stakeholder register is the primary output — it includes identification AND assessment data (power, interest, influence, engagement level), not just names and emails.
- Both supporters and opponents must be in the register — resistant stakeholders represent risks, not administrative inconvenience.
- This process repeats at phase gates, major scope changes, or whenever new stakeholders emerge — it is not a one-time event.
- Expert judgment from compliance, legal, and operations is essential in regulated industries — the PM cannot identify all stakeholders alone.
- Regulatory bodies (Bangladesh Bank, BSEC, CDBL, external auditors) are always stakeholders in regulated-sector projects — include them from Day 1.
- The stakeholder engagement assessment matrix captures current vs desired engagement levels — use it during identification to build the engagement plan later.
- Sponsor pressure to exclude inconvenient stakeholders violates the Fairness value in the Code of Ethics — push back professionally and document.
- The stakeholder register feeds into Plan Stakeholder Engagement and Plan Communications — accuracy here protects every downstream plan.
- Re-executing Identify Stakeholders when new parties emerge is a PMI process requirement, not optional housekeeping.
- **If you missed a stakeholder, you did not do Identify Stakeholders — you made a list; re-execute the process and find everyone who matters before they find you.**
