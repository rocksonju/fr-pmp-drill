# PMP Exam Master Brief: Control Resources

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Control Resources is the process of ensuring that the physical and team resources assigned and allocated to the project are available as planned, monitoring the planned versus actual utilization of resources, and taking corrective action when necessary. It sits in the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group under Resource Management, and it covers both physical resources (equipment, materials, facilities, infrastructure) and team resources in contexts where team performance directly drives physical output. The process is easily confused with Manage Team, which focuses on interpersonal dynamics and team development — Control Resources is about resource availability, utilization efficiency, and variance from the resource management plan. Key outputs include work performance information, change requests, and updates to the resource management plan, project documents, and project management plan. Cross-domain bleed is significant: resource shortfalls drive schedule variance (Control Schedule), cost overruns (Control Costs), and quality issues (Control Quality) simultaneously. In Agile environments, the concept of dedicated team allocation and sustainable pace replaces formal resource calendars, but the underlying principle — monitor, compare, adapt — is identical. On the exam, Control Resources questions often test whether you know the difference between a resource conflict requiring corrective action versus a resource change requiring a formal change request through Perform Integrated Change Control.

**In one sentence:** Control Resources ensures physical and team resources are available when needed, compares actual versus planned utilization, and drives corrective actions or change requests when resource performance deviates from the resource management plan.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal resource calendars, resource histograms, utilization tracking against baselines; variances trigger variance analysis and, if significant, change requests |
| Agile | Team capacity is managed sprint-by-sprint; resource availability is visible on the team board; sustainable pace and WIP limits replace formal utilization tracking |
| Hybrid | Phase-level resource baselines with Agile-style capacity management within iterations; resource conflicts at phase gates addressed via formal change control |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. When a critical resource is unexpectedly unavailable, the PM's first step is to assess impact on the project schedule and cost before escalating or sourcing a replacement. Real-world PMs immediately call vendors or HR — analysis precedes action in PMI's world.
2. Resource conflicts between projects must be resolved by the functional manager or resource manager, not by the PM unilaterally. Real-world PMs negotiate directly with their counterparts and secure resources through informal channels.
3. Physical resource shortfalls require a corrective action documented in the issue log — they do not automatically trigger a change request unless the schedule baseline or cost baseline will be affected. Real-world PMs conflate all problems with scope changes.
4. Workarounds for resource constraints (substituting a lower-grade material, using a less experienced technician) must be evaluated for quality and risk impact before implementation. Real-world PMs implement workarounds on the fly without formal analysis.
5. In Agile, protecting team capacity from external interruptions (context switching, ad-hoc work requests from outside the sprint) is a resource control activity. Real-world Agile teams let the organization pull team members away without formally accounting for the sprint capacity loss.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Fairness:** Allocating scarce resources equitably across work packages and team members — not favoring high-visibility deliverables at the expense of foundational work.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is accountable for anticipating resource shortfalls and escalating proactively, not waiting until the shortage causes visible schedule slippage.
- **Respect:** When team members are over-allocated, the PM has a responsibility to address unsustainable workloads — sustained over-allocation without action disrespects team members as people.
- **Honesty:** Reporting resource utilization accurately, including underutilization that may indicate scope delivery issues or team disengagement.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Resource Management Plan | Defines how resources will be acquired, allocated, managed, and released; contains resource calendars and utilization rates | This is both an input and an output of Control Resources (updates) — know the direction |
| Physical Resources | Equipment, materials, supplies, facilities, infrastructure — non-human project inputs | Do not confuse with team resources; Control Resources covers both but questions often test physical resource specifics |
| Resource Calendar | Shows when and how long resources are available for the project | Absence from the resource calendar does not mean a resource is free — it means availability is unconfirmed |
| Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS) | Hierarchical representation of resources by category and type | RBS is a planning artifact; in Control Resources, it supports identifying what type of resource is underperforming |
| Resource Histogram | Bar chart showing resource utilization over time; highlights over-allocation and under-utilization | A histogram showing consistent over-allocation signals a resource conflict requiring action |
| Utilization Rate | Actual resource hours used / Planned resource hours × 100% | Under-utilization can be as damaging as over-allocation — both indicate planning or execution failure |
| Issue Log | Document recording issues, owners, and resolution status | Resource conflicts that cannot be resolved at the PM level are escalated as issues, not immediately change requests |
| Team Performance Assessment | Evaluation of team effectiveness in achieving project objectives | Control Resources and Manage Team both reference team performance — in Control Resources the focus is utilization efficiency; in Manage Team it is interpersonal development |
| Corrective Action | Actions taken to bring future resource performance in line with the plan | Corrective actions are implemented within PM authority; schedule/cost baseline changes require change requests |
| Change Request | Formal request to modify the project scope, schedule, cost baseline, or resource management plan | A resource substitution that changes quality standards or scope requires a change request — a like-for-like swap may not |
| Work Performance Information | Analyzed resource data (utilization rates, availability gaps, corrective action status) — output of Control Resources | Contrast with Work Performance Data (raw actuals) which is the input |
| Resource Optimization Techniques | Resource leveling (adjusts schedule around resource constraints) and resource smoothing (adjusts within float without extending schedule) | Leveling can extend the schedule; smoothing cannot — know the difference |
| Procurement Documents | Contracts and agreements that define physical resource supply terms | Resource shortfalls from vendors may trigger contract performance clauses, not just internal corrective actions |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| A critical piece of equipment is delayed by the vendor | Assess impact on project schedule and cost baseline | Impact analysis precedes corrective action or escalation |
| Two project managers are competing for the same specialist | Escalate the conflict to the resource/functional manager | Cross-project resource conflicts are above the PM's authority to resolve unilaterally |
| A team member reports a key material is running out faster than planned | Update the issue log and investigate root cause (utilization rate vs. waste vs. scope change) | Documentation and root-cause analysis precede procurement action |
| Resource utilization data shows the team is consistently at 120% capacity | Discuss with the functional manager and consider resource leveling or scope reprioritization | Over-allocation must be addressed through proper resource management channels, not ignored |
| A substitute material is proposed due to supply chain disruption | Evaluate the substitute's impact on quality and risk before approving | Workarounds require impact analysis — implementing without analysis violates quality and risk management |
| The resource management plan no longer reflects current conditions | Submit a change request to update the resource management plan baseline | Baseline documents cannot be informally updated — formal change control applies |
| An approved resource is reassigned by the functional manager without notice | Document in the issue log, assess impact, and escalate to the project sponsor if schedule baseline is threatened | Unauthorized resource reassignment is an issue requiring escalation, not silent acceptance |
| Agile team capacity drops 20% due to unplanned organizational demands | Facilitate a sprint planning adjustment with the product owner to reprioritize the sprint backlog | Sprint backlog must reflect real team capacity — proceeding with original commitments under reduced capacity sets the team up for failure |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-table**

| Escalation Scenario | Correct PM Action |
|---|---|
| Functional manager insists on reassigning a team member despite the PM's objection and documented project impact | Escalate to the project sponsor and present quantified schedule/cost impact; do not accept the reassignment without formal acknowledgment of consequences |
| A vendor consistently delivers materials below specification — project team has been accepting them informally | Stop accepting non-conforming materials, document the quality issue, engage the procurement team to enforce contract terms, and evaluate whether a change request is needed |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Formal resource calendars and histograms tracked against the resource management plan
- Over-allocation thresholds trigger mandatory resource leveling or change requests
- Physical resource shortfalls documented in the issue log with escalation timelines
- Vendor resource performance tracked against procurement contracts

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Agile: Sustainable pace principle — team capacity is visible and protected; WIP limits prevent over-allocation
- Sprint capacity is re-assessed each planning session; velocity tracks team resource effectiveness over time
- Hybrid: Formal resource calendars at phase level; Agile capacity management within sprints
- Cross-team dependencies managed through Scrum of Scrums or similar coordination mechanisms

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC:** Skilled regulatory compliance resources (legal, audit, IT security) are scarce and shared across programs; resource conflicts require PMO-level arbitration; team member certifications (CFA, CAMS) may be contractually required and create replacement constraints
- **Construction / Infrastructure:** Equipment availability is a critical path driver; resource control failures cause immediate schedule impact; union rules govern working hours and overtime, creating formal constraints on resource flexibility
- **IT / Software:** Key personnel dependencies (architects, security engineers) create concentration risk; bus factor analysis informs contingency resource planning
- **Pharmaceuticals / Healthcare:** Validated equipment and qualified personnel requirements create regulatory resource constraints that cannot be resolved with simple substitution

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Negotiate directly with a peer PM to acquire their team member when a resource conflict arises | This is how resources actually move in most organizations — informal negotiation between PMs is the norm | PMI requires resource conflicts to be escalated to the functional manager or resource manager; peer negotiation bypasses authority structures |
| Immediately procure a replacement resource when one becomes unavailable | Acting quickly to maintain schedule looks proactive and decisive | The PM must first assess impact before deciding whether replacement, schedule adjustment, or a change request is the right response |
| Accept a lower-specification substitute material to keep the schedule | Schedule pressure is real; the vendor is available; the material is "close enough" | Substituting materials without evaluating quality and risk impact violates quality management standards and may require a change request |
| Update the resource management plan directly without a change request when conditions change | The PM knows the situation better than anyone; updating the plan feels like good document maintenance | Resource management plan is a component of the project management plan — changes require formal change control if they affect baselines |
| Ignore team over-allocation if the team is still meeting deliverable deadlines | If deliverables are being produced, the over-allocation is manageable — why create a conflict? | Sustained over-allocation is a risk to team health, retention, and future performance; PMI expects the PM to address it proactively |
| Reassign tasks internally when a resource is unavailable without updating project documents | This is routine task-level management that happens constantly without paperwork | If the reassignment affects schedule, cost, or quality, it must be documented; undocumented changes erode the integrity of project records |
| Ask the sponsor to override the functional manager's resource decision | Escalating to the sponsor feels like using appropriate authority channels | The sponsor is not the resource owner; escalation should go to the functional manager first, then the PMO or sponsor if unresolved |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager is managing a large construction project. The PM discovers that the site supervisor has been approving the use of substandard concrete batches from a vendor to avoid supply delays and keep the schedule on track. The material specification was defined in the project scope statement and quality management plan. The site supervisor explains that the variance is "within acceptable limits" based on his personal judgment, and that no formal testing was done. The project is currently on schedule and within budget. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Commend the site supervisor for keeping the project on track and document the decision in the lessons learned register
B. Halt use of the non-conforming material, conduct formal quality testing per the quality management plan, and document the issue in the issue log
C. Perform a cost-benefit analysis to determine whether the material variance is worth addressing given the schedule benefits achieved
D. Inform the sponsor that the site supervisor made an unauthorized decision and request his removal from the project

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A development team using Scrum has committed to 40 story points for the current two-week sprint. Three days into the sprint, the VP of Engineering asks the Scrum master to assign two team members to support an urgent production incident at another client. The Scrum master complies without informing the product owner. By the end of the sprint, the team delivers 22 story points. In the sprint retrospective, the team attributes the shortfall to the mid-sprint resource diversion. What should have been done FIRST when the VP made the resource request?

A. Comply with the VP's request since the VP outranks the Scrum master and the business need is urgent
B. The Scrum master should have immediately protected the team, informed the product owner, and escalated to the organizational level where the trade-off decision could be made formally
C. Reduce the sprint commitment to 22 story points at the start of the sprint based on available capacity
D. Complete the sprint with the reduced team and absorb the velocity impact without escalation

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project is in its third phase. The phase plan allocated a senior database architect for 30% of his time over six weeks. In week three, the architect's functional manager informs the PM that the architect has been reassigned to an emergency regulatory project and will not be available for the remaining three weeks. The PM's project has two critical deliverables dependent on the architect's work in weeks four through six. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Immediately source a replacement architect from an external vendor to avoid any schedule delay
B. Assess the impact of the resource loss on the schedule and cost baselines, document in the issue log, and escalate to the functional manager with a formal request to maintain the commitment
C. Adjust the project schedule internally to accommodate the resource gap without stakeholder notification
D. Contact the regulatory project PM directly and negotiate shared access to the architect

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is reviewing resource utilization reports for the fourth consecutive week. The reports show that two work packages are running at 130% resource utilization while three other work packages are running at 60% utilization. The project is currently on schedule. The resource management plan defines 85%–115% as the acceptable utilization range. What is the PM's BEST single action?

A. Wait and monitor since the project is on schedule and the average utilization across all work packages is within range
B. Apply resource leveling to shift resources from the under-utilized work packages to the over-utilized ones, assess schedule impact, and document the analysis
C. Immediately submit a change request to add resources to the over-utilized work packages
D. Ask the team leads of the over-utilized work packages to increase working hours temporarily

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A Kanban team notices that three types of physical resources (test devices, development environments, and shared lab time) are each showing consistent utilization above 90%, creating bottlenecks. The team wants to resolve the issue. Place the following actions in the correct order: (i) Identify which resource bottleneck is causing the greatest throughput impact; (ii) Implement a WIP limit adjustment for the constrained resource stage; (iii) Propose a resource capacity increase to the product owner and sponsor; (iv) Measure the throughput impact of the WIP limit change after two weeks.

A. (iii) → (i) → (ii) → (iv)
B. (i) → (ii) → (iv) → (iii)
C. (i) → (iii) → (ii) → (iv)
D. (ii) → (i) → (iv) → (iii)

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT an output of the Control Resources process?

A. Work performance information
B. Change requests
C. Resource management plan updates
D. Team performance assessments

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A project manager is under pressure from the project sponsor to report resource utilization as "on plan" in the monthly executive dashboard, even though actual utilization data shows three critical resources are running at 145% for the past six weeks. The sponsor argues that showing over-utilization will trigger a portfolio-level resource audit that will delay other projects. The PM knows that the current over-utilization is creating quality risks and increasing the likelihood of team burnout. What should the PM do?

A. Agree with the sponsor and report utilization as on-plan since the sponsor has the authority to make this decision
B. Report accurate utilization data in the dashboard, document the sponsor's request in the issue log, and escalate to the PMO if the sponsor insists on inaccurate reporting
C. Compromise by reporting utilization as "slightly elevated" without disclosing the 145% figure to soften the impact
D. Report accurate data internally but use the sponsor's preferred language in the executive dashboard to manage upward relationships

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid program has five component projects. Resource allocation is managed centrally by the PMO. The PMO resource manager notifies component project PMs that a shared infrastructure specialist, currently allocated to two projects, will be fully reassigned to one project due to a critical regulatory deadline in that project. The other project PM has not been informed. The affected PM learns about the change from the infrastructure specialist directly. The communications management plan requires the PMO to notify affected PMs of resource changes within 24 hours. The notification is now four days late. What should the affected PM do FIRST?

A. Contact the PMO resource manager, document the late notification, and request a formal assessment of the schedule and cost impact of the resource change
B. Escalate directly to the executive sponsor of the program since the PMO failed to follow the communications plan
C. Accept the situation since the regulatory project has higher priority and adjust the project schedule internally
D. Reassign internal team members to cover the specialist's work without formal analysis

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A project manager at a capital markets firm is managing a hybrid technology implementation project. A key vendor is consistently delivering hardware 20%–30% below the contracted delivery schedule. The vendor's PM claims the delays are due to global supply chain disruptions. The project's critical path is impacted. The PM has issued three formal written notifications to the vendor per the contract terms. The vendor's performance has not improved. The project schedule baseline shows a four-week delay is now projected. What should the PM do NEXT?

A. Issue a fourth written notification to the vendor to establish a more complete paper trail before escalating
B. Engage the procurement manager and legal team to formally assess contract remedies, escalate the performance failure, and submit a change request for the projected schedule impact
C. Source an alternative vendor immediately and cancel the contract with the current vendor
D. Absorb the schedule delay and adjust the project schedule baseline without a change request

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A project manager is performing Control Resources and identifies the following: (1) Two work packages are 15% over their allocated resource hours; (2) A critical subcontractor has missed two delivery milestones; (3) A team member has voluntarily taken on additional work from outside the project, reducing their availability by 25%; (4) The project is currently on schedule but the resource forecast shows a risk of 10% overrun at completion. Which THREE of the following actions are most appropriate for the PM to take?

A. Document the work package over-utilization in the issue log and perform root-cause analysis
B. Immediately replace the subcontractor to eliminate future delivery risk
C. Formally notify the subcontractor of the missed milestones per the contract terms and engage the procurement manager to assess remedies
D. Have a direct conversation with the team member about their availability commitment and document the adjustment in the resource management plan if needed
E. Submit a change request to expand the project budget by 10% based on the resource forecast
F. Update the risk register with the forecasted resource overrun as a new risk item and develop a response plan

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Quality Management and Stewardship principles. Non-conforming materials must be formally addressed per the quality management plan — this is not a judgment call for the site supervisor. Halting use and conducting formal testing is the correct first step. PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility: the PM is accountable for ensuring deliverables meet specifications. Option A rewards a governance violation. Option C treats a quality safety issue as a financial trade-off. Option D addresses the personnel issue before the quality issue, which is the wrong priority order.
**Mindset Lesson:** When quality standards are violated — regardless of schedule impact — halt, test, document, and then decide.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — Scrum values include transparency and commitment. The Scrum master's role is to protect the team and ensure the sprint commitment is based on actual capacity. Mid-sprint resource diversions require formal acknowledgment at the organizational level, not quiet compliance. The product owner needed to be informed immediately so they could decide on trade-offs. Option A violates the PM/Scrum master's responsibility to protect the team. Option C would have been appropriate if done before the sprint, not reactively. Option D conceals the impact from stakeholders.
**Mindset Lesson:** In Agile, mid-sprint resource diversions must be surfaced to the product owner and resolved at the organizational level — not absorbed silently.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — impact assessment before action. The PM must quantify the impact of the resource loss before deciding on a course of action. Documenting in the issue log and escalating formally maintains governance integrity. Option A skips analysis and commits to a cost. Option C hides the impact from stakeholders. Option D bypasses the functional manager's authority and the PM's own chain of command.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a planned resource is lost, quantify and document before you source, negotiate, or absorb.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Resource Management — resource leveling is the correct technique when utilization thresholds are breached and schedule float allows reallocation. The PM should analyze whether shifting resources from under-utilized to over-utilized work packages resolves the variance within the existing schedule. Option A ignores documented threshold breaches — the plan defines 85%–115% as acceptable, and 130% is a breach regardless of schedule status. Option C is premature — internal reallocation should be attempted first. Option D creates unsustainable workloads.
**Mindset Lesson:** When utilization thresholds are breached, apply resource management techniques first — adding resources or hours is the last resort.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide and Theory of Constraints. The correct sequence is: identify the binding constraint (i), implement a WIP limit to protect throughput at the bottleneck (ii), measure the impact before escalating for more resources (iv), and only then propose capacity increase if the WIP limit alone is insufficient (iii). Option A escalates for resources before attempting internal optimization. Option C escalates before testing the WIP adjustment. Option D implements WIP limits blindly without identifying the primary constraint.
**Mindset Lesson:** In Agile resource control, identify the binding constraint before implementing WIP changes, and measure before requesting additional capacity.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Team performance assessments are an output of Manage Team, not Control Resources. The outputs of Control Resources are: work performance information, change requests, project management plan updates (resource management plan, schedule baseline, cost baseline), project document updates (assumption log, issue log, lessons learned register, physical resource assignments, resource breakdown structure, risk register), and organizational process assets updates. Team performance assessments evaluate team dynamics and are specifically tied to the Manage Team process.
**Mindset Lesson:** Team performance assessments belong to Manage Team — Control Resources produces work performance information about physical and team utilization, not team development evaluations.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty. Reporting inaccurate data at the request of a sponsor violates the PM's ethical obligation to stakeholders who rely on accurate information. The PM must report truthfully and document the sponsor's request as a risk/issue. If the sponsor insists on misrepresentation, the PM escalates to the PMO or appropriate oversight body. Option A surrenders professional integrity to positional authority. Option C is a form of data manipulation. Option D creates two conflicting records — a form of deception.
**Mindset Lesson:** Accurate reporting is a non-negotiable PMI obligation — sponsor authority does not override honesty requirements.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Communications Management Plan — the PM's first step when a plan violation occurs is to document the breach and assess impact, not immediately escalate. The 24-hour notification requirement was violated; the PM should raise this with the PMO resource manager and formally request impact assessment. Option B escalates too quickly without giving the PMO an opportunity to respond. Option C accepts an undocumented change without assessing impact. Option D implements an unauthorized workaround.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a communications plan is violated, document and address through the responsible process owner before escalating to senior leadership.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Procurement Management — after three formal notifications without improvement, the PM has exhausted the notification remedy. The next step is to engage the procurement manager and legal team to exercise contractual remedies (liquidated damages, cure notice, or termination for default). A change request for the schedule baseline impact is required because the four-week delay affects the schedule baseline. Option A adds a fourth notification — this is ineffective after three have failed. Option C is a procurement decision requiring procurement manager involvement, not a unilateral PM action. Option D absorbs a schedule baseline change without change control.
**Mindset Lesson:** After repeated vendor notifications without improvement, escalate to procurement and legal — and simultaneously file the change request for the schedule impact.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, D
**PMI Justification:** (A) Documenting work package over-utilization in the issue log and investigating root cause is the correct first response to a utilization variance — this is the core Control Resources activity. (C) Formally notifying the subcontractor per contract terms and engaging the procurement manager is the correct response to missed milestones — this enforces contractual obligations. (D) Addressing the team member's availability directly and documenting the adjustment is the correct response to a voluntary capacity reduction — it resolves the resource commitment issue through proper channels. (B) Replacing the subcontractor is a major procurement decision that requires procurement manager involvement, not unilateral PM action. (E) Submitting a budget change request based on a forecast risk is premature — a risk response plan and further analysis are needed first. (F) Updating the risk register is analytically correct but it is not among the three most operationally immediate actions at this stage.
**Mindset Lesson:** In Control Resources, the three immediate obligations are: document variances, enforce contract terms, and address resource commitment gaps — financial and vendor termination decisions require additional process steps.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Control Resources monitors physical and team resource availability and utilization against the resource management plan
- The process covers both physical resources (equipment, materials) and team resources — do not limit it to people
- Variances are documented in the issue log; root-cause analysis precedes corrective action
- Cross-project resource conflicts are escalated to the functional manager — the PM does not negotiate peer-to-peer
- Resource substitutions require quality and risk impact evaluation before implementation
- Resource management plan changes require formal change requests — no informal baseline updates
- Over-allocation must be addressed: resource leveling (may extend schedule) or resource smoothing (within float)
- Vendor resource failures trigger contractual remedy processes, not just additional notifications
- In Agile: protect team capacity; mid-sprint diversions require product owner involvement and formal trade-off decisions
- Sustained over-allocation violates respect for team members and creates retention and quality risk
- **When a resource variance is detected, the PMI reflex is: assess impact first, document in issue log second, act at the appropriate authority level third — never skip the analysis.**
