# PMP Exam Master Brief: Estimate Activity Resources

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Estimate Activity Resources is the process of estimating the type and quantities of material, human, equipment, and supplies required to perform each scheduled activity. It sits inside the Planning Process Group and Resource Management Knowledge Area, and its outputs feed directly into Estimate Activity Durations, Develop Schedule, and Estimate Costs — making it a critical upstream dependency for both time and cost baselines. The process requires analyzing the scope of each activity against available resource options, organizational constraints, and market conditions before committing to resource quantities and types. On the exam, this process appears in cross-domain questions that blend Schedule, Cost, and Resource Management — the resource estimates produced here are load-bearing inputs to the critical path and the cost estimate. Key techniques include bottom-up estimating (aggregating activity-level estimates), analogous estimating (leveraging historical data), and parametric estimating (unit-rate calculation). The process also explicitly considers alternatives analysis — evaluating different resource options such as using equipment versus manual labor, or senior versus junior staff, before committing. Resource calendars from Plan Resource Management constrain what is available; the estimates produced here must respect those constraints. **In one sentence:** Estimate Activity Resources quantifies what it will take — in people, equipment, materials, and supplies — to complete each scheduled activity, providing the factual foundation from which durations and costs are derived.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal bottom-up estimation at the activity level; resource estimates documented in a Resource Breakdown Structure; each activity has an assigned type, quantity, and duration basis; estimates are baselined and change-controlled |
| Agile | Team capacity drives story point velocity rather than activity-by-activity resource estimation; team self-estimates collectively in sprint planning; estimation is relative and iterative, refined each cycle |
| Hybrid | Governance-tier activities (regulatory milestones, integration events) estimated formally; delivery activities estimated by the team using agile techniques; resource estimates roll up to a hybrid baseline |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Resource estimates must be produced at the activity level, not the phase or deliverable level — real-world PMs often estimate at too high a level and then wonder why the schedule collapses during execution.
2. Alternatives analysis is a required technique before locking estimates — real-world PMs accept the first viable resource option; PMI expects evaluation of multiple options to optimize cost, time, and quality tradeoffs.
3. The Resource Breakdown Structure is an output of this process, not just a reference document — real-world PMs often produce only a list; PMI expects a hierarchical structure that maps resource types to the WBS.
4. Expert judgment used in estimation must be documented with the basis of estimates — real-world PMs accept expert input without recording assumptions; PMI requires the basis to be traceable for future audits and replanning.
5. Resource requirements drive duration estimates, not the reverse — real-world PMs assign resources to fit a predetermined schedule; PMI sequence is estimate resources first, then derive durations from those resource assignments.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Honesty:** Estimates must reflect genuine analysis — deliberately low-balling resource estimates to win project approval, then revising upward during execution, violates the honesty obligation.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is responsible for producing defensible estimates with traceable assumptions; accepting unrealistic estimates without documented basis is an abdication of responsibility.
- **Fairness:** Resource estimates must not systematically under-resource work assigned to certain teams or individuals based on bias or favoritism.
- **Respect:** When leveraging expert judgment, the expert's input must be fairly represented in the basis of estimates — misattributing or misrepresenting expert opinions disrespects their contribution.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Resource Requirements | Output of Estimate Activity Resources; documents type and quantity of resources needed per activity | Do not confuse with the Resource Management Plan — requirements are the quantified output; the plan is the governance framework |
| Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS) | Hierarchical representation of resources by category and type; output of this process | RBS is also the abbreviation for Risk Breakdown Structure — context (resource vs. risk) determines which is meant |
| Basis of Estimates | Documentation explaining how estimates were derived, assumptions made, range of possible outcomes, and confidence level | Omitting basis of estimates is a frequent wrong answer — PMI always expects estimates to be traceable |
| Bottom-Up Estimating | Estimating resource requirements for individual activities and rolling them up to a total; most accurate but most time-consuming | Exam often presents bottom-up as too expensive or slow; PMI preference is accuracy for complex work |
| Analogous Estimating | Using historical data from similar past activities as the basis for current estimates; less accurate, appropriate for early-stage or when time/data is limited | Analogous uses historical project data — do not confuse with parametric, which uses statistical unit rates |
| Parametric Estimating | Using a statistical relationship between historical data and other variables (e.g., 5 staff-days per 1,000 lines of code); requires validated model | Parametric is more accurate than analogous only when the model is validated — an unvalidated parametric model can be less accurate |
| Alternatives Analysis | Evaluating different resource options (e.g., make vs. buy, senior vs. junior, equipment vs. manual) to optimize the estimate | PMI expects alternatives analysis to occur before finalizing estimates — skipping it is a wrong answer |
| Resource Calendar | Shows availability, capacity, and working times of each resource; INPUT to this process (not output) | Common trap: candidates think resource calendars are created here — they are an input from Plan Resource Management |
| Activity Resource Requirements | Type and quantity of resources identified for each activity; feeds into duration and cost estimation | Distinct from Resource Requirements document — activity resource requirements is the detailed, activity-level version |
| Published Estimating Data | External databases providing production rates, unit costs, and resource benchmarks for specific industries | Used in parametric and analogous estimating; exam tests whether you know to use external data when internal data is absent |
| Staff-Hours / Staff-Days | Unit measures for human resource effort estimates | Confusing effort (staff-days) with duration (calendar days) is a classic trap — 40 staff-hours of effort may take 5 calendar days with one resource or 1 day with five |
| Three-Point Estimate (PERT for Resources) | (O + 4M + P) / 6 for expected resource quantity; also used for duration | When applied to resources, PERT quantifies uncertainty in how many resources are needed, not just how long something takes |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Activity durations seem too long and the sponsor wants them shortened" | Review resource estimates — more resources may reduce duration; do not compress schedule without analyzing resource alternatives | Duration derives from resource assignments; resource analysis precedes schedule compression |
| "The team is estimating at the deliverable level, not the activity level" | Decompose deliverables to activity level before accepting resource estimates | Activity-level estimation is required; deliverable-level estimates are insufficiently granular for schedule and cost |
| "Historical data from a similar project exists but the PM is using expert judgment alone" | Incorporate the historical data using analogous or parametric techniques alongside expert judgment | Historical data improves estimate accuracy; relying solely on expert judgment when data exists is not PMI best practice |
| "Two resource options exist: hiring a specialist or training an existing employee" | Conduct alternatives analysis comparing cost, time, quality, and risk of each option before estimating | PMI requires alternatives analysis before committing to a resource type |
| "Estimates have been produced but no assumptions or confidence range is documented" | Develop the Basis of Estimates documenting assumptions, ranges, and confidence levels | Undocumented estimates cannot be audited, replanned, or defended to stakeholders |
| "An activity requires specialized equipment with a 6-week procurement lead time" | Reflect the procurement lead time in the resource estimate and update the schedule accordingly | Physical resource constraints (lead time, availability) are inputs to activity sequencing and duration |
| "The project is in early planning with limited scope definition" | Use analogous estimating with stated assumptions and wide confidence ranges | When scope is uncertain, analogous is the appropriate technique; document the uncertainty explicitly |
| "Sponsor insists resource estimates are too high and demands a 20% reduction" | Present the Basis of Estimates to the sponsor and conduct alternatives analysis to find legitimate reduction options | Do not accept arbitrary cuts without analysis; PMI expects defensible estimates, not sponsor-directed numbers |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Scenario Signal | Correct Escalation Pattern |
|---|---|
| "Resource manager says estimates are too high but offers no data to support the reduction" | Request supporting data; if none is provided, escalate to sponsor with original estimates and documented disagreement |
| "Estimates keep changing because scope is undefined" | Escalate the scope uncertainty to the sponsor as a constraint; complete scope definition before finalizing resource estimates |

```
    YES → Analogous OR Parametric Estimating
    NO  → Expert Judgment + Bottom-Up Estimating

  For each activity: evaluate resource TYPE options
  (e.g., senior vs. junior / equipment vs. labor / internal vs. contract)
         |
         v

  Type of resource → Quantity needed → Unit of measure
  (staff-days, equipment-hours, cubic meters, etc.)
         |
         v

  Activity Resource Requirements (per activity)
  Basis of Estimates (assumptions, ranges, confidence)
         |
         v
OUTPUT LAYER:
  Resource Requirements
  Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS)
  Basis of Estimates

```

**Mnemonic:** **TRADE-B**
T — Techniques: analogous, parametric, bottom-up, expert judgment
R — Resource Calendars as key input
A — Alternatives Analysis before committing
D — Document Basis of Estimates
E — Estimate type AND quantity per activity
B — Build the Resource Breakdown Structure as output

**Memory hook sentence:** "Before signing off on resources, smart PMs always make a TRADE-B."

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Bottom-up estimation at the activity level; each WBS work package broken into activities with explicit resource type and quantity
- Basis of Estimates documented and formally reviewed; uncertainty ranges expressed using three-point estimates
- RBS formally produced and cross-referenced with WBS to produce the RAM
- Physical resource estimates include procurement lead times and delivery logistics embedded in the schedule

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Team capacity (velocity, sprint capacity in story points or hours) replaces activity-level resource estimation for delivery work
- Resource estimation focuses on team composition (skill mix, number of people) rather than per-activity quantities
- Hybrid: governance milestones use formal bottom-up estimates; sprint delivery uses team capacity planning
- Estimation accuracy is expected to improve over iterations — early estimates are acknowledged as rough; refinement is built into the planning cadence

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC:** Regulatory change projects must estimate compliance review resource hours separately from development effort; BSEC review cycles require dedicated specialist time that cannot be compressed
- **Construction / Engineering:** Parametric estimating is standard (unit rates per square meter, per ton, per linear meter); published estimating databases (RSMeans, etc.) are primary sources
- **IT / Software:** Skill-specific resource estimates matter enormously — a generic "developer" estimate is insufficient; estimates must distinguish frontend, backend, security, QA, and DevOps roles
- **Healthcare / Pharmaceuticals:** Clinical resource estimates must account for regulatory-mandated review cycles and approval timelines that are not compressible regardless of resource quantity

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Estimate resources at the deliverable or phase level to save planning time" | Phase-level estimation is faster and sufficient for early planning; experienced PMs use rolling-wave and stay high-level | PMI requires activity-level estimates because deliverable-level estimates are too imprecise to drive schedule and cost baselines |
| "Accept the sponsor's resource reduction request and revise estimates downward" | Sponsors have authority and experienced PMs know when to be pragmatic about stakeholder relationships | PMI requires estimates to be based on analysis; arbitrary downward revisions without alternatives analysis violate the basis-of-estimates principle |
| "Resource calendars are created during Estimate Activity Resources" | Resource calendars seem like a natural output of estimation — you are figuring out what resources you need | Resource calendars are an INPUT, created during Plan Resource Management; this process uses them but does not produce them |
| "Use analogous estimating for all activities because it is faster" | Analogous is efficient and experienced PMs know that speed matters when stakeholders want quick answers | Analogous is appropriate only when sufficient historical similarity exists; applying it universally without that basis produces inaccurate estimates |
| "The RBS is just a list of required resources — no hierarchy is needed" | A flat list is simpler and just as informative in most practical situations | PMI defines the RBS as a hierarchical structure organized by category and type — the hierarchy enables cross-referencing with the WBS in the RAM |
| "Effort and duration are the same; estimating one gives you the other" | In single-resource scenarios this appears true and experienced PMs sometimes conflate the two | Effort (total work units) and duration (calendar time) are distinct — 80 hours of effort by two people = 5 calendar days, not 10 |
| "Skip alternatives analysis when the preferred resource option is obvious" | When the answer is clear, analysis feels like bureaucratic overhead | PMI requires alternatives analysis as a technique of this process — skipping it means the estimate lacks defensibility and optimization |
| "Bottom-up estimating is too time-consuming; use parametric for everything" | Parametric is faster and looks precise due to its mathematical basis | Parametric accuracy depends entirely on the validity of the underlying statistical model; bottom-up is the PMI default for accurate estimates when models are unvalidated |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1**

A project manager at a capital markets firm is estimating resources for a regulatory reporting system upgrade. The PM has identified resource requirements and documented them in a spreadsheet. When the compliance director reviews the plan, she asks why the specialized data governance roles are estimated at junior analyst rates when the regulation requires certified specialists. The PM admits the lower rates were used to keep the budget within the sponsor's target. The compliance director flags this as a potential regulatory and ethical issue. What is the PM's primary obligation at this point?

A. Revise the estimates to reflect certified specialist rates and present the revised budget to the sponsor for approval
B. Perform alternatives analysis to determine if a junior-to-specialist mentorship model can meet regulatory requirements at lower cost
C. Maintain the current estimates until the budget is formally approved, then request a change
D. Escalate only if the compliance director formally documents her concern in writing

**Q2**

An agile team is planning a new product release. During sprint planning, the team estimates each user story using planning poker. A senior developer consistently estimates higher than the rest of the team and cites technical debt that others are not accounting for. The Product Owner pressures the team to accept the lower consensus estimates to preserve velocity targets. The senior developer raises the issue to the Scrum Master. What should the Scrum Master do FIRST?

A. Accept the lower estimates to align with the Product Owner's velocity targets and document the senior developer's concern as a risk
B. Facilitate a technical discussion to surface the technical debt concerns and allow them to influence the estimates transparently
C. Escalate the estimation conflict to the project sponsor as a resource risk
D. Split the user stories to separate technical debt work from feature work so each can be estimated independently

**Q3**

A hybrid project PM has used parametric estimating for governance milestone activities and team capacity planning for agile delivery activities. During execution, the parametric model for compliance review activities is found to be based on outdated regulatory cycle times — the current BSEC approval process takes three times longer than the historical baseline. The PM must decide how to rebaseline the affected estimates. What is the BEST FIRST action?

A. Apply a 3x multiplier to all remaining compliance activity estimates and update the schedule
B. Validate the new BSEC cycle time with the compliance officer, update the parametric model, re-estimate affected activities, and submit a change request
C. Switch from parametric to analogous estimating for all remaining compliance activities to reduce reliance on the flawed model
D. Absorb the schedule impact within the management reserve and continue without revising the baseline

**Q4**

A project manager is estimating resources for a software development project. Two options exist for a critical integration module: (1) assign two senior developers for 3 weeks, or (2) assign four junior developers for 6 weeks with a senior developer in a review role for 1 week. The sponsor prefers Option 2 because it reduces cost. The PM's risk assessment suggests Option 2 carries a 35% probability of rework that would consume an additional 4 weeks of junior developer time. Which of the following BEST supports the PM's resource estimate decision?

A. Select Option 1 because the sponsor's cost preference does not override risk-adjusted analysis
B. Select Option 2 because the sponsor's authority on cost decisions must be respected
C. Present a risk-adjusted cost comparison of both options with probability-weighted outcomes and let the sponsor decide with full information
D. Select Option 1 and document the sponsor's preference as a risk in the risk register

**Q5**

An agile project team needs to sequence the following Estimate Activity Resources activities for an upcoming release. Arrange them in the correct PMI-aligned order:
1. Review resource calendars to confirm team availability for the release period
2. Decompose features into user stories for estimation
3. Conduct planning poker to estimate story points per user story
4. Calculate team velocity based on historical sprint data
5. Adjust capacity plan based on confirmed availability and planned velocity

A. 2, 4, 1, 3, 5
B. 1, 2, 4, 3, 5
C. 4, 1, 2, 3, 5
D. 2, 3, 4, 1, 5

**Q6**

Which of the following is NOT an output of the Estimate Activity Resources process?

A. Resource Requirements
B. Resource Breakdown Structure
C. Basis of Estimates
D. Resource Calendars

**Q7**

A project manager estimates that 120 staff-days of effort are required for a complex data migration activity. To meet a hard deadline, the PM plans to assign 10 resources to the activity, estimating a duration of 12 calendar days. The sponsor approves the plan. After two weeks, only 60% of the migration is complete. The PM investigates and discovers that the activity has significant sequential dependencies between sub-tasks that prevent full parallelization — at most 4 resources can work simultaneously. What is the PM's primary failure in the estimating process?

A. Using an incorrect estimating technique for the activity type
B. Failing to conduct alternatives analysis before committing to the resource and duration estimate
C. Not validating the resource parallelization assumptions during estimation
D. Accepting a hard deadline before resource estimates were complete

**Q8**

A hybrid project team is distributed across Dhaka, Singapore, and London. The PM has produced bottom-up resource estimates for all delivery activities. The Dhaka-based team members report that their estimates assumed uninterrupted availability, but they are also supporting a parallel BAU production support rotation that takes 30% of their time. The PM was unaware of this constraint. The schedule and cost baseline have already been approved. What should the PM do NEXT?

A. Absorb the 30% reduction within existing schedule buffer and monitor
B. Revise the resource estimates to reflect actual availability, update affected schedule and cost estimates, and submit a change request
C. Escalate to the sponsor that the Dhaka team's commitments violate the project's resource plan
D. Negotiate with the Dhaka resource manager to reduce BAU support to 15% so estimates remain valid

**Q9**

A project manager at a regulated financial firm is using parametric estimating for a compliance reporting project. The parametric model predicts 8 staff-days per regulatory report revision cycle. The compliance officer, who has 15 years of experience with BSEC reporting, believes the model is too optimistic and estimates 14 staff-days based on her experience with recent regulatory complexity increases. The PM must resolve the discrepancy before finalizing the estimate. What is the BEST course of action?

A. Use the parametric model because it is data-driven and objective
B. Use the expert's estimate because her domain authority supersedes statistical models
C. Use a three-point estimate combining the parametric model output, the expert's estimate, and a best-case scenario, and document both inputs in the Basis of Estimates
D. Escalate the disagreement to the sponsor and let the sponsor decide which estimate to use

**Q10**

A project manager is finalizing resource estimates for a large infrastructure project. Which THREE of the following would most appropriately trigger a revision to the activity resource estimates after they have been baselined?

A. A key team member resigns and the replacement resource has significantly lower productivity in the relevant technical domain
B. The sponsor requests a 10% across-the-board efficiency improvement without providing any analytical basis
C. A change request is approved that adds three new work packages to the WBS
D. The resource manager confirms that overtime will not be permitted for the duration of the project
E. A team member completes a training course that improves their efficiency on a non-critical path activity
F. A new regulatory directive requires additional compliance testing cycles that were not in the original scope

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility. The estimate was deliberately misrepresented to meet a budget target. The obligation is to correct the estimate to reflect reality, inform the sponsor, and seek proper approval. Option B (alternatives analysis) is a legitimate technique but does not first address the misrepresentation — honesty requires disclosure before optimization. Option C (wait for approval) perpetuates the misrepresentation. Option D (wait for written documentation) imposes a condition on an unconditional ethical obligation.
**Mindset Lesson:** PMI hardline: when an estimate is knowingly inaccurate, the obligation to correct and disclose is immediate — alternative solutions come after honesty, not instead of it.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — Team estimation principles; psychological safety. Estimates must reflect the team's genuine technical assessment. Suppressing legitimate technical concerns to hit velocity targets produces unrealistic plans. Option A documents a risk but does not resolve the estimation integrity issue. Option C (escalate to sponsor) is premature and bypasses the team's self-organizing authority. Option D (split stories) may be useful but does not first address why the senior developer's technical perspective is being suppressed.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile estimation, the Scrum Master's role is to protect the integrity of the estimation process — velocity targets are constraints to be surfaced, not levers to override technical judgment.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Estimate Activity Resources; change control process. When a parametric model is found to be invalid, the correct response is to validate the new baseline data, update the model, re-estimate, and formally change the baseline through the change control process. Option A (3x multiplier) applies a correction factor without validating the new data. Option C (switch to analogous) abandons a fixable model without basis. Option D (absorb in reserve) sidesteps the baseline integrity obligation.
**Mindset Lesson:** When an estimating model is invalidated by changed conditions, the fix is model correction plus formal change control — not workarounds that obscure the baseline impact.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Alternatives Analysis; stakeholder engagement. The PM's role is to provide decision-quality information, not to override sponsor authority or simply defer to preference. A risk-adjusted cost comparison quantifies the true decision and respects the sponsor's authority to decide with full information. Option A overrides the sponsor without providing analysis. Option B defers without analysis. Option D selects without transparency.
**Mindset Lesson:** PMI expects the PM to present risk-adjusted alternatives — the sponsor has authority over the decision, but the PM has the obligation to ensure that decision is fully informed.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — Release planning sequence. Features must be decomposed into estimable user stories (2) before estimation can begin; historical velocity establishes the baseline rate (4); resource calendars confirm actual availability for the specific period (1); planning poker estimates individual stories (3); capacity is then adjusted based on availability and velocity (5).
**Mindset Lesson:** Agile resource estimation follows a decompose-then-calibrate sequence — you cannot estimate stories you have not yet decomposed, and you cannot plan capacity without both velocity data and confirmed availability.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Process outputs. Resource Calendars are an INPUT to Estimate Activity Resources, not an output. They are produced during Plan Resource Management and used here to constrain estimates. Outputs of this process are: Resource Requirements, Resource Breakdown Structure, and Basis of Estimates.
**Mindset Lesson:** Resource calendars flow into estimation as a constraint — they are never created by the estimation process.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Estimate Activity Resources; parallelization assumptions. The fundamental failure is not validating whether the activity could actually utilize all 10 resources simultaneously. Bottom-up estimation requires understanding task structure, including sequential dependencies that limit concurrency. Option A is incorrect — the technique choice was not the primary failure; the assumption validation was. Option B (alternatives analysis) would not have uncovered this if the sequential dependency was never examined. Option D (hard deadline acceptance) is a secondary issue.
**Mindset Lesson:** Resource estimates that assume full parallelization must validate that the activity structure actually permits it — sequential dependencies are a constraint that must be surfaced during estimation, not discovered in execution.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Change control; Estimate Activity Resources accuracy. When a material constraint emerges that invalidates baselined estimates, the correct response is to revise the estimates to reflect actual availability and process a formal change request. Option A (absorb in buffer) is inappropriate when the impact is significant and systematic. Option C (escalate) is partially correct but the PM must first quantify the impact. Option D (negotiate BAU reduction) may be pursued but does not address the immediate need to correct the baseline.
**Mindset Lesson:** Undisclosed availability constraints invalidate resource estimates — the PM must surface the true picture, revise the baseline, and go through change control rather than quietly absorbing the impact.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Three-point estimating; Basis of Estimates. When credible quantitative data (parametric model) and credible expert judgment disagree, three-point estimating captures the range of uncertainty and both inputs become part of the documented basis of estimates. Option A dismisses valid expert knowledge. Option B dismisses valid data. Option D escalates a technical estimation decision that the PM should resolve analytically.
**Mindset Lesson:** When data and expert judgment conflict, three-point estimating is the PMI mechanism for quantifying and transparently documenting the uncertainty — neither source should be dismissed unilaterally.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, C, F
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Resource estimate updates. A (team member replacement with lower productivity) directly changes the quantity or time required per activity — a material estimate revision trigger. C (approved change adding WBS work packages) adds new activities that require new resource estimates — the baseline must expand. F (new regulatory directive adding testing cycles) changes scope and therefore resource requirements. Option B (sponsor request without analysis) does not justify a revision — estimates must be analysis-based. Option E (efficiency improvement on non-critical path activity) may reduce cost but does not warrant a formal baseline revision for minor gains. Option F (clarification: training on non-critical path activity) — same reasoning as E.
**Mindset Lesson:** Resource estimate revisions are triggered by changes in scope, availability, or validated productivity data — sponsor preferences and minor efficiency gains do not meet the threshold for baseline revision.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Estimate Activity Resources = quantify type and quantity of resources per scheduled activity
- Primary outputs: Resource Requirements, Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS), Basis of Estimates
- Resource Calendars are an INPUT, not an output — they come from Plan Resource Management
- Alternatives Analysis is a required technique — evaluate options before committing to resource type
- Bottom-up = most accurate; analogous = fastest; parametric = precise only with validated models
- Effort (staff-days) ≠ Duration (calendar days) — a parallelization assumption must be validated
- Basis of Estimates is non-negotiable — undocumented estimates are indefensible and unauditable
- RBS is hierarchical — a flat list of resources is insufficient for PMI standards
- Estimates must drive the schedule, not conform to it — resource analysis precedes duration estimation
- Agile equivalent: team capacity planning by velocity and sprint availability replaces activity-level estimation
- Three-point estimating resolves conflicts between data models and expert judgment
- **Burn-in reflex: Estimate resources at the activity level, document every assumption in the Basis of Estimates, and conduct alternatives analysis before committing — the estimate you baseline today is the contract you defend tomorrow.**
