# PMP Exam Master Brief: Develop Project Charter

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Develop Project Charter is the first formal process in project management — the act of authorizing the project to exist and granting the project manager the authority to apply resources. It occurs once (or at each phase in a phased project) within the Initiating Process Group under Integration Management. The charter does not describe *how* work will be done; it captures high-level scope, objectives, major constraints, assumptions, key stakeholders, and the business case link. Without a charter, the PM has no formal authority, the project has no organizational sanction, and stakeholders have no agreed starting reference. The charter is signed by a sponsor or initiating entity with sufficient organizational authority — the PM rarely authors it alone. In regulated industries like banking or capital markets, the charter may align to a Project Initiation Document (PID) format required by Bangladesh Bank, BSEC, or internal audit. Exam weight is relatively low as a standalone process but bleeds heavily into Integration, Stakeholder, and Communications domains — expect charter concepts embedded in scenario stems across 20–30% of the exam. **In one sentence:** The charter is the organizational contract that births the project, defines its purpose, and hands the PM legitimate authority.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Full charter document produced before planning begins; scope, schedule, and cost constraints documented at high level; sponsor-signed formal authorization |
| Agile | Lightweight charter or team canvas created collaboratively; may be a single page or even a shared understanding artifact; still captures vision, constraints, and PM authority |
| Hybrid | Charter authorizes the project at the organizational level; agile delivery approach is noted as tailoring decision; may reference sprints/iterations while retaining formal authorization structure |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **The PM's authority comes from the charter, not from their title or seniority.** Real-world instinct says authority comes from organizational rank or relationships — PMI says it comes from the signed document. No charter = no authority, regardless of informal power.
2. **The sponsor creates the charter, not the PM.** In practice, PMs frequently write their own charters. PMI is clear: the initiating entity (sponsor, PMO, executive) authorizes it. The PM may assist in drafting but does not self-authorize.
3. **Identifying stakeholders begins at chartering, not during planning.** Practitioners wait until the project is fully scoped to identify stakeholders. PMI requires high-level stakeholder identification as part of Initiating — they are listed in the charter.
4. **Assumptions and constraints belong in the charter at high level.** Experienced PMs defer assumptions documentation to planning. PMI expects known assumptions and constraints to be captured as early as chartering to avoid silent scope creep.
5. **A weak or missing business case is a charter risk, not a planning problem.** Real-world instinct treats business case as a pre-project finance exercise. PMI ties project justification to the charter throughout — if the business case changes materially, the charter may need updating.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** The PM must ensure the charter accurately reflects organizational commitment; accepting a charter with unrealistic constraints without raising concerns violates responsibility.
- **Honesty:** Known risks and constraints must be visible in the charter — omitting unfavorable information to secure project approval is dishonest and violates the Code of Ethics.
- **Fairness:** When a charter is developed without input from affected stakeholders, it may favor initiating entity interests; PMI expects transparent, inclusive identification of key stakeholders.
- **Respect:** In Bangladesh Bank or BSEC regulatory projects, respecting existing organizational authority structures during chartering (not bypassing senior approvers) aligns with PMI's respect value.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Project Charter | Formal document authorizing the project and the PM's authority to use resources | Confusing the charter with the project management plan — they are different documents at different levels of detail |
| Business Case | Justification document establishing the need, options, and recommended solution that feeds the charter | Business case is an INPUT to chartering, not produced during it — do not say chartering produces the business case |
| Benefits Management Plan | Document describing how and when project benefits will be realized; created by sponsor/business analyst | Also an INPUT; candidates confuse it as a charter output — it precedes and informs chartering |
| Initiating Entity | Organization or individual (sponsor, PMO, executive) that authorizes the project to begin | The PM is rarely the initiating entity on their own project — do not confuse PM with sponsor authority |
| Assumptions Log | Running record of all project assumptions, initialized during chartering | First created during Develop Project Charter, not during planning — a common sequence trap |
| Project Statement of Work (SOW) | Narrative describing products, services, or results the project will deliver; INPUT to chartering from the customer/sponsor | SOW is an input, not an output — do not say the charter contains a full SOW |
| Pre-Assignment | Resources already identified and committed before planning begins | Pre-assigned resources are documented in the charter under enterprise environmental factors — they limit planning freedom |
| Organizational Process Assets (OPAs) | Templates, historical records, lessons learned, policies used as inputs | Using OPAs from a similar banking project (e.g., prior core banking implementation) to draft the charter is correct PMI practice |
| Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs) | Organizational culture, market conditions, regulatory environment, existing systems | Bangladesh Bank regulations or BSEC listing requirements that constrain the project scope are EEFs — they shape the charter, not the other way |
| Project Selection Methods | Methods used by the initiating entity to choose among projects (BCR, NPV, IRR, payback period) | These are inputs to chartering, not processes the PM runs — the PM does not select projects; they execute the chosen one |
| High-Level Risk | Initial risk identification included in the charter at summary level | Not a full risk register — the charter only surfaces known high-level risks; the register is developed during planning |
| Milestone Schedule (High-Level) | Summary-level schedule in the charter listing major deliverables or phase gates | Not a detailed schedule — candidates confuse the charter's milestone list with the Schedule Management Plan output |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "The project has just been approved and you have been named PM" | Review the project charter | The charter is your primary authority document and source of high-level scope, constraints, and stakeholder list |
| "Stakeholders are disagreeing about project scope before planning starts" | Facilitate alignment and update charter if needed, then escalate to sponsor | Scope ambiguity at initiating is a charter-level issue; the sponsor resolves conflicts before planning commits resources |
| "The sponsor hands you a charter with unrealistic deadlines" | Discuss concerns with the sponsor before signing / accepting | Accepting an unachievable charter violates responsibility; the ethical and correct step is to raise concerns early |
| "You are asked to start work but no charter has been signed" | Request formal charter authorization before proceeding | Starting work without authorization exposes the organization to risk and the PM to lack of authority — PMI requires formal sanction |
| "The business case has significantly changed after charter approval" | Notify the sponsor and evaluate whether the charter needs updating | Charter validity is tied to the business case; a material change to justification may require re-authorization |
| "A new regulatory requirement (Bangladesh Bank circular) is discovered during chartering" | Incorporate the regulatory constraint into the charter assumptions and constraints section | Regulatory EEFs must be reflected in the charter to protect scope integrity throughout the project |
| "Two departments claim ownership over the project" | Escalate to the initiating entity/sponsor to clarify authority and update the charter | Organizational authority conflicts at initiating are resolved by the sponsoring entity, not by the PM unilaterally |
| "A key stakeholder is not listed in the charter" | Identify the stakeholder now and initiate the Identify Stakeholders process | Stakeholder identification begins in Initiating; missing stakeholders at chartering creates integration and communication risk |
| "The PM is asked to write the project charter themselves" | Draft for sponsor review and obtain sponsor signature for final authorization | PM may draft but must not self-authorize; the initiating entity's signature is the authorizing act |

### Escalation Edge Cases

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A sponsor insists on removing a known regulatory risk from the charter to make it look better for the board" | Refuse and escalate to PMO or higher governance | Omitting known risks to mislead decision-makers violates Honesty in the Code of Ethics — no sponsor pressure overrides this |
| "Executive pressures PM to start project activities before charter is signed to 'save time'" | Document the pressure and formally request authorization before deploying resources | Starting work without authorization is a governance breach; the PM must protect project integrity even under executive pressure |

**Mnemonic: B-SAM-O-PA**
Business case / SOW / Agreements / Meetings (Expert judgment) / Output: Charter / PA (PMO + Assumptions log)

**Memory hook sentence:** "Before Starting A Major Output, Project is Authorized."

**Mnemonic unpack:**
- B = Business Case (primary input driving why the project exists)
- S = Statement of Work (describes what will be delivered)
- A = Agreements / contracts (external project triggers)
- M = Meetings and Expert Judgment (key tools used)
- O = Output is the Charter (the deliverable)
- PA = PMO (organizational context) + Assumptions Log (secondary output)

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Full formal charter document required before any planning begins
- Sponsor signature is non-negotiable for resource authorization
- High-level scope, schedule, cost, and risk must appear as charter sections
- In capital markets system implementations, the charter often references regulatory compliance objectives (e.g., T+1 settlement, BSEC reporting mandates)
- Change control on the charter itself may require a formal change request if scope or business case shifts

### Agile / Hybrid

- Lean or one-page charter acceptable; vision, goals, and PM authority must still be explicit
- Team working agreement or team canvas may supplement or replace portions of the traditional charter
- In hybrid environments, the charter authorizes the project; agile delivery methodology is captured as a tailoring note or appendix
- Even in Scrum/Kanban environments, the PM/Scrum Master needs organizational sanction to spend budget and engage stakeholders
- Iterative refinement is expected — the charter may evolve through early sprints but must reflect current state before major investment decisions

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Bangladesh Bank projects:** ICT Policy and risk management frameworks require formal project authorization documents linked to the Board IT Committee approval — the charter often needs parallel sign-off from Compliance
- **BSEC-regulated entities:** Listed company projects affecting financial reporting or investor communications require governance-level charter approval, not just departmental sign-off
- **Capital markets technology projects:** Charter must reference integration dependencies with DSE/CSE systems and data privacy obligations under applicable laws
- **Regulated banking projects:** Charter assumptions often include central bank approval timelines as constraints — omitting these creates unrealistic expectations for sponsors

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Start planning immediately after verbal approval from the sponsor" | Sponsors verbally approve projects all the time in practice; waiting for paperwork feels bureaucratic | PMI requires formal written authorization (the charter) before resource expenditure — verbal approval is not organizational sanction |
| "Create a detailed project scope statement as part of chartering" | Experienced PMs know scope definition is critical and want to nail it early | Scope statement is a planning output (Define Scope), not an initiating output — chartering captures high-level description only |
| "The PM should develop the charter independently to save sponsor time" | PMs often draft charters because sponsors are too busy — it seems helpful and practical | The initiating entity must authorize; self-authorization violates the principle of organizational sanction and removes accountability |
| "If the project is small, skip the charter and go straight to planning" | Many organizations run small projects without formal charters — it seems proportionate | PMI recognizes tailoring but still requires authorization at some level; skipping charter means no formal authority for the PM |
| "Stakeholder identification should wait until the stakeholder register is built in planning" | Planning feels like the right time to do thorough stakeholder analysis with full scope in hand | PMI explicitly places Identify Stakeholders in Initiating — key stakeholders must be identified alongside the charter |
| "Accept the charter as signed even though it contains an unachievable deadline" | Accepting is the path of least resistance; the PM can manage expectations during execution | Accepting an unachievable commitment violates Responsibility in the Code of Ethics and sets the project up for failure from day one |
| "Update the risk register when new risks emerge during chartering" | Risk register is the standard risk management tool; updating it early seems proactive | The risk register does not yet exist during chartering — high-level risks go into the charter; the register is created during planning |
| "The project charter and project management plan are the same document" | In practice, many organizations combine them into one large document | PMI treats them as distinct: charter = authorization document; PMP = detailed management approach developed after the charter |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager at a major private commercial bank in Dhaka has been handed a project charter by the CFO for a core banking system upgrade. The charter has been pre-approved by the Board IT Committee and references a go-live date that, in the PM's expert judgment, is 6 months shorter than any comparable implementation in the industry. The PM privately calculates a 70% probability of failure at that timeline. The CFO explicitly tells the PM: "This date is non-negotiable — the regulator has expectations." What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Accept the charter as signed, document the risk in the risk register during planning, and proceed with planning under the approved timeline.
B. Formally communicate the timeline concern to the CFO and request a joint review of the schedule assumption with objective data before signing.
C. Escalate directly to Bangladesh Bank to inform the regulator the timeline is unrealistic.
D. Sign the charter but immediately begin developing a recovery plan in case the schedule fails.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A fintech startup is using Scrum to build a new capital markets data analytics platform. The product owner has drafted a one-page team charter collaboratively with the team. During the first sprint review, the key business sponsor — who has not attended any ceremonies — challenges the scope of what was delivered, saying it does not match what they authorized. The Scrum Master raises this with the project manager. What is the BEST action?

A. Add the sponsor to the sprint review invitee list and review the team charter with them to realign understanding of scope and authority.
B. Escalate to the PMO to clarify that Scrum ceremonies replace formal charter authorization in agile environments.
C. Ask the product owner to revise the product backlog to match the sponsor's expectations without discussing the charter.
D. Suspend the sprint cycle until a traditional project charter is signed by the sponsor.

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project at a DSE-listed insurance company is in the Initiating phase. The project manager has received two different versions of the business case — one from the IT department and one from the Business Operations division — and both claim their version was approved. The PM has been asked to begin chartering this week. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Use the more conservative of the two business cases to draft the charter and seek one approver's signature.

C. Pause chartering and escalate to the sponsor or governance body to resolve the conflicting business cases before proceeding.
D. Merge elements from both business cases into a single charter draft and present to both departments simultaneously.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility and Honesty values. PMBOK 7 — Stewardship principle. A PM who accepts an unachievable commitment without raising it is violating Responsibility: "We make commitments only when we are able to keep them." Option A creates a paper trail but silently accepts the problem — PMI views this as abdication of responsibility. Option C bypasses all organizational channels and engages the regulator directly, which is inappropriate and potentially damaging. Option D defers the problem rather than surfacing it — developing a recovery plan is planning for failure, not preventing it. The correct action is to communicate the concern with data before the commitment is locked in.

**Mindset Lesson:** PMI expects the PM to protect organizational integrity by surfacing unachievable commitments before they are ratified, even under executive pressure.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — Sponsor engagement; PMI stakeholder engagement principles. In agile environments, the charter (or team canvas) still represents shared authorization and scope understanding. A sponsor who has not engaged with ceremonies is a stakeholder engagement failure. Option B mischaracterizes PMI guidance — ceremonies do not replace formal authorization. Option C bypasses the root issue (the sponsor's misalignment) and will lead to recurring scope disagreements. Option D is disproportionate — suspending the project to impose a traditional charter ignores the agile tailoring already in place.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, authorization documents may be lean, but sponsor engagement with the project charter (in whatever form) is non-negotiable.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Integration management principle; governance stewardship. The PM cannot begin chartering without a single, agreed-upon business case, as the charter is derived from the business case. Option A and B both commit to a document under dispute — the PM would be chartering without valid authorization. Option D creates a new merged document without the authority to do so, and may satisfy neither party. PMI requires conflict resolution at the governance level before proceeding.

**Mindset Lesson:** Starting a process on a contested foundation is not progress — it manufactures a legitimacy crisis later; escalate first and document the resolution.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: B and C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Integration; regulatory EEF incorporation at chartering. Both B and C address the omission at the correct process stage. Option B fixes the document; Option C ensures the authorized party (sponsor) is aware and approves the change. Option A defers a scope-defining regulatory constraint to planning — this creates a false baseline. Option D treats a known constraint as a risk and pushes it to a register that does not yet exist in Initiating; risks belong in the charter at high level, then the register during planning.

**Mindset Lesson:** Known regulatory requirements are constraints, not risks — document them in the charter, not just the risk register.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — team formation and chartering sequence; PMI Stakeholder Management. Stakeholders must be identified before chartering, because the charter workshop attendees and its contents depend on knowing who has interests. Creating a backlog before a team charter exists puts the cart before the horse. Sprint zero planning is the final initialization activity after artifacts and team agreements exist. Option A and B place the charter before stakeholder identification — this misses the PMI principle that stakeholder input shapes the charter. Option D begins sprint zero planning too early.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile initiation, stakeholder identification unlocks the charter, and the charter unlocks the backlog — sequence matters even without a Gantt chart.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 — Develop Project Charter ITTOs. The project management plan is an OUTPUT of the Planning Process Group, not an input to Initiating. It does not exist when the charter is being developed. Options A, B, and D are all legitimate inputs: the business case and agreements trigger the project; EEFs shape the organizational context. Candidates confuse the PMP (planning document) with OPAs (organizational templates/policies) — OPAs are inputs; the PMP is not.

**Mindset Lesson:** Nothing from planning can feed initiation — the charter must come before the plan, always.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty value: "We do not engage in or condone behavior that is designed to deceive others." PMBOK 7 — Stewardship principle. Option A is an abdication — the PM has independent ethical obligations beyond what the sponsor delegates. Option C is an overreach — anonymous reporting bypasses direct communication and is disproportionate as a first step. Option D normalizes the deception by proceeding as if the Board is adequately informed. The PM's first obligation is to surface the issue through legitimate channels.

**Mindset Lesson:** The PM's honesty obligation does not stop at what the sponsor tells them to disclose — material omissions affecting Board decisions must be escalated.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Tailoring; Agile Practice Guide — hybrid environment guidance. Using both a formal charter and a lean team canvas serves different audiences with appropriate communication tools. Option A misapplies agile by eliminating governance artifacts required by the organization. Option C treats all stakeholders as needing the same communication — PMI requires communication to be tailored to audience. Option D introduces unnecessary delay; the team canvas can be drafted and used in parallel with charter approval, as long as it is clear it is not the authorizing document.

**Mindset Lesson:** In hybrid environments, governance artifacts and agile artifacts serve different audiences and must coexist — one does not replace the other.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stewardship; charter authority. The charter creates a formal record of pre-assigned resources. When a functional manager withdraws them in violation of the charter, this is a governance conflict that must be escalated to the sponsor — who has the authority to enforce the charter's commitments. Option A accepts an unauthorized scope/resource change without authority. Option C accepts the constraint without using available authority — the sponsor has not been engaged. Option D bypasses the sponsor entirely and violates organizational hierarchy — PMI does not support going around the sponsor without attempting sponsor resolution first.

**Mindset Lesson:** The charter is the PM's evidence in a resource conflict — present it to the sponsor as the basis for escalation, not as a last resort.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: B and D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Integration and Stakeholder Management. The two actions that belong immediately post-charter are (D) reviewing the charter to internalize authority and scope boundaries, and (B) beginning the Identify Stakeholders process — which is also an Initiating process that runs alongside or immediately after chartering. Option A (develop PMP) is a Planning process that requires a complete charter first but not necessarily the immediate next step. Option C (WBS) and Option E (assign team to work packages) are Planning and Executing activities respectively — far too early at this point.

**Mindset Lesson:** Post-charter, the PM's first job is to understand what they've been authorized to do (review charter) and who has a stake in it (identify stakeholders) — everything else is planning.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- The charter is the PM's authorization document — without it, the PM has no formal authority and no right to deploy resources.
- The charter is created by the initiating entity (sponsor/executive), not by the PM — the PM may draft but must not self-authorize.
- Business case, SOW, and agreements are inputs to chartering; the charter and assumptions log are outputs.
- High-level scope, milestones, assumptions, constraints, known risks, and key stakeholders belong in the charter — not detailed plans.
- Identify Stakeholders runs alongside or immediately after chartering — both are Initiating processes.
- Verbal or informal approvals do not constitute authorization — the signed charter is the organizational sanction.
- If the business case changes materially, revisit the charter with the sponsor before committing further resources.
- Regulatory constraints (Bangladesh Bank circulars, BSEC requirements) are EEFs that must appear in the charter — not deferred to planning.
- Ethics rule: raising concerns about an unachievable charter before signing is Responsibility; staying silent is a Code of Ethics violation.
- Escalation sequence for charter conflicts: PM → Sponsor → Governance body — never bypass the sponsor without first engaging them.
- **If you have no charter, you have no project — stop work, seek authorization, and document everything.**
