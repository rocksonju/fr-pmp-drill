# PMP Exam Master Brief: Identify Risks

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Identify Risks is the iterative process of identifying and documenting individual project risks and overall project risk exposure. It runs throughout the project life cycle — not once during planning — and produces the risk register as its primary output, which feeds every subsequent risk process. The process draws from virtually every other knowledge area: scope ambiguity creates risks, schedule compression creates risks, unclear stakeholder requirements create risks. On a predictive project, a structured risk identification workshop anchors the process; on agile, risks emerge from retrospectives, daily standups, and backlog refinement. Hybrid environments layer formal risk registers over sprint-level risk boards. In regulated contexts (banking, BSEC-compliant capital market projects, Bangladesh Bank IT programs), risk identification must also address compliance, regulatory, and auditability risks explicitly. Exam weight for Risk Management overall is roughly 8–11% of the PMP ECO, spread across all domains, with Identify Risks crossing Domain I (People), Domain II (Process), and Domain III (Business Environment). **In one sentence:** Identify Risks produces a living catalog of what could go wrong (or right) so the team can analyze, respond to, and monitor those uncertainties throughout the project.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal risk identification workshop using checklists, prompt lists, and expert judgment early in planning; outputs a structured risk register with unique IDs, descriptions, categories, and root causes |
| Agile | Risks surface continuously — in sprint planning, retrospectives, and daily standups; maintained on a risk-adjusted backlog or risk board; "risk-based spike" used to investigate unknowns before committing to a story |
| Hybrid | Formal risk register from predictive layer combined with visual kanban-style risk board for sprint-level risks; identification events at phase gates plus continuous identification within iterations |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Identify Risks is continuous, not a one-time planning event.** Real-world instinct: "We did the risk workshop in kickoff — we're done." PMI: every change, every new stakeholder, every iteration is a new identification trigger.
2. **Positive risks (opportunities) must be identified alongside threats.** Real-world instinct: focus on what can go wrong. PMI: upside uncertainty is equally a risk; failing to log opportunities is incomplete risk management.
3. **Everyone on the project team participates — not just the PM.** Real-world instinct: the PM or risk officer owns risk identification. PMI: broad participation (team, stakeholders, SMEs, sponsors) produces better coverage and buy-in.
4. **Risk causes and risk effects must be separated.** Real-world instinct: log the symptom ("system might be slow"). PMI: log the root cause and the effect separately to enable proper response planning.
5. **Secondary and residual risks from responses must be identified.** Real-world instinct: once a response is planned, the risk is handled. PMI: every response can create new risks; those new risks must re-enter the identification process.

### Ethics Angle

- **Honesty:** Document risks even when disclosing them is uncomfortable for sponsors or senior management; concealing known risks violates the PMI Code.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is obligated to surface risks that could harm the project, organization, or public — this is non-negotiable regardless of organizational pressure.
- **Fairness:** Risk identification must be inclusive; excluding minority stakeholder groups from identification workshops skews risk coverage and is inequitable.
- **Respect:** Risk brainstorming sessions must be psychologically safe — PMI expects the PM to create an environment where team members can raise risks without fear of blame.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Individual Project Risk | A specific uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives | Do not confuse with overall project risk, which is the combined effect of all uncertainties on the project as a whole |
| Overall Project Risk | The effect of uncertainty on the project as a whole, representing exposure from all individual risks plus general ambiguity | Exam often tests whether you treat individual risk vs overall risk differently — they require different responses |
| Risk Register | Document capturing identified risks, their descriptions, categories, root causes, triggers, potential responses (preliminary), and risk owners | The register is first created here, not in Plan Risk Responses — responses entered here are preliminary only |
| Risk Report | Summarizes overall risk exposure at portfolio/program/project level; first created in Identify Risks and updated in subsequent processes | Distinguish from risk register: register = individual risks; report = overall exposure + summary |
| Risk Appetite | The degree of uncertainty an organization is willing to accept in pursuit of its objectives | Not the same as risk threshold (the specific measurable level at which response is triggered) |
| Risk Threshold | The level of risk exposure above which risk response action is required | Thresholds are set by stakeholders and inform risk ratings; confusing with appetite causes wrong response planning |
| Risk Trigger (Early Warning) | Indicator that a risk is about to occur or has occurred | Triggers must be documented in the risk register; exam tests whether you act on triggers or wait for the risk to materialize |
| Prompt List | Checklist of risk categories used to stimulate identification (PESTLE, TECOP, VUCA, etc.) | Not a risk checklist of prior project risks — prompt lists are category frameworks, not specific risk lists |
| Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) | Hierarchical representation of risk categories relevant to the project | Used to organize the risk register and ensure systematic coverage; confused with WBS on exams |
| Assumption and Constraint Analysis | Technique for reviewing project assumptions and constraints as sources of risk | Assumptions that are false become risks; exam tests whether you log the assumption failure as a risk, not just flag the assumption |
| SWOT Analysis | Structured analysis of Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats to surface project risks | Used in Identify Risks to ensure both internal and external risk sources are covered |
| Delphi Technique | Anonymous expert consensus technique used to reduce bias in risk identification and estimation | "Anonymous" is the key word — if experts are identified, it is not Delphi; used when group dynamics would suppress honest input |
| Risk Owner | Person responsible for monitoring an identified risk and implementing the agreed response | Risk owner is assigned during identification (preliminary) and confirmed during response planning |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A team member mentions a potential issue during standup" | Log it in the risk register immediately | Risks identified anywhere, anytime must be captured; delaying logging risks losing context and violating continuous identification |
| "A new stakeholder joins the project mid-execution" | Conduct a targeted risk identification session with the new stakeholder | New stakeholders bring new perspectives and may reveal risks previously uncovered; re-run identification, not just onboarding |
| "The project scope is changed via approved change request" | Update the risk register to reflect new/changed risks from the scope change | Every approved change is a new identification trigger; failing to re-identify risks after changes is a top exam trap |
| "Sponsor says 'don't document that risk — it might alarm the steering committee'" | Document the risk regardless and report it appropriately | PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility): concealing known risks is a violation regardless of sponsor pressure |
| "Team identifies a risk that a competitor might deliver a similar product first" | Log it as an opportunity risk and assign a preliminary owner | Opportunities are risks too; the team should capture positive risks for exploitation or enhancement responses |
| "Risk workshop produces 200 risks from brainstorming" | Consolidate duplicates and assign to RBS categories before analysis | Raw brainstorm output must be structured; do not proceed to qualitative analysis with uncategorized, redundant lists |
| "PM is asked to fast-track risk management because of schedule pressure" | Maintain the risk identification process — compress response planning instead if time is critical | Skipping identification to save time is never PMI-correct; identification informs all downstream decisions |
| "A risk response is approved that involves a third-party vendor" | Identify secondary and residual risks created by the vendor response | Every response creates new risks; the vendor risk (secondary) must re-enter the register immediately |
| "Team is working in sprints and raises a new technical risk mid-sprint" | Add to the risk-adjusted backlog and assess during next sprint planning | In agile, new risks feed backlog refinement and sprint planning — not a separate formal risk process |
| "Bangladesh Bank compliance officer raises a regulatory change risk" | Document as high-priority risk with compliance category tag and escalate per governance plan | Regulatory risks in banking contexts (BB, BSEC) carry organizational and legal exposure; must be categorized, logged, and escalated immediately |

**Topic-specific escalation edge cases:**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "PM discovers a risk that, if it materializes, would breach BSEC disclosure rules" | Log the risk, assess severity, and escalate to sponsor and legal counsel before the next steering committee | Regulatory disclosure risks are not just project risks — they are organizational compliance risks requiring executive and legal awareness, not just PM-level handling |
| "A team member refuses to document a risk because 'management won't like it'" | Address the team member's concern, clarify the ethical obligation, and log the risk regardless | Psychological safety and honesty are both PMI values; the PM must model ethical behavior even under social pressure within the team |

**Mnemonic: "TICORP" — Triggers, Identify (both), Categorize, Owner, Register, Persist (iterative)**

- **T**riggers — know what starts the identification cycle
- **I**dentify both — threats AND opportunities
- **C**ategorize — use RBS, not raw lists
- **O**wner — assign preliminary owner at identification
- **R**egister — output is risk register + risk report
- **P**ersist — process repeats throughout the project

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Formal risk identification workshops scheduled at project start, phase gates, and after major changes
- RBS template maintained from organizational process assets; risk register in structured format with unique IDs
- Expert judgment panels drawn from functional managers, SMEs, and historical project teams
- Assumption and constraint logs formally reviewed as risk identification inputs

### Agile / Hybrid
- Risk identification embedded in ceremonies: sprint planning (new risks?), retrospectives (what surprised us?), daily standups (any new blockers?)
- Risk-adjusted backlog used instead of or alongside formal risk register
- "Risk spike" stories allocated for high-uncertainty items before commitment
- Risk boards (physical or digital) provide real-time visibility to the team
- Hybrid: maintain formal register at project/program level; use sprint-level boards for iteration risks

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets:** Identify Risks must include credit risk, liquidity risk, market risk, and operational risk categories relevant to the project's deliverables — not just project execution risks
- **BSEC-regulated projects:** Regulatory disclosure timelines are themselves risk triggers — a delay in identifying a compliance risk can cascade into legal exposure; PM must treat compliance calendar as a risk input
- **Bangladesh Bank IT programs:** Core banking system projects must include cybersecurity, data sovereignty, and system availability risks as mandatory identification categories; Bangladesh Bank circulars on IT governance define minimum risk categories
- Risk register in regulated industries must be audit-ready: documented owners, dated entries, version-controlled
- Organizational risk appetite statements from board/risk committee must be inputs to the identification process in banking contexts

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Wait until the risk materializes before adding it to the register" | Real PMs often manage by exception and deal with actual problems rather than hypotheticals | Identification requires proactive documentation; waiting for occurrence eliminates the ability to plan responses in advance |
| "Only the PM and risk officer should identify risks to maintain consistency" | Experienced PMs often control risk documentation to avoid noise from uninformed team members | PMI requires broad participation — the team, stakeholders, and SMEs each hold unique knowledge that the PM alone cannot replicate |
| "Risks identified during execution belong in the issue log, not the risk register" | Issues are a separate artifact; many PMs segregate current problems from future uncertainties | If uncertainty remains (the risk hasn't fully materialized), it stays in the risk register; only fully materialized issues move to the issue log |
| "Positive risks (opportunities) are outside scope of Identify Risks" | Most PM experience is threat-focused; opportunities feel like upside that doesn't need management | PMI explicitly includes opportunities in the definition of risk; failing to identify opportunities means missed exploitation chances |
| "Update the risk register only at the start of each phase" | Phase-gate updates are standard practice in waterfall environments | PMI requires continuous identification — any change, new stakeholder, or response triggers re-identification, not just phase gates |
| "Once a risk response is assigned, remove the original risk from the register" | Closed/responded risks seem like resolved items that clutter the register | Risks stay in the register until the risk window closes; responses are logged against the risk, not substituted for it; secondary/residual risks are added |
| "Use the risk register from the previous similar project as the output" | Experienced PMs leverage historical risk registers to save time | Historical registers are inputs (OPA), not outputs; Identify Risks must produce a new, project-specific register, not copy-paste a prior one |
| "Skip Delphi technique because it takes too long" | Time-pressured PMs favor faster workshops over structured anonymous rounds | When expert group dynamics would suppress honest input (e.g., junior staff around senior executives), Delphi is the correct tool; skipping it introduces bias |

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |

| "…first step in identifying risks…" | Gather information using checklists, prompt lists, interviews — do NOT jump to analysis |
| "…risk just identified during sprint retrospective…" | Log in risk register (or risk-adjusted backlog) and assess during next planning event |
| "…team member raises a concern in standup…" | Capture it as a risk immediately — concerns = potential risks, not issues |
| "…approved change request has been implemented…" | Trigger a new risk identification cycle — every approved change is a new risk event |
| "…new stakeholder added to the project…" | Conduct targeted risk identification with the new stakeholder before proceeding |
| "…sponsor asks PM not to document a risk…" | Document the risk regardless — PMI Code: Honesty + Responsibility override sponsor preference |
| "…secondary risk from approved response…" | Log immediately in risk register; secondary risks are treated exactly like original risks |
| "…project is in execution and a new risk is found…" | Update the risk register — Identify Risks is iterative, not planning-only |
| "…SWOT analysis was performed…" | SWOT is a tool in Identify Risks — use its outputs to populate the risk register |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM is leading a core banking system upgrade for a Bangladesh Bank-regulated institution. During a risk identification workshop, a senior developer identifies a risk that the new system may expose customer data if the legacy data migration script is reused. The developer adds that the CTO has already approved the migration script reuse and considers it non-negotiable. The sponsor is present and signals that raising this risk formally would delay the project approval. The PM has independently assessed that the probability of exposure is moderate and the impact on customer data privacy is high. What should the PM do?

A. Log the data exposure risk in the risk register with its probability, impact, and owner, and include it in the next risk report to the sponsor
B. Omit the risk from the register to respect the CTO's decision and avoid project delay, and document it privately for personal reference
C. Escalate only to the project sponsor verbally without logging the risk to maintain the sponsor relationship
D. Log the risk but mark it as "accepted" without analysis to avoid conflict with the CTO

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A product development team in a capital markets firm is working in two-week sprints. During sprint retrospective, three team members independently mention that the dependency on an external regulatory API is uncertain — the API provider has not confirmed version compatibility for the next release. The scrum master notes this in the retrospective action items. The PM (serving as a hybrid coordinator) is present. What is the BEST next action?

A. Treat the API dependency as an impediment and ask the scrum master to remove it from the sprint backlog
B. Log the API compatibility uncertainty as an identified risk in the risk register and add a risk spike story to the next sprint's backlog for investigation
C. Wait until the API provider confirms compatibility before logging anything formal
D. Escalate immediately to the program manager and request a sprint pause

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team is six months into a 12-month regulatory reporting system implementation. The PM has just received an approved change request adding three new regulatory report types. A business analyst raises concerns that the new scope likely introduces new data validation risks. A key stakeholder wants to move directly to sprint planning for the new reports. The PMO requires that risk identification be completed before sprint planning for any scope change. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Begin sprint planning immediately to satisfy the stakeholder and document risks as they emerge during development
B. Conduct a focused risk identification session on the new scope before sprint planning, per PMO requirements
C. Escalate to the PMO to request a waiver of the risk identification requirement given schedule pressure
D. Ask the business analyst to document the risks independently and submit them to the PM after sprint planning

**Q4 — Two-Correct, Pick-Best (Predictive)**

During a risk identification workshop for a greenfield BSEC-regulated equity trading platform, the facilitator receives 180 risk entries from brainstorming. The sponsor asks the PM to proceed immediately to qualitative risk analysis to save time. Which TWO actions should the PM complete before moving to qualitative analysis? Select the BEST answer pair.

A. Categorize risks using the RBS and remove duplicates; assign preliminary owners to each unique risk
B. Immediately score all 180 risks on a 5×5 probability-impact matrix
C. Archive low-priority risks without review to reduce the analysis burden

1. Review the risk-adjusted backlog for carry-over risks from the previous sprint
2. Identify new risks arising from sprint backlog items selected for this sprint

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A PM is wrapping up risk identification for a large infrastructure project. A major risk — that the chosen contractor has a history of regulatory violations with Bangladesh Bank — was surfaced by an anonymous source. The sponsor instructs the PM not to log the risk because it is "unverified" and might embarrass the contractor relationship. The PM has been unable to independently verify the claim within the available timeframe. What is the most ethical and PMI-correct action?

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Risk domain) and PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility) require the PM to document all known risks regardless of sponsor or executive preference. The risk register is the authoritative output of Identify Risks. A customer data exposure risk with moderate probability and high impact must be logged, analyzed, and communicated through the risk report. The sponsor's discomfort does not override the PM's ethical obligation or the process requirements.

- B fails: Private documentation without formal logging is a concealment strategy. PMI Code value: Honesty prohibits this.
- C fails: Verbal escalation without logging is not risk management — it leaves no audit trail and the risk can be dismissed without record.
- D fails: Marking a risk "accepted" without analysis skips Perform Qualitative and Quantitative Risk Analysis; acceptance is a response strategy, not a substitute for analysis.

**Mindset Lesson:** PMI expects the PM to document discomfort-inducing risks more rigorously, not less — organizational pressure to suppress risks is itself a warning sign.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide (Chapter 4, Risk Management in Agile) supports continuous risk identification through ceremonies. A risk spike story is the agile mechanism for investigating high-uncertainty items before committing to delivery. Logging the API uncertainty in the risk register (or risk-adjusted backlog) and creating a spike satisfies both the identification obligation and the agile principle of addressing uncertainty before it becomes an issue.

- A fails: The API dependency is not yet an impediment (it hasn't blocked work); it is an unresolved uncertainty — a risk, not an issue.
- C fails: Waiting for confirmation is reactive, not proactive; the risk must be logged now to enable analysis and response planning.
- D fails: Pausing a sprint for an unconfirmed risk is disproportionate; spikes handle investigation within the sprint cadence.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, risk identification happens in ceremonies — the retrospective mention is not informal chatter, it is a risk identification event that demands action.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Uncertainty Performance Domain) and the risk management plan (PMO requirement) both establish that scope changes trigger risk identification before subsequent planning. The FIRST action on any scope change is to identify new risks, then plan. The PMO requirement reinforces this as a governance obligation, not a suggestion.

- A fails: Moving to sprint planning before identification violates both PMI sequencing and the explicit PMO governance rule — "document as they emerge" is reactive risk management, not proactive.
- C fails: Requesting a PMO waiver of a governance requirement for schedule pressure inverts the priority hierarchy; PMI expects the PM to follow governance and negotiate schedule, not waive risk management.
- D fails: Delegating risk identification to the BA and deferring it post-sprint-planning means risks are identified after commitments are made, making responses costlier or impossible.

**Mindset Lesson:** Governance and risk identification requirements are not suggestions that can be traded away for schedule convenience — the PM champions the process even under stakeholder pressure.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: A and D**

**PMI Justification:** Before qualitative analysis, the risk register must contain clean, structured, owner-assigned risks. RBS categorization and duplicate removal (A) organize the raw brainstorm into analyzable entries. Validating root cause-risk-effect structure and confirming triggers (D) ensures each risk is correctly defined for probability-impact assessment. Together, these two actions fulfill the register preparation required before analysis.

- B fails: Scoring on a P-I matrix is qualitative analysis itself, not pre-analysis preparation; you cannot analyze before the register is structured.
- C fails: Archiving risks without review introduces bias and may eliminate risks that would score high if properly assessed — never acceptable in PMI methodology.

**Mindset Lesson:** Qualitative analysis is only as good as the register fed into it; garbage in = garbage analysis, and exam questions test whether you prepare before you analyze.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide supports systematic risk management within sprints. Reviewing carry-over risks first (step 1) establishes continuity with prior identification. New risks from sprint backlog items are identified next (step 2). Owners and severity are assigned before logging (step 4 before step 3) in some interpretations, but the sequence 1→2→4→3→5 follows the logic of identify → rate → register → act. B (1→2→4→3→5) correctly starts with carry-overs, then new identification, then rating, then logging, then spikes.

- A fails: Starting with new identification before reviewing carry-overs misses ongoing risks.
- C fails: Logging before assigning owners/severity creates an incomplete register entry.
- D fails: Starting with new identification and logging before reviewing carry-overs breaks continuity.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile risk management, carry-over risks from prior sprints are reviewed before identifying new ones — continuous identification means not losing what was already found.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Monte Carlo simulation is a tool of Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis, not Identify Risks. It models the probability distribution of outcomes based on already-identified risks. Identify Risks uses brainstorming, expert interviews, document review, assumption analysis, SWOT, prompt lists, and checklists — all of which surface new risks. Simulation quantifies; it does not identify.

- A, B, D are all valid Identify Risks tools and techniques per PMBOK 6/7 process definitions.

**Mindset Lesson:** Tools belong to specific processes — Monte Carlo is quantitative analysis, not identification; confusing them is a top exam trap in risk questions.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility) requires the PM to report risks known to them, even when unverified, with appropriate confidence notation. Logging the risk with a "low confidence / unverified" flag and assigning an owner to verify is the correct balance: it satisfies the obligation to document without overstating certainty. The risk of a contractor's regulatory violation history is exactly the kind of risk that must be investigated, not suppressed.

- B fails: Omitting the risk because it is unverified places organizational relationship concerns above the PM's ethical obligation to disclose known project risks.
- C fails: Private monitoring without informing the sponsor or steering committee is concealment — the PM cannot unilaterally suppress a risk from governance bodies.
- D fails: Delegating the disclosure decision to the sponsor converts the PM's ethical obligation into a sponsor's judgment call — PMI does not allow the PM to abdicate responsibility for risk documentation.

**Mindset Lesson:** "Unverified" is a notation in the risk register, not a reason to suppress a risk; the PM's obligation to document known uncertainties survives the absence of proof.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Stakeholder Performance Domain) and Communications Management principles require tailored communication by audience. The risk report (summary level) serves strategic stakeholders; subset views serve functional stakeholders; full register serves the execution team. Tailoring prevents information overload, maintains engagement, and ensures each audience receives actionable risk information at the right level of detail.

- A fails: Sending 95 risks to all stakeholders guarantees that strategic stakeholders disengage — information overload is a communication failure, not thoroughness.
- C fails: Passive availability (SharePoint posting) is not proactive communication; PMI expects the PM to actively tailor and distribute, not post-and-pray.
- D fails: Presenting all 95 risks in a steering committee is a steering committee-level failure — steering committees work from executive summaries, not raw registers.

**Mindset Lesson:** Effective risk communication is tailored to audience decision needs, not maximized for completeness — the PM curates, not dumps.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Uncertainty Performance Domain) and risk management plan principles require that risks meeting escalation thresholds be escalated regardless of manager preference. A compliance-impacting risk at a BSEC-regulated institution has high organizational exposure. The correct action is to: log at appropriate severity, notify the vendor per contractual obligations, inform internal compliance stakeholders, and escalate per the risk management plan if thresholds are exceeded. "Wait and see" from the manager is not aligned with PMI's proactive risk management standard.

- A fails: Following the manager's direction when it violates the risk management plan and regulatory obligations is not PMI-correct; the PM must follow the plan, not individual manager preferences.
- C fails: Escalating directly to BSEC before internal notification bypasses the organizational governance chain — the PM escalates internally first, then externally per legal/compliance guidance.
- D fails: Waiting for sprint review to assess materialization is reactive management of a compliance risk — the risk must be logged and acted upon immediately, not deferred to a ceremony.

**Mindset Lesson:** Manager "wait and see" on a compliance-impacting risk is not a valid instruction — the PM follows the risk management plan's escalation thresholds, not informal direction.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answer: A, D, F**

**PMI Justification:** Inputs to Identify Risks (PMBOK 6, Section 11.2.1 and PMBOK 7 context) include: Risk Management Plan (A — defines methodology, categories, roles), Assumption Log (D — assumptions are sources of risk), and Lessons Learned Register (F — prior project risk experience informs current identification).

- B: Risk register from a prior project is an organizational process asset (input category), but is not listed as a distinct named input — the lessons learned register captures this function.
- C: Project schedule is an input to Identify Risks (schedule constraints create risks), making it a partial distractor — however, in a select-3 format where A, D, F are the most cleanly defensible named inputs, C is a second-tier input.
- E: Risk response strategies are outputs of Plan Risk Responses — they are not inputs to Identify Risks.

**Mindset Lesson:** Know which artifacts are created in Identify Risks (risk register, risk report) versus which are consumed as inputs (risk management plan, assumption log, lessons learned) — the exam tests this direction of information flow.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Identify Risks is continuous — every change, new stakeholder, phase gate, and sprint is a new trigger
- Both threats AND opportunities are identified — positive risks are not optional
- Risk register and risk report are the two primary outputs created in this process
- Historical risk registers and lessons learned are inputs (OPA), not the output — do not copy-paste prior registers as deliverables
- Broad participation is required — PM, team, stakeholders, SMEs; no single-owner identification
- SWOT, Delphi, prompt lists, brainstorming, and assumption analysis are all valid tools — Monte Carlo is NOT (it belongs in quantitative analysis)
- Every approved change triggers re-identification — this is the #1 exam trap in this process
- Risks stay in the register until the risk window closes — "accepted" is a response, not a removal
- Secondary and residual risks from responses must be identified and logged immediately
- In banking/BSEC/Bangladesh Bank contexts, regulatory and compliance risks are mandatory identification categories and carry escalation obligations
- Ethics rule: the PM documents known risks even when sponsors or executives prefer suppression — Honesty and Responsibility are non-negotiable

**Burn-in reflex: When anything changes on the project — scope, team, environment, or response — run Identify Risks again before the next planning step.**
