# PMP Exam Master Brief: Plan Scope Management

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Plan Scope Management is the first of five Scope Management processes and the one that defines how scope will be defined, validated, and controlled throughout the project. Its two outputs — the scope management plan and the requirements management plan — tell the project exactly how requirements will be collected and refined, how the project scope statement will be developed, how the WBS will be created, how scope will be formally accepted, and how scope changes will be processed. This process is often tested indirectly: exam questions about scope creep, uncontrolled requirements growth, or stakeholder disagreements about deliverable acceptance almost always trace back to a missing or weak scope management plan. Exam weight for Scope Management as a domain is approximately 10–14% of the exam, and this planning process underpins every other scope question. Cross-domain bleed is significant: communications (how requirements are communicated), stakeholder engagement (who participates in requirements collection), and integration (how scope changes connect to change control) all intersect here. In Bangladesh Bank-regulated technology projects, scope is particularly high-stakes because regulatory mandates can introduce scope after planning is complete, requiring a disciplined change process rooted in what this plan establishes. **In one sentence:** Plan Scope Management produces the documented rules for how scope will be defined, collected, verified, and controlled for this specific project.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | A formal scope management plan and requirements management plan are produced; scope is defined fully upfront; changes go through formal change control; acceptance criteria are documented before work starts |
| Agile | Scope is emergent; the product backlog replaces the scope statement; requirements are elaborated iteratively sprint by sprint; the product owner continuously prioritizes and the team adapts; "acceptance" happens at every sprint review |
| Hybrid | Core governance boundaries (regulatory scope, contractual scope, budget-protected scope) are fixed and managed predictively; delivery scope within those boundaries is managed as an evolving backlog |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Planning how scope will be managed is more important than defining scope immediately.** Real-world instinct: experienced PMs jump to writing the scope statement or building the WBS. PMI: you must first agree on the process for doing these things — the scope management plan — before doing them. Skipping the plan-for-scope step creates ad hoc scope decisions later.
2. **The requirements management plan governs how requirements will be traced, not just collected.** Real-world instinct: collecting requirements is a stakeholder meeting exercise. PMI: requirements management includes traceability (linking requirements to deliverables, test cases, and business objectives) — without a traceability approach, you cannot prove scope completeness or manage scope change impact.
3. **Scope management plans must be tailored to the project lifecycle.** Real-world instinct: the organization has a standard scope process, so just use it. PMI: the scope approach in an agile project is fundamentally different from a predictive one; documenting this tailoring decision is not optional.
4. **Acceptance criteria must be defined in planning, not at delivery.** Real-world instinct: we will know it is right when we see it. PMI: undefined acceptance criteria at planning time is a scope risk; disagreements about deliverable quality at acceptance time are failures of planning, not failures of execution.
5. **Requirements management is a discipline, not a conversation.** Real-world instinct: requirements are gathered in a kickoff meeting and that is enough. PMI: requirements need prioritization, traceability, version control, and a defined process for how changes to requirements will be managed throughout the project — none of this is implicit.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** The PM is responsible for ensuring the scope management plan defines a realistic, achievable scope definition process — not one designed to make the project look better than it is to gain approval.
- **Fairness:** All relevant stakeholders should be identified in the requirements management plan as participants in requirements elicitation — excluding groups to expedite planning violates fairness.
- **Honesty:** If the scope management approach cannot realistically support the project's regulatory requirements (e.g., Bangladesh Bank ICT audit scope), the PM must surface this during Plan Scope Management, not hide it.
- **Respect:** The requirements management plan should acknowledge the legitimate authority of different stakeholders to define requirements in their domain; overriding subject matter experts is a respect violation.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Scope Management Plan | Component of the project management plan describing how scope will be defined, developed, monitored, controlled, and validated | Not the same as the scope statement; the scope management plan says HOW scope will be done, not WHAT the scope is |
| Requirements Management Plan | Component of the project management plan describing how requirements will be elicited, analyzed, prioritized, documented, validated, and traced | Do not confuse with the scope management plan; the requirements plan focuses on the requirements lifecycle, not the WBS or deliverable acceptance |
| Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) | A grid linking each requirement to its origin, associated deliverables, test cases, and current status | The RTM is a project document, not part of the project management plan; it is an output of Collect Requirements, not this process |
| Product Scope | The features and functions that characterize a product, service, or result | Measured against product requirements; distinct from project scope (the work required to deliver the product) |
| Project Scope | The work that must be performed to deliver a product, service, or result with the specified features and functions | Measured against the project management plan; scope creep violates the project scope baseline |
| Scope Baseline | Approved project scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary; the reference for scope performance measurement | The scope baseline is set in Define Scope and Create WBS — not in Plan Scope Management; this process only plans HOW to get there |
| Acceptance Criteria | Conditions that must be met before deliverables are formally accepted by the sponsor or customer | Must be defined in planning; acceptance criteria that emerge at delivery create disputes and scope conflicts |
| Progressive Elaboration | Iteratively developing scope detail as knowledge increases; appropriate and expected, especially early in a project | PMI does not expect complete scope definition on day one; progressive elaboration is planned, not reactive |
| Expert Judgment | Input to this process; PM draws on subject matter experts, PMO, and other PMs who have managed similar scopes | Expert judgment is an input to planning processes, not a substitute for documented scope process |
| Meetings | Key technique for Plan Scope Management; collaborative sessions with sponsor, team, and PMO to agree on scope approach | Virtual and cross-functional meetings across time zones require extra facilitation planning; do not assume in-person default |
| Data Analysis — Alternatives Analysis | Used to evaluate different scope definition and requirements management approaches before committing to one | Candidates overlook that this process involves deliberate choices about approach, not just filling a template |
| Lessons Learned Register | OPA input to this process; prior scope management approaches — what worked and what failed — should inform this plan | The lessons learned register is an input here, not an output; do not document lessons learned as part of scope planning |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Project is starting and scope has not been defined yet" | Develop the scope management plan before attempting to write the scope statement or build the WBS | The process for defining scope must be established before scope is defined; working in the wrong order produces an ungoverned scope definition |
| "Stakeholders are arguing about whose requirements take priority" | Reference (or develop) the requirements management plan to establish how requirements will be prioritized | The requirements management plan defines the prioritization process; ad hoc priority decisions create downstream conflict |
| "Team member asks how accepted deliverables will be handled" | Refer to the scope management plan which documents the validation and acceptance process | The scope management plan covers validation (Validate Scope process); team members should be directed to the documented process |
| "New regulatory requirement has emerged that may expand scope" | Raise a change request and assess impact against the scope baseline using the change management and scope management processes | The scope management plan defines how scope changes are processed; the process does not change because the trigger is regulatory |
| "Sponsor says acceptance criteria will be defined when the deliverable is ready" | Advise the sponsor that acceptance criteria must be defined in planning and document the risk if sponsor insists otherwise | Undefined acceptance criteria at planning is a scope risk; the PM's responsibility is to advise, document, and proceed through proper process |
| "Team is using agile and wants to skip the scope management plan" | Establish a scope governance approach appropriate for agile (e.g., product owner authority, backlog management rules) and document it | Even agile projects need agreement on how scope decisions are made; the form changes but the need for a documented approach does not |
| "Requirements keep changing after they were documented" | Assess whether a requirements management plan exists and whether it defines a requirements change process | Uncontrolled requirements change is a symptom of a missing or weak requirements management plan |
| "A project document shows requirements but no traceability to deliverables" | Establish requirements traceability as part of the requirements management plan and implement a traceability matrix | Requirements without traceability cannot be verified at completion; this is a scope integrity gap |

**Escalation edge cases:**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Business unit head is bypassing the requirements management plan and sending requirements directly to developers" | Meet with the business unit head to explain the requirements process and the risks of bypassing it; document the conversation | Escalation to sponsor is appropriate only after direct conversation fails; bypassing the process undermines scope control |
| "PMO requires a scope management approach that conflicts with the project's agile delivery method" | Document the conflict, propose a tailored approach that satisfies governance intent, and escalate to the PMO for resolution before planning proceeds | Unresolved process conflicts between PMO and project methodology create scope governance gaps; resolve before, not during, execution |

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Produce a formal scope management plan document covering: scope statement development process, WBS creation approach, deliverable acceptance process, scope change process, and WBS dictionary requirements
- In high-complexity projects (core banking system replacement), define multi-tier acceptance criteria covering functional, performance, security, and regulatory compliance dimensions
- Requirements management plan must address configuration management of requirements documents — critical in audit-heavy regulated environments
- For Bangladesh Bank-mandated projects, include a regulatory requirement tracking section in the requirements management plan

### Agile / Hybrid

- Replace scope management plan formality with product owner authority documentation, backlog governance rules, and sprint acceptance criteria
- Requirements management plan equivalent: define how stories will be written (INVEST criteria), how the backlog will be prioritized, and how requirements changes will enter the backlog
- In hybrid projects: separate planning artifacts for fixed-scope regulatory/contractual work versus iterative delivery scope
- Document explicitly which scope is frozen (contractual, regulatory) and which is emergent (feature delivery) — this is critical for BSEC-regulated capital markets projects

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Bangladesh Bank:** ICT Security Policy and Payment System regulations frequently introduce mandatory scope after planning; the requirements management plan must include a process for incorporating regulatory mandates as forced scope
- **BSEC:** Listed company project disclosures may constrain what can be labeled "in scope" without board approval; scope management plan must reference disclosure obligations
- **Financial sector audit requirements:** Every requirement should be traceable to a business need or regulatory obligation; an untraceable requirement is an audit finding in Bangladesh Bank-supervised institutions
- **Procurement scope:** In government-adjacent projects using CPTU frameworks, scope changes that trigger procurement re-solicitation have legal consequences; the scope management plan must reference procurement thresholds for scope changes

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Proceed directly to Collect Requirements since the scope management plan is an internal document that adds no value" | Experienced PMs often jump to requirements workshops; the scope management plan seems like bureaucratic overhead | The scope management plan defines how requirements will be collected and how scope decisions will be made; without it, there is no agreed process and conflicts are resolved ad hoc |
| "Update the requirements management plan when the first scope change appears" | Updating processes when issues arise is adaptive; this seems responsive | Requirements management processes should be established proactively in planning; reactive creation means the early scope decisions were made without a defined process |
| "The scope management plan is the same document as the project scope statement" | Both involve scope; conflating them is common even among experienced PMs | The scope management plan defines the HOW (process); the project scope statement defines the WHAT (deliverables, boundaries, acceptance criteria) — they are separate and complementary |
| "Acceptance criteria will be finalized with the customer at delivery" | Customer-driven acceptance is good practice; defining criteria upfront seems presumptuous when customer needs evolve | PMI requires acceptance criteria to be defined in planning; criteria defined at delivery cannot be used to measure performance during execution |
| "Since the agile team has a product owner, no requirements management plan is needed" | Product owners manage the backlog, which is the agile requirements repository | Even agile projects require agreement on how requirements will be elicited, who has authority to accept them, and how changes are processed; the product owner is a role, not a process |
| "The scope management plan should define what the project will deliver" | Scope and scope management planning seem synonymous | The scope management plan defines the process for defining scope — it does not contain the scope itself; scope content belongs in the project scope statement and WBS |
| "The requirements management plan is an output of Collect Requirements" | Requirements management planning seems naturally connected to requirements collection activity | Requirements management plan is an output of Plan Scope Management — it governs how Collect Requirements will be done, so it must exist first |
| "Skip requirements traceability for a small project" | Small projects seem to need less governance; traceability is often seen as overhead | PMI does not waive traceability based on project size; the level of formality may be reduced through tailoring, but the concept of linking requirements to deliverables applies on all projects |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager is developing the scope management plan for a regulatory compliance project at a Bangladesh Bank-licensed commercial bank. The compliance department head, who is also a key stakeholder, insists that certain audit trail requirements be excluded from the scope because they would significantly increase cost and timeline. The PM knows from prior experience that these audit trail requirements are legally mandated under Bangladesh Bank ICT Security Policy. The sponsor agrees with the compliance head's position because it makes the business case stronger.

What should the PM do?

A. Exclude the audit trail requirements from scope as directed by the sponsor and compliance head, since they have authority over scope decisions  
B. Document the mandatory nature of the requirements, include them in scope, and present the impact analysis to the sponsor with a recommendation to include them  
C. Escalate directly to Bangladesh Bank to clarify whether the requirements are mandatory, bypassing the sponsor  
D. Include the requirements in scope and present the plan to the sponsor without highlighting the compliance conflict to avoid political friction  

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A Scrum team at a capital markets firm is starting a new trading analytics platform. The product owner says the team does not need a requirements management plan because "the backlog IS the requirements plan." During the first sprint, three senior traders submit requirements directly to developers without going through the product owner. By Sprint 3, the team has implemented features that were never prioritized or accepted by the product owner.

What should the PM / scrum master do FIRST?

A. Enforce backlog-only input by refusing to implement any feature not in the sprint backlog, effective immediately  
B. Facilitate a team discussion to establish an agreed process for how requirements enter and are prioritized in the backlog, then document it as a team agreement  
C. Escalate to senior management that stakeholders are bypassing the product owner's authority  
D. Hold a retrospective to identify all unauthorized features and remove them from the codebase

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team at a BSEC-listed asset management company is developing a client portal. The project management plan has been approved with a scope management plan that requires all scope changes to go through the change control board (CCB). A key business analyst, under pressure from a senior executive, has added 12 new requirements to the requirements traceability matrix without a change request. The team lead has already begun analysis on three of these requirements.

What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Call an emergency CCB meeting to review all 12 requirements immediately  
B. Stop the analysis work on the three requirements, document the unauthorized additions, and raise change requests for the new requirements  
C. Accept the new requirements since they have already been documented in the RTM and work has started, which creates a fait accompli  
D. Speak privately with the senior executive to understand the business urgency before taking any process action

**Q4 — Two-Correct, Pick-Best (Predictive)**

A PM is developing the scope management plan for a large-scale IT infrastructure project. The project involves 40 stakeholders across 8 departments. The PMO requires that the scope management plan address how requirements will be prioritized when stakeholders disagree. The PM is considering two approaches:

Option X: Use a requirements prioritization workshop with a multi-voting technique to reach consensus  
Option Y: Define in the requirements management plan that the project sponsor has final authority on prioritization decisions after stakeholder input  

Which statement BEST describes the PMI-correct approach?

A. Use Option X only; consensus-based prioritization is more stakeholder-inclusive and produces better buy-in  
B. Use Option Y only; the sponsor has final authority and defining it clearly prevents future ambiguity  
C. Combine both: document in the requirements management plan that stakeholder input is gathered through structured sessions, and the sponsor has final authority when consensus is not reached  
D. Neither; the product manager (not the PM or sponsor) should own requirements prioritization

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility and Honesty values; PMBOK 7 — Stewardship principle. The PM has professional and ethical responsibility to ensure legally mandated requirements are not omitted from scope to make a business case look better. Option B fulfills Honesty (document the mandatory nature), Responsibility (include in scope), and stakeholder service (present impact analysis for informed decision). Option A follows authority without ethical judgment — a PMI failure. Option C bypasses the sponsor and goes directly to the regulator; PMI expects internal channels to be exhausted first. Option D includes the requirements but withholds critical information from the sponsor — this is deception by omission.

**Mindset Lesson:** Following authority that requires omitting legally mandated requirements is an ethics violation; document, include, and present the impact — then let the sponsor decide with full information.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide Chapter 4; PMBOK 7 — Team performance domain. The root cause is the absence of a documented backlog management process. Option B addresses the root cause through facilitation and documentation — the PMI-preferred approach to establishing process. Option A is enforcement without education or agreement — it addresses symptoms, not root cause. Option C is premature escalation before attempting process resolution. Option D (removing code) is an irreversible action that creates conflict without solving the process gap.

**Mindset Lesson:** When agile scope is bypassing the product owner, establish the process through facilitation before enforcing or escalating.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Change control; Scope management. The scope management plan has been violated: requirements were added without a change request and work has started. Option B correctly sequences: stop the work (contain), document (create accountability), raise change requests (restore process integrity). Option A (emergency CCB) skips stopping the work — active unauthorized analysis continues while the CCB meets. Option C accepts the violation as a fait accompli — this rewards bypassing change control. Option D (talking to the executive) may be appropriate later but cannot precede stopping the active unauthorized work.

**Mindset Lesson:** Unauthorized scope work must be stopped and documented before any escalation or approval conversation occurs.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder performance domain; Requirements management. PMI values stakeholder inclusion AND clear decision authority. Option C combines both: structured stakeholder input (respects fairness and inclusion) with defined sponsor authority (enables decisions when consensus fails). Option A (consensus only) creates gridlock in a 40-stakeholder environment where consensus may be impossible. Option B (sponsor authority only) skips stakeholder input, which reduces buy-in and quality. Option D (product manager authority) introduces a role not established in the project governance structure.

**Mindset Lesson:** Requirements prioritization needs both an inclusive input process and a defined decision authority — document both in the requirements management plan.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A (2, 4, 3, 1, 5)**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide Chapter 5; team formation sequencing. Product owner authority and escalation path (2) must be established first because all subsequent backlog and acceptance decisions depend on knowing who has that authority. The team agreement including backlog management process (4) is established before the backlog is created, so the backlog creation follows an agreed process. The backlog (3) is created next based on the product vision. Definition of Done (1) is established before sprint planning so the team knows what "done" means. Sprint planning (5) is last as it requires a prioritized backlog and an agreed DoD.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile scope management, authority and process agreements precede backlog creation; sprint planning is only possible after both exist.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 Section 5.1.3; PMBOK 7. How requirements will be prioritized and traced belongs in the requirements management plan — not the scope management plan. The scope management plan addresses: how the scope statement will be developed (A), how the WBS will be created (B), and how deliverables will be formally accepted (C). Requirements prioritization and traceability are requirements management plan content.

**Mindset Lesson:** Scope management plan = process for defining and accepting scope; requirements management plan = process for collecting, tracing, and changing requirements — they are parallel but distinct.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility; PMBOK 7 — Stewardship. Option B fulfills the PM's responsibility to document risks and assumptions honestly while seeking written confirmation that creates an audit trail for the decision. This protects the PM and the organization. Option A follows authority without ethical pushback — unacceptable when the PM has knowledge of material risks. Option C unilaterally overrides the project director — not PMI's expected approach; the PM should document and escalate, not act unilaterally. Option D jumps to escalation before exhausting the direct conversation and documentation step with the project director.

**Mindset Lesson:** When instructed to omit a known risk from scope, document it in the assumption log and risk register, and get written confirmation — this is both ethical and protective.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder performance domain; Communications management. The root cause is an engagement format mismatch, not a compliance failure. Option C correctly diagnoses the issue (format mismatch), proposes a solution (alternative engagement), and follows process (change request to update the plan). Option A continues a failing approach without adaptation — PMI does not reward persistence over effectiveness. Option B eliminates the meeting requirement without understanding why it is failing — removing a process without a replacement is worse than the original problem. Option D escalates before attempting to understand or solve the engagement problem.

**Mindset Lesson:** When stakeholders are not engaging with a process, diagnose the format before escalating the behavior.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Change control; Stewardship principle. The immediate priority is process restoration: raise retroactive change requests (restore transparency), educate the developer (address knowledge gap), and involve the functional manager (address accountability). Option A (technical block) addresses the symptom, not the behavior or process understanding. Option C (CCB + removal) is disproportionate and punitive before education has been attempted; removal may also destabilize the project. Option D (exempting small changes) dismantles the change control process that protects scope — this is the worst possible response.

**Mindset Lesson:** Unauthorized scope work requires retroactive change requests and process education before technical controls or escalation.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: B and C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Planning performance domain; Scope Management. Option B reflects the collaborative development of the requirements management plan — stakeholder involvement in deciding HOW requirements will be managed is the PMI-correct approach. Option C reflects using OPAs (lessons learned from similar projects) and the project charter as inputs to this process — standard PMI inputs. Option A is incorrect: acceptance criteria belong in the project scope statement (Define Scope), not the scope management plan. Option D is incorrect: the project scope statement does not belong inside the scope management plan; they are separate documents. Option E is incorrect: creating the WBS is part of Create WBS (a later process), not Plan Scope Management.

**Mindset Lesson:** Plan Scope Management defines the HOW; it does not contain scope content — acceptance criteria, WBS, and scope statements belong in later processes.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Plan Scope Management produces two outputs: the scope management plan (how scope is defined, validated, controlled) and the requirements management plan (how requirements are collected, traced, changed)
- The scope management plan defines the process — it does not contain the scope; scope content belongs in the project scope statement and WBS
- The requirements management plan belongs to this process, not to Collect Requirements; it governs how Collect Requirements is done
- Acceptance criteria must be defined in planning, not at delivery; undefined criteria at delivery are a planning failure
- The lessons learned register is an input to this process from OPAs, not an output
- Both plans are components of the project management plan; all changes go through integrated change control
- In agile, the scope management plan equivalent is: product owner authority documentation + backlog governance rules + sprint acceptance process
- Traceability (RTM) is a requirements management principle, not optional regardless of project size
- In Bangladesh Bank-regulated projects, the requirements management plan must include a process for mandatory regulatory scope additions
- Requirements prioritization authority must be explicitly defined — gaps in authority create stakeholder conflict
- **The scope management plan defines how scope decisions will be made; without it, every scope conflict becomes a political negotiation instead of a process decision.**
