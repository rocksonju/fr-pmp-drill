# PMP Exam Master Brief: Collect Requirements

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Collect Requirements is the process of determining, documenting, and managing stakeholder needs and requirements to meet project objectives. It sits in the Planning Process Group under Project Scope Management and produces the Requirements Documentation and Requirements Traceability Matrix — two artifacts that anchor every downstream scope, schedule, cost, and quality decision. Exam weight is moderate-to-high within the Scope domain, and it bleeds heavily into Stakeholder Engagement, Communications, and Quality Management because misidentified requirements cause rework, scope creep, and stakeholder conflicts throughout execution. The process relies on a rich toolkit — interviews, focus groups, facilitated workshops, prototypes, benchmarking, surveys, and context diagrams — each suited to different stakeholder types and project environments. In agile contexts, requirements are elicited continuously through backlog refinement, user stories, and acceptance criteria rather than front-loaded documentation. The critical PMI distinction is that requirements must be traced from business need through deliverable through acceptance — the RTM enforces that chain. Experienced PMs often stop at gathering; PMI demands you also manage, validate, and baseline requirements. **In one sentence:** Collect Requirements transforms vague stakeholder needs into documented, traceable, verifiable criteria that define what the project must produce.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Front-loaded elicitation sessions, signed-off requirements documentation, formal RTM updated throughout; changes go through integrated change control |
| Agile | Continuous elicitation via user stories, backlog refinement, and sprint reviews; requirements are "just enough" and elaborated incrementally; product owner owns prioritization |
| Hybrid | High-level scope baseline established predictively; detailed requirements elaborated per iteration; RTM maintained but at a feature/story level |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Requirements must be traceable to business objectives — real-world PMs often collect requirements without establishing the "why," but PMI requires every requirement to link back to a documented business need so scope decisions remain objective.
2. Stakeholder silence is not consent — real-world PMs assume non-objection means approval, but PMI mandates explicit elicitation and documented confirmation because unstated requirements are the leading cause of late-cycle rework.
3. The product owner/sponsor prioritizes requirements, not the PM — real-world PMs often negotiate requirements scope themselves, but PMI holds that the PM facilitates while the accountable business authority decides what stays.
4. Prototypes validate requirements before they are baselined — real-world PMs treat prototypes as design artifacts, but PMI positions them as elicitation tools that surface hidden requirements and reduce assumptions.
5. Requirements elicitation is iterative even in predictive projects — real-world PMs treat the requirements workshop as a one-time event, but PMI expects the PM to revisit and refine requirements as stakeholder understanding deepens.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Respect:** Actively solicit input from all affected stakeholders, including those with low power/high interest, not just the loudest voices.
- **Fairness:** Document conflicting requirements objectively; do not filter stakeholder needs to favor a preferred solution.
- **Honesty:** If requirements are infeasible within constraints, report that clearly rather than documenting aspirational requirements to appear compliant.
- **Responsibility:** Ensure the RTM is maintained and requirements are not quietly dropped or altered without documented change control.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Requirements Documentation | Record of all stakeholder requirements — functional, non-functional, quality, transition, business, and compliance requirements; serves as input to Define Scope | Confusing this with the scope statement — scope statement describes what is included; requirements doc describes what must be true about it |
| Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) | Grid linking requirements to their origin, deliverables, WBS components, test cases, and status; ensures no requirement is lost or unverified | Thinking the RTM is only for testing — it covers the full lifecycle from origin through acceptance, not just QA |
| Product Scope | Features and functions that characterize a product or service — what the deliverable IS | Confusing with project scope — project scope is the work needed; product scope is the characteristics of what is produced |
| Functional Requirement | Describes behavior a product must perform (e.g., "the system shall generate a monthly report") | Marking functional requirements as lower priority than non-functional — both carry equal traceability weight in PMI |
| Non-Functional Requirement | Describes quality or performance characteristics (e.g., response time under 2 seconds, 99.9% uptime) | Overlooking non-functional requirements in elicitation; PMI expects them explicitly documented, not assumed |
| Facilitated Workshop | Structured meeting of cross-functional stakeholders to define cross-functional requirements rapidly; higher trust output than serial interviews | Thinking interviews always produce more thorough requirements — workshops surface conflicts and consensus faster |
| Prototyping | Creating a working model to elicit feedback and validate requirements before baselining; iterative and tangible | Treating prototypes as final deliverables — they are elicitation tools; the prototype itself is not the product |
| Context Diagram | Visual depiction of the product scope showing business system, inputs, outputs, and external actors; scopes the boundary | Confusing context diagram with WBS — context diagram defines what the system exchanges with the environment, not the work breakdown |
| User Story | Short, informal description of a feature from the end-user perspective: "As a [role], I want [capability] so that [benefit]" | Assuming user stories replace the RTM in agile — stories must still be traceable to business value and acceptance criteria |
| Benchmarking | Comparing requirements practices or deliverable characteristics against industry best practice or competitor products to identify targets | Treating benchmarking as only a quality tool — it is explicitly listed as a Collect Requirements technique for identifying requirement standards |
| Focus Group | Moderated discussion with pre-qualified stakeholders to discover attitudes, expectations, and hidden requirements | Assuming focus groups replace individual interviews for all stakeholders — high-power, risk-averse stakeholders often require one-on-one interviews |
| Affinity Diagram | Groups large numbers of ideas or requirements into clusters for review and analysis — useful after brainstorming | Treating affinity diagrams as a Define Scope tool — they are an elicitation analysis technique in Collect Requirements |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Stakeholders disagree on requirements | Facilitate a requirements workshop to surface all views and work toward consensus | PMI prioritizes structured facilitation over escalation or unilateral PM decisions; escalation is last resort |
| Requirements keep changing mid-execution | Assess impact via integrated change control before accepting any change | No requirement change is accepted without documented impact analysis; PMI never skips this step |
| A new stakeholder is identified late | Conduct elicitation with them immediately and update the RTM | Unelicited stakeholders are a scope risk; late discovery does not exempt you from capturing their requirements |
| Sponsor wants to skip requirements doc and start building | Explain the risk and document requirements before design begins | PMI mandates traceability; building without documented requirements means you cannot verify acceptance criteria |
| Requirements are vague and high-level | Use prototyping or facilitated workshops to elaborate and validate them | Vague requirements must be made verifiable before baselining; PMI does not accept "we'll figure it out later" |
| Agile team has too many user stories for the sprint | Work with the product owner to prioritize by business value | Prioritization is the product owner's authority, not the PM's; PM facilitates, not decides |
| Two business units have conflicting requirements | Document both, analyze impact, and escalate to the sponsor for decision | Conflict resolution for requirements with organizational impact requires sponsor authority, not PM arbitration |
| Requirements are complete but untested | Ensure acceptance criteria are defined and linked in the RTM before baselining | Requirements without acceptance criteria cannot be verified — they are not complete per PMI definition |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Sponsor pressures PM to document only "approved" requirements and omit others | Document all stakeholder requirements with source attribution before filtering | Omitting documented requirements violates Honesty; the PM's responsibility is complete capture, not pre-filtered capture |
| Team starts detailed design before requirements sign-off | Stop design work and complete requirements elicitation and baseline | PMI sequence integrity: requirements must be baselined before scope is defined and design begins |

**Mnemonic:** **I-D-R-T** — Identify, Document, Review, Trace

**Memory hook:** "I Don't Rush Traceability" — the PM who skips tracing requirements to business needs pays double in rework.

**Mnemonic unpacked:**
- **I** — Identify all stakeholder needs using the full elicitation toolkit
- **D** — Document requirements completely: functional, non-functional, quality, and transition
- **R** — Review with stakeholders for explicit validation, not assumed acceptance
- **T** — Trace every requirement through the RTM from origin to acceptance criteria

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Front-load elicitation with all key stakeholders before scope baseline is approved
- RTM is a formal controlled document; changes require integrated change control
- Requirements documentation is a formal deliverable with version control and stakeholder signatures
- Transition requirements (training, cutover, data migration) are as critical as functional requirements

**Agile / Hybrid**
- User stories with acceptance criteria replace formal requirements documents as the primary artifact
- Product backlog serves as a living requirements register; refinement is a recurring ceremony
- Definition of Ready ensures stories are elaborated enough for sprint commitment
- In hybrid, a high-level requirements baseline may be predictively defined while detailed requirements are elaborated iteratively

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC context:** Regulatory requirements (compliance, audit trail, data residency, anti-money-laundering rules) must be documented as non-negotiable constraints, not business requirements — they are not subject to stakeholder prioritization or deferral
- Projects subject to SOX, Basel III, or local securities regulation (BSEC) require requirements traceability to specific regulatory clauses; the RTM must reference regulatory sources explicitly
- In financial services, non-functional requirements (latency, availability, security classification) often carry higher criticality than functional requirements and must be weighted accordingly in backlog prioritization

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Start building once the sponsor approves the high-level scope | Sponsors approve scope statements, and experienced PMs trust the sponsor's authority and want to show speed | High-level scope is not baselined requirements; building without a verified RTM means you cannot confirm acceptance criteria — rework is the guaranteed outcome |
| Use only interviews to collect requirements because they are most thorough | Experienced PMs know that one-on-one interviews are thorough and reduce group-think bias | PMI expects elicitation technique selection based on stakeholder type and complexity; workshops, prototypes, and surveys each surface requirements interviews miss |
| Ask the PM to resolve conflicting stakeholder requirements | The PM is accountable for scope and experienced PMs make decisions to keep the project moving | Requirements prioritization and conflict resolution for business requirements is the sponsor/product owner's authority; the PM facilitates but does not decide |
| Skip the RTM for simple projects to save time | Small projects feel over-administered with a full RTM; pragmatic PMs focus on delivery | PMI does not recognize project size as justification for skipping traceability; the RTM is always required and scales to fit — dropping it creates unverifiable acceptance criteria |
| Accept a verbal agreement that requirements are complete | Experienced PMs trust sponsor relationships and verbal confirmation to maintain project pace | PMI requires documented, explicit confirmation; verbal agreements cannot be referenced in change control or dispute resolution |
| Immediately escalate stakeholder disagreement to the sponsor | Experienced PMs know the sponsor has final authority and want to surface conflict early | Escalation is a last resort; PMI expects the PM to first facilitate resolution using conflict management tools and only escalate unresolvable disagreements |
| Document only the requirements the project team can deliver within budget | Budget-conscious PMs filter requirements to protect the business case | All stakeholder requirements must be documented; the scope baseline — not the PM — determines what is in or out; filtering pre-emptively destroys traceability |
| Treat the context diagram as the scope statement | Experienced PMs use the context diagram to communicate scope visually and assume it serves as the scope definition | The context diagram is an elicitation analysis tool that defines system boundaries; the scope statement is a separate artifact produced in Define Scope |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

During requirements elicitation, you discover that the Head of Compliance has requirements that conflict with features the COO's team has already committed to deliver. The COO is your project sponsor and has told you privately that compliance requirements "tend to be over-engineered" and should be minimized. The compliance officer is not on the steering committee. You have documented both sets of requirements in draft form. What should you do FIRST?

A. Defer to the COO's direction since the sponsor has final authority over scope decisions  
B. Document both sets of requirements fully and present the conflict to the steering committee with an impact analysis  
C. Remove the conflicting compliance requirements from the documentation to avoid escalation

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B  
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Fairness) requires complete documentation of all stakeholder requirements regardless of sponsor preference. Regulatory/compliance requirements in a banking context are non-negotiable constraints. The PM's role is to facilitate resolution through the correct governance body (steering committee) with full information, not to filter documentation based on sponsor opinion. Option A fails because sponsor authority does not extend to suppressing documented requirements. Option C is an ethical violation (Honesty). Option D weaponizes process to exclude a legitimate stakeholder.  
**Mindset Lesson:** The PM documents all requirements completely and surfaces conflicts to the appropriate governance body — sponsor preference never overrides complete documentation.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** C  
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide: the product owner has prioritization authority for business requirements, but regulatory requirements may override backlog prioritization decisions. The PM's role is to facilitate structured dialogue between the product owner and the regulatory authority to determine the nature of the requirement before escalating. Option A is wrong because PMI does not allow automatic deferral of potentially mandatory requirements. Option B usurps product owner authority. Option D escalates before facilitation — PMI escalation is a last resort.  
**Mindset Lesson:** When regulatory authority conflicts with product owner prioritization, facilitate resolution first; escalate only when the nature of the requirement cannot be resolved at team level.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B  
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / hybrid frameworks require that detailed requirements (stories with acceptance criteria) be baselined before design begins; the RTM must be current before technical work proceeds. The high-level baseline does not substitute for iteration-level elaboration. Option A confuses the level of the baseline. Option C creates a traceability gap — backfilling stories after design embeds unverified assumptions. Option D eliminates a required control.  
**Mindset Lesson:** In hybrid projects, the RTM must be updated at the iteration level before technical design begins — "it will inform later" is never acceptable.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B  
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK scope management requires all requirements be documented completely before scope decisions are made. The PM presents options (scope reduction, phasing, budget increase) but does not pre-filter the requirements register. Option A confuses documentation with scope decision — requirements are documented before decisions are made. Option C delays legitimate elicitation. Option D falsely classifies a known requirement as an assumption.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Requirements documentation is complete capture; scope decisions come after, through the change control and sponsor authority process.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A (B → C → A → D)  
**PMI Justification:** Agile retrospective is the correct first step to diagnose root cause (B). Training addresses the identified gap (C). The Definition of Ready establishes the standard going forward (A). Backlog review applies the new standard retroactively (D). Starting with A skips diagnosis. Starting with C solves a symptom without confirming the cause. PMI always roots corrective action in diagnosis before solution.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Corrective action in agile follows: diagnose (retrospective) → solve (training) → institutionalize (DoR) → apply (backlog update).

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C  
**PMI Justification:** Decomposition is a tool used in Define Scope (to create the WBS) and in Define Activities — it is not listed as a Collect Requirements technique. Valid Collect Requirements tools include interviews, facilitated workshops, focus groups, questionnaires/surveys, prototyping, benchmarking, brainstorming, affinity diagrams, mind mapping, nominal group technique, observation, and document analysis. Options A, B, and D are all explicitly listed PMBOK Collect Requirements tools.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Know precisely which tools belong to which process — Decomposition is a Define Scope and Create WBS tool, not Collect Requirements.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B  
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Fairness, Responsibility) requires complete disclosure when a known omission affects project scope. Proceeding to sign-off with known incomplete requirements is an ethical violation. The schedule inconvenience does not override honesty obligations. Option A defers documented requirements as "future phase" without stakeholder knowledge — this is dishonest. Option C weaponizes change control to exclude an elicitation-phase requirement. Option D substitutes informal confirmation for documented elicitation.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Known omissions must be disclosed before sign-off regardless of schedule pressure — honesty is not conditional on convenience.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** C  
**PMI Justification:** PMI Communications Management and Collect Requirements both emphasize matching elicitation methods to stakeholder context. Distributed teams with time zone constraints require tailored multi-modal approaches; a single method (surveys only or workshops only) excludes some stakeholders or reduces quality. Option A subordinates elicitation quality to product owner convenience. Option B mandates workshops regardless of feasibility for distributed teams. Option D creates siloed, unreconciled requirements that increase conflict risk.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Elicitation technique selection must account for stakeholder geography, availability, and communication preferences — no single method fits all contexts.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** A  
**PMI Justification:** Integrated change control applies to all scope changes including regulatory-driven changes; informal work on unapproved requirements is a scope control violation. The PM must stop informal work, formalize the change request, assess impacts on scope/schedule/cost/quality, update the RTM, and obtain approval before work resumes. Option B normalizes unauthorized scope work. Option C is close but "stop, then assess" — the assessment (impact analysis) is part of the formal change request process and must happen concurrently with the request, not sequentially after stopping. Option D transfers PM responsibility to the sponsor.  
**Mindset Lesson:** Regulatory requirements do not bypass integrated change control — they get expedited, not circumvented.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, D  
**PMI Justification:**  
- A is correct: RTM explicitly links requirements to business objectives, deliverables, and acceptance criteria (PMBOK scope management).  
- B is wrong: RTM is applicable in all project approaches; in agile it may be maintained at story level but it is not replaced — the product backlog alone does not provide requirement-to-acceptance traceability.  
- C is correct: RTM is an input to Validate Scope and Close Project and is used to verify all requirements were addressed.  
- D is correct: Source and status are standard RTM columns per PMBOK.  
- E is wrong: RTM changes that affect the scope baseline require integrated change control.  
- F is correct: PMBOK and Agile Practice Guide both recognize that in hybrid projects, the RTM is maintained at the feature/story level.  
**Mindset Lesson:** The RTM is a living traceability document used across the entire project lifecycle — it scales to the approach but is never eliminated.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Collect Requirements is the Planning process that transforms stakeholder needs into documented, traceable, verifiable criteria.
- Outputs: Requirements Documentation and Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) — both are inputs to Define Scope.
- RTM links requirements to business objectives, deliverables, WBS components, acceptance criteria, and status.
- Key tools: interviews, facilitated workshops, prototyping, focus groups, surveys, benchmarking, affinity diagrams — match tool to stakeholder type.
- In agile: user stories with acceptance criteria and the product backlog serve as the living requirements register; elaboration is continuous.
- Product owner or sponsor prioritizes and resolves requirement conflicts — PM facilitates, not decides.
- All requirements must be documented completely before scope decisions are made — do not pre-filter.
- Regulatory requirements are non-negotiable constraints — they do not enter the backlog prioritization debate.
- Verbal agreement is insufficient — requirements sign-off must be documented explicitly.
- Any change to baselined requirements, including regulatory-driven changes, goes through integrated change control.
- **The PM who documents all requirements completely, traces every one to a business objective, and never accepts verbal sign-off will outscore the PM who builds fast every time.**
