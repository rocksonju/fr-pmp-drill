# PMP Exam Master Brief: Develop Project Management Plan

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Develop Project Management Plan is the sole Planning process in Integration Management and the anchor document for every subsequent planning activity across all ten knowledge areas. It integrates all subsidiary plans and baselines into a single coherent document that defines how the project will be executed, monitored, controlled, and closed. The output — the project management plan — is not a static deliverable; it evolves through progressive elaboration and must be formally updated via integrated change control. On the PMP exam, this process appears in roughly 8–12% of questions either directly or as the background context for integration-heavy scenarios. Cross-domain bleed is extreme: every other Planning process feeds into or is constrained by what this process establishes. In regulated environments such as Bangladesh Bank-supervised institutions or BSEC-listed entities, the plan must align with governance policies, audit requirements, and regulatory reporting cadences. Understanding that the plan is an output of negotiation and team involvement — not a PM solo exercise — is the central PMI mindset shift candidates must internalize. **In one sentence:** The project management plan is the formally approved, integrated roadmap that tells every stakeholder how the project will be done, measured, and changed.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | A comprehensive, formally approved plan document integrating all subsidiary plans; changes go through integrated change control; plan is the performance measurement baseline reference |
| Agile | Planning is continuous and lightweight; the "plan" may be a product roadmap, release plan, and sprint plan rather than a single document; updated every iteration based on inspect-and-adapt |
| Hybrid | Core governance plan is predictive (scope baseline, change control process, stakeholder comms plan); delivery cadence within work packages may be agile; tailoring decisions documented explicitly |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **The project management plan is built with the team, not for the team.** Real-world instinct: the PM drafts the plan alone and presents it. PMI: team involvement in planning increases buy-in, accuracy, and realism; a plan produced without team input is a red flag.
2. **Progressive elaboration is a feature, not a failure.** Real-world instinct: a good plan is complete on day one. PMI: plans are intentionally high-level early and elaborated as more information is known; attempting to plan everything upfront in an uncertain environment is a risk, not a best practice.
3. **The plan is approved, not just completed.** Real-world instinct: finishing the document is enough. PMI: formal approval by sponsor and key stakeholders signals commitment and baseline establishment; without approval, there is no change control baseline to protect.
4. **Tailoring the plan to the project is mandatory, not optional.** Real-world instinct: use last project's plan as a template. PMI: every project context demands explicit tailoring decisions about life cycle, methodology, and governance; copy-paste planning ignores project-specific risks.
5. **The plan drives all project work — if it is not in the plan, it should not be happening.** Real-world instinct: handle issues pragmatically as they arise outside the plan. PMI: all project activities, change decisions, and resource commitments trace back to the approved plan; deviations require formal change requests.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** The PM is accountable for ensuring the plan is realistic, not padded to protect personal performance. Presenting an unachievable plan to gain sponsor approval violates the Responsibility value.
- **Honesty:** If known constraints make the plan undeliverable, the PM must surface this during planning — not suppress it to avoid uncomfortable conversations.
- **Fairness:** All stakeholders with a legitimate planning stake (team, sponsor, key vendors) receive meaningful participation in planning; excluding voices to speed up planning is a fairness violation.
- **Respect:** The plan acknowledges resource constraints, cultural factors, and team capacity; ignoring these is disrespectful to people whose work the plan governs.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Project Management Plan | The single approved document integrating all subsidiary plans and baselines; primary output of this process | Do not confuse with project documents (issue log, risk register) — those are not part of the plan |
| Subsidiary Plans | Component plans covering each knowledge area (scope, schedule, cost, quality, resources, communications, risk, procurement, stakeholder engagement, plus change and configuration management) | Not all 10 subsidiary plans appear on every project; tailoring determines which are needed |
| Baselines | Scope baseline (WBS + WBS dictionary + scope statement), schedule baseline, cost baseline; the approved versions against which performance is measured | Baselines are part of the plan; project documents are not; this distinction is heavily tested |
| Progressive Elaboration | Iteratively developing plan detail as more information becomes available over the project life cycle | Exam trap: candidates think a detailed plan must exist before work starts; PMI accepts high-level planning with elaboration over time |
| Configuration Management Plan | Defines how project documents and deliverables will be identified, versioned, and controlled | Separate from change management plan; confusing the two is a common error |
| Change Management Plan | Defines how change requests will be submitted, reviewed, approved or rejected, and communicated | The change management plan is part of the project management plan — not an output of Perform Integrated Change Control |
| Tailoring | The deliberate adaptation of processes, methods, tools, and governance to fit the specific project context | PMI mandates tailoring decisions be documented; "we used our standard template" is not tailoring |
| Lessons Learned Register | A project document capturing knowledge gained; inputs to future project planning but not part of the project management plan itself | Do not select updating the project management plan when the exam asks about capturing lessons learned |
| Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs) | External/internal conditions that influence the project but the PM cannot control (regulations, culture, market, BSEC compliance requirements) | EEFs are inputs, not constraints the PM ignores; regulatory frameworks like Bangladesh Bank guidelines shape the plan |
| Organizational Process Assets (OPAs) | Policies, procedures, templates, historical data, and lessons learned owned by the organization | OPAs provide starting points; the PM tailors from OPAs, does not blindly adopt them |
| Approved Project Charter | Authorization document that provides initial scope, budget, milestones, and PM authority; primary input to Develop Project Management Plan | The charter authorizes the project; the management plan defines how to execute it — they are not interchangeable |
| Kickoff Meeting | Formal team event signaling transition from planning to execution; often associated with this process completion | The kickoff occurs at end of planning / start of execution, not mid-planning — a timing trap |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Project is about to begin and there is no project management plan" | Develop the project management plan before starting execution activities | Executing without an approved plan means no baseline for performance measurement or change control |
| "Sponsor wants to start work while planning is still underway" | Negotiate a rolling wave plan that covers imminent work in detail while allowing high-level planning for later phases | Progressive elaboration enables starting execution on well-defined work without fabricating false certainty about later work |
| "Team members disagree on how the schedule will be managed" | Facilitate development of the schedule management plan as part of project management plan development | The plan development process is collaborative; disagreements are resolved during planning, not ignored |
| "A regulatory requirement (e.g., Bangladesh Bank reporting) is discovered after planning begins" | Raise a change request to update the project management plan and relevant subsidiary plans | Post-approval discoveries that affect scope or approach require formal change control even during planning phase |
| "Stakeholder asks why a decision was made differently than the organization's standard methodology" | Point to the tailoring decisions documented in the project management plan | Tailoring rationale must be documented; "we decided" without documentation is not defensible |
| "Project manager inherited a project with an outdated plan" | Assess gaps, then work with sponsor and team to update the plan through change control | Do not simply follow the old plan or create a new one from scratch; update through the formal process |
| "Sponsor asks for the project to proceed with only a charter, skipping the management plan" | Explain the risk of proceeding without a management plan and document sponsor's decision | The PM's responsibility is to advise on risk; if sponsor overrides, document the decision and associated risks |
| "New team member asks how scope changes will be handled" | Refer them to the change management plan within the project management plan | The plan is the authoritative source; the PM does not give ad hoc answers that may contradict the documented process |

**Escalation edge cases:**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Functional manager refuses to release resources committed in the project management plan" | Escalate to the sponsor after documenting the impact | Resource commitments in the approved plan are not voluntary; non-compliance requires sponsor intervention, not re-planning on the spot |
| "PMO mandates a process the PM believes is inappropriate for this agile project" | Document tailoring rationale and seek approval through the project management plan update process | PMO mandates are EEFs; deviation requires documented tailoring justification, not unilateral non-compliance |

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Define all subsidiary plans before execution kickoff; level of detail depends on project complexity and risk
- In high-compliance environments (BSEC-listed company raising capital), the plan must address audit trails, document retention policies, and regulatory reporting milestones
- Configuration management plan is critical when deliverables include regulated documents (prospectus, offering circulars)
- Formal sponsor approval with signature is required before baseline is set; verbal approval is not sufficient

### Agile / Hybrid

- Replace monolithic plan document with a planning hierarchy: product vision, release plan, iteration plan
- Team agreements (working agreements, definition of done) serve as the equivalent of certain subsidiary plan elements
- Planning cadence is continuous; the "plan" at any point is the current sprint backlog plus the rolling wave release plan
- Change requests are replaced by backlog refinement; however, changes affecting budget or scope baseline still require sponsor approval in hybrid projects

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Bangladesh Bank / Financial sector:** Regulatory compliance plans must align with ICT security guidelines, AML/CFT policy frameworks, and Bangladesh Bank circular-driven reporting requirements — these become explicit constraints in the project management plan
- **BSEC-listed entities:** Capital market projects must include investor communication plans and disclosure obligation schedules as part of stakeholder communications subsidiary plan
- **Audit requirements:** Plan must include document control provisions supporting internal and external audit access; configuration management plan is not optional in regulated banking technology projects
- **Procurement constraints:** Government procurement rules (CPTU framework in Bangladesh) constrain how procurement management plan is developed; PMI's plan development must accommodate these as EEFs

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Begin executing since the team is ready and the charter is approved" | The charter is the authorization to proceed; experienced PMs have started work on charter approval alone in resource-scarce environments | The project management plan must be approved before execution begins; the charter authorizes the project, not the work approach |
| "Update the project management plan yourself to reflect the new requirement" | PMs own the plan and routinely make administrative updates; updating it personally is efficient | All post-approval changes to the plan require a formal change request through integrated change control; unilateral updates bypass the baseline |
| "Use the previous project's plan as the project management plan with minor edits" | Template reuse is efficient and aligns with organizational standards; experienced PMs do this routinely | PMI requires explicit tailoring to the current project context; adopting a prior plan without documented tailoring ignores this project's unique risks and constraints |
| "Document lessons learned in the project management plan" | Lessons learned are important planning inputs; putting them in the plan seems logical | Lessons learned are captured in the lessons learned register (a project document), not in the project management plan itself |
| "The kickoff meeting should happen before the plan is approved" | Early team engagement and communication are good practices; starting the kickoff early generates momentum | The kickoff meeting marks the transition from planning to execution and signals that the plan is complete and approved; holding it before approval is premature |
| "The PM should develop the plan and then present it to the team for their awareness" | PMs have planning authority and the team needs direction, not debate | PMI requires team involvement in developing the plan; awareness-only sharing produces an unrealistic plan and reduces team commitment |
| "Since the project is agile, no project management plan is needed" | Agile projects move fast and heavyweight documentation slows delivery | Even agile projects require documented agreements on governance, change, and stakeholder engagement; the form changes but the need for planned agreements does not |
| "The sponsor wants the plan changed; update it per their request" | Sponsors have authority over the project; accommodating their requests is relationship management | Sponsor requests for plan changes must go through integrated change control like any other change; the PM does not update the plan on verbal instruction |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager at a Bangladesh Bank-regulated fintech company is developing the project management plan for a core banking system upgrade. The sponsor, under pressure from the board, asks the PM to set an aggressive cost baseline that the PM knows is 25% below the realistic estimate. The sponsor says the low number is needed to get regulatory approval for the budget. The PM's own bottom-up estimate, validated by two senior engineers, shows the realistic figure. The sponsor assures the PM that overruns will be "managed later."

What should the PM do?

A. Accept the sponsor's direction and document the realistic estimate as a separate risk register entry so the true cost is captured somewhere  
B. Refuse to approve the project management plan until the realistic cost estimate is reflected in the cost baseline  
C. Present both estimates to the sponsor in writing, explain the ethical and financial risk of the lower baseline, and request a decision in writing before the plan is approved  
D. Escalate immediately to the Bangladesh Bank regulator since the inaccurate budget will be used in a regulatory submission

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

Several stakeholders say they were unaware the project had started. The PM discovers there is no documented stakeholder engagement plan and no communications plan in the minimal project management plan the team created. The scrum master believes these plans are "waterfall artifacts" not needed in Scrum.

What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Add a stakeholder engagement plan and communications plan to the project management plan through the change control process  
B. Run a retrospective to surface the communication gap and let the team self-organize a solution  
C. Have an immediate conversation with the scrum master to align on which governance elements must exist even in an agile project, then update the plan accordingly  
D. Notify senior management that the scrum master is not following PMI standards

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty value. The PM has a duty to provide accurate information to decision-makers. Option C fulfills the Honesty obligation (present both estimates in writing, explain the risk) while also fulfilling Responsibility (request written decision before approval, creating an audit trail). Option A is incorrect because burying the accurate number in the risk register while approving a false baseline is dishonest — it provides the appearance of compliance without the substance. Option B is partially correct in spirit but unilaterally refusing approval is escalation without communication, skipping a step. Option D is premature escalation to the regulator before internal channels are exhausted; PMI expects the PM to resolve issues at the lowest appropriate level first.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a sponsor asks you to document something you know is false, your job is to surface the conflict in writing and request a decision — not to choose between silent compliance and immediate escalation.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stewardship principle; Agile Practice Guide Chapter 6 (tailoring). Even in Scrum, governance elements that protect stakeholder engagement and communication are not optional. Option C is correct because it addresses the root cause (a misunderstanding about what agile eliminates) before jumping to process or escalation. Option A is technically correct but skips the necessary conversation with the scrum master — implementing change without alignment creates a new conflict. Option B (retrospective) is appropriate for team-internal process issues, not for a governance gap with stakeholder impact. Option D is punitive escalation without first attempting resolution — a PMI red flag.

**Mindset Lesson:** Agile eliminates unnecessary documentation, not accountability for stakeholder engagement; those are never optional regardless of methodology.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder engagement; Risk principle. The PM must inform the sponsor of material risks before approval, not after. Option C is correct: inform the sponsor (transparency), request delay (responsible), document formally (accountability). Option A approves a plan the PM knows contains an inaccurate baseline — a honesty/responsibility violation. Option B unilaterally stopping procurement is within the PM's authority in theory but skips informing the sponsor first — the correct sequence is inform then act. Option D pressuring the developer to change their estimate to fit a desired outcome is a form of manipulation and creates false accuracy.

**Mindset Lesson:** Approving a plan you know contains a material error to meet a political deadline is not schedule management — it is a documented integrity failure.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: B (A and D)**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Tailoring; PMBOK 6 Section 11.1. Option A is correct: referencing and tailoring from an OPA is PMI's expected approach. Option D is correct: documenting the plan AND orienting new members/vendors addresses both the documentation gap and the communication gap. Option B (skipping the plan) is incorrect because undocumented processes fail when new stakeholders join — exactly what this stem describes. Option C (recreating from scratch) wastes effort and ignores available OPAs. The combination of A and D covers documentation, tailoring rationale, and team orientation — addressing all stated concerns.

**Mindset Lesson:** Referencing an organizational standard in the plan plus orienting new team members is more PMI-correct than either documentation or conversation alone.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A (3, 5, 2, 1, 4)**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide Chapter 5 — Agile planning. The correct sequence starts with team agreements (working agreements, definition of done) because these define how the team will operate before backlog content is shaped. Story mapping comes next to visualize user journeys. Backlog creation follows. Release planning sequences the backlog into milestone targets. Sprint planning is last as it requires an ordered backlog and defined working agreements. Option B skips working agreements to the end, producing a story map without agreed-upon standards. Option C starts with backlog before story mapping, which typically precedes and informs backlog structure. Option D puts working agreements first (correct) but then jumps to backlog before story mapping.

**Mindset Lesson:** Agile planning begins with how the team will work together before deciding what they will build.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Section 4.2 (Project Work); PMBOK 6 Section 4.3. The lessons learned register is a project document, not a component of the project management plan. Options A, C, and D are all legitimate components of the project management plan: the change management plan (subsidiary plan), the scope baseline (approved scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary), and the schedule management plan (subsidiary plan) are all explicitly listed as project management plan components.

**Mindset Lesson:** Project documents (issue log, risk register, lessons learned register, assumption log) and project management plan components are a heavily tested distinction — know the separation cold.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility values. Approving a plan that contains a known false commitment is a direct honesty violation. Option B is the only choice that addresses the problem through transparency before approval. Option A disguises the issue as a risk, allowing an inaccurate plan to be formally approved — this is dishonesty via omission. Option C removes the sponsor from a decision they must make about resource strategy; HR resolving an architect departure does not fix the plan's false commitment. Option D introduces a vague placeholder rather than transparency — the sponsor's decision should be made with full information.

**Mindset Lesson:** Allowing a sponsor to approve a plan based on information you know to be false is a honesty violation regardless of how the false information was introduced.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder performance domain; Communications Management. Communication plans are living components that should be assessed and updated when they are not achieving their objectives. Option C is correct because it follows PMI's continuous improvement mindset (assess, propose, submit change request). Option A treats the approved plan as immutable — plans are updated when they stop serving their purpose. Option B makes changes outside change control, which is unauthorized. Option D escalates without first attempting to solve the actual problem (communication format mismatch); escalating non-responsiveness before improving the approach is premature.

**Mindset Lesson:** An approved plan that is not working is an invitation for a change request, not a shield against improvement.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Change control; Scope management. Unauthorized scope additions violate the project management plan's change control provisions. Option B is correct: stop the work (contain the breach), document it (create accountability), and raise a change request (respect the process). Option A allows unauthorized scope to proceed, rewarding bypass behavior and undermining the plan. Option C skips the intermediate steps of stopping the work and raising a formal change request — escalation to sponsor is appropriate after the breach is contained and documented, not as the first action. Option D (warning to team lead) is a people management action, not a change control action — it may be appropriate later but is not the first priority.

**Mindset Lesson:** Unauthorized work must be stopped before it is escalated — stopping and documenting comes before sponsor notification.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: B and C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Team performance domain; Planning performance domain. Option B reflects the collaborative planning principle — team involvement increases plan quality and commitment. Option C reflects the use of OPAs with tailoring — the correct PMI approach to leveraging organizational knowledge without blind adoption. Option A (PM drafts alone) violates the collaborative development principle. Option D (wait for all subsidiary plans to be complete) is impractical and misunderstands progressive elaboration — integration occurs iteratively. Option E (kickoff the day after charter approval) is premature; the kickoff occurs after planning is complete and the project management plan is approved.

**Mindset Lesson:** The project management plan is built collaboratively from organizational knowledge, not authored in isolation from a blank page.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Develop Project Management Plan is the sole Planning process in Integration Management and produces the single approved document governing all project work
- The plan integrates ALL subsidiary plans and baselines — scope, schedule, cost, quality, resources, communications, risk, procurement, and stakeholder engagement
- The plan is developed collaboratively with the team, not written by the PM alone
- Progressive elaboration is intentional — early high-level planning elaborated over time is PMI-correct, not a planning failure
- Formal sponsor approval is required before baselining; verbal approval is not sufficient
- All post-approval changes go through integrated change control — no exceptions, including sponsor requests
- The kickoff meeting marks end of planning / start of execution — not the beginning of planning
- Project documents (lessons learned register, risk register, issue log) are NOT part of the project management plan
- Tailoring to project context is mandatory and must be documented; copying a prior project's plan without tailoring is a risk, not an efficiency
- In regulated environments (Bangladesh Bank, BSEC), the plan must explicitly address compliance reporting, audit access, and regulatory milestone constraints
- **If the plan does not authorize it, stop the work, document the breach, and raise a change request — every time, no exceptions.**
