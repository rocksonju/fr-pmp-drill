# PMP Exam Master Brief: Manage Team

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Manage Team is the process of tracking team member performance, providing feedback, resolving issues, and managing team changes to optimize project performance. It sits in the Executing Process Group under Resource Management and runs continuously from first team member onboarding through final handoff. The inputs are rich with human data: team performance assessments, work performance reports, lessons learned register, and the resource management plan. Key outputs are change requests, updates to the project management plan, and enterprise environmental factor updates — meaning that how you manage people feeds back into how the organization runs. The process sits at the intersection of leadership, conflict management, and stakeholder dynamics: a team member dispute is also a stakeholder issue; a performance problem may be a scope ambiguity issue in disguise. In regulated industries such as banking or capital markets under Bangladesh Bank or BSEC guidelines, managing team behavior also touches compliance and conduct risk, making the process even more consequential. The exam tests whether you act as a servant leader who coaches first, escalates last, and documents always. Approximately 9–13% of exam questions touch Resource Management topics, with Manage Team bleeding into communications, stakeholder, and risk domains heavily.

**In one sentence:** Manage Team is the continuous process of keeping human performance on track through coaching, conflict resolution, and feedback — always choosing the lowest-escalation intervention that works.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | PM actively monitors performance against the resource management plan; uses formal performance reviews, conflict resolution protocols, and HR escalation paths defined at planning |
| Agile | Scrum Master/coach removes impediments, facilitates retrospectives for self-diagnosis, relies on the team to surface and resolve interpersonal issues; escalation to PM is a last resort |
| Hybrid | PM maintains formal accountability structures for compliance and reporting while enabling team self-organization within sprints; performance feedback is continuous not annual |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Coach before you command.** Real-world instinct: a PM with authority issues a directive and expects compliance. PMI: use coaching, mentoring, and interpersonal skills first; directives are a last resort after collaborative problem-solving fails.
2. **Conflict is normal and often productive.** Real-world instinct: suppress conflict quickly to keep the project moving. PMI: conflict managed openly leads to better solutions; the PM facilitates resolution rather than imposing it.
3. **Formal escalation comes after you exhaust direct resolution.** Real-world instinct: go to HR or the sponsor when things get uncomfortable. PMI: attempt direct resolution first, document the attempt, escalate only when the conflict cannot be resolved at the team level.
4. **Rewards must be aligned with team culture and individual preferences.** Real-world instinct: give everyone the same recognition (public praise, bonus). PMI: tailor recognition to the individual; some cultures (e.g., Bangladesh collectivist norms) prefer team recognition over individual spotlight.
5. **Poor performance is a PM accountability, not just HR's.** Real-world instinct: hand a poor performer off to the functional manager or HR immediately. PMI: the PM owns root-cause analysis, coaching, re-baselining expectations, and only escalates after documented attempts to resolve.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** The PM must document performance issues accurately even when it is uncomfortable; falsifying or softening performance records violates PMI's Code.
- **Respect:** Conflict resolution must preserve dignity; any resolution that humiliates a team member — even a non-performer — violates the Respect value.
- **Fairness:** Assignments, recognition, and corrective action must be applied consistently across team members regardless of seniority, gender, or personal relationships.
- **Honesty:** Providing false or inflated performance feedback to protect a team member at the expense of project transparency is a Code violation regardless of intent.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Manage Team | Executing process: track performance, provide feedback, resolve issues, manage changes to optimize project performance | Do not confuse with Develop Team (which builds skills) — Manage Team acts on existing performance |
| Conflict Resolution Techniques (5) | Withdraw/Avoid, Smooth/Accommodate, Compromise/Reconcile, Force/Direct, Collaborate/Problem-Solve | PMI preference is Collaborate first; Force produces the least durable resolution |
| Collaborate/Problem-Solve | Win-win conflict technique: both parties address root cause together | Trap: forcing feels fast; PMI marks Collaborate correct unless stem says "time critical" |
| Smooth/Accommodate | Emphasize areas of agreement; de-emphasize differences temporarily | Not a permanent fix; use when preserving relationship is the immediate priority |
| Compromise/Reconcile | Both parties give something up; "lose-lose" in PMI framing | Trap: sounds reasonable; PMI prefers Collaborate unless time/power constraints make it unavoidable |
| Force/Direct | PM or authority figure imposes a solution; win-lose | Only defensible when safety, legal compliance, or imminent deadline demands immediate resolution |
| Emotional Intelligence (EI) | Self-awareness + self-regulation + empathy + motivation + social skill; PM uses EI to manage team dynamics | Trap: EI sounds "soft"; it is a testable leadership competency on the exam |
| Resource Management Plan | Guides how to acquire, develop, manage, and release team resources; baseline for Manage Team decisions | Trap: if you don't know the plan, you can't manage to it — always check the plan before acting |
| Team Performance Assessments | Evaluations of team effectiveness: skills, collaboration, results | Input to Manage Team; output of Develop Team — know the direction |
| Work Performance Reports | Processed performance data communicated to stakeholders; used to identify resource-related variance | Trap: work performance DATA is raw; reports are processed — Manage Team uses reports |
| Interpersonal and Team Skills | Leadership, influencing, negotiation, conflict management, EI, facilitation, motivation | Broad tool bucket for Manage Team; exam uses these interchangeably but each has a distinct scenario trigger |
| Recognition and Rewards | Formal/informal acknowledgement of desired behavior; calibrated to individual and cultural norms | Trap: one-size-fits-all recognition is NOT PMI-correct; always individualize |
| RACI Chart | Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed; clarifies roles and reduces conflict | Ambiguous RACI is a root cause of team conflict — update it proactively |
| Colocation / Virtual Teams | Colocation: same physical space; virtual: geographically dispersed; both affect communication and conflict management | Hybrid projects often have both; tailor approach accordingly |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Two team members in open conflict disrupting the team | Meet privately with each party separately, then together | Direct resolution preserves relationship; going to HR first is premature escalation |
| Team member consistently missing deadlines | Investigate root cause (skills gap? workload? unclear requirements?) | PMI demands root-cause analysis before corrective action; assuming laziness is a trap |
| Functional manager contradicts PM's task assignment | Refer to the resource management plan and RAM/RACI | Authority ambiguity is resolved by documented agreements, not by who speaks louder |
| High performer asks for a team reward, not individual recognition | Honor the team recognition preference | Cultural and individual reward preferences override PM's default assumption |
| Team morale drops after a project setback | Hold a retrospective or team meeting to surface issues collaboratively | Self-diagnosis by the team is the agile/hybrid PMI answer; unilateral motivational speech is not |
| PM learns a team member has been falsifying status reports | Address directly with the team member first, document, then escalate per governance | Ethics requires honest confrontation before formal action; ignoring it is a Code violation |
| Conflict between two senior technical experts over approach | Facilitate a problem-solving session; use objective data | Collaborate is PMI's preferred technique; choosing one expert's view without process is Force |
| Team member requests reassignment citing personal stress | Acknowledge, discuss workload and timeline options, involve HR if needed | EI and empathy first; dismissing wellbeing concerns is a Respect violation |

**Escalation edge cases:**

| Edge Case Trigger | PMI-Correct Behavior |
|---|---|
| Conflict involves potential policy or legal violation (e.g., Bangladesh Bank conduct rules, BSEC compliance) | Escalate immediately per governance — legal/compliance issues bypass direct resolution preference |
| PM has attempted direct resolution twice with no improvement | Document attempts, then escalate to functional manager or sponsor per the conflict resolution path in the project charter |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

```
CONFLICT / PERFORMANCE ISSUE ARISES
          |
          v
[1] Understand Root Cause (ask, listen, review data)
          |
          v
[2] Is it a legal / compliance / safety issue?
     YES --> Escalate immediately (skip steps 3-4)
     NO  --> Continue
          |
          v
[3] Try COLLABORATE / Problem-Solve with parties involved
          |
          v
[4] Resolved? YES --> Document + monitor
             NO  --> Try COMPROMISE as fallback
          |
          v
[5] Still unresolved? Document all attempts --> ESCALATE to functional mgr / sponsor
          |
          v
[6] Update lessons learned, resource management plan
```

**Mnemonic: U-C-R-E-D** — Understand, Collaborate, Resolve, Escalate, Document

- **U**nderstand: diagnose before acting
- **C**ollaborate: PMI's preferred conflict mode
- **R**esolve: close the loop and confirm resolution
- **E**scalate: only after collaborative attempts fail
- **D**ocument: always, regardless of outcome

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Performance reviews follow the cadence in the resource management plan (weekly, sprint-end, monthly)
- Conflict resolution procedures are formally documented in the project charter or team operating agreement
- Change requests to the project management plan required for significant resource reassignment
- Formal performance documentation feeds HR processes; PM maintains a paper trail

### Agile / Hybrid
- Team self-manages interpersonal issues through retrospectives; PM/SM facilitates not directs
- Conflict surfacing is a sprint ceremony outcome, not a surprise — psychological safety enables it
- Velocity drops are the agile proxy for performance degradation; diagnose in retrospective before acting
- Recognition happens at sprint demos and iteration reviews, not only at project close

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Bangladesh Bank / BSEC context:** Conduct risk and employee behavioral standards are regulated; a team member's compliance violation (e.g., unauthorized disclosure of market-sensitive data) requires immediate escalation regardless of PMI's "collaborate first" preference
- **Capital markets:** Project teams may include rotating secondees from compliance, legal, or operations; authority over these team members is dotted-line, making RACI clarity essential
- **Regulated industries:** Performance documentation may be subject to audit; inflated reviews create institutional risk beyond the project
- **Remote/virtual teams across time zones** (common in BSEC-regulated fintech projects): asynchronous conflict resolution protocols must be planned explicitly

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Escalate the conflict to HR immediately | Real-world: HR owns people problems; experienced PMs remove themselves from uncomfortable situations | PMI: escalation is a last resort; PM owns the first resolution attempt — going to HR first is abdicating responsibility |
| Remove the underperforming team member from the project | Real-world: fastest way to remove the problem and protect the schedule | PMI: removal without root-cause analysis and coaching attempts violates servant leadership; it is also a drastic change request requiring justification |
| Use Force/Direct to resolve a conflict quickly during a critical milestone | Real-world: speed matters near a deadline; just decide and move on | PMI: Force yields compliance without commitment and damages trust long-term; only use when safety or compliance demands immediate action |
| Publicly praise a top performer in front of the whole team | Real-world: public recognition boosts morale and sets an example | PMI: recognition must match individual and cultural preferences; public praise can embarrass introverts or create resentment in team-oriented cultures |
| Tell the team member their performance review is fine to keep them motivated | Real-world: short-term kindness to preserve motivation | PMI: dishonest feedback violates Honesty and Responsibility; the PM must provide accurate assessments even when they are difficult |
| Let the team resolve the conflict themselves without any PM involvement | Real-world: agile teams are self-organizing; trust the team | PMI: self-organization applies to work; the PM is responsible for ensuring conflicts that threaten project objectives are resolved — complete hands-off is not servant leadership |
| Issue a written warning immediately after one missed deadline | Real-world: sets a firm precedent; protects PM legally | PMI: formal warnings precede root-cause investigation here; the process requires understanding before corrective action |
| Have the sponsor intervene in the team conflict | Real-world: sponsor has authority and can cut through politics | PMI: sponsor involvement in team-level conflict is premature escalation; it also undermines PM authority and team trust |

## 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "Two team members are in conflict" | Direct resolution first; meet separately then together; Collaborate technique |
| "Team member's performance is declining" | Root-cause analysis first (skills, workload, clarity?); coach before escalate |
| "Team member is not meeting commitments" | Investigate privately, provide honest feedback, document — do NOT escalate on first occurrence |
| "Team morale is low after a setback" | Facilitate team discussion / retrospective; do NOT issue directives unilaterally |
| "Functional manager overrides your assignment" | Reference resource management plan / RACI; resolve through documented authority |
| "Senior team member disagrees with project decision" | Facilitate problem-solve session using objective data; do not force or avoid |
| "Team member asks to be removed from the project" | Explore root cause first (workload, conflict, personal?); consider alternatives before granting |
| "You notice a team member is falsifying reports" | Confront directly and privately first; document; escalate per governance — this is an ethics violation |
| "FIRST thing the PM should do" | Always check plan/documentation, understand root cause, then act |
| "BEST leadership style for a high-performing self-directed team" | Delegating / servant leadership; coaching and directing are for less experienced teams |
| "Recognition and reward" | Individualize; ask what motivates this person/culture before defaulting to public praise |
| "Conflict during a critical sprint" | Still try Collaborate unless there is a compliance/safety trigger forcing immediate escalation |
| "Performance issue persists after coaching" | Document all attempts, then escalate to functional manager per governance |
| "Team member violates compliance policy" | Escalate immediately — compliance/legal overrides the "direct resolution first" preference |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are managing a regulatory technology project for a Bangladesh Bank-supervised bank. Your highest-performing developer has been completing work at exceptional speed, but you recently discover he has been copying code from a licensed vendor library without attribution, which violates the project's open-source compliance policy. He has single-handedly kept the project on schedule. Raising the issue formally will likely delay the project by three weeks and expose the bank to potential licensing penalties. Your project sponsor informally suggests you "handle it quietly" without creating a formal record. What should you do?

A. Honor the sponsor's request; document only that "code quality issues were addressed" in the lessons learned to avoid the formal process  
B. Meet privately with the developer, explain the compliance violation, require remediation, and create an accurate record of the issue and corrective action  
C. Remove the developer from the project immediately and report the violation to HR to protect yourself from liability  
D. Do nothing until the project closes; then address the licensing issue during post-project audit to avoid disrupting the schedule

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

Velocity has dropped 15% over two sprints. The product owner attributes the drop to unclear backlog refinement. The scrum master reports that team discussions are becoming increasingly one-sided, dominated by three vocal senior members. As project manager, what is the BEST next action?

B. Work with the scrum master to introduce structured retrospective techniques (e.g., silent writing, round-robin) that give all voices equal access  
C. Escalate to the product owner to prioritize backlog clarity before addressing team dynamics

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stewardship domain and PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility values. Option B is the only answer that satisfies all four Code values: it confronts the issue honestly, creates an accurate record (Responsibility), protects the institution from compounding risk (Stewardship), and does not humiliate the developer (Respect). Option A violates Honesty — documenting a sanitized version of events is falsification by omission. Option C is disproportionate as a first action; immediate removal without a coaching conversation skips due process and violates Fairness. Option D is willful concealment — ignoring a known violation is a Code of Ethics breach regardless of project pressure.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor pressure to "handle it quietly" is a trap; the PM's accountability to the PMI Code supersedes the sponsor's informal preference when ethics are at stake.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide Chapter 4 (Servant Leadership) and PMBOK 7 Team performance domain. The root cause is psychological safety and facilitation failure, not just backlog clarity. Option B directly addresses the facilitation problem by restructuring how voices are heard — this is a servant leader intervention through a process fix. Option A (time limits) is a directive command, not facilitation — it addresses symptom not system. Option C defers to the product owner on a team dynamics issue — the PM/SM owns team health. Option D (one-on-ones without structural change) gathers data but misses the system fix.

**Mindset Lesson:** Velocity problems in agile are symptoms — always trace back to team dynamics and psychological safety before adjusting backlog processes.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team domain and PMBOK 6 Resource Management — Interpersonal and Team Skills (conflict management). Direct resolution with both parties is the correct first step regardless of functional manager pressure. Option A (remove contractor) is escalation/removal before investigation — premature and potentially unfair. Option C defers to the functional manager — the PM owns conflict resolution first. Option D defers entirely to avoid timeline disruption — this allows the conflict to fester and violates PM responsibility.

**Mindset Lesson:** When authority figures demand quick action on conflict, the PMI reflex is still to understand before acting — even with a deadline five days away.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team performance domain; Manage Team tools — Interpersonal and Team Skills. Both B and C have merit: consulting the resource management plan is correct procedurally, but a direct conversation with the team member is the BEST first action because it combines data gathering and empathy in one step. Option A (risk register update) treats the person as a risk object without human engagement — PMI rejects this as first action. Option D (notify functional manager) is premature escalation on the first occurrence of decline.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a human performance issue arises, talk to the human first — documentation and escalation follow, they do not precede.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team domain and Agile Practice Guide retrospective-as-improvement-mechanism. The correct sequence is: structured problem-solve first (because informal retrospective discussion has already failed twice), document outcome, update working agreement (to formalize the resolution), escalate only if conflict recurs. Option A escalates to sponsor immediately — premature. Option B removes the tech lead's duties without a problem-solve process — punitive and disproportionate. Option D abandons PM responsibility — self-organization does not mean PM inaction when retrospectives have repeatedly failed.

**Mindset Lesson:** When two informal attempts fail, the PM upgrades the intervention to structured facilitation — not escalation.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 Resource Management — Manage Team process (Section 9.4). Resource leveling is a Schedule Management tool used in Develop Schedule and Control Schedule — it is not a Manage Team tool. Conflict management, emotional intelligence, and interpersonal/team skills are all explicitly listed in PMBOK 6 Section 9.4.2 as tools and techniques of Manage Team.

**Mindset Lesson:** EXCEPT questions on process tools require knowing which Knowledge Area owns each technique — resource leveling belongs to schedule, not team management.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty (provide accurate information) and Fairness (make decisions without favoritism or prejudice). Option B is the only answer that applies accurate, objective evaluation to both individuals irrespective of personal relationships or personality preferences. Option A inflates the moderate performer's rating — this is dishonest regardless of kind intent. Option C (consult sponsor) is avoiding a clear duty — the PM owns performance evaluations. Option D penalizes excellent performance because of personality — this is bias and a Fairness violation.

**Mindset Lesson:** Performance evaluations must reflect actual results, not relationships or personalities — PMI's Honesty and Fairness values have no exception for "close colleagues."

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder domain and PMBOK 6 Communications Management. The root cause is a gap in the communications management plan — no protocol existed for what team issues trigger stakeholder notification. Option B fixes the root cause structurally. Option A (copy all stakeholders on all internal communication) is over-communication — it floods stakeholders and violates the principle of tailored communication. Option C (attend all meetings) is operationally impractical and creates a surveillance dynamic that damages team trust. Option D (performance issue escalation) treats a process gap as an individual failure — premature and misdirected as a first action.

**Mindset Lesson:** When stakeholders feel "out of the loop," fix the communications plan — not the person who didn't communicate spontaneously around a missing protocol.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team domain — escalation is appropriate after documented direct resolution attempts fail. Simultaneous rebaselining of sprint scope protects the regulatory deadline without abandoning the conflict. Option A (wait until after deadline) allows a known conflict to continue damaging the team — PM responsibility requires acting now. Option C (remove one team member) is a drastic action without authority confirmation and may violate staffing agreements — it skips escalation. Option D (sponsor intervenes in team meeting) is inappropriate escalation level and public confrontation damages trust.

**Mindset Lesson:** Escalation after two documented failed attempts is not giving up — it is the correct procedural step; it must be paired with scope/schedule protection to be complete.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, D**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty, Responsibility; PMBOK 7 Stewardship domain. A: private confrontation first — due process and respect. C: accurate documentation is always required for an ethics issue. D: escalation per governance after the private conversation is the correct sequence. Option B (notify client immediately) is premature — the PM does not go external before completing internal process. Option E (quietly correct timesheets) is falsification and a Code violation. Option F (lessons learned) is a good practice but not among the three required actions — it is downstream from the primary response.

**Mindset Lesson:** Ethics violations require private confrontation first, accurate documentation always, and governance escalation after — never silence, never unilateral correction, never premature external disclosure.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Manage Team = track performance + provide feedback + resolve issues + manage changes in Executing
- Tools: conflict management, EI, interpersonal/team skills, recognition/rewards — NOT resource leveling (that's schedule)
- Conflict preference order: Collaborate → Compromise → Smooth → Force; Withdraw is never a permanent solution
- Ethics violations and compliance issues (Bangladesh Bank, BSEC) override "direct resolution first" — escalate immediately
- Root-cause analysis before corrective action — always ask "why" before "what now"
- Recognition must be tailored to individual and cultural preferences — public praise is not universally positive
- Performance evaluations must be accurate — inflating reviews for relationships or reducing for personality is a Code violation
- Escalate only after two documented direct resolution attempts fail; document every attempt
- Agile teams self-organize work; PM still owns conflict resolution when retrospectives repeatedly fail
- Communications gaps that leave stakeholders uninformed are fixed in the communications management plan, not by blaming individuals

**When a team conflict arises, your first move is always direct, private, collaborative resolution — escalation is the last step, not the first reflex.**
