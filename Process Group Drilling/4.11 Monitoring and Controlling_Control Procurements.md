# PMP Exam Master Brief: Control Procurements

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Control Procurements is the process of managing procurement relationships, monitoring contract performance, making changes and corrections as appropriate, and closing out contracts when complete. It sits in the Procurement Management knowledge area within the Monitoring and Controlling process group and is one of the most legally and commercially complex processes in the PMBOK framework. On the PMP exam, Procurement Management carries approximately 9% of questions, and Control Procurements bleeds into Integrated Change Control (all contract modifications flow through the change control system), Risk Management (contract types shift financial risk between buyer and seller), and Stakeholder Management (vendors are key stakeholders with competing interests). The buyer's PM must track deliverables, review invoices, manage claims, conduct inspections and audits, and ensure the seller is performing against the agreed statement of work. Claims administration — the process of documenting, monitoring, and resolving disputed changes or constructive changes — is a distinctly exam-heavy subtopic that most practitioners underestimate in formal rigor. Contract change control is separate from but integrated with project change control; no contract modification is valid without following both systems. Procurement performance reviews formally assess whether the seller is on track, and their findings feed directly into whether the contract continues, is modified, or is terminated for default or convenience. The process outputs include work performance information, change requests, and updates to the procurement documentation, which form the evidentiary record for any future claims or litigation. **In one sentence:** Control Procurements is the disciplined, legally grounded oversight of vendor performance and contract compliance — ensuring that every change, claim, and corrective action is documented and processed through both the contract and the project change control systems.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal contract performance reviews on a defined schedule; payment tied to verified deliverable acceptance; all changes processed through contract change control and project CCB; claims documented and resolved through formal dispute resolution mechanisms |
| Agile | Contracts structured as time-and-material or outcome-based; frequent delivery checkpoints replace formal milestone reviews; collaborative vendor relationship emphasized; change is expected and accommodated through adaptive contract structures |
| Hybrid | Fixed-price contract for clearly defined deliverables; T&M or fixed-fee-per-sprint for agile workstreams; formal contract change control at program level; vendor performance reviewed at sprint demos and formal quarterly reviews |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. All contract changes — including verbal directions given to the seller — must be documented and processed through the contract change control system before they are binding; real-world PMs give verbal direction constantly and expect compliance without paperwork.
2. Claims (disputed changes or constructive changes) must be documented, submitted, and resolved through the contract's dispute resolution process — not managed through relationship negotiation alone, which is the instinct of most experienced PMs.
3. Payment is authorized only upon verified acceptance of deliverables against the contract's acceptance criteria — not upon seller invoice submission; practitioners often approve payment when the seller requests it rather than when acceptance is confirmed.
4. Terminating a contract for default requires documented evidence of seller non-performance against specific contract requirements — not just PM dissatisfaction; jumping to termination without a paper trail exposes the buyer to counter-claims.
5. The buyer is responsible for maintaining the complete procurement file — all correspondence, invoices, change orders, and inspection reports — as the evidentiary foundation for any future claims or audits; most PMs treat procurement documentation as the seller's responsibility.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Responsibility:** Enforcing contract terms consistently and without favoritism; not allowing relationship comfort with a vendor to override documented non-performance.
- **Honesty:** Accurately documenting all claims, including constructive changes where the buyer's own direction caused scope expansion, even when this is uncomfortable to disclose.
- **Fairness:** Applying acceptance criteria consistently across all deliverables and all vendors; not selectively waiving standards based on vendor relationship history.
- **Respect:** Engaging the seller in dispute resolution through agreed processes before escalating to litigation or termination; maintaining professionalism in all contract correspondence.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Claims Administration | The process of documenting, monitoring, and resolving contested changes where buyer and seller disagree on whether a change is within scope or who bears the cost | Treating claims as informal disagreements to be managed through conversation; PMI treats claims as formal legal matters requiring documentation and defined resolution processes |
| Constructive Change | A change in the seller's scope of work caused by buyer direction, behavior, or inaction that was not formally documented as a contract change order | Assuming constructive changes are only the seller's problem; buyers who give undocumented verbal direction can trigger legitimate claims against themselves |
| Contract Change Control System | The formal system for managing changes to a contract, separate from but integrated with the project's overall change control system | Confusing contract change control with project change control; both must be followed — a project-approved change that is not also processed through contract change control is not binding on the seller |
| Procurement Performance Review | A structured review of seller progress against the contract's cost, schedule, scope, and quality requirements; informs decisions about continuing, modifying, or terminating the contract | Treating procurement performance review as equivalent to a vendor check-in meeting; it is a formal assessment with documented findings |
| Inspections and Audits | Structured reviews of seller work products, processes, and facilities to verify compliance with contract requirements; buyer has the right to conduct these per most contracts | Assuming inspections are optional or dependent on seller invitation; most contracts grant the buyer inspection rights at any reasonable time |
| Work Performance Information | Analyzed data about contract performance — deliverable status, acceptance decisions, invoice status, claim status — produced as an output of Control Procurements | Confusing work performance data (raw inputs) with work performance information (analyzed outputs ready for reporting and decision-making) |
| Procurement Documentation (updated) | The complete set of contract records including original contract, amendments, correspondence, inspection reports, invoices, and claims records; serves as the evidentiary file | Treating procurement documentation as an administrative afterthought; it is the legal record for any future audit, claim, or dispute |
| Payment Systems | The mechanism by which seller invoices are reviewed, matched to accepted deliverables, and approved for payment; typically integrated with accounts payable | Approving payment upon invoice receipt rather than upon verified deliverable acceptance; payment must follow acceptance, not precede it |
| Termination for Default | Contract termination triggered by seller failure to perform according to contract requirements; requires documented evidence of specific non-performance | Initiating termination for default without a documented performance record; undocumented termination exposes the buyer to breach-of-contract counter-claims |
| Termination for Convenience | Contract termination at the buyer's discretion, typically with payment for work completed and reasonable seller costs; does not require seller fault | Confusing termination for default (seller's fault, evidence required) with termination for convenience (buyer's choice, payment obligations apply) |
| Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) | Formal mechanisms for resolving contract disputes short of litigation, including negotiation, mediation, and arbitration; sequence is typically defined in the contract | Skipping ADR and moving directly to litigation or termination; the contract's dispute resolution clause defines the required sequence |
| Earned Value (for procurement) | EV metrics (CPI, SPI) applied to seller performance data to assess whether the seller is delivering value for money expended | Applying EVM only to internal project performance; seller contracts with cost components should also be monitored via EVM |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Seller requests payment for work the PM has not formally accepted | Verify deliverable acceptance against contract acceptance criteria before approving payment | Payment follows acceptance; approving payment before acceptance acceptance waives the buyer's leverage and violates contract terms |
| Seller claims an oral instruction from the PM constituted a change order | Document the claim formally, research the instruction history, and process through contract dispute resolution | Constructive change claims are legitimate legal matters — dismissing them informally creates litigation risk |
| PM identifies seller performance falling below contract requirements | Issue a formal written notice of non-performance and allow the seller the cure period specified in the contract | Termination for default requires documented notice and a cure period; jumping to termination without this process exposes the buyer |
| A change to the project scope will affect the seller's statement of work | Submit a change request through project CCB AND initiate contract change control simultaneously | Both systems must be engaged; a project-approved change that bypasses contract change control is not binding on the seller |
| Seller submits a claim for additional compensation for buyer-caused delays | Acknowledge receipt formally, document the claim, and process through the contract's claims resolution process | Claims must be formally acknowledged and processed — ignoring or verbally denying a claim does not make it go away legally |
| Procurement performance review reveals seller is consistently behind schedule | Issue a corrective action request formally, document findings, and monitor resolution with defined timeframes | Verbal coaching of a non-performing seller without documentation provides no basis for subsequent termination for default |
| Contract is complete and final deliverables have been accepted | Close out the contract formally: verify all deliverables accepted, resolve open claims, complete final payment, archive procurement file | Informal contract completion without documented closure leaves legal exposure for future claims and audit findings |
| PM wants to audit the seller's processes and facilities | Exercise audit rights per the contract terms; notify the seller with reasonable advance notice | Most contracts grant audit rights; PMs should not wait for seller problems to exercise them — proactive audits are a Monitor Risks tool |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Seller and buyer cannot resolve a disputed change after direct negotiation | Invoke the next step in the contract's ADR process (typically mediation or arbitration) | The contract defines the escalation sequence; skipping to litigation or termination before exhausting ADR violates the contract dispute resolution clause |
| A seller's subcontractor is causing delays but the PM has no contract with the subcontractor | Address the issue with the prime seller — the prime is contractually responsible for all subcontractor performance | Privity of contract means the buyer's legal relationship is with the prime seller only; contacting subcontractors directly bypasses the contractual chain |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Fixed-price contracts require rigorous acceptance criteria management; payment milestones must be tied to specific, measurable deliverable acceptance events.
- Claims administration process defined in the contract should be rehearsed with the project legal/procurement team at contract kick-off, not discovered at the moment of a dispute.
- Procurement file management is critical — all correspondence, directions, meeting minutes where scope is discussed, and inspection reports must be retained as potential legal evidence.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Time-and-material contracts require more frequent verification of actual hours and rates; risk of cost overrun is borne by the buyer.
- Outcome-based or fixed-price-per-sprint contracts shift more risk to the seller but require extremely precise acceptance criteria per sprint.
- In hybrid environments, the formal contract change control system must be clearly distinguished from the team's agile change process — changes that affect contract deliverables must flow through both.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC / Bangladesh Bank context:** Technology vendor contracts in Bangladeshi banking must comply with Bangladesh Bank ICT Security Guidelines, which require specific audit rights clauses, data residency provisions, and vendor risk assessment documentation. BSEC-listed companies procuring technology solutions must disclose material contracts. Procurement files may be subpoenaed in regulatory investigations — completeness of procurement documentation is a regulatory compliance matter, not just a project management practice. Claims against government-linked counterparties may require specific ADR processes defined by public procurement regulations (Public Procurement Rules 2008 in Bangladesh).
- For construction: retention clauses, performance bonds, and liquidated damages provisions are standard contract mechanisms that directly govern how non-performance is financially managed; the PM must understand these to exercise Control Procurements effectively.
- For pharmaceutical: Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) audit rights over contract manufacturers are regulatory requirements, not optional procurement tools.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Approve the seller's invoice because the seller says the work is done | Experienced PMs trust long-standing vendor relationships and avoid payment delays that sour the relationship | Payment must follow formal deliverable acceptance against contract criteria — approving payment before acceptance waives the buyer's most important leverage |
| Verbally tell the seller to stop work due to poor performance | Verbal stop-work orders feel fast and decisive in a crisis | Stop-work orders must be issued in writing per the contract; verbal orders create ambiguity about the formal start of any cure period and expose the buyer legally |
| Contact the seller's subcontractor directly to resolve a performance issue | The subcontractor is causing the actual problem; going direct seems efficient | Privity of contract means the buyer's only contractual relationship is with the prime seller; direct contact with subcontractors bypasses the contract chain and undermines the prime's accountability |
| Negotiate a disputed change informally and move on | Negotiation feels collaborative and preserves the relationship | Informal resolution of claims without documentation leaves no enforceable record; the claim can resurface at contract close or in litigation |
| Skip the inspection because the seller has a strong track record | Experience with the seller builds trust; inspections feel redundant and slow | Contract audit and inspection rights exist regardless of relationship history; waiving them informally may legally weaken the buyer's ability to reject defective work later |
| Issue a termination for default letter immediately when the seller misses a milestone | Immediate consequences signal seriousness and protect the schedule | Termination for default requires documented notice, a defined cure period per the contract, and evidence of non-performance; premature termination exposes the buyer to breach-of-contract liability |
| Handle a constructive change claim by asking the seller to drop it as a relationship gesture | The relationship is good and the claim seems opportunistic | Constructive change claims are legally legitimate; asking a seller to waive a valid claim without compensation is unethical and potentially legally problematic |
| Close the contract verbally after the final deliverable is received | Final delivery feels like the natural end; formal closure seems like paperwork overhead | Contract closure must be formal and documented: verified acceptance of all deliverables, resolution of all claims, completion of final payment, and archiving of the procurement file |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1**

You are the PM for a core banking platform implementation project at a commercial bank in Bangladesh. Your fixed-price software vendor has submitted a formal invoice claiming an additional BDT 4.2 million for development work they assert was directed by your technical lead in a series of email exchanges over three months. You have no signed change orders for this work. The contract has a clear clause stating that no change is binding without a signed change order. The vendor's legal team has sent a formal letter asserting a constructive change claim. The sponsor wants to reject the claim outright since no change order was signed. What should you FIRST do?

A. Reject the claim in writing because no signed change order exists, which is an absolute contract requirement.
B. Review all email correspondence with the technical lead, assess whether a constructive change actually occurred, document the findings, and engage the contract's dispute resolution process.
C. Terminate the contract for default because the vendor is attempting to claim payment for unauthorized work.
D. Pay the invoice to preserve the vendor relationship and avoid litigation risk.

**Q2**

Your agile development team is working with a vendor under a time-and-material contract to build a regulatory reporting dashboard. The vendor's team attends sprint reviews and demos completed features. At the end of sprint 4, the product owner verbally accepts the sprint deliverables during the review meeting. The vendor submits an invoice citing the verbal acceptance. The formal contract requires written acceptance for each sprint deliverable before payment is authorized. What is the BEST action?

A. Approve the invoice because the product owner's verbal acceptance at the sprint review constitutes formal acceptance for agile projects.
B. Decline the invoice and issue the formal written acceptance document before approving payment, per the contract's acceptance requirements.
C. Escalate to the sponsor to decide whether verbal agile acceptance overrides the written contract requirement.
D. Ask the vendor to resubmit the invoice after the next sprint since the sprint 4 deliverables are still under review.

**Q3**

You are managing a hybrid project procuring a trading system upgrade from a vendor under a fixed-price contract. Three months into delivery, the vendor notifies you that a change in Bangladesh Bank's IT security guidelines — issued after contract signing — requires additional security controls that were not in the original statement of work. The vendor requests a formal change order for additional cost and time. Your legal team says the contract contains a "changes in law" clause that obliges the buyer to compensate the seller for regulatory changes imposed after contract execution. The sponsor wants to deny the request to protect the project budget. What should you FIRST do?

A. Deny the vendor's request because the budget impact is the sponsor's priority.
B. Review the "changes in law" clause with the legal team, confirm the regulatory change qualifies, and if so, process a contract change order for the additional scope and cost.
C. Ask the vendor to absorb the cost as a relationship gesture since both parties benefit from regulatory compliance.
D. Escalate to the CCB to approve the project change without engaging the contract change control process.

**Q4**

During a procurement performance review, you discover that the seller has delivered three of five contracted deliverables on time and within spec, but the remaining two are four weeks late with no recovery plan provided. The contract specifies a 30-day cure period after formal written notice before a termination for default can be initiated. Two statements about the BEST immediate action are plausible. Which answer is BEST?

A. Issue a formal written notice of non-performance immediately, triggering the 30-day cure period, and document all findings from the procurement performance review.
B. Terminate for default immediately because four weeks of delay on 40% of deliverables constitutes material breach.
C. Schedule a meeting with the seller to discuss the delay informally and give them two weeks to provide a recovery plan before formal notice.
D. Issue a change request to extend the contract schedule by four weeks to accommodate the seller's delay.

**Q5**

An agile squad is procuring data engineering services from a vendor under a fixed-fee-per-sprint contract. The vendor's team has been attending standups and contributing code. At retrospective, the squad identifies that the vendor's code quality is consistently below the agreed Definition of Done. The product owner suggests simply adding a "vendor quality" story to the next sprint backlog as a corrective action. What is the BEST approach for the PM?

A. Add the vendor quality story to the next sprint as the product owner suggests, since agile teams self-correct iteratively.
B. Issue a formal written performance notice to the vendor citing the specific Definition of Done criteria that have not been met, and initiate the contract's corrective action process.
C. Terminate the vendor contract for default because consistent quality failures across multiple sprints constitute material non-performance.
D. Escalate to the vendor's account manager informally to request better resources for the next sprint.

**Q6**

Which of the following is NOT an output of the Control Procurements process?

A. Closed procurements
B. Work performance information
C. Procurement statement of work
D. Change requests

**Q7**

You are the PM for a government infrastructure project. During contract execution, you discover that a junior member of your team has been accepting seller deliverables informally by email without conducting the required formal inspections, and has been verbally approving payments to the vendor. Three payment cycles have been completed this way. The project is now under a government audit. What should you FIRST do?

A. Correct the acceptance records retroactively to reflect formal inspection findings, then brief the auditor on the corrected records.
B. Disclose the process failures to the auditor proactively, retroactively conduct inspections on the accepted deliverables to the extent possible, and implement corrective controls immediately.
C. Brief the sponsor privately and let the sponsor decide whether to disclose to the auditor.
D. Coach the team member privately and submit a corrected process improvement plan to the auditor without disclosing the specific payment irregularities.

**Q8**

You are managing a hybrid procurement for a capital markets data platform. The seller has submitted a claim alleging that a three-week delay in the buyer's delivery of test environment credentials — which the buyer was contractually obligated to provide — caused the seller to miss a contractual milestone. Your sponsor argues that the seller should have raised the issue sooner and therefore the claim is invalid. The contract's dispute resolution clause requires negotiation, then mediation, then arbitration in sequence. What is the BEST action?

A. Reject the claim because the seller failed to raise the issue proactively and therefore waived their right to claim.
B. Engage the seller in formal negotiation as the first step of the contract's ADR process, assess the merits of the claim against the documented timeline of credential delivery, and attempt to reach a negotiated resolution.
C. Escalate immediately to arbitration to resolve the dispute quickly and protect the project schedule.
D. Issue a unilateral change order reducing the contract value to offset the seller's alleged delay.

**Q9**

A hybrid project is procuring software development from two separate vendors under separate contracts. Vendor A's deliverable is a dependency for Vendor B's workstream. Vendor A has fallen four weeks behind, causing Vendor B to report idle resources and requesting compensation for standby costs under a force majeure clause in their contract. Your sponsor wants you to deny Vendor B's claim and direct Vendor A to accelerate. What should the PM FIRST do?

A. Deny Vendor B's claim because Vendor A's delay is not a force majeure event under standard contract interpretation.
B. Review both contracts to assess whether Vendor B's claim has contractual merit, document the dependency failure, issue a corrective action notice to Vendor A, and engage Vendor B's claim through the contract's claims administration process.
C. Direct Vendor A to accelerate verbally and ask Vendor B to absorb the standby cost as a relationship gesture.
D. Terminate Vendor A's contract for default and replace them to eliminate the dependency risk immediately.

**Q10**

A procurement performance review of a major IT infrastructure vendor reveals three concurrent issues. Select ALL actions the PM should take. (Select 3)

A. Issue a formal written notice of non-performance for the schedule delay, specifying the cure period per the contract.
B. Conduct an unannounced inspection of the vendor's delivery facility to assess root cause of quality failures.
C. Approve an informal verbal extension of the delivery milestone to give the vendor time to recover.
D. Document the claim for additional costs submitted by the vendor and route it through the contract's claims administration process.
E. Terminate the contract for convenience immediately to protect the project from further delays.
F. Update the procurement documentation with findings from the performance review, change requests, and all formal correspondence.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Claims administration requires that the PM assess the merits of a constructive change claim even when no signed change order exists — the absence of a change order does not automatically invalidate a constructive change (PMBOK 12.3, Claims Administration). Option A (outright rejection) ignores the legal doctrine of constructive change and creates significant litigation exposure. Option C (termination for default) is wholly disproportionate — the vendor is asserting a payment claim, not failing to perform. Option D (paying immediately) is capitulation without assessment and may be incorrect if no constructive change actually occurred.
**Mindset Lesson:** Claims must be assessed on merits through the formal process — neither reflexive rejection nor reflexive payment is correct; the process determines the outcome.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Contract terms govern payment authorization; a written contract requirement for written acceptance is not overridden by agile practice norms (PMBOK 12.3, Payment Systems). Option A incorrectly treats agile verbal acceptance as sufficient when the contract explicitly requires written acceptance. Option C inappropriately escalates a clear contract compliance matter to the sponsor. Option D invents an entirely new reason to delay payment that has no basis in the scenario.
**Mindset Lesson:** Agile practices do not override contract terms — if the contract requires written acceptance, the PM must provide it regardless of how the team operates.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** "Changes in law" clauses are binding contractual provisions; if the clause applies, the buyer is obligated to process a change order (PMBOK Contract Change Control System). Option A (deny) exposes the buyer to breach-of-contract liability if the clause applies. Option C (absorb as relationship gesture) is unethical — the seller has a contractual right that should not be waived through social pressure. Option D (CCB only) misses the contract change control system, which must also be engaged for changes affecting the seller's obligations.
**Mindset Lesson:** Contract clauses are binding on both parties — the PM's job is to apply them correctly, not to advocate for the buyer's financial preference over clear legal obligation.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Termination for default requires documented notice and the contractual cure period — issuing formal written notice is the prerequisite step (PMBOK 12.3, Contract Termination). Option B (immediate termination) skips the required cure period and exposes the buyer to breach-of-contract liability. Option C (informal discussion first) delays the formal process and weakens the documented performance record needed for any subsequent termination. Option D (extend the schedule) rewards non-performance without a recovery plan and eliminates the seller's incentive to perform.
**Mindset Lesson:** Formal written notice triggers the cure period — this step cannot be skipped or informalized regardless of the relationship or the urgency.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Consistent quality failures against documented acceptance criteria (Definition of Done) require formal written notice through the contract's performance management process (PMBOK 12.3, Procurement Performance Reviews). Option A (sprint backlog story) treats a vendor contract compliance issue as an internal agile team matter — the contract governs the relationship, not the team's backlog practices. Option C (termination for default) is premature without formal notice and a cure period. Option D (informal escalation to account manager) bypasses the contract's formal corrective action mechanism.
**Mindset Lesson:** Vendor quality failures against contract criteria are contract compliance matters requiring formal process — they cannot be resolved through the team's agile practices alone.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** The Procurement Statement of Work is an input to (and created during) Plan Procurement Management and is part of the procurement documents used as input to Conduct Procurements — it is not an output of Control Procurements. Closed procurements (A), work performance information (B), and change requests (D) are all explicit outputs of Control Procurements per PMBOK.
**Mindset Lesson:** The SOW is created during planning — by the time Control Procurements runs, the SOW is an input artifact, not something being produced.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility require proactive disclosure to the auditor; retroactively correcting records without disclosure constitutes concealment (Code of Ethics, Honesty). Option A (correcting records then briefing auditor on corrected records) misrepresents the audit situation to the auditor. Option C (briefing sponsor privately) delegates an ethics decision inappropriately. Option D (concealing payment irregularities) is a direct ethics violation — process improvement plan without disclosure does not satisfy the Honesty standard.
**Mindset Lesson:** When process failures are discovered during an active audit, proactive disclosure is the only PMI-compliant path — correction without disclosure is concealment.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** The contract's ADR process defines the required sequence — negotiation must precede mediation and arbitration (PMBOK 12.3, Claims Administration; dispute resolution clause). Option A (reject the claim) incorrectly treats the seller's failure to raise the issue earlier as a waiver; whether delay in notification affects the claim is a legal question to be assessed in ADR, not a unilateral buyer decision. Option C (skip to arbitration) violates the contract's required ADR sequence. Option D (unilateral change order) is not within the PM's authority and constitutes an inappropriate response to a disputed claim.
**Mindset Lesson:** The contract's ADR sequence is mandatory — buyers cannot skip steps because they believe the claim is weak; that assessment is made within the process.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Both claims must be assessed on their contractual merits; Vendor A's corrective action and Vendor B's claims administration are parallel processes (PMBOK 12.3). Option A (deny Vendor B's claim outright) is premature without reviewing the contract and the documentary record. Option C (verbal acceleration direction and asking for relationship gesture) bypasses both contracts' formal change and claims processes. Option D (terminate Vendor A immediately) skips the required notice and cure period and may not be contractually available at this stage.
**Mindset Lesson:** Multi-vendor dependency failures require parallel formal action on each contract — relationship management instincts do not substitute for contractual process.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, D, F
**PMI Justification:** A — formal written notice of non-performance is required to document the schedule issue and trigger the cure period. D — the vendor's cost claim must be formally documented and routed through claims administration, regardless of its apparent merit. F — updating procurement documentation with all findings, change requests, and correspondence is a core Control Procurements output obligation. B (unannounced inspection) is not standard — most contracts require reasonable advance notice for inspections, making this answer problematic. C (verbal extension) is wrong — all contract changes must be formal and written. E (immediate termination for convenience) is disproportionate and premature when formal corrective processes have not been exhausted.
**Mindset Lesson:** Control Procurements always generates documentation — notice of non-performance, claims routing, and procurement file updates are non-negotiable parallel actions.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Control Procurements monitors seller performance against contract terms — not just project performance against the project plan.
- All contract changes, including verbal directions to the seller, must be processed through the contract change control system to be binding.
- Claims administration treats disputed changes as formal legal matters requiring documentation and defined ADR resolution — not informal negotiation.
- Constructive changes occur when buyer behavior changes the seller's scope without a signed change order; the seller has a legitimate claim.
- Payment is authorized only upon formal acceptance of deliverables against the contract's acceptance criteria — never upon invoice submission alone.
- Termination for default requires documented non-performance, formal written notice, and the contractual cure period — premature termination creates breach-of-contract exposure.
- Privity of contract means the buyer's only contractual relationship is with the prime seller; subcontractor issues are addressed through the prime.
- ADR sequence (negotiate, mediate, arbitrate) is defined in the contract and must be followed in order before litigation.
- The procurement file — all correspondence, change orders, inspection reports, invoices, and claims records — is the legal evidentiary foundation for audits and disputes.
- **When in doubt about a contract issue: document it formally, process it through the contract's defined mechanism, and never resolve it informally.**
