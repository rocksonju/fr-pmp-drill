# PMP Exam Master Brief: Control Schedule

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Control Schedule is the process of monitoring the status of the project to update the project schedule and managing changes to the schedule baseline. It sits in the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group under Schedule Management, and its core function is to identify schedule variances, understand their root causes, and take corrective or preventive action through formal change control. The process uses Earned Value Management (EVM) metrics — Schedule Variance (SV) and Schedule Performance Index (SPI) — to quantify schedule health, and applies schedule compression techniques (crashing and fast-tracking) as recovery tools when approved through a change request. Key inputs include work performance data, the schedule baseline, the project schedule, and the schedule management plan; key outputs are work performance information (schedule-specific), change requests, and schedule forecasts. Cross-domain bleed is extensive: Control Schedule questions mix with Cost Management (EVM), Risk Management (schedule risk responses), Resource Management (resource leveling and crashing), and Integrated Change Control. The exam weights Schedule Management at approximately 10–12% of questions, with significant agile emphasis on velocity-based forecasting and iteration burndown. Agile schedule control manifests through velocity tracking, burndown/burnup charts, and sprint goal completion rates rather than EVM.

**In one sentence:** Control Schedule enforces disciplined management of schedule deviations by measuring variance with EVM, applying compression techniques through change control, and producing actionable forecasts that drive transparent stakeholder decisions.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | EVM metrics (SV, SPI) quantify schedule health against the baseline; crashing and fast-tracking are formal corrective actions routed through CCB; schedule forecasts update EAC and completion dates |
| Agile | Velocity tracking, burndown/burnup charts, and iteration review outcomes control schedule; no formal schedule baseline; sprint retrospectives surface impediments affecting throughput |
| Hybrid | Phase milestones from the predictive baseline are monitored with EVM; agile increment velocity informs rolling-wave schedule updates; schedule compression at phase level uses crashing/fast-tracking |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. SPI measures efficiency, not absolute delay — real-world PMs think "behind schedule" and "behind budget" interchangeably; PMI requires precise calculation of SV and SPI before any action, because the numbers drive the correct recovery choice.
2. Crashing always adds cost; fast-tracking always adds risk — experienced PMs choose one intuitively based on budget availability, but PMI expects the PM to assess both options and present them as change requests before choosing.
3. Schedule recovery without a change request is scope creep for schedule — real-world PMs tell teams to "work overtime" informally; PMI requires a formal change request for corrective action, including schedule compression.
4. A negative SPI does not automatically mean crash or fast-track — root cause analysis must precede recovery selection; the cause (resource issue, technical complexity, external dependency) determines the right response.
5. Agile teams control schedule through throughput transparency, not baseline adherence — PMI expects PMs on agile projects to use velocity and burndown as control tools, not to impose a predictive schedule baseline on iterative work.

**Ethics Angle**

- Honesty: Reporting accurate SV and SPI figures even when they reflect poorly on team or PM performance; not gaming metrics by reclassifying incomplete work.
- Responsibility: Escalating schedule impacts to the sponsor before they become unrecoverable; not absorbing known delays silently.
- Fairness: Applying schedule pressure equitably across the team; not scapegoating individuals for systemic schedule failures.
- Respect: Involving team members in schedule recovery planning rather than mandating overtime unilaterally.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Schedule Variance (SV) | SV = EV - PV. Negative = behind schedule; positive = ahead of schedule | SV is measured in dollars (EVM), not time units — a common exam trap |
| Schedule Performance Index (SPI) | SPI = EV / PV. SPI < 1 = behind schedule; SPI > 1 = ahead of schedule | SPI < 1 means inefficient schedule performance, not necessarily a missed deadline |
| Earned Value (EV) | EV = BAC × % complete (budgeted value of work actually performed) | EV is the "value earned," not the actual money spent — do not confuse with AC |
| Planned Value (PV) | Budgeted cost of work scheduled to be done by a specific point in time | PV is what SHOULD have been done by now; SV compares EV to PV |
| Actual Cost (AC) | Actual cost incurred for work performed to date | AC is used in CPI (Cost), not SPI (Schedule) — separate the two |
| Crashing | Schedule compression technique: adding resources (cost) to critical path activities to shorten duration | Crashing always increases cost; apply only to critical path activities for efficiency |
| Fast-Tracking | Schedule compression technique: overlapping activities previously planned in sequence | Fast-tracking increases risk of rework; does not add cost directly but may add rework cost |
| Schedule Baseline | Approved version of the project schedule used as the reference for measuring performance | The baseline can only be changed through formal change control — not updated informally |
| Schedule Forecast | Updated completion date or EAC for schedule based on current performance trends | Output of Control Schedule; informs sponsor of revised expectations |
| To-Complete Schedule Performance Index (TSPI) | TSPI = (Remaining Work) / (Remaining Time Authorized). Measures efficiency needed to meet a date | TSPI > 1 means the remaining work must be done more efficiently than current SPI |
| Resource Leveling | Technique to resolve resource over-allocation by shifting activities; may extend the schedule | Resource leveling can extend the schedule; not a schedule recovery technique |
| Critical Path Method (CPM) | Identifies the longest path through the network; drives project completion date | Only crashing activities on the critical path shortens the project duration |
| Variance Analysis | Comparing actual schedule performance to the baseline to identify causes of deviation | Variance analysis precedes corrective action; identify cause before selecting recovery |
| What-If Scenario Analysis | Evaluating alternative schedule scenarios to find the best recovery option | Used before selecting crashing vs. fast-tracking; PMI expects analysis before action |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "SPI = 0.75 is reported in the status meeting" | Conduct variance analysis to determine root cause | SPI alone does not dictate the recovery approach; cause determines response |
| "Critical path activity is 2 weeks behind" | Analyze root cause; evaluate crashing vs. fast-tracking; prepare change request | Schedule recovery techniques require a CR; do not implement before CCB review |
| "Sponsor asks for a revised completion date" | Calculate a schedule forecast based on current SPI and remaining work | Schedule forecasting is a formal Control Schedule technique; produce numbers before communicating |
| "Team is behind schedule due to resource unavailability" | Raise a change request for resource addition (crashing) or assess fast-tracking options | Resource-driven delays require resource solutions; confirm root cause before selecting technique |
| "Fast-tracking is proposed as the recovery approach" | Assess the risk of rework and dependency violations before recommending | Fast-tracking increases rework risk; quantify risk before committing to the approach |
| "Non-critical path activity is delayed" | Monitor the float; assess whether the delay is consuming schedule reserve | Non-critical delays only matter if they consume float and potentially shift the critical path |
| "Agile team's burndown chart shows they will not complete sprint goal" | Raise the issue in the daily standup; assess whether stories should be moved to the backlog | Agile schedule control uses transparency and adaptation, not baseline revision |
| "PM is pressured to report SPI as higher than calculated" | Report accurate EVM figures; document the pressure and explain the consequence of misreporting | Ethics: honesty in reporting schedule data is non-negotiable regardless of stakeholder pressure |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Schedule is 3 weeks behind and sponsor is unaware" | Immediately inform the sponsor with a schedule impact analysis and recovery options | PMI: proactive escalation before the situation becomes unrecoverable is a responsibility obligation |
| "CCB rejects the crashing change request citing budget constraints" | Work with the sponsor and CCB to explore fast-tracking, scope reduction, or deadline extension | The PM does not unilaterally choose alternatives; CCB rejection requires a new options analysis |

**Mnemonic: C-A-V-R-A-C**
Calculate EVM → Analyze variance → Variance confirmed → Root cause → Action (CR) → CCB approves.

**Memory hook:** "CAVRAC — the cave is dark until you calculate your way out."

- C = Calculate SV and SPI
- A = Analyze root cause (why is there a variance?)
- V = Validate the data (is the measurement correct?)
- R = Review recovery options (crash, fast-track, scope change)
- A = Action: raise a Change Request
- C = CCB decides

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- EVM is the primary schedule health measurement tool; SV and SPI are reported in schedule status reports.
- Schedule baseline is frozen at project planning; changes require formal CCB approval.
- Critical path analysis determines where crashing will be most effective.
- Large infrastructure projects often use schedule reserve (contingency time) as the first line of schedule defense before crashing.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Velocity and burndown/burnup charts replace EVM for schedule control in iterative work.
- Release burnup charts show cumulative story points delivered vs. target; a flattening burnup signals schedule risk.
- Sprint retrospectives serve as the corrective action mechanism for throughput impediments.
- Hybrid projects use EVM at the phase level while tracking velocity at the sprint level; the PM must reconcile both signals for steering committee reporting.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC: Regulatory submission deadlines are hard constraints; schedule delays that threaten regulatory compliance must be escalated immediately. BSEC reporting windows for capital market projects (e.g., ERP cutover, trading system upgrades) are non-negotiable; crashing is typically the only option.
- Construction / Infrastructure: Float analysis and critical path management are legally significant; contract penalties for schedule delays mean rigorous EVM tracking is a contractual obligation.
- IT / Software: Agile velocity forecasting is the primary schedule control tool; sprint goal completion rate is the leading indicator of release schedule risk.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Immediately crash the schedule when SPI falls below 1.0" | Behind schedule = add resources is the reflexive real-world response | PMI requires root cause analysis before selecting recovery; the cause determines the correct response, not the SPI alone |
| "Fast-track the critical path to recover schedule without raising a CR" | Fast-tracking feels like a technical decision, not a formal change | PMI: all schedule recovery actions are corrective action change requests; implementing without a CR bypasses change control |
| "SPI < 1 means the project will definitely be late" | Behind schedule metric feels conclusive and alarming | SPI measures efficiency, not absolute delay; the project may still finish on time with remaining float or schedule reserve |
| "Crash non-critical path activities to recover schedule" | Adding resources broadly feels like it helps everywhere | Crashing non-critical activities does not shorten the project completion date; only critical path crashing reduces duration |
| "Update the schedule baseline to reflect the current delay" | Updating the baseline feels honest and reflects reality | The schedule baseline can only be changed through CCB-approved change control; unilateral baseline updates undermine project governance |
| "Fast-tracking is safer than crashing because it doesn't add cost" | No direct cost increase makes fast-tracking seem risk-free | Fast-tracking increases rework risk and dependency violations; it adds indirect cost risk through potential rework |
| "Report only SPI to the sponsor when behind schedule" | SPI is the official metric and feels sufficient | The sponsor needs the schedule forecast (revised completion date), not just the efficiency index; SPI without context is insufficient for decision-making |
| "If the team works overtime informally, no CR is needed" | Overtime feels like a team-level decision within the PM's authority | PMI: overtime and resource additions that impact cost are corrective actions requiring a formal change request |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1**

A project manager is overseeing an IT system migration for a bank. At the midpoint status review, the following EVM data is reported: PV = $500,000; EV = $400,000; AC = $480,000. The CFO, who is the project sponsor, tells the PM to adjust the percent complete figures upward to make the SPI look closer to 1.0 before the board presentation. The CFO says the project will "catch up" next month and the board would "overreact" to the real numbers. What should the PM do?

A. Adjust the figures as requested since the CFO has authority and the project may indeed catch up.
B. Explain to the CFO that adjusting EVM figures misrepresents project status; report accurate metrics and provide context in the board presentation.
C. Report the accurate SPI but omit the SV from the board presentation to reduce alarm.
D. Escalate to the project steering committee before the board presentation.

**Q2**

An agile team is three sprints into a six-sprint release. Their planned velocity is 40 story points per sprint. Actual velocity over the three sprints has been 28, 30, and 26 story points respectively. The product owner has not reduced the backlog. The release burnup chart shows a significant gap between the projected delivery line and the target. The release manager is not aware of this trend. What should the scrum master do FIRST?

A. Increase the team's capacity by adding two new developers for the remaining sprints.
B. Inform the release manager immediately about the velocity shortfall and its implication for release scope.
C. Ask the team to work overtime in the next sprint to close the velocity gap.
D. Wait one more sprint to confirm whether the trend is temporary before escalating.

**Q3**

A hybrid PM is managing a digital transformation program. A predictive phase covering infrastructure setup is on the critical path and is 3 weeks behind schedule (SPI = 0.82). The agile development sprints that depend on the infrastructure are at risk of starting late. The PM has identified two options: crash the infrastructure setup by adding two contractors ($80K additional cost) or fast-track by overlapping the network configuration with server provisioning (30% rework risk). What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Implement crashing immediately since the critical path is at risk.
B. Implement fast-tracking since it does not add direct cost.
C. Conduct a what-if scenario analysis comparing the cost and risk of both options; prepare a change request for CCB review.
D. Escalate to the sponsor to choose between crashing and fast-tracking.

**Q4**

A PM calculates the following for a project at the 50% milestone: PV = $600,000; EV = $540,000; AC = $590,000. The sponsor asks for both the schedule and cost health assessment. The PM also needs to forecast the revised completion date. Which two metrics are MOST relevant for the sponsor presentation?

A. SPI = EV/PV = 0.90 (behind schedule, 10% schedule efficiency gap)
B. CPI = EV/AC = 0.92 (behind cost efficiency, spending more than earned)
C. SV = EV - PV = -$60,000 (behind schedule by $60K in earned value terms)
D. AC = $590,000 (actual spending is within budget)

**Q5**

An agile team is using a Kanban board for a continuous delivery project at a financial institution. Cycle time has increased from an average of 3 days to 9 days over the past two weeks. Work in progress (WIP) limits are being violated. The product manager wants to add 15 new urgent items to the board immediately. Place the following actions in the correct order for the PM/scrum master:

A. Enforce WIP limits; explain to the product manager that adding items now will increase cycle time further.
B. Analyze the cause of the cycle time increase (blocked items? resource availability?).
C. Remove the WIP blockers or reassign work to restore flow before accepting new items.
D. Facilitate a workflow review with the team to reassess WIP limits and process.

**Q6**

Which of the following is NOT a technique used in the Control Schedule process?

A. Variance analysis
B. What-if scenario analysis
C. Resource leveling
D. Decomposition

**Q7**

A project is 4 weeks behind schedule. The PM proposes fast-tracking the last three critical path activities by overlapping them. The risk assessment shows a 40% probability of rework on the second activity if it overlaps with the first. The PM has not informed the sponsor of this risk. The PM believes the rework probability is manageable. What should the PM do before implementing the fast-tracking?

A. Implement fast-tracking and monitor for rework; inform the sponsor only if rework occurs.
B. Raise a change request documenting the fast-tracking plan and the 40% rework risk; present to CCB and sponsor.
C. Implement fast-tracking to recover schedule and document the risk in the risk register without escalating.
D. Ask the team lead to assess whether the rework risk can be reduced before deciding.

**Q8**

A hybrid project team is delivering a regulatory reporting platform for a capital markets firm in Bangladesh. The BSEC submission deadline is fixed and cannot be moved. The project is currently 6 weeks behind the schedule baseline (SPI = 0.78). The available budget has a $200K contingency reserve. The PM presents two options to the CCB: (1) Crash critical path activities at $150K additional cost, reducing the delay to 2 weeks. (2) Fast-track with 35% rework probability, no additional cost, with a risk of missing the BSEC deadline if rework occurs. What communication strategy should the PM use when presenting to the CCB?

A. Recommend fast-tracking since it preserves the contingency reserve and is technically viable.
B. Present both options with full quantitative impact analysis (cost, schedule, risk probability) and recommend crashing given the non-negotiable regulatory deadline.
C. Recommend deferring the decision until more schedule data is available next week.
D. Ask the CCB to decide without a recommendation to avoid appearing biased.

**Q9**

A PM is managing a large construction project. At the 70% completion milestone, the critical path has zero float and two non-critical activities have consumed their total float. A new external dependency has emerged that will delay a critical path activity by 2 weeks. The sponsor asks the PM to assess recovery options. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Crash the delayed critical path activity immediately to recover the 2-week delay.
B. Assess whether fast-tracking adjacent critical path activities can recover the delay; model the cost and risk.
C. Analyze the full schedule network impact: identify all new critical path implications and revised float for all affected activities; then evaluate recovery options.
D. Inform the sponsor the project will be 2 weeks late and recommend a baseline revision.

**Q10**

A project is approaching its final quarter. EVM analysis shows: SPI = 0.88, CPI = 0.91, TSPI = 1.15. The team is currently working at 90% capacity. Which of the following actions are appropriate for the PM to take? (Select THREE)

A. Report the schedule and cost variances accurately to the sponsor with a revised forecast.
B. Raise a change request to crash selected critical path activities using contingency reserve.
C. Update the schedule baseline immediately to reflect the current performance trend.
D. Conduct a variance analysis to identify root causes before selecting recovery techniques.
E. Inform the team that TSPI > 1 means the project is unrecoverable and recommend cancellation.
F. Assess the feasibility of fast-tracking based on the TSPI and current team capacity.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty; PMBOK 7 — Monitoring and Controlling. Accurate EVM reporting is non-negotiable; adjusting figures to misrepresent project status is a fundamental ethics violation. Option A complies with unethical instruction. Option C is partial honesty (still misleading by omission). Option D delays the ethical obligation to report honestly; the PM must address the CFO directly first.
**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor pressure to adjust metrics is an ethics dilemma, not a communication style choice; accurate reporting with context is always the correct PMI response.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — transparency and empirical process control; escalation of material delivery risks. A persistent velocity shortfall is a material release risk requiring immediate stakeholder communication. Option A adds resources without root cause analysis. Option C mandates overtime without team consultation. Option D delays escalation, allowing the problem to worsen.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, declining velocity trends are release-level risks; transparency with the release manager is required before the gap becomes unrecoverable.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Control Schedule; what-if scenario analysis precedes recovery selection. Both options have significant implications (cost vs. rework risk); the PM must analyze both and route through CCB. Option A acts without analysis or CR. Option B acts without analysis or CR. Option D abdicates the PM's analytical responsibility before presenting to the sponsor.
**Mindset Lesson:** Schedule compression technique selection requires quantitative scenario analysis before committing; the CCB decides, not the PM alone.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** A and B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — EVM. The sponsor needs both schedule efficiency (SPI) and cost efficiency (CPI) to understand overall project health. Option A (SPI) addresses the schedule question. Option B (CPI) addresses the cost question. Option C (SV in dollar terms) is useful but less interpretable than SPI without context. Option D (AC alone) provides no efficiency signal.
**Mindset Lesson:** EVM sponsor presentations require both SPI (schedule) and CPI (cost) as the minimum health indicators; raw dollar figures without efficiency context are insufficient.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B → C → A → D
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — Kanban; flow management. Diagnose the root cause of cycle time increase first (B); resolve the blockers to restore flow (C); enforce WIP limits to prevent further degradation (A); then systemic process improvement (D). Adding new items (product manager's request) is correctly blocked by step A.
**Mindset Lesson:** In flow-based agile, restore system health before accepting new work; adding items to a broken flow system makes the problem worse.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 — Control Schedule techniques include variance analysis, critical path method, what-if scenario analysis, resource optimization (leveling, smoothing), schedule compression, and performance reviews. Decomposition is a Planning process technique (Define Activities, Create WBS), not a Monitoring and Controlling technique. Options A, B, C are all legitimate Control Schedule techniques.
**Mindset Lesson:** Decomposition belongs in planning; control processes use analysis and compression techniques, not decomposition.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility and Honesty; PMBOK — Integrated Change Control. Fast-tracking with a material rework risk requires a change request and sponsor/CCB awareness. Implementing without disclosure is a responsibility and honesty violation. Option A defers disclosure until damage occurs. Option C documents without escalating, which is insufficient for a 40% probability risk. Option D delays without addressing the governance obligation.
**Mindset Lesson:** Material risks associated with schedule recovery techniques must be disclosed to the sponsor and CCB before implementation — not after.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Stakeholder Engagement; Risk Management; regulatory context. Non-negotiable regulatory deadlines change the risk calculus: the 35% rework probability in fast-tracking creates an unacceptable risk of missing the BSEC deadline. The PM's role is to present quantitative analysis and make a recommendation aligned with project constraints. Option A ignores the regulatory risk. Option C defers without reason. Option D abdicates the PM's advisory responsibility.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a deadline is a hard regulatory constraint, the PM must recommend the option that protects compliance even at higher cost — and quantify the analysis to support the recommendation.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Control Schedule; network analysis. Before recommending recovery, the PM must understand the full schedule network impact: which activities are now critical, how float has shifted, and what the realistic recovery options are. Option A crashes before analysis. Option B evaluates one option without full network understanding. Option D capitulates to delay before exploring recovery.
**Mindset Lesson:** Full network impact analysis precedes recovery option selection; never crash or accept delay before understanding the complete schedule ripple effect.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, B, D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Control Schedule; EVM; change control. A: transparent reporting is mandatory. B: crashing using contingency reserve is a formal corrective action via CR. D: root cause analysis precedes recovery selection. Option C: updating the baseline without CCB approval violates change control. Option E: TSPI > 1 is demanding but not necessarily unrecoverable; recommending cancellation without analysis is premature. Option F: fast-tracking assessment is appropriate but must follow root cause analysis (D) — it is a valid action but not one of the three most critical.
**Mindset Lesson:** TSPI > 1 is a challenge signal, not a cancellation signal; respond with root cause analysis, a crashing CR, and accurate reporting — in that order.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Control Schedule = monitor schedule health, identify variances, and manage changes through formal change control.
- SV = EV - PV (negative = behind); SPI = EV / PV (less than 1 = behind schedule efficiency).
- SPI < 1 does not automatically trigger crashing — conduct root cause analysis FIRST.
- Crashing adds resources to critical path activities; always increases cost; requires a CR.
- Fast-tracking overlaps sequential activities; increases rework risk; requires a CR; does not directly add cost.
- Only crash critical path activities — crashing non-critical activities does not shorten project duration.
- Schedule forecast (revised completion date) is an output of Control Schedule; report it to the sponsor proactively.
- In agile, schedule control uses velocity, burndown/burnup charts, and retrospectives — not EVM or schedule baseline.
- The schedule baseline can only be changed through CCB-approved change control.
- TSPI > 1 means remaining work must be done more efficiently than current performance; assess feasibility before acting.
- Never adjust EVM figures to improve optics; accurate reporting is a non-negotiable ethics obligation.
- **PMI reflex burn-in: When the schedule is behind, FIRST calculate SV/SPI, THEN analyze root cause — only THEN select a compression technique and raise a change request.**
