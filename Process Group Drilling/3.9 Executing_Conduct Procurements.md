# PMP Exam Master Brief: Conduct Procurements

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Conduct Procurements is the Executing process where the project team obtains seller responses to bid documents, selects sellers based on pre-established criteria, and awards agreements that formalize the working relationship. It sits squarely in the Procurement Management knowledge area and carries roughly 9–10% exam weight within the Business Environment and stakeholder-adjacent domains, with significant cross-bleed into Risk (seller risk transfer), Communications (negotiation and bid conference management), and Legal/Regulatory compliance. The process transforms the procurement management plan, procurement documents, and source selection criteria from Planning into tangible, contractual commitments. A qualified seller list, proposals evaluated against independent cost estimates, and a formal selection rationale are the cornerstones of a defensible procurement. Procurement negotiations are not optional pleasantries — they are a structured process aimed at aligning on terms, schedule, and obligations before agreement execution. The PM's role is not to pick the lowest price but to apply the documented selection methodology faithfully, even when stakeholder pressure suggests otherwise. Conduct Procurements feeds directly into Control Procurements by establishing the baseline agreement against which performance is measured.

**In one sentence:** Conduct Procurements converts procurement planning artifacts into legally binding seller agreements by systematically soliciting, evaluating, and selecting qualified sellers through documented, bias-resistant processes.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal bid documents (RFP, RFQ, IFB) issued to a qualified seller list; proposals evaluated against weighted source selection criteria; independent cost estimates used as a benchmark; negotiation and contract award conclude the process |
| Agile | Preferred vendors or supplier partnerships replace formal bidding in many iterations; time-and-material or fixed-fee-per-sprint agreements common; seller relationships are collaborative rather than adversarial; value delivery over contractual rigidity |
| Hybrid | Core infrastructure procurements may follow formal RFP/contract cycle; software or service components use vendor partnerships with agile delivery terms embedded in master agreements |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Source selection criteria must be established during planning and applied consistently — real-world PMs often adjust criteria after seeing proposals, but PMI treats post-hoc criterion changes as a fairness and ethics violation.
2. The independent cost estimate is a benchmark for reasonableness, not a negotiating floor — experienced PMs sometimes use it as a ceiling, but PMI frames it as a sanity check against unrealistic or inflated bids.
3. Negotiation follows evaluation — real-world PMs often negotiate while still evaluating; PMI expects evaluation completion before negotiations begin to prevent bias.
4. Lowest price does not equal best value — PMI consistently rewards selecting the seller who best meets documented criteria even when cheaper options exist.
5. Procurement decisions must be documented — real-world PMs trust verbal agreements or email trails; PMI mandates formal agreements and decision audit trails for every selection.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Respect:** Treat all bidders fairly; provide equal access to information through bid conferences and addenda so no seller gains an unfair informational advantage.
- **Fairness:** Apply source selection criteria as documented — deviating because of relationships or preferences violates procurement integrity.
- **Honesty:** Independent cost estimates and evaluation scores must not be fabricated or reverse-engineered to justify a pre-selected seller.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is accountable for the integrity of the procurement process, including disclosing any conflicts of interest with bidders before evaluation.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| RFP (Request for Proposal) | Solicitation document requesting detailed proposals including approach, qualifications, and price; used when requirements are complex or solution-flexible | Do not confuse with RFQ — RFP seeks approach AND price; RFQ seeks price only |
| RFQ (Request for Quotation) | Solicitation document requesting price quotations for standard, well-defined goods or services | Trap: RFQ is NOT used when you need the seller to propose a solution — use RFP |
| IFB (Invitation for Bid) | Solicitation document used when requirements are clear and award is based primarily on price; common in government/construction | Trap: IFB is not appropriate when quality differentiation or technical approach matters |
| Source Selection Criteria | Weighted factors established during Plan Procurements used to evaluate and rank seller proposals objectively | Trap: Criteria cannot be changed after proposals are received without invalidating the process |
| Seller Proposals | Responses from prospective sellers to bid documents; form the basis of evaluation | Trap: Proposals are NOT agreements — they are offers that must be evaluated before negotiation |
| Independent Cost Estimates | Internal or third-party estimates prepared to benchmark against seller bids for reasonableness | Trap: ICE is a benchmark, not a negotiating ceiling or contractual figure |
| Qualified Seller List (Prequalified) | List of vetted vendors who meet baseline qualification standards, reducing evaluation burden | Trap: Being on the list does not guarantee selection — all criteria still apply |
| Bid Conference (Pre-Bid Meeting) | Meeting with all prospective sellers to clarify requirements and ensure equal understanding; all Q&A issued as formal addenda | Trap: Questions answered verbally at the conference are not binding — only written addenda are |
| Agreement (Contract) | Formal, mutually binding document defining obligations, deliverables, payments, and terms; output of Conduct Procurements | Trap: Letters of intent or MOUs are not contracts — only a signed agreement creates binding obligations |
| Procurement Negotiations | Structured dialogue to align on terms, schedule, roles, and price before agreement signing | Trap: Negotiation does not mean the PM accepts whatever the seller proposes — scope, risk, and terms are all negotiable |
| Advertising | Public notices in newspapers, professional journals, or online portals to expand the qualified seller pool | Trap: Advertising is an optional tool, not a mandatory step — it is used when the existing seller list is insufficient |
| Lump Sum (Fixed Price) Contract | Seller bears cost risk; total price is fixed regardless of actual costs | Trap: Scope must be fully defined or the buyer absorbs scope creep risk through change orders |
| Time and Material (T&M) | Hybrid contract where labor rates are fixed but quantity is variable; buyer bears volume risk | Trap: T&M with no cap-on-cost leaves the buyer exposed to unlimited cost growth |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Seller proposals received; team wants to pick the lowest bid | Apply source selection criteria as documented | PMI mandates objective, criteria-based evaluation — price alone is not determinative |
| Vendor approaches PM directly with a revised offer after submission deadline | Reject the unsolicited revision and evaluate the original proposal | Post-deadline changes compromise process integrity and fairness to all bidders |
| ICE shows one proposal is 40% above market | Request written clarification from the seller before disqualifying | Discrepancy may reflect scope misunderstanding — gather facts before eliminating |
| Stakeholder executive prefers a specific vendor before evaluation is complete | Document the preference, apply the criteria anyway, escalate if pressure continues | Source selection must be criteria-driven; undisclosed bias is an ethics violation |
| Bid conference question reveals ambiguity in the SOW | Issue a written addendum to all prospective sellers immediately | All bidders must receive the same information; verbal clarifications are not binding |
| Two sellers score identically on evaluation criteria | Apply the tie-breaking criteria documented in the procurement management plan | Ad-hoc tie-breaking without documented criteria is a fairness risk |
| Seller selected; legal team wants minor contract changes before signing | Allow legal to finalize then execute the agreement | Contract changes before signing are normal legal review — document all modifications |
| Project team member reveals they have a financial interest in a bidder | Remove the team member from evaluation activities and report per organizational policy | Conflict of interest must be managed proactively — non-disclosure is an ethics violation |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Senior executive insists on sole-sourcing a vendor without documented justification | Request and document a formal sole-source justification before proceeding | Bypassing competitive procurement without justification violates fairness and organizational governance |
| Selected seller requests contract price increase before signing, citing scope growth | Verify whether scope actually changed from the RFP baseline before agreeing | If scope is unchanged, the seller must honor the proposed price — undocumented scope claims cannot drive pre-award changes |

**Mnemonic:** A-B-C-N-A — Advertise, Bid, Criteria, Negotiate, Award

**Memory Hook:** "Any Buyer Can Navigate Agreements" — the five steps from market outreach to contract award in order.

**Mnemonic Unpack:**

- **A** — Advertise / issue solicitation documents to qualified sellers
- **B** — Bid conference + receive Bid proposals from sellers
- **C** — Evaluate proposals against documented Criteria (use ICE as benchmark)
- **N** — Negotiate terms with shortlisted or selected seller(s)
- **A** — Award the Agreement (contract execution)

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**

- Full formal procurement cycle: RFP/RFQ/IFB, bid conference, structured evaluation, formal negotiation, written contract
- Legal and contracts team involvement is standard
- Competitive bidding typically mandated by organizational or government policy
- Source selection criteria are weighted and documented before solicitation

**Agile / Hybrid**

- Preferred vendor or partnership models replace open bidding in many agile contexts
- Master services agreements with agile delivery addenda are common
- Sprint-level procurement for specialist services may use T&M with a capped cost
- Seller collaboration is ongoing, not one-time at award — retrospectives include seller performance
- Hybrid: formal contract for the program; agile delivery terms embedded as an addendum

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**

- Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC (Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission): Procurement of technology systems or consulting services may require regulatory pre-approval; vendor due diligence (AML/KYC compliance, data residency) is mandatory; sole-sourcing of critical financial infrastructure must be board-documented
- Government / Public Sector: Competitive procurement is legally mandated; failure to follow process can invalidate contracts
- Healthcare / Pharmaceuticals: FDA-regulated vendors require qualification audits before selection; agreements must include regulatory compliance clauses
- Construction: IFB with bonding and insurance requirements; lowest qualified bid often legally required

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Select the lowest-priced proposal | Cost control is a core PM responsibility; price is immediately visible and easy to defend to sponsors | PMI requires evaluation against ALL documented source selection criteria — price is one factor, not the only one |
| Negotiate with the preferred seller before completing evaluation | Saves time; pre-negotiating locks in the relationship early | Negotiation before evaluation completion introduces bias and compromises the integrity of the selection process |
| Adjust source selection criteria after seeing proposals to favor a better-known vendor | Experienced PMs recognize that criteria may not have anticipated all proposal dimensions | Post-hoc criterion changes invalidate the fairness of the process and constitute an ethics violation |
| Accept a verbal clarification at the bid conference as binding | Verbal clarifications feel authoritative when given by the PM or sponsor | Only written addenda issued to ALL prospective sellers are contractually valid clarifications |
| Award to the seller recommended by a senior executive without applying criteria | Organizational politics are real; executive sponsorship accelerates procurement | Bypassing documented criteria for political reasons violates PMI's Fairness principle and procurement governance |
| Use the independent cost estimate as the maximum allowable bid | ICE represents market rates and seems like a natural ceiling | ICE is a benchmark for reasonableness, not a contractual cap — a proposal above ICE triggers clarification, not automatic rejection |
| Skip formal agreement and rely on a letter of intent to start work | LOIs accelerate project start; formal contracts take time | Letters of intent are not binding contracts — work commenced without a formal agreement creates unprotected legal and financial exposure |
| Allow a team member with a vendor relationship to remain on the evaluation panel | The team member's expertise is valuable and they claim to be objective | Undisclosed or unmanaged conflicts of interest violate the Fairness and Honesty principles — recusal is mandatory |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager at a large capital markets firm is overseeing the procurement of a new trading platform. Three vendors submitted proposals. During evaluation, a colleague reveals that the evaluation committee chair has a prior consulting relationship with Vendor B — the highest-scoring vendor by a narrow margin — and received personal compensation from Vendor B two years ago. The evaluation is 90% complete. The chair denies any current financial relationship. The project sponsor wants to finalize vendor selection this week to meet a regulatory deadline. What should the project manager do FIRST?

A. Allow the evaluation to complete since the financial relationship ended two years ago and the deadline is pressing
B. Remove the committee chair from the evaluation, recuse them formally, and determine whether their prior scoring should be reviewed
C. Report the situation to the sponsor and let the sponsor decide how to proceed given the deadline pressure
D. Document the concern in the issue log and proceed with selection, noting the disclosure

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A software product team is running two-week sprints and procures a specialized UX research vendor on a T&M basis. After sprint 3, the vendor's hours are tracking 35% above the initial estimate with no formal change request. The team lead says the vendor is delivering excellent work and the overrun reflects scope discovered during sprint execution. The product owner has not updated the backlog to reflect the additional scope. What should the project manager do?

A. Stop the vendor's work immediately until a change request is approved
B. Request that the vendor submit an updated estimate and work with the product owner to formally update the backlog and assess budget impact
C. Approve the additional hours since the work is delivering value and scope discovery is inherent to agile
D. Issue a formal contract modification for the increased hours without product owner involvement

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid program has a formal contract with a systems integrator for infrastructure work (predictive phase) and an agile delivery component for the application layer. The systems integrator submits a change request for a 15% price increase, citing regulatory compliance requirements not in the original SOW. The agile team's sprint review simultaneously reveals that the application vendor is behind on three user stories. The project sponsor asks the PM to address both immediately. What should the project manager do FIRST?

A. Convene a joint meeting with both vendors to address all issues simultaneously
B. Review the original SOW and contract terms with legal to determine whether the compliance requirements constitute a legitimate change before responding to the integrator's price request
C. Approve the integrator's change request to avoid regulatory risk and focus the agile sprint planning on the delayed stories
D. Escalate both issues to the sponsor for direction before taking any independent action

**Q4 — Two-Correct Pick-Best (Predictive)**

During evaluation of RFP responses for a core banking system replacement, two proposals score within 2 points of each other on a 100-point scale. The lower-scoring proposal has a 20% lower price. The source selection criteria were established before solicitation and weight technical approach at 40%, implementation experience at 30%, and price at 30%. What is the BEST course of action?

A. Select the lower-priced vendor since the price differential is significant enough to outweigh a 2-point technical gap
B. Award to the higher-scoring vendor in accordance with the documented weighting and document the rationale
C. Re-weight the criteria to give price a higher percentage since budget constraints have increased since planning
D. Conduct additional negotiations with both vendors to see if the higher-scoring vendor will reduce their price before deciding

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A project team is using an iterative approach and has identified a need for a specialized data analytics vendor. Place the following actions in the CORRECT order for an agile-friendly procurement:

1. Define the vendor selection criteria aligned with sprint goals and team needs
2. Issue a lightweight solicitation to preferred vendors on the approved list
3. Conduct a brief evaluation including a working session or proof of concept
4. Award a T&M agreement with defined rate cards and a cost ceiling
5. Integrate vendor into the sprint team and include them in retrospectives

Which sequence is correct?

A. 2, 1, 3, 4, 5
B. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
C. 1, 3, 2, 4, 5
D. 2, 3, 1, 4, 5

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT a valid output of the Conduct Procurements process?

A. Selected sellers
B. Agreements (contracts)
C. Procurement management plan
D. Change requests

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A project manager receives an unsolicited call from the CEO of a vendor that submitted a proposal but has not yet been evaluated. The CEO mentions that if selected, the vendor will provide complimentary training licenses for the project team — a benefit worth $15,000. The CEO frames this as standard client onboarding support. The project manager's evaluation panel has not yet scored this vendor. What should the project manager do?

A. Accept the offer as it benefits the project team and does not influence the selection criteria
B. Thank the CEO and note that the offer will not influence the evaluation, then proceed normally
C. Disclose the unsolicited offer to the evaluation committee and procurement governance body, and exclude it from the evaluation
D. Decline the offer verbally and document it in the project files without notifying anyone else

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Agile or Hybrid)**

A project manager is managing a hybrid procurement where an external vendor is embedded with the agile development team. Sprint retrospectives reveal that the vendor team members feel excluded from backlog refinement sessions and are receiving requirements late, causing rework in each sprint. The product owner says vendor inclusion in refinement is unnecessary overhead. What is the BEST communication and engagement strategy?

A. Direct the vendor to accept the current process since the product owner controls the backlog
B. Escalate to the sponsor immediately since the product owner is causing vendor performance issues
C. Facilitate a conversation with the product owner and vendor lead to establish a lightweight vendor inclusion protocol for backlog refinement, documented in the team working agreement
D. Issue a formal contract modification requiring the product owner to include vendor representatives in all ceremonies

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A project manager is overseeing a hybrid program procuring two vendors: one for regulatory reporting (fixed-price contract, predictive) and one for mobile application development (T&M, agile). The regulatory reporting vendor has missed two consecutive milestone deliveries and attributes the delays to unclear requirements in the original SOW. The mobile vendor is on track. The program steering committee meets in three days. The project manager's functional manager advises waiting for the steering committee to raise the issue formally. What should the project manager do?

A. Wait for the steering committee meeting as the functional manager advised to maintain proper escalation channels
B. Immediately invoke the contract's default notice provisions and place the vendor on formal notice without waiting for the steering committee
C. Meet with the regulatory vendor to understand whether the SOW ambiguity is legitimate, document findings, and prepare a structured briefing for the steering committee rather than waiting passively
D. Issue a change request to clarify the SOW and grant the vendor a time extension without steering committee input

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select THREE)**

A project manager is about to begin the Conduct Procurements process for a large infrastructure project. Which THREE of the following actions are consistent with PMI best practice for this process?

A. Establish source selection criteria now that proposals have been received so the team can adapt to what sellers actually offered
B. Hold a bid conference and issue all clarifications as written addenda to all prospective sellers
C. Use the independent cost estimate to benchmark the reasonableness of received proposals
D. Apply the documented source selection criteria consistently across all proposals without modification
E. Select the vendor recommended by the project sponsor to accelerate the procurement timeline
F. Document the evaluation results and selection rationale in the procurement file for audit purposes

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**

**Correct Answer:** B

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Fairness principle: The PM must manage conflicts of interest proactively. A prior compensated relationship with a vendor under evaluation constitutes a conflict of interest regardless of its age. PMBOK 7 (Stakeholder Performance Domain) and procurement governance require that evaluation integrity be protected. The committee chair must be recused and prior scores reviewed. Deadline pressure (Option A) does not override ethics obligations. Delegating the decision to the sponsor (Option C) is insufficient — the PM must act. Documenting without acting (Option D) is passive and inadequate.

**Mindset Lesson:** Deadline pressure never justifies tolerating a conflict of interest in a procurement evaluation.

---

**Q2**

**Correct Answer:** B

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — scope discovered during iterations must be reflected in the backlog and budget baseline. T&M contracts require monitoring of hours against estimates (Control Procurements awareness). Stopping work immediately (Option A) is disproportionate. Unilaterally approving overruns (Option C) bypasses change control. Modifying the contract without the product owner (Option D) excludes the appropriate stakeholder. Option B integrates agile backlog management with procurement cost control.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile procurements, scope discovery must feed the backlog and the budget simultaneously — not just the delivery team's velocity.

---

**Q3**

**Correct Answer:** B

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Project Work Performance Domain) — before agreeing to a price change, the PM must verify whether the claimed basis for the change is legitimate by reviewing the original SOW and contract. Addressing both simultaneously (Option A) avoids prioritization. Approving without review (Option C) is financially reckless. Escalating without independent analysis (Option D) abdicates PM responsibility. The integrator's price change carries higher financial and contractual consequence and requires fact-gathering first.

**Mindset Lesson:** A seller's change request claim is a hypothesis — verify against the original contract before committing to any financial response.

---

**Q4**

**Correct Answer:** B

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Procurement Performance Domain) — source selection criteria are established before solicitation and must be applied as documented. The weighting of 40/30/30 means technical approach dominates; a higher-scoring vendor on those criteria must be awarded regardless of price differential. Re-weighting after seeing proposals (Option C) is a process and ethics violation. Negotiating price before award decision (Option D) is inappropriate in a competitive formal procurement. Option A ignores the documented weighting.

**Mindset Lesson:** Source selection criteria are a commitment to fairness — applying them faithfully protects the process even when the outcome is more expensive.

---

**Q5**

**Correct Answer:** B

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — even in agile, procurement intent and criteria must precede solicitation (1 before 2). A working session or proof of concept (3) validates the vendor against criteria before award (4). Integration and retrospective inclusion (5) follow agreement execution. Option A solicits before defining criteria — a process failure. Options C and D disorder the evaluation relative to solicitation.

**Mindset Lesson:** Agile procurement compresses the cycle but does not eliminate the logical dependency: define criteria, then solicit, then evaluate, then award.

---

**Q6**

**Correct Answer:** C

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 — The Procurement Management Plan is an OUTPUT of Plan Procurement Management, not Conduct Procurements. Conduct Procurements outputs include: selected sellers (A), agreements (B), change requests (D), resource calendars, project documents updates, and organizational process assets updates. Option C is tempting because procurement is top of mind, but the plan is a planning output, not an executing output.

**Mindset Lesson:** Know which process produces which output — confusing Planning outputs with Executing outputs is the most common ITTO trap in procurement questions.

---

**Q7**

**Correct Answer:** C

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Fairness and Honesty: An unsolicited offer of value from a vendor under active evaluation is a procurement integrity threat regardless of how it is framed. The PM must disclose it to the governance body and evaluation committee to maintain process transparency. Accepting (Option A) is an ethics violation. Acknowledging without disclosure (Option B) is insufficient. Documenting without notifying governance (Option D) does not protect process integrity.

**Mindset Lesson:** Any unsolicited benefit from a vendor under evaluation must be disclosed to the governance body — silence is not neutrality, it is a compliance failure.

---

**Q8**

**Correct Answer:** C

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — vendor integration into agile teams requires explicit working agreements that define collaboration norms. The PM's role is to facilitate alignment between the product owner and vendor, not to override the product owner (Option D) or accept a status quo causing rework (Option A). Escalating immediately (Option B) is premature — the PM should first attempt to resolve through facilitation. Option C reflects the servant-leader/facilitator mindset.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a vendor embedded in an agile team experiences exclusion, the PM facilitates a working agreement — not a contract modification or escalation.

---

**Q9**

**Correct Answer:** C

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Project Work Performance Domain) — the PM must investigate root causes before escalating or taking contractual action. Waiting passively (Option A) ignores the PM's responsibility to manage issues proactively. Invoking default notices without investigation (Option B) may be premature if SOW ambiguity is legitimate. Granting extensions without committee input (Option D) bypasses governance. Option C gathers facts, prepares structured communication, and preserves escalation quality.

**Mindset Lesson:** Escalation is most effective when the PM arrives with documented facts and a proposed path — not just a problem statement.

---

**Q10**

**Correct Answers:** B, C, F

**PMI Justification:**
- **B correct:** Bid conference with written addenda to all sellers is a core Conduct Procurements tool (PMBOK — Advertising and Conferences).
- **C correct:** Independent cost estimate as a benchmark is a named input/tool in Conduct Procurements.
- **F correct:** Documenting evaluation results and selection rationale is mandatory for governance, audit, and OPA updates.
- **A wrong:** Source selection criteria must be established in planning, not after proposals are received — post-hoc criteria creation is a fairness violation.
- **D** is actually correct behavior but B, C, F are the three most definitively process-anchored answers; A explicitly contradicts PMI process and is the anchor trap.
- **E wrong:** Sponsor recommendations do not substitute for documented criteria-based selection.

**Mindset Lesson:** Multiple-response procurement questions almost always include a "criteria established after proposals" trap — it is always wrong regardless of how it is framed.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Conduct Procurements executes the procurement plan by soliciting, evaluating, and awarding agreements to selected sellers.
- Source selection criteria are established in Planning and cannot be changed after proposals are received.
- The bid conference is mandatory when clarification is needed — all Q&A must be issued as written addenda to ALL prospective sellers.
- The independent cost estimate benchmarks proposal reasonableness — it is not a price ceiling or a contractual figure.
- Lowest price does not equal best value — evaluate all weighted criteria as documented.
- Negotiation follows evaluation — do not negotiate with a preferred seller before evaluation is complete.
- Conflicts of interest in the evaluation committee must be disclosed and managed by recusal, not just noted.
- Letters of intent are not contracts — formal signed agreements are required before binding obligations exist.
- Sole-source procurement requires formal documented justification before bypassing competitive bidding.
- Any unsolicited offer from a vendor under evaluation must be disclosed to the procurement governance body immediately.
- **When procurement pressure mounts — apply the criteria, protect the process, document everything.**
