# PMP Exam Master Brief: Monitor Communications

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Monitor Communications is the process of ensuring that the information needs of the project and its stakeholders are met throughout the project lifecycle. It sits in the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group under Communications Management, and it functions as the quality control loop for all project communications — verifying that messages are reaching the right people, in the right format, at the right frequency, and producing the intended understanding and response. The process is frequently underestimated on the exam because it appears to be "just checking emails" — in reality, it requires the PM to assess whether the communications management plan is effective, identify gaps in information flow, and trigger updates through formal change control when the plan needs to be revised. Outputs include work performance information, change requests, and updates to project documents and the project management plan. Cross-domain bleed is significant: communication gaps drive stakeholder engagement failures (Monitor Stakeholder Engagement), missed risks (Monitor Risks), and undetected resource issues (Control Resources). In Agile environments, communication is embedded in ceremonies — daily standups, sprint reviews, retrospectives — and the concept of "information radiators" replaces formal reports. On the exam, Monitor Communications questions often test whether the PM can distinguish between a communications execution failure (do it differently now) and a communications plan failure (change the plan formally).

**In one sentence:** Monitor Communications verifies that the communications management plan is working — information is reaching the right stakeholders at the right time — and drives change requests when the plan itself needs revision.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal communications management plan; periodic review of whether stakeholders are receiving and understanding information; variance from plan triggers change requests; stakeholder feedback formally assessed |
| Agile | Information radiators (task boards, burn charts) replace formal reports; ceremonies serve as structured communication channels; teams self-organize information sharing; retrospectives include communications effectiveness review |
| Hybrid | Formal status reports at phase-gate level; Agile-style transparency within iterations; stakeholder-specific communication tailoring based on engagement assessment |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. If stakeholders are not receiving the information they need, the PM must first determine whether this is an execution problem or a plan problem — then act accordingly. Real-world PMs immediately change how they communicate without assessing whether the plan itself is at fault.
2. Communication effectiveness is measured by whether the receiver understands the message and can act on it — not by whether the PM sent the message. Real-world PMs equate "I sent the email" with "communication occurred."
3. Updating the communications management plan requires a formal change request through Perform Integrated Change Control. Real-world PMs revise their communication approach on the fly without updating the plan.
4. Cultural, linguistic, and organizational differences affect message interpretation — the PM must account for these in both the plan and the monitoring process. Real-world PMs assume a shared organizational language guarantees shared understanding.
5. In Agile, the product owner is a communication hub between business stakeholders and the development team — disruptions to this communication channel are a systemic risk, not a team-level issue. Real-world Agile practitioners treat product owner unavailability as a minor scheduling inconvenience.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Honesty:** Communicating project status accurately — including bad news — even when stakeholders will respond negatively. Selective disclosure or omission of negative information is a form of dishonesty.
- **Respect:** Tailoring communications to the cultural and linguistic background of the recipient; delivering difficult messages with sensitivity and timeliness.
- **Responsibility:** The PM is accountable for ensuring that all relevant stakeholders have the information they need to make decisions — information hoarding is a governance failure.
- **Fairness:** Ensuring all stakeholders with a legitimate need receive project information consistently — not selectively informing allies and withholding from critics.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Communications Management Plan | Documents communication needs, formats, frequency, channels, and responsible parties for each stakeholder or group | This plan is an input AND can be updated (output) in Monitor Communications |
| Communication Channels Formula | N × (N−1) / 2, where N = number of stakeholders | Adding one stakeholder does not add one channel — it adds N−1 channels to the existing network |
| Work Performance Information | Analyzed data on communications effectiveness — output of Monitor Communications | Contrast with Work Performance Data (raw actuals); information is the analyzed output |
| Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix | Maps each stakeholder from Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Leading; shows current vs. desired state | Gaps between current and desired state indicate communication strategy failures, not just stakeholder personality issues |
| Communication Audit | Systematic review of whether communication activities are achieving their intended outcomes | This is a Monitor Communications activity — do not confuse with a quality audit |
| Feedback Loop | Mechanism by which the receiver confirms receipt and understanding of a message | Absence of a feedback loop means the PM cannot confirm communication effectiveness |
| Information Radiator | Agile tool (physical or digital board) that makes project status visible to all team members without requiring formal reports | Information radiators reduce the need for status meetings — they are a Monitor Communications mechanism, not a planning tool |
| Push Communication | Information sent to specific recipients (email, reports, memos) | Push communication does not confirm receipt or understanding |
| Pull Communication | Information made available for recipients to access (intranet, repository, wiki) | Pull communication requires recipients to be motivated to seek information — effectiveness is harder to verify |
| Interactive Communication | Real-time multi-directional exchange (meetings, phone calls, video) | Interactive communication is most effective for complex, sensitive, or high-stakes messages |
| Communication Technology | Methods and tools used to transmit information — email, collaboration platforms, face-to-face meetings | Technology selection must match urgency, sensitivity, and stakeholder tech-literacy |
| Lessons Learned Register | Repository for communication lessons that informs future projects and current plan improvements | Lessons about communication failures must be documented and used to improve the plan |
| Change Request | Formal request to modify the communications management plan when current approach is not meeting stakeholder needs | Minor ad-hoc adjustments are execution corrections; structural changes to frequency, format, or channels require change requests |
| Expert Judgment | Input from communications specialists, cultural advisors, or technical writers to assess communication effectiveness | Expert judgment is especially important when cross-cultural or multi-language communication is involved |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| A key stakeholder says they "never receive project updates" | Verify whether the communications management plan is being followed and whether the stakeholder's information needs are correctly captured in the plan | The root cause may be execution failure or plan failure — diagnosis precedes action |
| Stakeholder feedback indicates status reports are too technical for the audience | Assess whether the communications management plan accounts for this stakeholder's preferences | If the plan is at fault, a change request is needed; if it is an execution issue, the PM corrects the approach immediately |
| A stakeholder escalates to the sponsor saying they are "out of the loop" | Treat as a communications performance issue: investigate, document in the issue log, and review the stakeholder's position in the stakeholder register and communications plan | Escalation by a stakeholder is a signal that Monitor Communications has failed — respond with analysis, not defensiveness |
| Project team is sending conflicting messages to external stakeholders | Establish a single point of communication for external messages and update the communications management plan | Conflicting messages are a plan-level problem requiring a formal fix, not just ad-hoc correction |
| A regulatory authority requests communications in a specific format not currently used | Assess whether the change requires a formal change request to update the communications management plan | Regulatory requirements may constitute mandatory changes to communications protocols — not discretionary adjustments |
| PM observes that stakeholders are not attending status meetings despite being on the distribution list | Investigate root causes (meeting format, time, relevance, competing priorities) before redesigning the communication approach | Root-cause analysis must precede any plan revision |
| Information radiator has not been updated for 10 days in an Agile project | Facilitate a team discussion to re-establish the update cadence and surface any blockers | Information radiator degradation is a symptom of a communication culture issue requiring the PM/Scrum master's attention |
| Sponsor complains that they receive too much detail and not enough strategic summary | Adjust reporting format for the sponsor and, if the communications plan needs structural revision, submit a change request | Stakeholder communication preferences are valid inputs to the communications plan — the PM should respond with analysis and formal update |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-table**

| Escalation Scenario | Correct PM Action |
|---|---|
| A team member shares sensitive project financial data with an external party in violation of the communications management plan | Document the breach, assess the impact, engage legal/compliance, and escalate per organizational data governance protocols — do not handle informally |
| The product owner in an Agile project is consistently unavailable for sprint reviews, leaving business stakeholders without a communication channel | Escalate to the product owner's management to resolve the availability issue; this is a systemic communications risk, not a minor scheduling matter |

**Mnemonic: FACTS**

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Formal communications audit schedule aligned with reporting cycles
- Communications management plan reviewed at major milestones
- Change requests required when communication frequency, format, or channel must change structurally
- Stakeholder engagement assessment matrix formally updated at each review cycle

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Agile: Information radiators are the primary monitoring mechanism; retrospectives include communications effectiveness; daily standup is the baseline communication check
- Definition of Done may include communication requirements (e.g., sprint review demonstration to stakeholders is mandatory for acceptance)
- Hybrid: Formal status reports at phase gates; informal information radiators within iterations; product owner bridges internal and external communication channels

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC:** Regulatory communications (BSEC filings, board disclosures, AML reports) have legally mandated formats, frequencies, and recipients — Monitor Communications must verify regulatory compliance, not just stakeholder satisfaction
- **Healthcare / Pharmaceuticals:** Clinical trial communications must follow FDA/ICH-E6 guidelines; audit trails of all communications may be required
- **Construction / Infrastructure:** Multi-party projects (owner, contractor, subcontractor, regulator) require formal communication protocols — monitoring includes verifying that RFIs, submittals, and instructions to proceed are flowing correctly
- **IT / Software:** Distributed or remote teams create communication monitoring challenges; asynchronous communication channels require explicit feedback loops to confirm understanding

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Update the communications approach immediately when a stakeholder complains | Responding quickly to stakeholder feedback seems proactive and responsive | The PM must first diagnose whether this is an execution failure or a plan failure before changing anything — ad-hoc changes without diagnosis may create new problems |
| Add more communication channels when stakeholders say they are not informed | More channels seem to guarantee better coverage | More channels increase complexity and noise; PMI requires diagnosis of why current channels are failing before adding new ones |
| Informally adjust report formats when a stakeholder requests a change | It is courteous and efficient to accommodate stakeholder preferences directly | If the communications management plan specifies the format, changing it without a change request violates the plan and may set a precedent that undermines the plan's authority |
| Assume communication is effective if the PM sent the message | Sending is measurable; receiving and understanding are harder to verify and feel like the stakeholder's problem | PMI defines communication effectiveness by the receiver's understanding and ability to act — not by the sender's actions |
| Skip the communications management plan update if changes are "minor" | Minor changes seem below the threshold for formal change control; updating the plan feels bureaucratic | All changes to baselined plans require change control — what seems minor to the PM may be significant to stakeholders or auditors |
| Hold more meetings when stakeholders say they need more communication | Meetings feel like direct engagement and seem to solve communication gaps immediately | More meetings without addressing the root cause of the gap wastes time and may signal poor planning — the PM should diagnose before prescribing |
| Rely on the team to self-organize communications in Agile without PM oversight | Agile teams are self-organizing; PM oversight of communications feels like micromanagement | Even in Agile, the PM/Scrum master is responsible for ensuring external stakeholder information needs are met — internal self-organization does not cover external communication obligations |
| Archive all communications in a repository and assume stakeholders will access what they need | Pull communication infrastructure seems comprehensive and efficient | Pull communication requires stakeholders to seek information; without confirmation that they are doing so, the PM cannot verify effectiveness |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager is overseeing a regulatory compliance implementation project at a securities firm. The communications management plan requires that the regulatory authority receive monthly progress reports. The project has been running three weeks behind schedule for the past two months. The compliance officer asks the PM to omit the schedule variance data from the next regulatory report to "avoid triggering a regulatory inquiry that would slow the project down further." The PM knows the regulatory authority has a legitimate need for accurate progress data. What should the PM do?

A. Comply with the compliance officer's request — the compliance officer has subject matter expertise in regulatory relations and the PM should defer
B. Include the accurate schedule variance data in the regulatory report and inform the compliance officer that the communications plan requires complete and accurate reporting
C. Submit the report with the variance data but frame it in the most favorable language possible to minimize regulatory concern
D. Delay the regulatory report by one month to give the project time to recover the schedule before reporting

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

An Agile project team has been running for four sprints. The product owner holds sprint reviews but rarely invites business stakeholders, arguing that "the team needs to focus, not perform for an audience." As a result, business stakeholders have been receiving only written summaries of sprint outcomes prepared by the product owner. Three business stakeholders have told the project sponsor that they feel disconnected from the project and do not trust the summaries. The project sponsor asks the PM to address the issue. What should the PM recommend FIRST?

A. Replace the product owner since the communication failure reflects poor stakeholder engagement skills
B. Facilitate a discussion with the product owner about the purpose of sprint reviews and the stakeholders' legitimate need for direct engagement, then update the stakeholder engagement plan
C. Schedule separate stakeholder briefings by the PM to supplement the sprint reviews
D. Ask the three stakeholders to submit their concerns in writing to the product owner directly

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project is in phase 2. The communications management plan specifies monthly status reports for tier-2 stakeholders and weekly reports for tier-1 stakeholders. A tier-2 stakeholder has recently been elevated to tier-1 status following a change in the project's regulatory scope that significantly increases their involvement. The PM has continued to send monthly reports to this stakeholder for six weeks following the tier change. The stakeholder contacts the PM to complain that they are not receiving sufficient information to fulfill their new regulatory oversight role. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Begin sending weekly reports to the stakeholder immediately to address the urgent information gap
B. Update the stakeholder register and stakeholder engagement assessment matrix, then submit a change request to revise the communications management plan to reflect the stakeholder's new tier-1 status
C. Apologize to the stakeholder and explain that the communications plan has not yet been updated
D. Escalate the situation to the project sponsor since the regulatory scope change should have been caught earlier

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is reviewing communications effectiveness data from the past quarter. The data shows that: (1) 85% of stakeholders rated project status reports as "somewhat useful"; (2) three tier-1 stakeholders have not acknowledged receipt of the last two status reports; (3) the communications management plan was last reviewed eight months ago; and (4) the project scope has changed twice since then. Which single action addresses the most critical underlying problem?

A. Follow up with the three tier-1 stakeholders to confirm receipt of the reports
B. Conduct a communications audit and submit a change request to update the communications management plan to reflect current project scope and stakeholder needs
C. Increase the frequency of status reports to all stakeholders
D. Schedule a town hall meeting to gather stakeholder feedback on communications quality

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A Scrum team's product owner has just been replaced due to an organizational restructuring. The new product owner has never worked with the team. Business stakeholders report confusion about project priorities, sprint goals, and expected delivery dates. The team is unsure which backlog items are now highest priority. Place the following actions in the correct order: (i) Conduct a stakeholder communication needs assessment with the new product owner; (ii) Hold a backlog refinement session to re-confirm priorities with the new product owner; (iii) Update the stakeholder engagement plan and communications management approach; (iv) Facilitate a stakeholder introduction session between the new product owner and key business stakeholders.

A. (ii) → (i) → (iv) → (iii)
B. (iv) → (i) → (ii) → (iii)
C. (i) → (iv) → (ii) → (iii)
D. (i) → (ii) → (iv) → (iii)

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT a valid output of the Monitor Communications process?

A. Work performance information
B. Change requests
C. Project management plan updates
D. Communications technology selection

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A project manager discovers that a team lead has been sending informal project updates to external clients directly, bypassing the formal communications management plan. The updates contain technically accurate information but are not reviewed or approved by the PM before distribution. One client has made a procurement decision based on an informal update that included a preliminary delivery date that the PM has not confirmed. The team lead argues that "it speeds things up." What should the PM do?

A. Permit the practice as long as the information is accurate and the PM is copied on future communications
B. Address the protocol violation directly with the team lead, update the communications management plan to clarify the approval process, and notify the affected client of the preliminary nature of the delivery date communicated
C. Report the team lead to HR for violating the communications management plan
D. Accept the outcome since the client already made their decision and reversing course would damage the client relationship

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Agile or Hybrid)**

A global hybrid project spans four countries and three languages. The communications management plan specifies English as the project language for all formal documentation. Three stakeholders in one country have limited English proficiency and have been receiving formal reports that they cannot fully interpret. These stakeholders are technical subject matter experts whose sign-offs are required for phase-gate approvals. The project has missed two phase-gate sign-offs because of this communication barrier. What should the PM do?

A. Hire professional translators for all project documents to resolve the language barrier
B. Assess the root cause of the sign-off delays, engage expert judgment on cross-cultural communication strategies, update the stakeholder communication approach for these stakeholders, and submit a change request to the communications management plan
C. Request that the three stakeholders improve their English proficiency as a condition of project participation
D. Remove the sign-off requirement for these stakeholders since the communication barrier prevents them from fulfilling the role effectively

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A hybrid program manager oversees six component projects. A component project PM has been providing the program manager with weekly status reports as required by the program communications plan. For the past four weeks, the component PM's reports have consistently downplayed schedule risks that the program manager has identified through independent data sources. When confronted, the component PM states that the risks "are under control" and does not need to escalate them formally. The program manager believes the risks require program-level visibility. What should the program manager do?

A. Accept the component PM's judgment since they are closest to the work and have the most information about risk status
B. Formally escalate the communication discrepancy to the portfolio/program steering committee and request an independent project review
C. Discuss the communication gap with the component PM, document the risks in the program risk register, and formally request that the component PM revise the reporting approach to include full risk transparency per the program communications plan
D. Replace the component PM since selective risk reporting suggests a performance issue

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A project manager conducting Monitor Communications identifies the following findings: (1) Two stakeholders have not responded to any communications in four weeks; (2) The project's stakeholder engagement matrix shows three stakeholders have regressed from "supportive" to "neutral" over the past month; (3) Status reports are being distributed in a format that 60% of stakeholders rate as "difficult to understand"; (4) A new regulatory requirement has introduced a stakeholder group that is not currently in the communications management plan. Which THREE of the following actions are most appropriate?

A. Investigate the reasons for stakeholder non-response and engagement regression, and document findings in the issue log
B. Immediately increase communication frequency for all stakeholders to address the engagement regression
C. Revise the status report format based on stakeholder feedback and submit a change request to update the communications management plan
D. Add the new regulatory stakeholder group to the stakeholder register and submit a change request to update the communications management plan
E. Archive the four-week communication records to establish an audit trail and take no further action until the next reporting cycle
F. Hold an all-hands stakeholder meeting to resolve all four communication issues simultaneously

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility. The PM has an obligation to provide accurate information to stakeholders with a legitimate need, including regulatory authorities. The communications plan requires complete and accurate reporting. Omitting variance data at the request of a subject matter expert does not transfer the PM's ethical obligation. PMBOK 7 Stewardship: protecting stakeholders (including regulators) from decisions made on incomplete information is a core PM responsibility. Option A defers professional judgment inappropriately. Option C is selective disclosure — still a form of manipulation. Option D deliberately delays required reporting.
**Mindset Lesson:** Regulatory communication obligations cannot be selectively fulfilled — accuracy is mandatory regardless of political consequences.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Stakeholder Engagement — sprint reviews are designed to provide transparency to business stakeholders, not just product reviews for the team. The product owner's gatekeeping of stakeholder access undermines the Agile principle of transparency and direct stakeholder engagement. The correct first step is a facilitated conversation to align on purpose, followed by a plan update. Option A escalates to personnel action without attempting resolution. Option C creates parallel communication channels that bypass rather than fix the root issue. Option D asks stakeholders to navigate a dysfunctional dynamic rather than fixing it.
**Mindset Lesson:** Sprint reviews are stakeholder communication events — product owner gatekeeping of business stakeholders violates Agile transparency principles.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Monitor Communications — when a stakeholder's information needs change materially (tier-1 vs. tier-2 reclassification), the communications management plan must be formally updated through change control. Sending weekly reports without updating the plan is an ad-hoc workaround that does not document the change or ensure consistency. Option A addresses the symptom without fixing the plan. Option C apologizes without acting. Option D escalates unnecessarily — this is within the PM's authority to resolve.
**Mindset Lesson:** Stakeholder tier changes must be reflected in the communications management plan through formal change control, not just informal practice adjustments.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** The most critical underlying problem is that the communications management plan is eight months old and has not been updated to reflect two scope changes — all three other symptoms (stakeholder non-acknowledgment, low usefulness ratings, and the need for a follow-up) are downstream effects of a stale plan. A communications audit diagnoses the current state, and a change request formalizes the update. Options A and D address symptoms, not the root cause. Option C adds frequency without improving relevance.
**Mindset Lesson:** When communications are underperforming, audit the plan first — stale plans generate downstream symptoms that look like execution problems.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** Stakeholder Engagement and Communications Management — with a new product owner, the first step is to understand their communication style and stakeholder relationship needs (i). The next step is to create visibility and trust through direct stakeholder introductions (iv). Backlog refinement (ii) then aligns priorities with business input, and the updated plan (iii) documents the new communication approach. Option A puts backlog refinement before stakeholder communication assessment. Option B puts introductions before understanding communication needs. Option D delays stakeholder introduction unnecessarily.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a key communication hub is replaced, assess communication needs before re-engaging stakeholders or reprioritizing work.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 — Communications technology selection is an activity within Plan Communications Management, not an output of Monitor Communications. The outputs of Monitor Communications are: work performance information, change requests, project management plan updates (communications management plan, stakeholder engagement plan), and project document updates (issue log, lessons learned register, stakeholder register). Technology selection decisions are made during planning, not monitoring and controlling — though monitoring findings can trigger a change request to update technology choices.
**Mindset Lesson:** Communications technology is selected during planning — monitoring outputs are work performance information, change requests, and document updates.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Communications Management Plan — unauthorized communications bypass the approval process and create accountability gaps. The PM must address the protocol violation, clarify expectations with the team lead, and notify the affected client that the delivery date was preliminary and unconfirmed. This protects the client's decision-making integrity and reinforces the communications plan. Option A accepts a governance violation. Option C escalates to HR before attempting resolution — disproportionate. Option D prioritizes client relationship over honesty.
**Mindset Lesson:** Unauthorized project communications must be addressed through protocol correction and client transparency — not tolerated for efficiency.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Monitor Communications — when communication barriers directly cause project milestone failures (missed phase-gate sign-offs), the PM must treat this as a systemic problem requiring a plan update. Expert judgment on cross-cultural communication, stakeholder-specific adaptations, and a formal change request are the appropriate response. Option A is prescriptive without diagnosis. Option C violates respect for stakeholders and is impractical. Option D removes critical governance requirements without a legitimate basis.
**Mindset Lesson:** Communication barriers that cause project milestone failures require root-cause analysis, expert judgment, and a formal plan update — not workarounds.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** Monitor Communications and Escalation. The program manager's first obligation is to address the communication gap directly with the component PM and document the identified risks at the program level. Formally requesting revised reporting transparency per the program communications plan gives the component PM an opportunity to comply before escalation. Option A abdicates program oversight responsibility. Option B escalates to senior leadership before attempting direct resolution — disproportionate. Option D personnel action is premature.
**Mindset Lesson:** Communication discrepancies between component and program levels must be addressed directly first, with formal escalation reserved for non-compliance after direct engagement.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, D
**PMI Justification:** (A) Investigating non-response and engagement regression with documentation in the issue log is the correct response to stakeholder engagement signals — these are Monitor Communications findings that require root-cause analysis. (C) Revising the status report format based on stakeholder feedback and submitting a change request to the communications management plan addresses the format problem through proper channels. (D) Adding new regulatory stakeholders to the register and updating the communications management plan is mandatory when a new stakeholder group is identified — this is a direct Monitor Communications obligation. (B) Increasing frequency without diagnosis may worsen the engagement problem — frequency is not always the root cause. (E) Archiving records without acting on four identified issues is an abandonment of monitoring responsibilities. (F) A single all-hands meeting cannot effectively address four distinct and different communication issues.
**Mindset Lesson:** Monitor Communications findings drive three types of actions: investigate and document, update the plan through change control, and add new stakeholders to the register — never absorb findings passively.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Monitor Communications verifies that the communications management plan is achieving its intended outcomes — not just that messages were sent
- Communication effectiveness is measured at the receiver, not the sender — "I sent it" does not equal "communication occurred"
- Gaps between actual and desired stakeholder engagement states are a direct signal of communications plan failure
- Root-cause analysis first: determine whether the gap is an execution failure (correct now) or a plan failure (change request required)
- All structural changes to the communications management plan require a formal change request — no informal revisions
- In Agile: information radiators and ceremonies are the monitoring mechanism; retrospectives assess communications effectiveness
- Cultural, linguistic, and organizational differences affect message interpretation — the PM must account for these explicitly
- New stakeholders, scope changes, and tier reclassifications all trigger communications management plan updates via change control
- Communication channel count = N × (N−1) / 2; adding stakeholders exponentially increases communication complexity
- Selective or inaccurate communication to stakeholders with legitimate information needs violates PMI's honesty and responsibility ethics
- **When communications are failing, the PMI reflex is: diagnose root cause first, correct execution gaps immediately, submit a change request for plan failures — never assume sending equals communicating.**
