# PMP Exam Master Brief: Manage Project Knowledge

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Manage Project Knowledge is the execution-phase process of using existing organizational knowledge to achieve project objectives and creating new knowledge that contributes to organizational learning. Unlike traditional lessons-learned approaches — which defer knowledge capture to project close — PMI demands active, continuous knowledge management throughout execution. The process has two distinct dimensions: leveraging existing knowledge (codified in OPAs, prior project records, expert networks) and generating new knowledge (insights from current execution that feed future projects). In banking and capital markets environments in Bangladesh, this process is particularly high-stakes: institutional knowledge about Bangladesh Bank IT examination readiness, BSEC regulatory filing procedures, or core banking system quirks is often tacit, residing in the heads of experienced employees who may leave mid-project. This process bleed covers every knowledge area because knowledge is the substrate of all PM activity — lessons from risk events feed the risk register, lessons from quality failures feed QC, and lessons from stakeholder conflicts feed communications. On the exam, this process is tested through its deceptively simple outputs (lessons learned register updates, knowledge management tools) and through PMI's insistence that knowledge transfer is a PM responsibility, not an HR function. **In one sentence:** Manage Project Knowledge ensures that both existing organizational knowledge is applied to the current project and new knowledge generated during execution is captured and shared for future organizational benefit.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | PM formally updates the lessons learned register at defined milestones; knowledge management tools (wikis, document libraries) are maintained per the communications management plan; knowledge transfer events are scheduled activities. |
| Agile | Retrospectives are the primary knowledge generation mechanism; the team reflects at the end of each sprint and updates shared working agreements, practices, and backlogs based on learning; knowledge is embedded in practice, not only in documents. |
| Hybrid | Sprint retrospectives feed lessons into the project-wide lessons learned register; regulated track post-mortems feed compliance knowledge bases; both streams contribute to the organization's knowledge repository. |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Tacit knowledge cannot simply be documented — it must be transferred through human interaction.** Real-world PMs send emails summarizing what experts know. PMI recognizes that tacit knowledge (skill, judgment, experience) transfers through mentoring, shadowing, and conversation — not documentation alone.
2. **Manage Project Knowledge is an Executing process, not a Closing process.** Real-world instinct is that lessons learned happen at project end. PMI insists the lessons learned register is updated continuously during execution — closing only finalizes it.
3. **The PM facilitates knowledge creation; the team owns it.** Experienced PMs often hoard institutional knowledge as personal value. PMI expects the PM to create conditions for collaborative knowledge creation and sharing, not to be the sole knowledge repository.
4. **Sharing knowledge that protects the organization is not optional.** If a PM discovers a systemic quality issue that has implications beyond the current project, PMI's Code of Ethics (Responsibility) requires sharing that knowledge with relevant organizational stakeholders, even if it reflects badly on the team.
5. **Knowledge management tools are selected based on project context, not defaulted to email.** Real-world teams rely on email threads as a knowledge repository. PMI expects deliberate selection of appropriate tools — wikis, expert directories, collaborative platforms — tailored to the project environment.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** PMs are accountable for ensuring organizational knowledge gained on their projects is not lost to turnover or project close — hoarding knowledge to maintain job security is an ethical violation.
- **Honesty:** Lessons learned must reflect actual events honestly — sanitizing failures or over-attributing successes corrupts the organizational knowledge base.
- **Respect:** Knowledge sharing must include diverse voices; silencing junior team member insights in lessons capture violates respect for all contributors.
- **Fairness:** Access to knowledge resources (expert networks, prior project archives) must be equitably distributed across the team, not reserved for senior members.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Lessons Learned Register | Running document updated throughout execution capturing what worked, what failed, and recommendations for future projects | Not finalized at close — updated during execution; closing transfers it to the lessons learned repository (OPA) |
| Explicit Knowledge | Knowledge that can be codified — written, photographed, diagrammed (procedures, designs, test results) | Explicit knowledge transfer via documentation is insufficient for tacit knowledge; exam stems about "complex skill transfer" require human interaction answers |
| Tacit Knowledge | Knowledge embedded in experience, judgment, and intuition — cannot be fully codified | Transfer requires mentoring, shadowing, storytelling, communities of practice — not document creation |
| Lessons Learned Repository | OPA where finalized lessons learned are stored for organizational access after project close | Contrast with lessons learned register (live project artifact) vs. repository (permanent OPA) |
| Knowledge Management Tools | Wikis, intranets, collaborative platforms, expert directories, communities of practice | Selecting appropriate tools is a PM responsibility; defaulting to email is a tailoring failure |
| Communities of Practice | Groups of practitioners sharing expertise across project and organizational boundaries | PMI favors CoPs for tacit knowledge transfer — useful where expert knowledge must cross departmental silos |
| Interpersonal and Team Skills | Facilitation, active listening, leadership — tools for enabling knowledge sharing | Knowledge transfer is a people process, not just a documentation activity |
| Organizational Learning | The process by which an organization improves future performance by incorporating lessons from experience | Manage Project Knowledge directly contributes to organizational learning, not just project-level improvement |
| Information Management | Techniques for creating and connecting information for distribution to project stakeholders | Distinct from knowledge management: information management handles explicit data; knowledge management addresses the tacit-to-explicit transfer challenge |
| Knowledge Transfer Events | Structured activities (workshops, debriefs, expert interviews) designed to capture and share project-specific knowledge | Scheduling these is a PM execution responsibility, not an end-of-project activity |
| OPA Updates | Updates to organizational process assets — including lessons learned repository additions — are key outputs of Manage Project Knowledge | OPA updates flow FROM projects INTO the organization; EEFs flow FROM the organization INTO projects |
| Networking | Building relationships within and outside the project to access needed expertise | Informal expert networks are a legitimate knowledge management tool in PMI's framework |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A key subject matter expert is leaving the project in two weeks" | Arrange structured knowledge transfer sessions (shadowing, interviews, documentation reviews) immediately | Tacit knowledge requires human interaction for transfer; waiting until the expert's last day makes transfer impossible |
| "The team has learned a better way to handle a recurring process" | Document the improvement in the lessons learned register and share with the team for immediate adoption | Continuous lessons capture during execution is PMI's expectation; saving it for close-out wastes the learning |
| "A completed project faced a similar technical challenge to the current one" | Access the lessons learned repository from the prior project and apply relevant insights to current planning/execution | Leveraging existing organizational knowledge is one of the two key purposes of this process |
| "Team members are not sharing what they know with each other" | Facilitate knowledge-sharing activities (workshops, pair work, communities of practice) to create knowledge-sharing culture | Knowledge hoarding is a team dysfunction the PM must actively address through facilitation |
| "A junior team member proposes an innovative approach based on recent experience" | Capture the idea in the lessons learned register and evaluate it through the appropriate change/adoption process | All knowledge has value; innovation from junior members must be captured and evaluated equitably |
| "The project involves technology unfamiliar to the team" | Identify expert resources (internal CoPs, OPA knowledge bases, external SMEs) and plan knowledge acquisition activities | Leveraging existing knowledge before creating new knowledge is the efficient execution path |
| "Stakeholder asks why the same mistake made on a previous project is being repeated" | Acknowledge the gap, access prior lessons learned, implement a corrective action, and update the lessons learned register | Failing to apply prior lessons is a PM accountability failure, not just a team oversight |
| "PM is about to close the project" | Transfer the lessons learned register to the organizational lessons learned repository as part of close-out activities | Closing is when the register becomes the repository update — but capture happened during execution |
| "Team retrospective surfaces a systemic quality problem affecting other projects" | Report the finding to PMO or relevant organizational governance body | Lessons with organization-wide implications must be escalated beyond the project — responsibility principle |
| "Two experts on the team have conflicting approaches to solving a technical problem" | Facilitate a structured knowledge exchange and document both approaches with rationale in lessons learned | Conflict between experts is a knowledge-generation opportunity; PM facilitates, not dictates |

**Topic-Specific Weak-Spot Scenarios — Escalation Edge Cases**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "PM discovers that a critical process knowledge gap exists because the prior PM did not document lessons" | Conduct expert interviews and facilitate knowledge capture immediately; escalate to PMO to flag the documentation gap | The PM cannot wait for documentation to exist — active knowledge harvesting from available humans is the first response |
| "Sponsor asks PM to keep project lessons confidential to protect the business unit's reputation" | Decline and explain that lessons learned are organizational assets and must be documented honestly per PMI Code of Ethics | Sponsor authority does not override the PM's professional obligation to honest knowledge capture |

```
MANAGE PROJECT KNOWLEDGE — KNOWLEDGE FLOW

   ORGANIZATIONAL KNOWLEDGE BASE (OPA)
   [Prior lessons, procedures, expert directories]
              |
              | (EXISTING KNOWLEDGE IN)
              v
   +-----------------------------+
   |  PROJECT EXECUTION          |
   |  - Apply prior lessons      |
   |  - Use expert networks      |
   |  - Leverage CoPs            |
   |  - Enable team learning     |
   +-----------------------------+
              |
              | (NEW KNOWLEDGE GENERATED)
              v
   [LESSONS LEARNED REGISTER]  <-- Updated continuously
              |
              | (AT PROJECT CLOSE)
              v
   [LESSONS LEARNED REPOSITORY]
   (Permanent OPA — feeds future projects)

   TWO FLOWS:
   TACIT knowledge  --->  Human interaction (mentoring, CoP, workshops)
   EXPLICIT knowledge --->  Documentation, wikis, intranets
```

**Mnemonic: T-E-C-L**
**"Tacit Needs Humans, Explicit Needs Documents, Capture Continuously, Leave a Legacy"**

- **T**acit — transfer through people, not paper
- **E**xplicit — encode in systems and documents
- **C**ontinuously — lessons learned register updated throughout execution
- **L**egacy — at close, transfer to repository for organizational benefit

The failure mode: treating all knowledge as explicit (documentable) and deferring all capture to project close.

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Knowledge management plan is a component of the project management plan; identifies tools, repositories, and transfer responsibilities
- Milestone-based knowledge reviews supplement continuous lessons capture
- Configuration management integrates with knowledge management to ensure versioned lessons and process updates
- Expert elicitation sessions are scheduled execution activities, not ad hoc

### Agile / Hybrid
- Sprint retrospectives are the primary explicit knowledge creation ritual — every sprint produces actionable insights
- Working agreements evolve based on team learning — knowledge is embedded in practice, not only in documentation
- Product backlog refinement incorporates lessons from prior sprints
- In hybrid: retrospective insights from agile stream feed formal lessons learned register for regulatory track
- "Definition of Done" updates based on quality lessons represent practical knowledge management

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Bangladesh Bank IT Examinations:** Knowledge of examiner focus areas and prior findings is high-value institutional knowledge; must be preserved in accessible repositories, not in individual heads
- **BSEC Regulatory Filing:** Tacit knowledge about filing timelines, common rejection reasons, and liaison contacts is mission-critical for capital markets projects; CoPs or expert interview programs are warranted
- **Core Banking Projects:** Integration knowledge (API behavior, legacy data quirks) is highly tacit and concentrated in a few individuals; structured knowledge transfer before resource transitions is a risk management imperative
- **High-Turnover Banking IT:** Bangladesh's competitive banking IT market means frequent staff turnover; PM must design knowledge management as a project risk response, not an administrative function
- **Regulatory Audit Trails:** In regulated industries, lessons learned documentation may be subject to regulatory review; accuracy and completeness carry compliance implications beyond organizational learning

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Document all lessons learned at project close to ensure accuracy and completeness" | Waiting until close gives full project perspective; real-world PMs do retrospectives at close | PMI requires continuous capture during execution; waiting until close loses timely learning and violates the process definition |
| "Send the expert's documented procedures to the team as a knowledge transfer" | Documentation is tangible, scalable, and auditable; experienced PMs equate documentation with knowledge transfer | Tacit knowledge cannot be fully transferred through documentation; human interaction (mentoring, workshops) is required for experiential knowledge |
| "The PM should personally maintain the lessons learned register as the primary knowledge keeper" | PM has the broadest project view; centralizing knowledge management ensures consistency | PMI expects collaborative, team-wide knowledge creation; PM facilitates, not hoards — individual ownership creates single point of failure |
| "Decline to share unflattering lessons that reflect poorly on the organization" | Protecting organizational reputation is a real management responsibility; experienced PMs exercise discretion | Honesty in lessons learned is a professional obligation; sanitizing lessons corrupts the knowledge base and violates the Code of Ethics |
| "Knowledge management activities are low priority during execution — focus on deliverables first" | Deliverables drive project success; knowledge management is overhead | Manage Project Knowledge is an Executing process with equal standing; deferring it creates organizational knowledge loss that compounds across projects |
| "Use email chains as the primary knowledge repository for project decisions" | Email is ubiquitous, searchable, and familiar; most organizations run on email | PMI requires deliberate knowledge management tool selection; email chains are unstructured, non-transferable, and not OPA-compliant |
| "If a senior manager asks that project issues not be included in lessons learned, comply as directed" | Senior management has authority; compliance preserves relationships and reduces conflict | The PM's responsibility to organizational knowledge integrity overrides management preference; honest capture is an ethical obligation |
| "Transfer knowledge only when a team member exits the project" | Knowledge transfer is triggered by departure in most organizations | PMI expects knowledge to flow continuously, not only at exit — departure-triggered transfer is a reactive failure mode |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM is managing a core banking implementation at a BSEC-licensed custodian bank. During execution, the team discovers that a prior project's lessons learned repository contains falsified data — problems were omitted and successes were inflated. The PM's current project is benefiting from some of those (false) prior lessons. The PM's manager acknowledges the problem but says correcting historical records would "create unnecessary noise" and asks the PM to ignore it. What should the PM do?

A. Follow the manager's guidance and focus on capturing accurate lessons for the current project only.
B. Report the falsified historical lessons to the PMO and relevant governance body, and recommend a review of the affected prior project's outputs.
C. Add a disclaimer to the current project's lessons learned register noting that prior OPA data is unreliable.
D. Discuss with the project sponsor and let the sponsor decide whether to report the falsification.

The team's retrospective at end of Sprint 4 surfaces that two developers consistently do not share their solutions with teammates, leading to knowledge silos and rework when they are unavailable. The scrum master has noted this in two prior retrospectives without improvement. What is the BEST next action for the scrum master?

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Section 5; Responsibility, Section 4) requires PMs to report known integrity violations in organizational assets. Falsified lessons learned corrupt the organizational knowledge base and expose future projects to risk. Option A (focus on current project only) allows the falsification to persist and enables future projects to be misled. Option C (disclaimer only) is insufficient — it acknowledges the problem without correcting it. Option D (refer to sponsor) is inadequate because the sponsor has not been identified as having authority over PMO records; the PMO itself and governance bodies are the appropriate recipients.
**Mindset Lesson:** The PM's ethical obligation extends to the organization's knowledge integrity, not only the current project.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide (Chapter 6, Agile Retrospectives and Team Agreements) establishes that retrospectives are the mechanism for surfacing and correcting team behavioral patterns. Facilitated agreement-making (working agreements) is the appropriate tool for persistent behavioral issues like knowledge hoarding. Option A (escalate as performance issue) escalates before exhausting team-level resolution, which contradicts the agile principle of self-organization and team accountability. Option C (deprioritize stories) avoids the root cause and introduces schedule risk. Option D (document and accept) is passive acceptance of a destructive pattern.
**Mindset Lesson:** Retrospectives are the correct forum for team behavioral change in agile — escalation comes only after team-level resolution is exhausted.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** The root cause is process failure — sprint planning did not include a step to review relevant prior lessons. The first corrective action is to fix the process immediately to prevent recurrence. Option A (corrective action for the flaw) addresses the symptom, not the cause — the flaw must be corrected but that alone does not prevent the same process failure in future sprints. Option C (escalate to sponsor) is premature; the PM has sufficient authority to fix sprint planning process. Option D (update lessons learned) is necessary but only partially addresses the failure — it captures the lesson without changing the behavior.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a process failure causes a problem, fix the process first — the symptom and the root cause require separate responses.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Team Performance Domain) and Manage Project Knowledge theory both recognize that tacit knowledge transfers through human interaction, while explicit knowledge is captured through documentation. Both dimensions of knowledge exist in this scenario — the developer has both documentable procedures (explicit) and judgment-based experience (tacit). Option A alone fails to transfer tacit knowledge. Option B alone fails to capture explicit knowledge for long-term access. Option D is disproportionate and not warranted when alternative transfer mechanisms exist.
**Mindset Lesson:** Tacit and explicit knowledge require different transfer mechanisms; one-size-fits-all knowledge transfer approaches leave critical knowledge behind.

---

**Q5 — Correct Sequence: C → A → D → B → E**

**PMI Justification:** The defect-causing testing shortcut is the highest priority — it requires a corrective action change request (C) before any other action. All three lessons are then documented in the lessons learned register (A). The successful testing improvement is embedded in the Definition of Done (D) as an immediate practice change. The API pattern is shared externally with the CoP (B) to benefit broader organizational knowledge. The standup improvement, being a practice recommendation, goes to the PMO (E) as a broader organizational suggestion. Immediate corrective action takes priority over knowledge documentation, which takes priority over practice standardization, which takes priority over organizational sharing.
**Mindset Lesson:** Correct the defect first, capture the lesson second, embed the improvement third, share the success last.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: D**

**PMI Justification:** Earned Value Analysis is a tool of the Monitor and Control Cost process, not of Manage Project Knowledge. Knowledge management tools (A), information management tools (B), interpersonal and team skills (C), and expert judgment (E) are all legitimate tools in the Manage Project Knowledge process per PMBOK 6 (which maps directly to PMBOK 7's integrated approach). Earned Value measures financial performance — it has no role in knowledge creation or transfer.
**Mindset Lesson:** Know which tools belong to which process — EVA belongs to cost control, not knowledge management.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Section 5) requires that lessons learned reflect accurate information. A PM's professional obligation to honest knowledge capture supersedes a PMO director's preference for relationship management. Option A (comply with framing guidance) allows sanitized lessons to corrupt the repository — a direct ethics violation. Option C (two versions) creates a dual-record system that undermines integrity. Option D (omit contested items) produces an incomplete and misleading knowledge base.
**Mindset Lesson:** Lessons learned are organizational truth, not diplomatic narrative — accuracy is a professional obligation, not a choice.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Team and Stakeholder Performance Domains) and knowledge management theory both support structured, regular cross-team knowledge exchange as the most effective silo-breaking mechanism. Combining cadences and a shared register ensures both tracks benefit from each other's learning without forcing format alignment or structural merger. Option A (force retrospective documentation into compliance format) imposes bureaucracy on the agile track and misaligns the retrospective's purpose. Option C (merge tracks) is a structural change requiring formal change control and is disproportionate. Option D (compliance lead attends retrospectives) creates a one-directional knowledge bridge and does not give the compliance track access to agile insights.
**Mindset Lesson:** Silos break through structured sharing events, not structural mergers or one-way observation.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Uncertainty and Planning Performance Domains) requires the PM to proactively manage risks by engaging alternatives and presenting options with analysis. The knowledge gap is an active execution risk requiring immediate response — not waiting (A) or proceeding without mitigation (D). Escalating to the regulatory body (C) is inappropriate — regulatory bodies do not manage project resource gaps; that is an internal escalation failure. The sponsor has already been informed; the PM's next step is to present a structured options analysis and recommendation to enable a sponsor decision.
**Mindset Lesson:** Knowledge gaps are execution risks — the PM's job is to identify alternatives and present options, not to wait or proceed blindly.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:** A community of practice pairing legacy and digital experts (A) addresses tacit knowledge transfer through human interaction — the highest-value knowledge management activity for this team mix. Continuous lessons learned register updates (C) are explicitly required during execution, not deferred to phase gates. Peer learning sessions (E) mirror the CoP approach and directly address the tacit knowledge transfer need through structured human interaction. Option B (phase-gate only capture) contradicts the continuous capture requirement of Manage Project Knowledge. Option D (formal documentation only) fails for tacit knowledge transfer. Option F (weekly reports to PM) centralizes knowledge in the PM rather than building team-wide knowledge sharing.
**Mindset Lesson:** Tacit knowledge between experienced and new team members transfers through structured human interaction — documentation alone fails this knowledge type.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Manage Project Knowledge is an Executing process — lessons capture starts at day 1, not at project close.
- Two flows: existing knowledge IN (leverage OPAs and prior lessons) and new knowledge OUT (lessons learned register → repository).
- Tacit knowledge transfers through human interaction only — mentoring, shadowing, communities of practice; documentation is insufficient.
- Explicit knowledge is captured in systems — wikis, intranets, document libraries.
- The PM facilitates knowledge creation; the team owns it — individual hoarding is a dysfunction, not an asset.
- Lessons learned register is updated continuously; it becomes a repository OPA update at project close.
- Sanitizing lessons learned violates the Code of Ethics — accuracy is an obligation, not a choice.
- In high-turnover environments (Bangladesh banking IT), knowledge transfer is a risk management imperative, not an HR courtesy.
- Retrospectives are the agile knowledge engine — each sprint produces actionable organizational learning.
- **When a key expert leaves mid-project, your FIRST move is structured human knowledge transfer — mentoring and shadowing before documentation, not after.**
