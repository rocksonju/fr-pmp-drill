# PMP Exam Master Brief: Control Quality

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Control Quality is the process of monitoring and recording results of quality management activities to assess performance and recommend necessary changes. It operates throughout the project lifecycle, comparing actual deliverables against quality requirements defined in the Quality Management Plan. Unlike Manage Quality (which audits processes), Control Quality inspects actual work products — outputs, deliverables, and project results. It produces verified deliverables that feed Validate Scope, creating a critical handoff sequence. Exam weight is moderate (~7–9% of quality-related questions), but it bleeds into scope, risk, and stakeholder domains heavily. In regulated industries like banking, Control Quality is often where audit trails are born — every defect found here becomes a risk event and a compliance data point. The distinction between this process and Manage Quality trips up experienced PMs because both involve quality, but the scope, timing, and outputs differ fundamentally.

**In one sentence:** Control Quality inspects deliverables against standards, produces verified deliverables, and feeds the formal acceptance process — it is inspection-driven, not audit-driven.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|------|-------------------|
| **Predictive** | Formal inspection gates, acceptance criteria defined upfront, Statistical Process Control charts, defect logs, rework cycles tracked against schedule buffer |
| **Agile** | Built-in quality via Definition of Done (DoD), sprint reviews serve as quality checkpoints, teams self-inspect continuously rather than a separate QC phase |
| **Hybrid** | Iterative deliverables inspected at sprint end against formal acceptance criteria; defects logged in both team board and formal quality register; regulatory gates still apply |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Control Quality happens BEFORE Validate Scope.** Real-world instinct: "the client will check it." PMI instinct: the PM verifies quality internally first, then presents verified deliverables to the sponsor/client for formal acceptance. Never let the customer find your defects for you.
2. **Defects found in Control Quality must be repaired, not explained away.** Experienced PMs negotiate timelines when defects surface. PMI says: repair first, adjust schedule after — quality is not tradeable.
3. **Prevention over inspection is the quality philosophy — but Control Quality is still necessary.** Real-world PMs think catching defects late means QC failed. PMI views Control Quality as a required safety net even when prevention is working.
4. **Statistical sampling is preferred when 100% inspection is impossible or cost-prohibitive.** Experienced PMs resist sampling because it feels like cutting corners. PMI endorses it as a validated technique for high-volume deliverables.
5. **Quality metrics are compared to baselines, not to stakeholder opinions.** Real-world instinct: if the client is happy, quality is fine. PMI: quality is measured against documented, pre-agreed standards — stakeholder satisfaction is a separate variable.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** Reporting defects honestly even when it delays delivery or embarrasses the team. In Bangladesh Bank/BSEC regulatory submissions, concealing known defects is a compliance violation, not just a PMI concern.
- **Honesty:** Accurately recording nonconformance data in quality control measurements — falsifying QC logs is an ethics breach under PMI Code.
- **Fairness:** Applying the same inspection standards to all deliverables regardless of which team member produced them.
- **Respect:** Treating rework as a process improvement opportunity, not a blame exercise — Control Quality data informs retrospectives without targeting individuals.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Control Quality** | Process of inspecting deliverables against quality standards; produces Verified Deliverables | Do NOT confuse with Manage Quality (audits processes); CQ inspects products |
| **Verified Deliverables** | Output of Control Quality; inputs to Validate Scope | Sequence trap: Validate Scope cannot start without Verified Deliverables from CQ |
| **Quality Control Measurements** | Documented results of CQ activities; input to Manage Quality for process analysis | Measurements go back upstream to Manage Quality — bidirectional flow |
| **Defect Repair** | Corrective action to fix a nonconforming deliverable; triggers Change Request | Defect repair always requires a formal change request — not informal fixes |
| **Definition of Done (DoD)** | Agile checklist of conditions a deliverable must meet to be considered complete | In hybrid, DoD supplements formal acceptance criteria — both apply |
| **Statistical Process Control (SPC)** | Method using control charts to determine if a process is stable and predictable | A process can be IN control but still produce defects if spec limits differ from control limits |
| **Control Chart** | Displays process data over time with upper/lower control limits (UCL/LCL) | Rule of Seven: 7 consecutive points on one side of mean = special cause variation — act even without breach of limits |
| **Attribute Sampling** | Pass/fail inspection — conforming or nonconforming | Used when measurement is binary; contrast with variable sampling (continuous measurement) |
| **Variable Sampling** | Measures degree of conformance on a numeric scale | More informative than attribute sampling but costlier — PMI recommends where feasible |
| **Prevention Cost vs Appraisal Cost** | Prevention = stopping defects; Appraisal = inspection/testing costs | Both are cost of conformance; do NOT classify appraisal as cost of nonconformance |
| **Cost of Nonconformance** | Internal failure (rework, scrap) + External failure (warranty, recalls, reputational loss) | External failure costs are always higher than internal — this drives "catch early" mindset |
| **Inspection** | Formal examination of work product to determine compliance with standards | Inspection ≠ testing; inspection covers documentation, deliverables, not just functional tests |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Deliverable fails quality inspection" | Document the nonconformance and raise a change request for defect repair | Formal process: CQ → defect log → change request → rework cycle |
| "Customer rejects deliverable during acceptance" | Determine if defect was present during Control Quality | Sequence issue: if CQ passed it, process breakdown occurred; if CQ missed it, update QC process |
| "Sponsor asks PM to approve deliverable despite known defect" | Refuse approval and formally document the nonconformance | Ethics + Responsibility: PMI Code prohibits suppressing known defects |
| "Team disputes whether a deliverable meets DoD" | Reference the agreed DoD and acceptance criteria; facilitate resolution against documented standards | Standards, not opinions, resolve quality disputes |
| "Quality metrics show increasing defect trend over 3 sprints" | Conduct root cause analysis and update the quality management approach | Trend data is a signal for process improvement, not just rework |
| "Control chart shows 7 points below mean but within limits" | Investigate for special cause variation (Rule of Seven) | Rule of Seven applies even without limit breach — this is a common exam trap |
| "Stakeholder requests informal acceptance bypass to save time" | Decline and follow the formal Validate Scope process | Skipping Validate Scope creates scope acceptance risk and contract exposure |
| "New regulatory standard is issued mid-project (e.g., BSEC audit requirement)" | Update the Quality Management Plan and quality baselines via change control | Regulatory changes are external events requiring formal change request, not informal adjustments |
| "Test results show process is in control but deliverables still fail customer specs" | Check if control limits match specification limits — adjust process target | In-control ≠ meets specs; the process target may need recalibration |

**Topic-Specific Weak-Spot Scenarios — Escalation Edge Cases**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "QC manager wants to ship with known minor defect to meet milestone" | Formally document risk, raise change request for waiver with sponsor approval | PMI: all known defects must be disclosed; delivery decision belongs to sponsor via formal process, not PM unilaterally |
| "Audit finds that QC measurements were not being recorded for 3 weeks" | Stop, assess scope of gap, implement corrective action, update PMIL log | Process breakdown must be corrected before resuming — retrospective correction of records is secondary to fixing the process |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

```
CONTROL QUALITY — DECISION FLOW

       Deliverable Produced
               |
               v
     [Inspect vs. Quality Standards]
       /                    \
  CONFORMS              DOES NOT CONFORM
      |                         |
      v                         v
 Verified                 Document Defect
 Deliverable             (Quality Control
      |                   Measurements)
      v                         |
 VALIDATE SCOPE              Change Request
 (Sponsor Acceptance)      (Defect Repair)
                                 |
                                 v
                         Rework / Repair
                                 |
                                 v
                        Re-Inspect (loop back)
```

**Mnemonic: "INSPECT-VERIFY-ACCEPT" (IVA)**
- **I** = Inspect deliverable against standards (Control Quality)
- **V** = Verify it passes (Verified Deliverable is the output)
- **A** = Accept it formally (Validate Scope — separate process)

IVA sequence is unbreakable on the PMP exam. Any question that tries to blend inspection with acceptance is testing whether you respect this boundary.

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Formal inspection gates at phase boundaries (phase-gate reviews)
- Quality control measurements documented in project management information system (PMIS)
- Acceptance criteria defined in Scope Statement and WBS Dictionary
- Defect density tracked as a KPI against quality baseline
- Formal nonconformance reports (NCR) generated and tracked through closure

### Agile / Hybrid
- Definition of Done replaces or supplements formal acceptance criteria
- Sprint Review is the primary quality gate — team demonstrates working software
- Continuous integration/continuous delivery (CI/CD) pipelines serve as automated Control Quality
- Technical debt tracking is the agile analog of defect log
- In hybrid: sprint-level DoD + phase-gate inspection for regulatory deliverables (common in banking IT projects)

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Bangladesh Bank / BSEC regulated projects:** Audit trail requirements mean every Control Quality activity must be documented and timestamped — informal verbal approvals do not constitute QC evidence
- **Core banking system implementations:** Test case traceability matrix linking requirements to test results is a Control Quality artifact required for Bangladesh Bank CBS approval
- **Capital markets systems:** BSEC requires UAT sign-off documentation — Control Quality outputs are formal evidence of compliance, not internal records
- **ISO 9001 environments:** Control Quality maps to verification activities in the product realization process — gap between QMS requirements and project QC is a common audit finding
- **High-regulation projects:** Separate QA/QC roles mandated; PM must ensure independence between the team producing deliverables and the team inspecting them

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Proceed to Validate Scope immediately after deliverable is complete" | Experienced PMs skip internal QC when client relationship is strong and timeline is tight | Validate Scope requires Verified Deliverables — skipping CQ violates process sequence and creates undisclosed defect risk |
| "Inform the sponsor of the defect and let them decide whether to accept" | Real PMs escalate decisions upward to avoid accountability | PMI: inform after a change request is raised; escalation without formal documentation is process bypass |
| "Update the Quality Management Plan when a defect is found" | Seems proactive — finding a defect suggests the plan needs updating | CQ output is Quality Control Measurements + Verified Deliverables; updating QMP requires change control and belongs to Manage Quality process |
| "Conduct a quality audit when defects are repeatedly found" | Quality audit sounds like the right escalation for recurring defects | Quality audits belong to Manage Quality (a Planning/Executing process), not Control Quality; the CQ response is root cause analysis + corrective action |
| "Accept the deliverable with a note that it will be fixed in the next phase" | Common in agile projects where partial completion is normal | PMI predictive framework: a deliverable either passes QC or it does not; conditional acceptance without formal change request is nonconformance handling, not quality control |
| "Use inspection to test all deliverables regardless of volume" | Thoroughness instinct — 100% inspection seems safest | PMI endorses statistical sampling when 100% inspection is cost-prohibitive or technically unfeasible; blind 100% inspection may itself introduce errors |
| "Skip QC for low-risk deliverables to save time" | Risk-based thinking — experienced PMs triage effort | PMI does not endorse skipping QC based on PM's risk judgment alone; tailoring is done at planning time, not ad hoc during execution |
| "Defect found by client during UAT is acceptable — UAT is the quality gate" | UAT feels like a formal quality gate in many organizations | PMI views client-found defects as Control Quality failures; the PM's job is to present defect-free deliverables to Validate Scope |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM is managing a core banking system upgrade for a Bangladesh Bank-regulated institution. Control Quality testing has revealed a critical defect in the transaction reconciliation module. The project sponsor, a senior VP, instructs the PM to proceed with the go-live milestone as planned, noting that the defect affects only 0.3% of transactions and will be patched in the next release. The sponsor states that Bangladesh Bank's audit is three months away and the defect will be fixed by then. The PM has not yet submitted a formal defect report. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Proceed with go-live as instructed by the sponsor; the defect rate is below the acceptable threshold and sponsor has authority  
B. Document the defect formally in the quality control measurements and raise a change request for defect repair before go-live  
C. Escalate to the project steering committee immediately without documenting the defect  
D. Proceed with go-live but inform the Bangladesh Bank compliance team of the known defect

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A Scrum team is two sprints into a capital markets data platform project. During the Sprint Review, the product owner consistently accepts backlog items that the development team considers incomplete against the DoD. The QA team lead raises this as a quality concern in the retrospective. In the next sprint, the same pattern repeats. What is the BEST action for the Scrum Master to take?

A. Escalate to the project sponsor that the product owner is compromising quality  
B. Revise the Definition of Done to lower the bar so fewer items are flagged as incomplete  
C. Facilitate a working session with the product owner and team to align on DoD criteria and create shared understanding before the next sprint  
D. Remove the QA team lead from retrospective discussions to prevent recurring conflict

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team is delivering a regulatory reporting module for a BSEC-licensed broker. The development track delivers sprints under a DoD; the overall project has a phase-gate inspection before submission to BSEC. At the phase-gate review, the inspection team identifies 14 minor defects and 2 critical defects. The delivery manager argues that all sprint items passed DoD, so the phase-gate defects are invalid. The PM needs to act. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Side with the inspection team and order immediate rework of all 16 defects  
B. Facilitate a meeting between the sprint team and inspection team to resolve the DoD vs. acceptance criteria gap  
C. Submit the deliverable to BSEC and note the defects in the risk register  
D. Update the Quality Management Plan to eliminate the phase-gate inspection since DoD already covers quality

---

**Q4 — Two-Correct, Pick-Best (Predictive)**

A PM's Control Quality process shows a consistent upward trend in defect density over the last four reporting periods. The defects are concentrated in one functional module. The PM needs to respond. Which of the following is the BEST next action?

A. Add more QC inspectors to the failing module to increase inspection coverage  
B. Conduct a root cause analysis on the defect-dense module and recommend a corrective action  
C. Escalate to the functional manager responsible for the module and request staffing changes  
D. Update the risk register with a new risk for project quality failure

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

2. Demonstrate working feature in Sprint Review  
3. Move card to "Done" column

A. 1 → 5 → 2 → 4 → 3  
B. 5 → 1 → 2 → 4 → 3

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A PM discovers during Control Quality that a batch of user acceptance tests was recorded as "pass" by a junior tester who did not actually run the tests. The sprint is already closed and the deliverable has been presented to the product owner as verified. The PM is aware that correcting the record will re-open the sprint, delay the release, and likely damage the tester's employment. What is the PMI-correct action?

A. Do not reopen the sprint; the product owner accepted the deliverable in good faith  
B. Quietly run the tests retrospectively and update the records if they pass  
C. Disclose the inaccuracy to the product owner, re-run the tests, and correct the quality records formally  
D. Raise it with the tester's manager privately and allow them to handle it without involving the product owner

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A PM is managing a hybrid banking digitization project. Control Quality measurements show a pattern of defects that correlates with requirements ambiguity in the customer onboarding module. The development team is remote, the QA team is co-located with the PM, and the business analyst is on-site with the client. What is the BEST communication approach to address the quality issue?

A. Send a formal written defect report to all stakeholders and wait for responses  
B. Hold a joint working session with the BA, development team, and QA team to clarify requirements and update acceptance criteria  
C. Ask the QA team to increase inspection frequency until the defect rate drops

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM is overseeing a hybrid project delivering a risk management system for a bank. The QA team has flagged that 23% of deliverables in the current phase failed first-pass inspection — significantly above the 5% threshold in the Quality Management Plan. The team argues that the acceptance criteria are unclear and the threshold itself is unrealistic. The project is behind schedule. The sponsor is pushing for accelerated delivery. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Grant a temporary waiver on the quality threshold to unblock delivery, given schedule pressure from the sponsor  
B. Escalate immediately to the sponsor that the QA threshold cannot be met and request a scope reduction  
C. Conduct a root cause analysis on the inspection failures and assess whether the threshold, criteria, or process needs updating via formal change control  
D. Reduce the QA team's involvement to accelerate delivery while tracking defects in the risk register

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Delivery Domain + PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility). Control Quality requires formal documentation of all nonconformances. Known defects must be disclosed via change requests, not suppressed by sponsor directive. The 0.3% rationalization is a real-world negotiation tactic; PMI treats it as irrelevant — if the defect exists and is known, it must be formally addressed. Option A fails because sponsor authority does not override ethical obligation to report defects. Option C fails because escalation without documentation is process bypass. Option D fails because informal notification to Bangladesh Bank without internal formal process is both procedurally and ethically incomplete.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor rank does not override the obligation to formally document and process a known defect through change control.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide Chapter 4 (Implementing Agile) + Scrum Guide (Sprint Review, Retrospective). The Scrum Master's role is to facilitate, not escalate or lower standards. The root cause is misalignment on DoD — the fix is shared understanding, not organizational escalation (A), definition degradation (B), or silencing dissent (D). PMI's agile mindset prioritizes team self-organization and collaborative resolution over hierarchical escalation.

**Mindset Lesson:** When quality standards are consistently misapplied, the first fix is alignment and shared understanding — not escalation or redefinition.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team Performance Domain + Hybrid approach tailoring. In a hybrid environment, DoD and formal acceptance criteria coexist and may have different scopes. Before declaring either party right, the PM's FIRST action is to understand the gap. Option A is premature — ordering rework before understanding the conflict could be waste if the inspection criteria are flawed. Option C bypasses internal resolution and creates regulatory exposure. Option D removes a required quality gate.

**Mindset Lesson:** In hybrid environments, conflicts between agile quality gates and predictive inspection gates are resolved by understanding the gap first, not by declaring one superior.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Delivery Domain, quality) + Quality Management (root cause analysis as CQ response). Option B is best because a trend requires a cause investigation, not a volume response. Option A (more inspectors) addresses symptom, not cause. Option C is a HR escalation that bypasses quality process. Option D (risk register) is appropriate but secondary — fixing the problem comes before logging it as a risk.

**Mindset Lesson:** A defect trend is a root cause problem, not an inspection coverage problem — always investigate cause before increasing inspection volume.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide (Kanban, flow-based quality) + DoD application. Correct sequence: peer review against DoD (1) → deploy to staging for QA testing (5) → demonstrate in review (2) → product owner acceptance (4) → move to Done (3). The card moves to "Done" only after all quality gates are cleared AND acceptance is received. Option B skips peer review before staging. Option C demoes before QA testing. Option D skips peer review entirely.

**Mindset Lesson:** "Done" means accepted, verified, and tested — not just coded; the card moves last.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / PMBOK 6 process ITTOs. Quality Audits are an output of Manage Quality (an Executing process), not Control Quality. Verified Deliverables (A), Quality Control Measurements (B), and Change Requests for defect repair (C) are all legitimate Control Quality outputs. This question tests the CQ vs. Manage Quality boundary, which is one of the most heavily tested quality distinctions on the exam.

**Mindset Lesson:** Quality Audits belong to Manage Quality; Control Quality produces Verified Deliverables and measurements — know this boundary cold.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty value, Section 5) + Responsibility value. Falsified test records are a material misrepresentation. PMI's honesty standard requires correcting inaccurate information regardless of consequence. Option A perpetuates the misrepresentation. Option B (retrospective quiet fix) is ethically problematic — it conceals the original failure. Option D (manager handling privately) does not correct the quality record and leaves the product owner with false information.

**Mindset Lesson:** Honesty requires correcting false records even when the personal cost is high — there is no PMI-endorsed quiet workaround.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder and Communication Domains + Agile Practice Guide (colocation and communication effectiveness). Root cause is requirements ambiguity — the fix requires the BA (who has client context), the dev team (who produces against requirements), and QA (who inspects) to align. Option A (written report) is too slow and lacks the interaction quality needed for ambiguity resolution. Option C treats a requirements problem as an inspection problem. Option D escalates a solvable team-level issue prematurely.

**Mindset Lesson:** When defects trace to requirements ambiguity, the solution is direct engagement between the people who write, implement, and test requirements — not escalation or increased inspection.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Delivery Domain, Measurement Domain) + Quality Management Plan baseline management. A 23% failure rate against a 5% threshold is a significant variance that requires understanding before action. Root cause analysis determines whether the threshold is wrong, the criteria are ambiguous, or the process has broken down — three completely different solutions. Option A (waiver) bypasses quality governance without evidence. Option B escalates without analysis. Option D removes a quality mechanism under schedule pressure, which is the classic PMI trap.

**Mindset Lesson:** Never grant quality waivers or reduce quality oversight under schedule pressure without a root cause investigation — schedule and quality are both baselines, not one-way levers.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: B and D**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / PMBOK 6 ITTO mapping. Verified Deliverables (B) are the primary CQ output that feeds Validate Scope. Quality Control Measurements (D) feed back into Manage Quality for process improvement analysis. Quality Checklists (A) are an input to CQ, not an output. Quality Metrics (C) are an output of the Quality Management Plan (Planning) and an input to CQ. Quality Management Plan Updates (E) require change control and are not a direct CQ output.

**Mindset Lesson:** Control Quality outputs flow forward (Verified Deliverables → Validate Scope) and backward (QC Measurements → Manage Quality) — it is a two-directional feed process.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Control Quality inspects deliverables; Manage Quality audits processes — never swap these two
- The output sequence is: Control Quality → Verified Deliverables → Validate Scope (cannot skip)
- Every defect found requires a formal change request — no informal fixes, no silent patches
- Rule of Seven: 7 consecutive points on one side of mean = special cause variation, act immediately
- Cost of conformance = prevention + appraisal; cost of nonconformance = internal + external failure
- Statistical sampling is a PMI-endorsed technique, not a quality shortcut
- Quality Audits belong to Manage Quality, not Control Quality
- In hybrid: DoD and formal acceptance criteria coexist — a gap between them must be resolved before escalation
- In regulated environments (Bangladesh Bank, BSEC), QC records are compliance artifacts — falsification is both a PMI ethics breach and a regulatory offense
- Sponsor authority does not override the obligation to document and formally process known defects

**When you see a deliverable defect in any question — document it, raise a change request, re-inspect — never skip, hide, or informally accept.**
