# PMP Exam Master Brief: Estimate Costs

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Estimate Costs is the process of developing an approximation of the monetary resources needed to complete project work, and it is the second process in the Cost Management Knowledge Area, sitting entirely in the Planning Process Group. The process uses the scope baseline (WBS + WBS Dictionary + Scope Statement), the schedule baseline, and the resource management plan as its primary drivers, reflecting that cost estimates are inseparable from scope and time decisions. The primary outputs are activity cost estimates and basis of estimates — the latter being a critical document that records assumptions, constraints, range, and confidence level for each estimate, making estimates auditable and defensible. On the PMP exam, Estimate Costs questions account for a disproportionate share of cost-domain difficulty because the exam tests not just formula mechanics but the judgment to select the right estimating technique given project phase, data availability, and stakeholder expectations. Cross-domain bleed is heavy: risk reserves (contingency vs. management reserve), procurement cost estimates, and resource cost rates all appear in Estimate Costs scenarios. A common exam trap is confusing Estimate Costs (activity-level estimates) with Determine Budget (aggregating those estimates into a baseline) — the two processes are sequential and distinct. Practitioners routinely conflate estimating with budgeting, or skip basis-of-estimates documentation, which PMI tests as a governance failure. In one sentence: Estimate Costs produces the defensible, technique-driven monetary approximations for each work activity, governed by the rules established in the Cost Management Plan.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal activity cost estimates are developed using analogous, parametric, or bottom-up methods per the Cost Management Plan; basis of estimates is fully documented for audit and change control purposes. |
| Agile | Cost estimates are expressed as burn rate per sprint or story-point cost ratios; estimates are intentionally rough at the release level and refined progressively as backlog items are elaborated. |
| Hybrid | High-level analogous or parametric estimates govern the predictive portions; agile workstreams use velocity-based cost approximations; both feed a unified cost register with documented confidence levels. |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. The basis of estimates is as important as the estimates themselves — real-world PMs frequently omit documentation of assumptions and methodology, but PMI treats undocumented estimates as uncontrollable liabilities because stakeholders cannot evaluate their validity.
2. Contingency reserves are part of the cost estimate; management reserves are not — experienced PMs often lump all reserves into the budget discussion, but PMI requires contingency (known-unknown risk) to be estimated at the activity level while management reserve is added at the project level during Determine Budget.
3. Costs must be estimated for all resources, not just labor — practitioners focus on labor and often underestimate materials, equipment, and vendor costs; PMI tests holistic resource cost coverage explicitly.
4. The estimating method must match the available data and project phase — experienced PMs apply bottom-up by habit because it feels rigorous, but applying it before the WBS is complete produces false precision; PMI tests the judgment to select the phase-appropriate technique.
5. Cost estimates are iterative and will be refined — practitioners treat early estimates as commitments and resist revision; PMI expects progressive elaboration and treats refusal to update estimates as a planning integrity failure.

**Ethics Angle**

- Honesty: Basis of estimates must disclose assumptions truthfully, including uncertainty ranges; optimistic-biased estimates that understate range to please sponsors violate PMI's honesty standard.
- Responsibility: The PM is accountable for the accuracy representation of estimates; presenting parametric estimates as bottom-up precision (or vice versa) misrepresents reliability.
- Fairness: Applying consistent estimating methods across all work packages prevents favored teams from receiving inflated estimates (padding) while others are under-resourced.
- Respect: Soliciting input from subject matter experts and acknowledging their estimating expertise in the basis of estimates respects their contribution and produces better cost data.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Activity Cost Estimate | An approximation of the cost of resources required to complete a scheduled activity; includes all direct and indirect costs. | Activity cost estimates do not include management reserve — that is added at the project level in Determine Budget. |
| Basis of Estimates | Documentation supporting activity cost estimates: methodology used, assumptions, constraints, range, confidence level. | Basis of estimates is an output of Estimate Costs, not a risk document — it is a cost governance artifact. |
| Analogous Estimating | Uses historical cost data from similar past activities/projects; top-down; fast, lower accuracy. Formula: Not a single formula — expert judgment applied to historical actuals. | Appropriate when limited detail is available; do not use when WBS and resource plans are fully defined. |
| Parametric Estimating | Statistical model using a cost-per-unit rate × quantity. Formula: Estimate = Unit Rate × Quantity. | Only valid when the unit rate is statistically reliable and the relationship is scalable to project conditions. |
| Bottom-Up Estimating | Estimate each work package or activity individually and sum upward. Most accurate; most time-intensive. | Requires a complete WBS; applying bottom-up without a defined WBS creates false precision. |
| Three-Point Estimate (PERT) | E = (O + 4M + P) / 6; Standard Deviation = (P − O) / 6; Variance = [(P − O) / 6]² | For triangular distribution: E = (O + M + P) / 3 — the exam will specify which to use; default to PERT unless told otherwise. |
| Contingency Reserve | Budget included within the cost baseline to address identified risks (known-unknowns); part of the activity or work package estimate. | Contingency reserve IS part of the cost baseline. Management reserve is NOT in the baseline. |
| Management Reserve | Budget held outside the cost baseline for unknown-unknown risks; authorized by management, not the PM. | The PM does not control management reserve; requesting it requires management approval and typically a change request. |
| Cost of Quality (CoQ) | Total cost of conformance (prevention + appraisal) plus cost of non-conformance (internal + external failures). | CoQ is an input consideration to cost estimating — higher quality investment now often reduces non-conformance costs later. |
| Reserve Analysis | Technique to determine contingency reserve amounts, typically as a percentage of activity cost or using decision tree / Monte Carlo analysis. | Reserve analysis produces contingency reserves within the estimate, not management reserves outside it. |
| Vendor Bid Analysis | Comparing vendor proposals to estimate reasonable costs; used when procurement is involved. | Vendor bids are estimates, not commitments; the lowest bid is not necessarily the best cost estimate for planning purposes. |
| EAC (Estimate at Completion) | EAC = BAC / CPI — if current efficiency continues; EAC = AC + (BAC − EV) — if remaining work at planned rate; EAC = AC + [(BAC − EV) / (CPI × SPI)] — schedule-sensitive. | The stem always signals which formula applies; "work will continue at current efficiency" = BAC/CPI. |
| ETC (Estimate to Complete) | ETC = EAC − AC (remaining cost from status date to completion). | ETC is not the same as remaining budget (BAC − EV); ETC incorporates performance forecasting. |
| TCPI | TCPI = (BAC − EV) / (BAC − AC) for original baseline; (BAC − EV) / (EAC − AC) for revised. | TCPI > 1.0 means efficiency must improve — this is a warning signal, not an achievement target. |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "WBS is complete; resource plan is approved; ready to estimate costs" | Apply the estimating methodology defined in the Cost Management Plan to each work package | The Cost Management Plan governs the technique to use; ad hoc method selection contradicts approved governance. |
| "Sponsor requests a cost estimate at project charter stage before scope is defined" | Provide a rough order of magnitude (ROM) estimate with a clearly documented range (−25% to +75%) and confidence level | ROM is the phase-appropriate technique; claiming higher precision before scope definition misrepresents reliability. |
| "Stakeholder challenges an estimate as too high with no supporting rationale" | Refer to the basis of estimates document to explain the methodology, assumptions, and confidence range | Basis of estimates is the audit and defense document for cost estimates; undocumented estimates cannot be defended. |
| "Two SMEs produce significantly different estimates for the same activity" | Facilitate a reconciliation session; document the basis and assumptions behind each estimate; select the most defensible approach | Divergent estimates signal differing assumptions; resolving them produces a better estimate and surfaces hidden risks. |
| "Project is in early planning; detailed WBS not yet available" | Use analogous or parametric estimating with documented confidence range | Bottom-up requires WBS completeness; applying it prematurely introduces false precision. |
| "A previously estimated work package scope has changed via approved change request" | Update the activity cost estimates and basis of estimates to reflect the approved scope change | Approved scope changes require corresponding cost estimate updates; failing to update produces baseline misalignment. |
| "Contingency reserve amount is disputed by the sponsor as excessive" | Present the reserve analysis methodology and the identified risks driving the reserve | Reserve analysis is traceable to identified risks; the sponsor's challenge is a scope-of-risk discussion, not a budget negotiation. |
| "Team member inflates estimates to create personal schedule buffer" | Address through individual performance management and enforce the Cost Management Plan's estimating standards | Estimate padding is a governance violation; it corrupts the cost baseline and creates false risk coverage. |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Management instructs the PM to reduce estimates by 20% to fit the approved budget before scope is adjusted" | Reject the arbitrary reduction; present the basis of estimates and initiate scope/quality trade-off analysis through integrated change control | Forcing estimates to fit a budget without scope adjustment creates a false baseline; the PM's integrity requires honest estimates. |
| "Vendor submits a bid significantly below all other bids and the internal estimate" | Conduct vendor bid analysis; investigate assumptions behind the outlier bid before accepting it as a cost benchmark | An anomalous low bid may reflect missing scope, unrealistic assumptions, or a buy-in strategy — accepting it without analysis introduces schedule and quality risk. |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Estimating technique matches project phase: analogous at charter, parametric at high-level planning, bottom-up after WBS completion.
- Basis of estimates is a formal document, version-controlled, and referenced in change requests when estimates are updated.
- Contingency reserve is calculated per risk register, using reserve analysis (percentage, expected monetary value, or Monte Carlo simulation for complex projects).
- Three-point estimating (PERT) is applied to high-uncertainty activities, especially those on the critical path.

**Agile/Hybrid**
- Story-point cost ratios or sprint burn rates replace activity-level estimates; cost per point is calibrated over the first 2–3 sprints.
- Rolling-wave refinement: high-level estimates for distant epics, detailed estimates only for next 1–2 sprints in backlog refinement.
- Hybrid: predictive WBS activities use formal estimating techniques; agile work packages use burn rate approximations; both are captured in a unified cost register with different confidence levels.
- Relative estimation (planning poker) is a team-based technique for agile story sizing that feeds into cost burn rate calculations.

**Industry/Regulatory Factors**
- Banking/Capital Markets/BSEC context: regulatory project cost estimates must often include compliance verification costs, data remediation costs, and technology audit costs as separate line items; basis of estimates must reference regulatory requirements to withstand external audit.
- Government/Defense: Independent Cost Estimates (ICE) may be required alongside the PM's estimates; significant variance between ICE and PM estimates requires reconciliation before baseline approval.
- Construction: unit cost databases (RS Means, regional indices) provide parametric baselines; location factors must be documented in basis of estimates.
- IT/Software: function point analysis, use-case point estimating, and COCOMO models are valid parametric approaches; the chosen model must be documented in basis of estimates.
- Pharmaceutical: regulatory submission and validation activities carry significant cost uncertainty and require explicit contingency reserves with documented rationale.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Use bottom-up estimating at project initiation" | Bottom-up feels rigorous and comprehensive; experienced PMs want defensible numbers early. | Bottom-up requires a complete WBS; at initiation, the WBS does not exist; false precision misleads stakeholders. |
| "Omit basis of estimates to save time on a fast-moving project" | Documentation feels like overhead; experienced PMs trust their judgment and verbal communication. | Basis of estimates is required for change control, audit, and dispute resolution; its absence makes estimates indefensible. |
| "Include management reserve in the activity cost estimate" | Reserve is reserve; experienced PMs want a single contingency buffer at the activity level for flexibility. | Management reserve is held outside the cost baseline and controlled by management, not by the PM at the activity level; mixing them corrupts the baseline. |
| "Accept the lowest vendor bid as the cost estimate for procured work" | Low bids save money; experienced PMs welcome cost savings and assume bids are competitive. | Anomalously low bids may reflect missing scope or buy-in strategies; vendor bid analysis is required before using any bid as a planning estimate. |
| "Adjust estimates downward to match the approved budget" | Budget is a real constraint; experienced PMs feel pressure to make the plan fit the money available. | Forcing estimates to fit a budget without scope change creates a false baseline; honest estimates must precede budget negotiation. |
| "Use the same estimating technique for all work packages regardless of detail available" | Consistency feels like good practice; switching methods seems arbitrary to experienced PMs. | The Cost Management Plan authorizes technique selection based on data availability; mechanical consistency ignores phase-appropriate accuracy requirements. |
| "Treat the cost estimate and the cost budget as the same document" | In practice, the estimate feeds directly into the budget; the distinction feels academic. | Estimate Costs and Determine Budget are separate processes with distinct outputs; conflating them bypasses the aggregation and reconciliation steps that produce a defensible baseline. |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are the PM of a securities trading platform upgrade at a regional bank. The project sponsor, who controls budget allocation, asks you to submit cost estimates to the steering committee that reflect a ±5% accuracy range. Your analysis shows the estimates, developed with partial WBS and analogous data, legitimately carry ±20% uncertainty. The sponsor believes a tighter range will expedite committee approval. You have not yet completed the WBS. What is the correct action?

A. Submit the estimates at ±5% as requested; the sponsor is accountable for accuracy to the committee.
B. Submit the estimates at ±20%, clearly documenting the current project phase, the analogous method used, and the conditions under which the range will narrow.
C. Delay submitting any estimate until the WBS is complete and bottom-up precision is achievable.
D. Submit ±5% to the committee but include a private risk register entry noting the true uncertainty range.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

An agile product team is six sprints into a fintech platform build. The product owner notices that the burn rate per sprint is consistently 30% higher than the initial estimate based on story-point cost ratios from Sprint 1. The team lead argues the initial estimates were too low and the current burn rate is more accurate. The PMO is holding the team to the original estimate. What is the BEST approach?

A. Adhere to the original estimate; deviations should be tracked as cost variance and addressed through corrective action.
B. Update the cost estimate using actual sprint burn rate data, document the revised basis of estimates, and communicate the updated forecast to the PMO and sponsor through the project reporting mechanism.
C. Ask the team to reduce scope to bring the burn rate back in line with the original estimate.
D. Escalate immediately to the steering committee that the project is over budget.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid regulatory compliance project. The predictive workstream has fully documented bottom-up estimates. The agile workstream is about to begin but has no cost estimates — the agile team lead argues that story-point velocity will emerge after two sprints and estimates are premature. The project sponsor requires a unified cost forecast before the first sprint begins. What should you FIRST do?

A. Wait for two sprints of velocity data before producing agile workstream cost estimates.
B. Apply analogous estimating or a high-level burn rate assumption to the agile workstream, document the confidence level as ROM, and include it in the unified forecast with explicit caveats.
C. Exclude the agile workstream from the cost forecast until velocity is established.
D. Override the agile team lead and require the team to produce bottom-up estimates before Sprint 1.

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is estimating costs for a construction project with a complete WBS, detailed resource plan, and access to a regional unit cost database with validated statistical reliability. Which TWO estimating approaches are most appropriate for this project?

A. Analogous estimating using costs from a similar project completed five years ago in a different region.
B. Parametric estimating using the validated unit cost database.
C. Bottom-up estimating from the WBS work packages.
D. Expert judgment alone, based on the project manager's personal experience.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile team is setting up cost estimating for a new digital banking product. Place the following in the correct order: (1) Calibrate cost per story point using first 2–3 sprint actuals, (2) Develop high-level burn rate estimate for release planning, (3) Estimate cost for sprint backlog items using refined cost-per-point ratio, (4) Document burn rate assumptions in basis of estimates.

A. 2, 4, 1, 3
B. 1, 2, 3, 4
C. 4, 2, 1, 3
D. 2, 1, 3, 4

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

All of the following are tools or techniques used in Estimate Costs EXCEPT:

A. Reserve analysis
B. Vendor bid analysis
C. Funding limit reconciliation
D. Three-point estimating

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are estimating costs for a government infrastructure project. A senior leader tells you to exclude certain high-risk activities from the cost estimate to make the project appear more affordable in the funding proposal. These activities are documented in the WBS and the risk register. If excluded, the official estimate will understate actual expected costs by approximately 35%. What do you do?

A. Exclude the activities as instructed; leadership bears responsibility for the funding proposal.
B. Include all WBS activities in the estimate, document the full basis of estimates including the high-risk activities, and formally communicate the risk of underfunding to the appropriate authority.
C. Include the activities in an internal working estimate but submit the reduced figure to the funding committee.
D. Raise the risk in the risk register and proceed with submitting the reduced estimate, since the risk is now documented.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Agile or Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team has completed bottom-up cost estimates for the predictive workstream and a burn rate estimate for the agile workstream. The finance department requests a single cost report in a traditional EVM format. The agile team's costs cannot be directly mapped to EVM without significant distortion. What is the BEST communication strategy?

A. Convert agile burn rate data to EVM format even if the conversion distorts the actual cost picture.
B. Produce a hybrid cost report that presents EVM data for the predictive workstream and burn rate / forecast data for the agile workstream, with a narrative explaining the two methodologies and their reconciliation at the program level.
C. Require the agile team to adopt EVM tracking going forward to simplify reporting.
D. Report only the predictive workstream in EVM format and present agile costs as a separate, unintegrated line item with no reconciliation.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are the PM of a hybrid capital markets data platform project. Your bottom-up estimates show a 25% cost overrun against the approved budget, driven by underestimated data migration complexity. The Cost Management Plan's variance threshold is ±15%. The sponsor instructs you to reforecast using a more optimistic parametric model to bring the estimate back under the threshold without a formal change request. What is the correct course of action?

A. Apply the optimistic parametric model as instructed to avoid triggering the change request process.
B. Maintain the bottom-up estimate as the most accurate reflection of known scope; initiate a change request since the variance exceeds the threshold; present both models to the sponsor with documented assumptions.
C. Average the bottom-up and parametric figures to produce a compromise estimate within the threshold.
D. Escalate directly to the steering committee without informing the sponsor.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty (mandatory standard). The PM must disclose the true uncertainty range, methodology, and conditions for improvement. Option A transfers ethical responsibility to the sponsor — PMI holds the PM personally accountable for the accuracy of estimates they submit. Option C creates unnecessary delay when ROM is the correct phase-appropriate output. Option D is a sophisticated ethics violation — presenting false precision publicly while maintaining a private truth.
**Mindset Lesson:** Accuracy ranges must reflect the actual estimating methodology and data quality; misrepresenting confidence to expedite approval is an ethics violation, not a stakeholder management skill.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide / PMBOK 7 — progressive elaboration and iterative planning. Actuals from six sprints provide more reliable cost-per-point data than an initial calibration estimate; cost estimates must be updated to reflect current reality. Option A freezes an invalidated estimate as a governance baseline — this creates a false picture. Option C reduces scope without analysis. Option D escalates prematurely without first updating the forecast.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, cost estimates are living forecasts; updating them with actual sprint data is not a control failure — refusing to update them is.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Analogous Estimating / Basis of Estimates. A unified forecast is required; the agile workstream cannot be excluded (Option C) or delayed (Option A) when a sponsor commitment requires it. ROM-level estimates with documented confidence are appropriate before velocity data exists. Option D misapplies a phase-inappropriate technique and undermines agile team autonomy without change control justification.
**Mindset Lesson:** Phase-appropriate estimating with honest confidence disclosure is always superior to delaying a required deliverable.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B and C
**PMI Justification:** With a validated unit cost database and a complete WBS, both parametric (B) and bottom-up (C) estimating are appropriate and provide complementary accuracy. Analogous (A) is less appropriate when more rigorous data exists and the historical project is geographically distinct. Expert judgment alone (D) is not a sufficient standalone estimating technique when validated data is available.
**Mindset Lesson:** When better data exists, use better techniques; defaulting to analogous when parametric or bottom-up data is available represents a missed opportunity for accuracy.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A — 2, 4, 1, 3
**PMI Justification:** Release planning requires a high-level estimate first (2), which demands documenting the assumptions behind it (4). Calibration with actual sprint data refines the cost-per-point ratio (1), which then enables more accurate sprint-level estimating (3). Beginning with calibration (Option B) reverses the sequence — calibration requires actuals that do not yet exist at project start.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile cost estimating, high-level planning estimates precede calibration; calibration refines, not replaces, the initial planning estimate.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** Funding limit reconciliation is a technique used in Determine Budget (the process that follows Estimate Costs), not in Estimate Costs itself. Reserve analysis (A), vendor bid analysis (B), and three-point estimating (D) are all explicitly listed tools and techniques for Estimate Costs in PMBOK 6 Section 7.2.
**Mindset Lesson:** Funding limit reconciliation operates on the aggregated budget, which does not yet exist during cost estimating at the activity level.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty (mandatory) and Responsibility. Excluding documented WBS activities to misrepresent project cost is deceptive and a direct ethics violation. The PM must include all scope in the estimate and formally communicate the underfunding risk. Option A cedes personal ethical accountability. Option C maintains a dual truth, which is a more sophisticated but equally clear violation. Option D documents the risk but still submits a false estimate.
**Mindset Lesson:** An estimate that excludes known scope is not a conservative estimate — it is a false one; the PM's professional obligation requires complete and honest cost representation regardless of leadership pressure.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Tailoring / Hybrid Communication. Forcing EVM onto agile data (Option A) distorts the cost picture. Requiring the agile team to change methodology (Option C) imposes an inappropriate technique without change control. Presenting unreconciled parallel reports (Option D) fails the finance department's need for a unified view. A hybrid report with explicit methodology disclosure and program-level reconciliation is both honest and complete.
**Mindset Lesson:** Hybrid cost reporting must transparently bridge methodologies; distorting one workstream's data to fit the other's format is a reporting integrity failure.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Integration / Code of Ethics — Honesty. The cost estimate must reflect actual project knowledge; the bottom-up estimate based on discovered complexity is the most accurate reflection of scope. The variance exceeds the documented threshold, requiring a change request — this is the control system working correctly, not a failure to be circumvented. Option A manipulates estimates to avoid governance. Option C compromises analytical integrity. Option D bypasses the sponsor, which is premature.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a cost variance exceeds the threshold, the correct response is a change request, not an estimate revision designed to avoid one.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** B, C, E
**PMI Justification:** Activity Cost Estimates (B) and Basis of Estimates (C) are the primary outputs of Estimate Costs; Project Document Updates (E) are also outputs (updates to assumption log, risk register, lessons learned, etc.). The Cost Baseline (A) is an output of Determine Budget. The Project Budget (D) is produced in Determine Budget, not Estimate Costs. The Cost Management Plan (F) is an output of Plan Cost Management, preceding this process.
**Mindset Lesson:** Estimate Costs produces estimates and their documentation — it does not produce baselines, budgets, or governance plans.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Estimate Costs follows Plan Cost Management; the Cost Management Plan governs every technique decision.
- Primary outputs: Activity Cost Estimates + Basis of Estimates (both required; one without the other is a governance failure).
- Technique selection is phase-dependent: ROM/Analogous early; Parametric mid-planning; Bottom-Up after WBS completion.
- Three-point PERT formula: E = (O + 4M + P) / 6 — use for high-uncertainty activities, especially on critical path.
- Contingency reserve IS part of the cost estimate (known-unknowns); management reserve is NOT (unknown-unknowns, management-controlled).
- Basis of estimates documents: methodology, assumptions, range, confidence level — it is the audit and defense document.
- Forcing estimates to fit a budget without scope change is a honesty violation and creates a false baseline.
- Vendor bid outliers require bid analysis before use; low bids may reflect missing scope.
- EAC formulas: BAC/CPI (current efficiency continues); AC + (BAC − EV) (remaining work at plan rate); know which the stem requires.
- **Reflex burn-in: If the stem says "how should costs be estimated" — the correct method depends on WBS completeness and project phase, never on sponsor pressure.**
