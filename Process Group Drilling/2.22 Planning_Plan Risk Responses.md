# PMP Exam Master Brief: Plan Risk Responses

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Plan Risk Responses is the process of developing options, selecting strategies, and agreeing on actions to address overall project risk exposure as well as individual project risks. It translates the prioritized output of qualitative (and where performed, quantitative) risk analysis into concrete action plans, assigned owners, and funded reserves. Response strategies differ by risk type: threats use avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept, or escalate; opportunities use exploit, enhance, share, accept, or escalate. The selection of strategy depends on risk priority, cost of the response versus expected risk impact, and stakeholder risk tolerance. This process has the broadest cross-domain bleed of any risk process — into Cost (contingency and management reserve allocation), Schedule (contingency time, schedule compression options), Procurement (insurance, contracts, indemnity clauses), Stakeholder Management (escalation paths and communication), and Resource Management (risk owner assignment). Exam weight sits within Risk Management (~10%) but risk response questions appear frequently in cost, procurement, and stakeholder stems. Residual risks and secondary risks must be explicitly planned for — a common oversight that leads to exam traps. **In one sentence:** Plan Risk Responses converts prioritized risks into assigned, funded, actionable plans that reduce threat exposure and maximize opportunity capture before the project enters execution.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal response strategies documented in the risk register for each high-priority risk; contingency reserves calculated; fallback plans defined for high-exposure risks; risk owners formally assigned and accepted |
| Agile | Risks addressed through backlog prioritization, spike stories, and built-in iteration buffers; team collectively owns and resolves risks; responses are often just-in-time rather than pre-planned in formal documents |
| Hybrid | Formal response planning at phase gates for high-priority risks; iterative sprint-level risk resolution for tactical risks; risk burndown chart tracks progress |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Escalate is a legitimate and required response strategy — real-world PMs treat escalation as a failure or last resort, but PMI treats it as the appropriate first strategy when a risk is outside the project's authority or capability to address.
2. Accepting a risk is an active decision, not the absence of one — practitioners use acceptance by default when they cannot think of a response, but PMI requires documented active acceptance (with a contingency plan) or passive acceptance (acknowledged and monitored).
3. Secondary risks — new risks created by a risk response — must be identified and planned for — practitioners often treat the primary response as sufficient and ignore what the response itself introduces.
4. Residual risks — risks remaining after a response is implemented — must be explicitly documented and accepted — practitioners assume residual exposure is zero if mitigation is applied.
5. Opportunities must have planned responses just as threats do — practitioners rarely plan for positive risks, but PMI expects exploit, enhance, share, accept, or escalate to be deliberately applied to each opportunity.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Honesty**: Disclosing to stakeholders when a risk has been escalated outside the project and the project team cannot guarantee the response outcome.
- **Responsibility**: Ensuring contingency plans are genuinely resourced and funded, not paper plans that will fail at implementation.
- **Fairness**: Assigning risk ownership to the person best positioned to manage the risk, not the most available or most junior team member.
- **Respect**: Obtaining risk owner agreement before assigning accountability — PMI expects risk owners to accept ownership, not be assigned without consultation.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Avoid (Threat) | Eliminating the risk by changing the project plan — adjusting scope, schedule, approach, or technology to remove the threat entirely | Avoid is not always possible; changing scope to avoid a risk may require a change request and sponsor approval |
| Transfer (Threat) | Shifting the financial consequence of a risk to a third party through insurance, contracts, warranties, or performance bonds | Transfer does not eliminate the risk; if the third party fails, residual exposure remains with the project |
| Mitigate (Threat) | Reducing the probability or impact of a risk to an acceptable threshold through early action | Mitigation does not aim to eliminate the risk — it reduces it; distinguish from avoid |
| Accept (Threat/Opportunity) | Acknowledging the risk and choosing not to actively respond; active acceptance includes a contingency plan; passive acceptance monitors without a plan | Acceptance is not inaction by default — active acceptance requires a defined contingency plan |
| Escalate (Threat/Opportunity) | Passing the risk to a higher authority (program, portfolio, sponsor, organizational management) because it is outside the project's scope or authority to manage | Escalated risks are removed from the project risk register and tracked at the appropriate level; the project cannot manage what it has escalated |
| Exploit (Opportunity) | Ensuring the opportunity definitely occurs by eliminating uncertainty — assigning best resources, accelerating work | Exploit is the strongest positive response; it guarantees the opportunity; do not confuse with enhance |
| Enhance (Opportunity) | Increasing the probability or impact of an opportunity | Enhance does not guarantee the opportunity; it improves the odds — contrasted with exploit |
| Share (Opportunity) | Allocating opportunity ownership to a third party better positioned to capture it (joint ventures, teaming arrangements) | Share is the opportunity analogue of transfer; it is proactive, not a default fallback |
| Contingency Reserve | Budget or time set aside for accepted known risks; released upon risk occurrence | Contingency reserve is in the cost baseline; management reserve is NOT |
| Fallback Plan | A pre-planned secondary response activated if the primary response proves insufficient | Fallback plans address the case where the primary response fails; they must be documented before execution |
| Residual Risk | The risk that remains after a planned response is implemented | Residual risks must be explicitly accepted (active or passive) — they are not assumed to be zero |
| Secondary Risk | A new risk that arises directly from implementing a risk response | Secondary risks must be assessed and planned for; they are added to the risk register |
| Risk Owner | The person accountable for monitoring a risk and implementing its response | The risk owner must accept the role; assignment without agreement violates PMI's collaborative principles |
| Contingency Plan | A pre-planned set of actions to be executed if a specific risk trigger occurs; funded from contingency reserve | A contingency plan is NOT the same as a contingency reserve — one is an action plan, one is budget |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Risk is outside the project's ability to control" | Escalate the risk to the appropriate authority (sponsor, program, portfolio) | Escalation is the correct PMI strategy when a risk exceeds the project's authority or capacity to manage |
| "Team wants to purchase insurance for a risk" | Classify this as a transfer strategy and document it in the risk register with assigned risk owner and cost | Transfer through insurance is a valid response; it must be documented and the residual risk acknowledged |
| "Response plan has been implemented but risk impact is higher than expected" | Activate the fallback plan if one exists; if not, execute the contingency reserve and develop an urgent response | Fallback plans exist precisely for primary response failure; contingency reserve funds the response |
| "Risk response creates a new risk" | Identify the secondary risk, add it to the risk register, and assess and plan a response for it | Secondary risks are a required deliverable of risk response planning; they cannot be ignored |
| "Low-priority risk with no planned response" | Document it as passively accepted on the watch list | Passive acceptance is an explicit decision — it is not the same as ignoring the risk |
| "Opportunity identified that the organization should pursue" | Assess which response strategy (exploit, enhance, share) is appropriate based on probability and organizational capability | Opportunities require the same deliberate response strategy selection as threats |
| "Sponsor asks if project can afford the risk response" | Compare the cost of the response to the risk exposure (probability × impact) to justify the investment | Response costs must be proportionate to risk exposure — over-responding wastes resources |
| "Risk owner has not acknowledged the risk assignment" | Obtain the risk owner's explicit acceptance of the role before finalizing the response plan | PMI requires risk owners to accept their accountability; forced assignment leads to unowned risks |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Risk will materialize regardless of project response due to external factors" | Escalate to the sponsor or program level; document as escalated and remove from project-level tracking | The project cannot manage risks outside its authority — holding them on the register creates false accountability |
| "Fallback plan fails and contingency reserve is depleted" | Issue a change request for additional funds and escalate to the sponsor immediately | Reserve depletion and plan failure is a project governance event requiring formal change control and sponsor awareness |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- All high and medium-priority threats and opportunities require documented response strategies, assigned owners, and funded plans
- Contingency reserves formally calculated from risk analysis and incorporated into the cost and schedule baselines
- Fallback plans documented for risks with high impact if the primary response fails
- Transfer strategies formalized through contract clauses, insurance policies, or performance bonds reviewed by legal/procurement

**Agile/Hybrid**
- Response plans embedded in sprint backlogs as spike stories, research tasks, or buffer allocation
- Risk owners typically emerge from the team rather than being formally assigned — the Scrum Master or product owner facilitates, not mandates
- In hybrid: formal response plans documented at phase gates; tactical responses handled within sprint planning; risk burndown chart tracks open risk count and severity over iterations

**Industry/Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking/Capital Markets/BSEC**: Regulatory and compliance risks must almost always use avoid (redesign to comply) or transfer (regulatory indemnification clauses); accepting a known compliance risk without explicit regulatory guidance approval violates governance standards and can constitute misconduct
- **Construction/Infrastructure**: Weather and geotechnical risks typically require a combination of mitigate (design adjustments) and transfer (contractor risk allocation in contract terms); schedule contingency buffers are contractually mandated in many jurisdictions
- **IT/Cybersecurity**: Vendor dependency risks often require share (multi-vendor strategy) or transfer (cyber insurance); passive acceptance of known critical infrastructure risks is increasingly prohibited by regulators
- **Pharmaceutical**: Clinical risk responses must be prospectively documented and submitted to regulatory bodies — response plans are regulatory compliance artifacts, not just internal project documents

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Accept all low-priority risks without planning a contingency" | Low-priority risks don't warrant effort and practitioners have learned to focus on high-priority risks | Active acceptance requires a contingency plan; passive acceptance requires explicit documentation on the watch list — neither is the same as ignoring |
| "Escalation means the project has failed to manage its risks" | Escalation feels like admitting defeat or incompetence in real-world PM culture | PMI treats escalation as a legitimate, first-choice strategy for risks outside the project's authority — it is not a failure |
| "Insurance always eliminates the risk" | Insurance payments restore financial losses, so the risk feels resolved | Transfer does not eliminate the risk — if the insurer defaults or the policy has exclusions, residual risk remains |
| "Mitigation and avoidance are the same strategy" | Both reduce risk exposure and practitioners often blend them mentally | Avoidance eliminates the risk by changing the plan; mitigation reduces but does not eliminate it — the exam distinguishes them sharply |
| "A contingency reserve and a contingency plan are the same thing" | Both are called "contingency" and both relate to responding to risks | A contingency reserve is budget; a contingency plan is a documented action plan — they serve different roles and appear in different project documents |
| "Secondary risks are addressed in the next risk identification cycle" | Secondary risks feel like future risks that don't need immediate attention | Secondary risks are identified and planned during risk response planning — deferring them leaves active response-generated exposures unmanaged |
| "The risk owner is whoever is most available" | Resource availability is a practical constraint that drives assignment decisions | PMI requires risk owners to be the person best positioned to manage the risk and to accept the role; convenience-based assignment leads to unowned responses |
| "Residual risk is zero once mitigation is applied" | The whole point of mitigation seems to be fixing the problem | Mitigation reduces, it does not eliminate — residual risk remains and must be explicitly accepted (active or passive) and documented |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager is planning risk responses on a regulatory compliance project. A legal risk related to a pending court ruling is classified as high-priority with a potential project-stopping impact. The project's legal counsel advises that the organization should escalate this risk to the executive team since it involves decisions above the project level. The project sponsor, who wants to avoid senior executive involvement before the project receives budget approval, asks the PM to keep the risk "on the project register as a mitigation item" and not escalate it. The PM knows the project team has no authority to mitigate a legal regulatory risk. What must the project manager do?

A. Follow the sponsor's instruction since the sponsor has budget authority and the PM reports to the sponsor.
B. Document the risk as a mitigation item on the register as instructed but send a separate private memo to the executive team.
C. Inform the sponsor that the risk is outside the project's authority to manage and must be escalated, and document this position; if the sponsor overrules, document the sponsor's decision and the PM's objection.
D. Remove the risk from the register as it is outside the project scope.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony/Engagement**

A Scrum team has identified a technical risk: the third-party API their product depends on may deprecate a key feature in the next 60 days. The team agrees this risk is high-priority. The product owner wants to "add mitigation to the backlog" but has not created a specific story or spike. The Scrum Master suggests the team address it in the next retrospective. The risk has a 60-day window before it becomes irreversible. What is the BEST course of action for the team?

A. Wait for the retrospective to formally plan the response as the Scrum Master suggests.
B. Create a spike story in the current sprint to research a fallback API option and add a mitigation story to the next sprint's backlog based on spike findings.
C. Escalate the risk to the product owner and wait for the product owner to prioritize the mitigation story in the next sprint planning.
D. Document the risk on the risk board and continue delivery work since the risk has not materialized yet.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project's risk response plan includes purchasing technology insurance to transfer the financial impact of a potential data center failure. The insurance has been procured. Two weeks into execution, the PM learns that the insurance policy contains an exclusion clause that removes coverage for the specific type of failure the risk response was designed to address. The next phase gate review is four weeks away. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Note the exclusion in the risk register and address it at the next phase gate review.
B. Immediately reassess the risk since the transfer strategy is no longer valid; develop an alternative response strategy and update the risk register and risk report.
C. Ask the insurance broker to amend the policy before taking any internal action.
D. Accept the remaining exposure passively since the exclusion is a contractual matter outside the PM's control.

**Q7 — Ethics/Honesty Hardline**

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1 — Correct Answer: C**
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility; PMBOK 7 Escalate strategy. Escalation is the correct response when a risk is outside the project's authority. The PM cannot ethically hold a regulatory legal risk on the project register as a mitigation item when no mitigation is available at the project level. Option A abdicates professional judgment to authority pressure. Option B is deceptive — back-channel communication contradicts transparent governance. Option D removes an active risk without authority to do so.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a risk exceeds project authority, escalation is a professional obligation, not an option — sponsor preference does not override this.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — risks with time-bounded windows require timely response, not deferred treatment. A 60-day irreversibility window makes waiting for a retrospective unacceptably risky. A spike story is the agile mechanism for investigating and resolving a high-priority technical uncertainty. Option A defers past the actionable window. Option C creates an unnecessary handoff to the product owner. Option D treats non-materialization as a reason for inaction.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, time-bounded risks are addressed within the current or next sprint through spikes — retrospectives are for reflection, not urgent risk response.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk domain — when a risk response proves invalid (coverage exclusion removes the transfer), the response must be immediately reassessed and replaced. Waiting four weeks to address a known response failure is a governance lapse. Option A defers a known invalid response. Option C may be pursued in parallel but is not the first PM action. Option D passive acceptance of a material risk without assessment and documentation is not compliant.
**Mindset Lesson:** A failed or invalidated risk response requires immediate reassessment and replacement — it cannot wait for a scheduled review.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and B**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk domain. Secondary risks (subcontractor availability) must be identified, added to the register, and planned for during risk response planning. Residual risks (15% settlement) must be explicitly accepted through either active (contingency plan) or passive (watch list) acceptance. Option C is wrong — the original risk remains open with residual exposure; it cannot be removed. Option D automatic transfer through contract is plausible but requires explicit contractual language and risk owner agreement — it is not an automatic PMI-required action.
**Mindset Lesson:** Every response generates secondary risks and residual risks — both require documented plans, not assumptions.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / PMBOK 6 — strategy is selected first, specific actions and triggers are defined next, risk owner acceptance is confirmed (as part of or after action definition), secondary risks are identified from the response actions, and the register is updated last. Option B puts owner acceptance before action definition, which is premature. Option C starts with owner assignment before strategy selection. Option D identifies secondary risks before owner confirmation.
**Mindset Lesson:** Strategy → Actions → Owner Acceptance → Secondary Risks → Register Update is the PMI sequence for risk response planning.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Risk domain. Enhance is an opportunity response strategy — it increases the probability or positive impact of an opportunity. It is not a threat response strategy. The threat response strategies are Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept, and Escalate. This is a process/strategy boundary question where knowing which strategy belongs to which risk type is required.
**Mindset Lesson:** Enhance = opportunities only; never use "enhance" in a threat response — threats are addressed by ATMAE.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: C**
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty. A risk register that inaccurately depicts a high-priority risk as fully mitigated creates false assurance for governance decision-making. The PM has a duty to ensure accurate reporting, regardless of sponsor preferences or milestone optics. Option A defers to technical expertise inappropriately — technical knowledge does not override reporting accuracy obligations. Option B is deceptive. Option D deliberately allows a false record to influence a governance decision.
**Mindset Lesson:** Inaccurate risk status reporting — even by a risk owner — is a PM responsibility to correct; governance depends on the integrity of the register.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder + Risk domains. Escalated risks are removed from the project risk register and owned at the program level — conflating them with project-owned risks creates confusion about accountability. Option A keeps escalated risks in the project register incorrectly. Option C limits the communication inappropriately. Option D obscures ownership distinctions that the sponsor specifically asked about.
**Mindset Lesson:** Escalated risks leave the project register — communicate ownership boundaries clearly so governance responsibility is not confused.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Escalate strategy. When a risk and its proposed response exceed the project's authority and budget, the PM must formally escalate with full context, not act unilaterally or defer passively. The escalation must include both risk options (original risk vs. response cost) so the program office can make an informed decision. Option A implements a plan outside budget authority without approval. Option C passive acceptance when action is required fails the project. Option D usurps program authority.
**Mindset Lesson:** When a risk response exceeds project authority, escalate with options — the program office needs full information to make the call, not a unilateral act or passive silence.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, F**
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / PMBOK 6 Risk Management:
- A: The updated risk register with all response details is the primary output.
- C: Secondary risk identification is an explicitly required activity of risk response planning.
- F: Risk owner assignment with acceptance is a required component.
- B is wrong: Probability distribution of cost outcomes is the output of quantitative risk analysis, not risk response planning.
- D is wrong: Escalation is applied when appropriate, not as a default for any risk that cannot be fully resolved.
- E: Contingency reserve amounts are an output of risk response planning, but when choosing a triad, A/C/F represent the most definitively cited core deliverables specific to this process.
**Mindset Lesson:** Risk response planning's signature outputs are the updated register (with full response plans), secondary risk identification, and confirmed risk owner acceptance — not reserve calculations, which are determined during quantitative analysis.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Threat strategies: Avoid (eliminate), Transfer (shift financial impact), Mitigate (reduce P or I), Accept (active = contingency plan; passive = watch list), Escalate (outside project authority).
- Opportunity strategies: Exploit (guarantee), Enhance (increase probability/impact), Share (partner for capture), Accept, Escalate.
- Escalation is a legitimate FIRST-choice strategy, not a failure — use it when the risk is outside the project's authority.
- Transfer does not eliminate risk — residual exposure remains and must be explicitly accepted.
- Secondary risks (created by the response itself) must be identified, assessed, and planned during risk response planning.
- Residual risks (remaining after response) must be explicitly accepted — they are never assumed to be zero.
- Active acceptance = documented contingency plan; passive acceptance = watch list monitoring; neither is the same as ignoring.
- Risk owners must accept their role — assignment without agreement produces unowned responses that fail in execution.
- Contingency reserve funds contingency plans (known risks); management reserve covers unknown-unknowns and is outside the cost baseline.
- Fallback plans must be pre-planned before execution — they are activated when the primary response proves insufficient.
- **Reflex burn-in: When the stem says "risk is outside the project's authority to manage," your answer is ESCALATE — not mitigate, not accept, not avoid.**
