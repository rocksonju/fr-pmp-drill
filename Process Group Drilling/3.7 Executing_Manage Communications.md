# PMP Exam Master Brief: Manage Communications

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Manage Communications is the Executing process that creates, distributes, stores, retrieves, manages, monitors, and disposes of project information in a timely and appropriate manner. It sits in the Communications Management knowledge area and carries roughly 8–10% of PMP exam weight when blended across communications-domain questions, with significant cross-domain bleed into Stakeholder Engagement, Risk Reporting, and Integration because all formal information flows pass through this process. The critical exam distinction is that this process executes the communications management plan — it does not design it (that is Plan Communications Management) and does not evaluate its effectiveness (that is Monitor Communications). The process produces actual communications artifacts: status reports, dashboards, meeting minutes, presentations, and notifications — not strategies or frameworks. The sender-receiver model underpins every communication event on the exam, meaning noise, encoding failures, and lack of feedback loops are testable failure modes. Communication channel count grows as n(n−1)/2, so team expansion exponentially multiplies the PM's coordination burden. In agile environments, information radiators, daily standups, and retrospectives are all expressions of this process operating in real time. Push, pull, and interactive communication modes each carry specific PMI preferences tied to message type, urgency, and stakeholder characteristics that must be mastered for scenario questions.

**In one sentence:** Manage Communications is the disciplined execution of the communications management plan — ensuring the right person receives the right information in the right format through the right channel at the right time, with confirmed receipt and feedback.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal communications management plan drives all distribution; stakeholder register maps individual preferences; status reports and meeting minutes follow a fixed cadence; change requests generated when communications scope changes; PMIS is the single source of truth for archived information |
| Agile | Information radiators (kanban boards, burndown charts) replace periodic reports; daily standups replace written status updates; retrospectives surface communication breakdowns and drive continuous improvement; whole-team osmotic communication removes information gatekeepers |
| Hybrid | Formal external communications (executives, regulators, clients) follow the plan and require documentation; internal team communications use agile channels; PM must code-switch between structured reporting and real-time transparency based on audience |

**Top 5 PMI-isms:**

1. PMI requires the PM to consult the communications management plan before selecting a channel — real-world PMs default to email or a quick call without checking documented stakeholder preferences.
2. PMI treats sending a message and communicating as entirely different things — real-world PMs consider a sent email as completed communication; PMI requires confirmed receipt and demonstrated understanding via the feedback loop.
3. PMI holds the PM accountable for communication outcomes — real-world PMs often blame stakeholders for not reading reports; PMI's view is that if a stakeholder is uninformed, the PM failed.
4. PMI demands that all communications changes — frequency, format, distribution list — go through an update to the communications management plan — real-world PMs adjust informally without documenting the change.
5. PMI prioritizes interactive communication for complex or sensitive messages — real-world PMs default to email because it creates a paper trail; PMI values fidelity and comprehension over convenience.

**Ethics Angle:**

- **Honesty:** Communicating accurate project status even when variances are unfavorable; softening or omitting bad news to protect relationships or funding violates PMI's honesty obligation.
- **Responsibility:** Ensuring all relevant stakeholders receive timely and complete information consistent with their role; omitting a stakeholder by design or neglect is a form of PM negligence.
- **Fairness:** Distributing project information equitably based on role; withholding information from certain stakeholders to gain positional advantage is a fairness violation.
- **Respect:** Selecting communication channels and language that honor stakeholder cultural, linguistic, and accessibility needs; ignoring these norms signals disregard for stakeholder dignity.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Push Communication | Information sent directly to recipients without their active request (email, memos, reports, newsletters) | Push ensures a delivery attempt; it does not guarantee comprehension — a feedback loop is still required |
| Pull Communication | Information placed in a shared repository for stakeholders to access on demand (PMIS, intranet, document library, information radiator) | Pull is appropriate for large audiences and reference material; it is not passive neglect — the PM must ensure content is current and accessible |
| Interactive Communication | Real-time, multi-directional exchange (meetings, calls, video conferences, workshops) — highest fidelity method | PMI favors interactive for complex, sensitive, or high-stakes topics; do not select it for routine distribution to large audiences |
| Communication Channels Formula | n(n−1)/2 where n = number of stakeholders | Adding one stakeholder to a group of 10 adds 10 new channels, not 1; the exam tests delta calculations |
| Sender-Receiver Model | Communication loop: encode → transmit → decode → acknowledge → feedback; noise is any distortion in this chain | Noise does not mean audio interference; it includes cultural differences, jargon, emotional state, and information overload |
| Information Radiator | Visible, real-time display of project status accessible without a request (kanban board, burndown chart, physical task wall) | Not agile-exclusive; any visible display qualifies; PMI treats it as a primary communication vehicle, not supplementary |
| PMIS (Project Management Information System) | Integrated software tools that store, retrieve, and distribute project information (SharePoint, Jira, MS Project, etc.) | Answers that rely only on email ignore PMIS governance requirements; all official communications must be archived |
| Communications Management Plan | Document specifying stakeholder information needs, formats, frequency, responsible parties, and escalation paths | This is an input to Manage Communications, not an output; it is created during Planning |
| Project Communications | Output of Manage Communications — the actual reports, dashboards, memos, presentations, and notifications produced | Distinguish the artifact (output) from the plan (input); the process consumes the plan and produces the artifact |
| Work Performance Reports | Formatted presentations of work performance information (dashboards, S-curves, EVM summaries, variance reports) | An input to Manage Communications from Monitor and Control processes; the PM uses these as source material |
| Feedback | Recipient's active response confirming receipt and understanding of a message | Absence of complaints or silence does not equal receipt; PMI requires an active, confirmable feedback mechanism |
| Active Listening | Communication technique requiring the receiver to paraphrase, question, or acknowledge to demonstrate comprehension | PMI always chooses active listening over passive receipt; a stakeholder who misunderstood is a PM feedback failure |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Stakeholders report they are not receiving updates" | Review the communications management plan to identify the distribution gap | Root cause must be diagnosed against the plan before changing channels, frequency, or content |
| "A key stakeholder says they feel out of the loop" | Consult the stakeholder register and communications plan to verify agreed-upon preferences and distribution | The plan is the contract for communications; verify adherence before improvising a new approach |
| "A team member distributed a sensitive report to the wrong audience" | Assess the impact of the disclosure, then implement corrective controls in the communications management plan | Contain the damage first; systemic improvement follows assessment |
| "Three new stakeholders have been added to the project" | Update the stakeholder register and communications management plan to capture their information needs and preferences | Adding stakeholders changes channel count and information requirements; the plan must reflect this |
| "The sponsor requests more frequent updates than the plan specifies" | Evaluate the request and log a change request to update the communications management plan if warranted | Communications scope changes require plan updates, not informal agreements; informal arrangements create governance gaps |
| "Status reports are consistently ignored by the executive team" | Consult the executives to understand their preferred format and update the communications approach accordingly | Communication effectiveness is the PM's accountability; a consistently ignored report is a format or channel mismatch, not a stakeholder failure |
| "The project team is dispersed across four time zones" | Design asynchronous and pull communication methods; schedule intentional overlap windows for interactive sessions | PMI recognizes time zone constraints as a tailoring driver requiring deliberate channel design; ignoring them creates inclusion failures |
| "A regulatory body requests project documentation" | Retrieve verified, complete documentation from the PMIS before distributing | Official external communications require accuracy verification against PMIS records before release |

**Escalation and Weak-Spot Sub-Table:**

| Escalation Scenario | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "A stakeholder is actively spreading misinformation about project status to other stakeholders" | Address directly with factual, documented communication and engage the sponsor if the behavior continues | PMI treats misinformation as a stakeholder engagement and communications risk; the PM owns the corrective communication response |
| "A functional manager requests project data that falls outside their authorized access per the communications plan" | Consult the communications plan and escalate to the sponsor if access is not authorized | Communications governance supersedes organizational hierarchy; the PM does not grant access outside the plan without formal approval |

### 6. Visual Memory Aid

```
MANAGE COMMUNICATIONS DECISION TREE
=====================================

Step 1: What type of message needs to be communicated?
          |
          v
   [Sensitive / Complex / High-stakes / Conflict-related?]
   YES --> INTERACTIVE (meeting, call, video conference)
   NO  --> Continue to Step 2

Step 2: Does the stakeholder need real-time self-service visibility?
          |
          v
   [Ongoing transparency / large audience / reference material?]
   YES --> PULL (PMIS, information radiator, shared repository)
   NO  --> Continue to Step 3

Step 3: Is this a routine update, formal report, or scheduled notification?
          |
          v
   [Planned cadence / administrative / broad distribution?]
   YES --> PUSH (email, memo, status report, dashboard distribution)
   NO  --> Return to stakeholder needs analysis

After EVERY communication event:
   Feedback received? --> YES: communication loop is complete
                      --> NO: follow up; delivery is not the same as communication
```

**Mnemonic:** I-P-P-F — Interactive, Pull, Push, Feedback

**Memory Hook:** "IPP-F every time: pick the method, then confirm with Feedback — no loop closed without it."

**Mnemonic Unpack:**
- **I** = Interactive — use for complex, sensitive, or real-time two-way needs
- **P** = Pull — stakeholder self-serves from PMIS or radiator; good for ongoing reference
- **P** = Push — routine, one-directional, documented distribution
- **F** = Feedback — the loop that converts transmission into actual communication

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project:**
- Communications management plan is a formal, baselined document requiring change control to modify.
- Status reports follow a fixed cadence (weekly, biweekly, monthly) with defined templates and distribution lists.
- Any change to frequency, format, or distribution requires a formal update to the communications management plan via integrated change control.
- PMIS serves as the single source of truth; all project communications must be archived and retrievable.
- Escalation paths are documented in the plan; stakeholder communication outside the plan requires justification.

**Agile/Hybrid:**
- Information radiators replace formal status reports for team-level transparency.
- Daily standups and retrospectives surface communication breakdowns in real time, eliminating the need for formal diagnostic cycles.
- Hybrid projects require a dual-track approach: agile channels for the delivery team, formal channels for sponsors, external parties, and governance boards.
- Sprint reviews function as structured stakeholder communication events with demonstrated deliverables as the primary artifact.
- Retrospectives drive continuous improvement of communication practices, not just technical delivery.

**Industry/Regulatory Factors:**
- **Banking/Capital Markets/BSEC:** Communications involving financial data, regulatory filings, or client disclosures require compliance review prior to distribution; informal channels (chat, verbal) are insufficient for audit trails and may violate regulatory obligations.
- Mandatory regulatory reporting cadences imposed by external bodies (BSEC, SEC equivalents, central banks) supersede internal communications plans and become constraints, not preferences.
- Sensitive regulatory communications require legal and compliance sign-off, creating lead-time requirements that must be built into the communications plan.
- Information security classifications determine which channels are permissible for transmitting specific categories of project data; the PM must map channel selection against data classification policies.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Send an email to all stakeholders with the updated status report" | Email is fast, creates a paper trail, and feels thorough for routine updates | PMI rejects this when the stem signals complex or sensitive information requiring interactive communication; also bypasses stakeholder channel preferences documented in the plan |
| "Hold a meeting to resolve the communication breakdown" | Meetings feel decisive and collaborative; experienced PMs default to them when problems arise | PMI expects the PM to first consult the communications management plan to diagnose the root cause; calling a meeting before diagnosing is reactive improvisation, not process adherence |
| "Update the communications management plan immediately" | Changing the plan feels proactive and systematic when communications are failing | PMI rejects this as the FIRST action when the plan already addresses the situation; the PM must follow the existing plan before revising it |
| "Distribute the complete report to all stakeholders to ensure full transparency" | Mass distribution feels equitable and signals openness | Violates need-to-know and access control principles; the communications plan defines who receives what; mass distribution bypasses governance |
| "Escalate to the sponsor whenever a stakeholder is dissatisfied with communications" | Sponsors have authority; escalating seems proactive and appropriately deferential | PMI expects PM to diagnose and resolve communications issues at the team/stakeholder level first; premature escalation signals lack of PM competence |
| "Use the most efficient channel regardless of stakeholder preference" | Efficiency is a core PM value; the fastest method seems like good service | PMI consistently prioritizes stakeholder-preferred channels over PM convenience; the communications plan captures preferences for a reason |
| "Verbal communication is sufficient for project updates to senior stakeholders" | Simple or trusted conversations do not seem worth documenting | PMI requires documented, retrievable communications for all decisions, agreements, and significant updates; verbal-only leaves no audit trail |
| "Once the report is sent, communication is complete" | Sending creates a record and shifts responsibility to the recipient | PMI's sender-receiver model requires confirmed receipt and feedback; transmission without acknowledgment is incomplete communication by definition |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM is managing a large infrastructure project for a municipal client. The project has experienced three consecutive schedule slippages, each reported accurately in the monthly status reports. The project sponsor, who has a close relationship with the client executive, pulls the PM aside before the next scheduled report and requests that the PM frame the report to emphasize upcoming milestones rather than document the delays, stating the client "doesn't need to see all the details right now." The communications management plan commits to monthly transparent status reports including variance data. The PM has already drafted an accurate report consistent with the plan. What should the PM do?

A. Follow the sponsor's suggestion to preserve the client relationship and protect the project's funding continuity.
B. Distribute the accurate status report as drafted, consistent with the communications management plan.
C. Delay the status report until the next milestone is achieved so the report naturally contains positive progress.
D. Escalate immediately to the portfolio manager before taking any other action.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony/Engagement**

A scrum team is midway through a sprint on a fintech product development project. A key business stakeholder mentions during an informal corridor conversation that they haven't seen the burndown chart recently and feel uncertain about sprint progress. The team uses a digital kanban board updated daily and sprint reviews occur every two weeks. The next sprint review is five days away. The stakeholder has not checked the digital board. What is the BEST response?

A. Schedule an immediate one-on-one status meeting with the stakeholder to give them a personal briefing.
B. Direct the stakeholder to the digital kanban board and confirm they have access to the current information radiator.
C. Add the stakeholder to the sprint review invitation and wait until that formal session to address their concern.
D. Create a formal status report and distribute it to the stakeholder by end of day.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project PM is managing a product rollout with both agile development sprints and waterfall-based vendor contracts. Internal team communications occur via agile channels (standups, kanban). The external vendor receives formal monthly status reports per the communications management plan. A vendor representative calls the PM to complain that they learned about a scope change affecting their deliverable through the internal team's chat platform, to which they had been informally added by a team member weeks earlier. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Remove the vendor from the internal chat platform immediately to prevent further unauthorized information exposure.
B. Apologize to the vendor and verbally brief them on all scope changes to date.
C. Review the communications management plan to determine the vendor's authorized communication channels and information scope.
D. Issue a formal change request to add the vendor to all internal communication channels going forward.

**Q4 — Two-Correct Pick-Best (Predictive)**

A PM is preparing the monthly executive dashboard for a complex capital markets technology project. The project is on schedule but has a cost variance of −12% (over budget by 12%). Two senior stakeholders have contradictory documented preferences: the CFO requires detailed financial tables with variance narratives, while the COO requires high-level visual summaries. Both preferences are captured in the communications management plan. Which approach BEST demonstrates PMI-aligned communications execution?

A. Prepare a single consolidated report that satisfies the most senior stakeholder's preference to save PM time.
B. Create tailored versions of the dashboard for each stakeholder per their documented preferences, including the accurate cost variance in both.
C. Present only the positive schedule data to the COO to avoid triggering executive concern over the cost position.
D. Schedule a joint executive meeting, present one version, and allow each stakeholder to ask questions about their specific concerns.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile project team has just completed a sprint. The product owner reports that several stakeholders received updates but repeatedly responded with questions already answered in prior communications, suggesting the information was not absorbed. Place the following actions in the CORRECT PMI-aligned sequence:

1. Update the information radiator to improve future transparency and accessibility.
2. Conduct a retrospective to identify the root cause of the recurring comprehension failures.
3. Verify that all current stakeholders have access to the PMIS and information radiator.
4. Adjust the sprint review format to include a dedicated Q&A walkthrough of key information.

A. 2, 3, 1, 4
B. 3, 2, 4, 1
C. 2, 1, 3, 4
D. 1, 2, 3, 4

**Q6 — EXCEPT/NOT**

All of the following are valid outputs of the Manage Communications process EXCEPT:

A. Project communications (reports, memos, dashboards, presentations)
B. Project management plan updates
C. Communications management plan (initial baseline document)
D. Organizational process assets updates

**Q7 — Ethics/Honesty Hardline**

A PM on a publicly funded healthcare infrastructure project discovers that a status report distributed two weeks ago contained an error: the reported percent complete was listed as 78% when the actual figure was 61%. The error occurred because a team member used outdated data from a superseded tracking sheet. The sponsor tells the PM not to issue a correction because the funding review committee meets next week and a correction "will complicate the approval." What must the PM do?

A. Wait until after the funding review, then issue a corrected report framed as a routine update.
B. Issue a corrected communications document immediately and notify all recipients of the prior error.
C. Issue a correction only to the funding committee, withholding it from the broader stakeholder list to limit disruption.
D. Document the sponsor's instruction in the issue log and defer to the sponsor's organizational authority.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team includes five co-located members, three remote developers, and two external compliance consultants. The PM is designing the communication approach for the sprint cycle. The compliance consultants are contractually entitled to formal monthly status reports. Internal team members work in two-week sprints. The PM is debating whether to apply a single unified approach or differentiate by stakeholder type. Which communication strategy is MOST aligned with PMI guidance?

A. Hold weekly all-hands video calls to ensure every stakeholder receives identical information simultaneously.
B. Use a single consolidated email distribution for all stakeholders on a biweekly basis to align with sprint cadence.
C. Apply agile channels (daily standup, kanban, sprint review) for the internal team, and deliver formal monthly reports to compliance consultants per their contractual entitlement.
D. Direct all stakeholders to monitor the digital kanban board and submit questions via a shared inbox as needed.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM is six weeks into executing a hybrid program for a banking institution. The internal team operates in agile sprints, but executive reporting follows a formal quarterly cadence. Mid-sprint, a critical regulatory change is announced that will materially affect project scope and cost estimates. The PM has updated the risk register and is preparing a change request. The next scheduled quarterly executive report is eight weeks away. What should the PM do regarding communications to the executive stakeholders?

A. Document the regulatory change in the risk register and include it in the next scheduled quarterly report as planned.
B. Continue normal sprint communications and allow the change request process to surface the issue to executives organically.
C. Issue an unscheduled, out-of-cycle executive communication immediately to inform decision-makers of the material regulatory impact.
D. Inform only the project sponsor verbally and allow the sponsor to decide whether to escalate further to the executive committee.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A PM is reviewing the effectiveness of the Manage Communications process at the midpoint of a large predictive project. Multiple stakeholders have independently reported that project communications feel disorganized, that they are uncertain where to find current project documents, and that they frequently receive reports that seem irrelevant to their role. Which THREE actions are MOST aligned with PMI guidance to address these issues?

A. Conduct stakeholder interviews or a structured review to identify specific communication pain points and root causes.
B. Review and update the communications management plan to address the identified gaps in organization, relevance, and distribution.
C. Replace all push communications with interactive meetings to ensure all recipients fully understand every update.
D. Ensure all project documentation is stored, organized, and retrievable in the PMIS as a single accessible source.
E. Increase the frequency of all status reports distributed to all stakeholders to compensate for the perceived gaps.
F. Tailor communication formats and distribution lists to individual stakeholder preferences as documented in the stakeholder register.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty): "We proactively share information and truthfully communicate." PMBOK 7 Stakeholder Performance Domain requires transparent communication. The communications management plan commits the PM to accurate monthly variance reporting. Concealing or softening negative status at a sponsor's request violates the honesty obligation regardless of relationship or funding context.
- A fails: following a sponsor's instruction to misrepresent status is an ethics violation; sponsor authority does not override the PM's ethical commitments.
- C fails: deliberately delaying a required report to manage optics is dishonest and constitutes a breach of the communications management plan.
- D fails: escalation to a portfolio manager is not required here; the PM has the authority and ethical obligation to distribute the accurate report without further approval.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor authority does not override the PM's ethical obligation to report accurately; the communications plan and ethics code together require honest distribution regardless of relational or financial pressure.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide, Chapter 4: information radiators make project status continuously visible as a pull communication model. The stakeholder's issue is lack of awareness about where to access the information, not lack of information. Directing them to the existing radiator is the most efficient and PMI-aligned response.
- A fails: scheduling an ad-hoc briefing duplicates information already on the radiator and creates unnecessary overhead, undermining the pull model.
- C fails: while a sprint review is appropriate in five days, dismissing an engaged stakeholder without addressing their immediate access concern is poor engagement practice.
- D fails: creating a new formal report replicates the radiator in a push format, adding work without value and eroding the agile transparency model.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile environments, the information radiator is the status report; the PM's communications role is ensuring stakeholders know how to access it, not duplicating it in other formats.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7: the communications management plan governs who receives what information through which channels. Before taking any corrective action — removing the vendor, apologizing, or formalizing — the PM must understand what the plan authorizes and where the process failure occurred. The root cause is a governance gap; accurate diagnosis precedes corrective action.
- A fails: removing the vendor from the chat before understanding the plan may itself violate a contract provision or agreed engagement approach.
- B fails: verbal updates are informal and do not address the systemic channel-governance failure.
- D fails: issuing a change request is premature; the PM must first determine whether a change is actually needed based on what the plan already authorizes.

**Mindset Lesson:** All communications corrective actions route through the communications management plan; improvised corrections without consulting the plan risk creating new violations.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder Performance Domain and Communications Management Plan execution: documented stakeholder preferences are plan commitments. Tailored reports to individual needs is standard PMI practice. The cost variance must be reported honestly in both versions.
- A fails: consolidating to the most senior stakeholder's preference ignores documented individual needs and is an efficiency-over-effectiveness trade-off PMI explicitly rejects.
- C fails: omitting the cost variance from the COO's report is dishonest and a direct violation of the PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty).
- D fails: a joint meeting may serve an alignment function but does not fulfill the ongoing dashboard reporting obligation per each stakeholder's documented preference.

**Mindset Lesson:** Tailored communications are not a luxury — they are the execution of the communications management plan; one-size-fits-all reporting is a plan compliance failure.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A (2, 3, 1, 4)
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide: retrospectives are the primary mechanism for identifying process improvement; they precede corrective action. After identifying root cause (Step 2), verify stakeholder access as a potential contributing factor (Step 3), then update the radiator to address the accessibility dimension (Step 1), then adjust the review format as a structural improvement (Step 4).
- B fails: verifying access before understanding root cause addresses a potential symptom before confirming the diagnosis.
- C fails: updating the radiator before verifying access is premature; the radiator update may not address the core problem.
- D fails: updating the radiator first treats a symptom without the benefit of retrospective diagnosis.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, the retrospective precedes corrective action; inspect the root cause before you adapt the tool or process.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6, Process 10.2 Outputs: the Communications Management Plan is an output of Plan Communications Management (Process 10.1), not Manage Communications (Process 10.2). Manage Communications consumes the plan as an input and produces project communications artifacts, project management plan updates, and OPA updates.
- A is a valid output (project communications).
- B is a valid output (project management plan updates, including the communications component).
- D is a valid output (OPA updates including lessons learned).

**Mindset Lesson:** The communications management plan is created in Planning and consumed in Executing — know which process produces versus consumes each component.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty and Responsibility): "We do not engage in deception." Allowing a known factual error in a distributed document to persist while stakeholders make funding decisions on false data constitutes deception by omission. Immediate correction is mandatory regardless of timing or political consequences.
- A fails: waiting until after the funding review allows the committee to make a material decision on false information; this is active deception.
- C fails: selective correction to only the funding committee while leaving the broader stakeholder group with inaccurate data is still a form of deception and creates a two-version information problem.
- D fails: no ethical obligation is subordinate to sponsor preference; documenting the instruction and deferring is a passive participation in the misrepresentation.

**Mindset Lesson:** Correcting a known factual error in distributed project communications is not optional; the timing of a funding review creates no ethical exception.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 hybrid tailoring and Agile Practice Guide: internal agile teams use agile channels; external contractual parties receive communications per their contractual entitlement. The communications management plan must accommodate both populations. Applying a single method to all stakeholders ignores documented needs and violates contractual terms.
- A fails: weekly all-hands meetings impose agile-team coordination overhead on compliance consultants and ignore the contractual formal report requirement.
- B fails: biweekly email to all stakeholders does not satisfy the compliance consultants' contractual entitlement to formal monthly reports.
- D fails: requiring compliance consultants to self-serve from a kanban board almost certainly violates their contractual right to receive formal reports and does not meet their professional obligations for documented status.

**Mindset Lesson:** Hybrid communications strategies must explicitly separate internal agile channels from external formal obligations; the communications plan must address both populations with appropriate methods.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Communications Management and Stakeholder Engagement: material changes affecting scope, cost, and regulatory compliance require immediate executive notification regardless of the scheduled reporting cadence. Waiting eight weeks allows executives to operate without material decision-relevant information. The scheduled cadence is a minimum floor, not an absolute ceiling.
- A fails: eight weeks is an unacceptably long delay for information material to executive decision-making on a regulated banking program.
- B fails: relying on the change request process to surface material regulatory news to executives organically is passive and too slow given the stakes.
- D fails: verbal notification to only the sponsor without a formal executive communication leaves the broader decision-making group uninformed and is insufficient for a material regulatory impact.

**Mindset Lesson:** Scheduled reporting cadences are minimums; material events with executive-level impact require immediate out-of-cycle communications regardless of the established plan cadence.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, B, D
**PMI Justification:** 
- A: Stakeholder input through interviews or structured review identifies specific root causes of disorganization and irrelevance; PMI requires diagnosis before corrective action.
- B: The communications management plan must be updated when stakeholder feedback identifies systemic gaps; that is the purpose of the monitoring feedback loop flowing into plan updates.
- D: PMIS as the single source of truth directly addresses the stakeholder complaint about not knowing where to find documents; this is a foundational infrastructure fix.
- C fails: replacing all push communications with meetings is a blanket overreaction that ignores message type, audience size, and channel appropriateness; PMI requires tailored channel selection.
- E fails: increasing frequency without identifying what is wrong adds volume without value; frequency is rarely the cause of disorganization or relevance problems.
- F fails: while tailoring to preferences is correct in principle, it requires first diagnosing root causes (A) and updating the plan (B); tailoring without diagnosis is premature and may not address the actual problem.

**Mindset Lesson:** Communications improvement follows the same sequence as all PM problem-solving: diagnose root cause, update the governing plan, implement structural fixes — volume and escalation are not substitutes.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Manage Communications executes the plan; it does not create it (Planning) or evaluate it (Monitoring and Control).
- The PM owns communication outcomes — if a stakeholder is uninformed, the PM is accountable, not the stakeholder.
- Push = delivery attempt; Pull = stakeholder self-serves from PMIS or radiator; Interactive = real-time two-way for complex or sensitive topics.
- Sending a message is not communicating — the feedback loop must close; confirmed receipt and understanding are required.
- Communication channels grow as n(n−1)/2; adding one stakeholder multiplies coordination complexity, not just linearly.
- Stakeholder complaints about too much or too little information require a plan update through change control, not an ad-hoc fix.
- In agile, information radiators and standups replace formal reports for the delivery team; the PM ensures stakeholders know how to access them.
- Honest, accurate reporting is non-negotiable; no sponsor instruction overrides the PMI Code of Ethics honesty obligation.
- PMIS is the single source of truth; verbal-only or email-only communication without archiving is a governance failure.
- Material events require out-of-cycle communications; the scheduled cadence is a floor, not a ceiling.
- **Reflex burn-in: Consult the communications management plan first — it defines the who, what, when, and how; deviation from it requires a change request, not a judgment call.**
