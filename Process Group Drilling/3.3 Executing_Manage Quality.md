# PMP Exam Master Brief: Manage Quality

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Manage Quality is the Executing process that translates the quality management plan into actionable quality activities across the project. Where Plan Quality Management defines standards and Control Quality measures outputs against those standards, Manage Quality sits in between — it audits processes, identifies improvements, and ensures the project is using the right methods to produce the right results. It is process-focused rather than product-focused: the work here is about whether the project's processes are capable of delivering quality outcomes, not about inspecting deliverables directly. Exam weight falls within the Delivery domain (approximately 15% of questions) with heavy cross-bleed into Process Improvement, Team Performance, and Stakeholder Engagement domains. In a predictive context, Manage Quality manifests as quality audits, process analysis, and design-for-quality reviews; in agile, it shows up as retrospectives, definition of done enforcement, and built-in testing cadences. Regulatory environments — especially financial services, banking, and BSEC-governed capital markets — amplify this process because non-compliant processes generate audit findings that cascade into reputational and legal risk. The process consumes quality metrics, the quality management plan, lessons learned, and risk data, and produces quality reports, test and evaluation documents, and change requests. PMI treats Manage Quality as a proactive, prevention-oriented activity: finding and fixing process gaps before they generate defects is always preferred over inspection-and-rejection cycles. **In one sentence:** Manage Quality ensures project processes are fit for purpose so that deliverables will meet quality standards — auditing how work gets done, not just what gets produced.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Quality audits conducted by QA team or PMO; formal process improvement plans; structured use of fishbone diagrams, FMEA, and process capability analysis; change requests raised to correct non-conforming processes |
| Agile | Retrospectives as the primary quality improvement vehicle; definition of done as the built-in quality gate; continuous integration and automated testing as process-quality tools; team self-inspection replaces separate QA function |
| Hybrid | Formal audit checkpoints at phase gates combined with sprint-level retrospectives; quality metrics tracked in dashboards reviewed by both delivery team and governance board; change requests follow predictive channels even when delivery is iterative |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Prevention over inspection — PMI expects PMs to fix the process so defects never reach inspection; real-world PMs often add more inspectors when defect rates rise.
2. Quality is built in, not bolted on — PMI treats quality as a design constraint from the start; experienced PMs commonly treat it as a final-phase gate.
3. Audits are learning tools, not punishment mechanisms — PMI frames quality audits as opportunities to identify best practices and share improvements; practitioners often experience audits as compliance exercises.
4. The cost of conformance is always preferable to the cost of non-conformance — PMI expects PMs to fund prevention and appraisal activities proactively; real-world budget pressure pushes PMs to defer quality investment.
5. Process improvement is continuous, not event-driven — PMI expects ongoing process analysis throughout the project; practitioners commonly reserve improvement discussions for post-project retrospectives.

**Ethics Angle**

- Respect: Quality audits must be conducted without bias or personal agenda; findings must respect the dignity of team members whose work is being examined.
- Fairness: Quality standards must be applied consistently across all work packages and all team members; selective enforcement violates PMI's fairness principle.
- Honesty: Quality reports must accurately reflect process performance even when results are unfavorable to the project or sponsor; sanitizing negative findings is an honesty violation.
- Responsibility: The PM is accountable for ensuring the project's processes are capable of meeting stakeholder quality expectations; delegating quality oversight entirely to a QA team does not discharge this responsibility.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Quality Audit | Independent, structured review of project processes to determine compliance with policies, standards, and procedures | Do not confuse with Control Quality (which inspects products/deliverables, not processes) |
| Cost of Quality (COQ) | Total cost of all efforts related to quality = Cost of Conformance + Cost of Non-Conformance | Exam often asks which category a specific cost falls into; rework is always Non-Conformance |
| Cost of Conformance | Prevention + Appraisal costs — money spent to avoid failures (training, documentation, testing equipment) | Do not assume appraisal = inspection of deliverables only; process reviews are also appraisal costs |
| Cost of Non-Conformance | Internal failure + External failure costs — money spent because of failures (rework, scrap, warranty, lost business) | Warranty claims are external failure costs, not prevention costs |
| Process Analysis | Systematic examination of processes to identify inefficiencies, non-value-added activities, and root causes of quality problems | Distinct from root cause analysis (which diagnoses a specific problem); process analysis is broader and proactive |
| Quality Metrics | Specific measurements that describe an attribute and how it will be measured during Control Quality | Defined in Plan Quality Management; used in Manage Quality to assess process performance |
| Design for X (DfX) | Technique to optimize a specific aspect of design (testability, manufacturability, reliability) during quality management | Not limited to manufacturing; applicable to any project delivering a product |
| Fishbone / Ishikawa Diagram | Cause-and-effect diagram used in process analysis to identify root causes of quality problems | A diagnostic tool — it identifies causes; it does not prescribe solutions |
| FMEA (Failure Mode and Effects Analysis) | Proactive technique to identify how processes or products could fail and prioritize risks by severity × probability × detectability | Used in Manage Quality to prevent failures, not in Control Quality to detect them |
| Quality Report | Output of Manage Quality; documents quality management issues, recommendations for corrective actions, and results of quality improvement activities | Do not confuse with quality control measurements (output of Control Quality) |
| Test and Evaluation Documents | Created or updated during Manage Quality to define how quality will be tested; used later in Control Quality | Created here (Executing), not in Plan Quality Management (Planning) |
| Continual Process Improvement | Iterative improvement of all processes based on quality findings; a core PMI principle under Manage Quality | PMI expects this to be ongoing throughout the project, not reserved for project close |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Quality audit reveals a process is not being followed | Document the finding and raise a change request to correct the process | Manage Quality outputs include change requests; the PM does not simply instruct the team to comply without formal documentation |
| Team is producing deliverables that keep failing inspection | Analyze the process producing the deliverables (root cause / process analysis) | Failures signal a process problem; adding more inspectors treats symptoms, not causes |
| Sponsor asks PM to skip the quality audit to save schedule | Explain the risk of skipping the audit and escalate if sponsor insists | PMI expects PMs to protect project processes; compliance cannot be waived unilaterally by a sponsor |
| A quality metric shows process performance is trending toward the control limit | Investigate and implement preventive action before the limit is breached | Prevention over detection; act before a defect occurs, not after |
| New regulation requires a process change mid-project | Raise a change request through integrated change control | Process changes are not informal; they require CCB review regardless of regulatory urgency |
| Team member raises a process improvement idea during a retrospective | Document it in lessons learned and evaluate for implementation via change request | PMI values lessons learned as a living document; ideas must be formally evaluated, not just verbally acknowledged |
| Quality report shows COQ is disproportionately weighted toward non-conformance costs | Increase investment in prevention and appraisal activities | High non-conformance costs signal under-investment in prevention; the fix is upstream, not more rework capacity |
| Stakeholder complains that quality standards are too strict and are causing delays | Review the quality management plan with the stakeholder and assess impact via change request | Standards cannot be unilaterally relaxed; changes go through formal change control |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do |
|---|---|
| Functional manager refuses to release subject-matter expert needed for quality audit | Escalate to sponsor; the PM does not have authority to override functional hierarchy unilaterally |
| Quality audit finding implicates a vendor's process, but the vendor disputes the finding | Refer to the contract's quality provisions; engage procurement and document formally before escalating |

        |

**Mnemonic:** **A P R D** — Audit, Process-analyze, Root-cause, Design-improve

**Memory hook:** "A PR drives quality" — every quality improvement starts with an Audit, Process analysis, Root cause diagnosis, and a Design fix.

**Unpack:**
- A = Audit (quality audits check process compliance)
- P = Process Analysis (systematic review of how work flows)
- R = Root Cause (Ishikawa, 5 Whys — find the source)
- D = Design Improvement (DfX, FMEA — build the fix in)

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Quality audits are formal, scheduled events with documented findings and required management responses.
- COQ analysis is explicit and tracked as part of project financials.
- Process improvement plans are standalone artifacts reviewed by PMO or governance board.
- Corrective and preventive actions flow through integrated change control.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Retrospectives replace formal quality audits as the primary process-improvement mechanism.
- Definition of done is the primary quality gate; violations trigger immediate team-level correction without formal change requests.
- Continuous integration pipelines serve as automated process-quality enforcement.
- In hybrid, formal audit checkpoints align with phase gates while sprints use retrospective-based improvement.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- Bangladesh Bank and BSEC-regulated projects (core banking implementations, trading platform upgrades) require documented quality audits as part of regulatory compliance — skipping audits is not a schedule trade-off, it is a regulatory violation.
- ISO 9001-aligned organizations treat Manage Quality as synonymous with the Quality Management System (QMS) audit cycle; findings must be tracked in a corrective action register.
- Financial services projects with SOX or IFRS implications require process audits to be conducted by independent parties, not project team members.
- Pharmaceutical and healthcare project analogs: FDA 21 CFR Part 11 mandates process validation documentation that maps directly to Manage Quality outputs.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Increase inspection frequency when defect rates rise | Intuitive operational response; more checking seems to catch more problems faster | Inspection is a non-conformance cost; it does not fix the process. PMI requires process analysis and correction, not more inspection |
| Ask the team to follow the process more carefully | Sounds like a leadership response; directing the team is within PM authority | Without understanding why the process is not being followed, verbal instruction does not solve the root cause; a formal audit finding and change request are required |
| Skip the quality audit to recover schedule | Experienced PMs make trade-offs under schedule pressure routinely | Audits are planned process activities; skipping them creates undocumented risk and violates the quality management plan without going through change control |
| Document quality issues and address them at project close | Lessons-learned culture supports end-of-project reflection | PMI expects continual process improvement during the project; deferring findings to closure allows defects to compound |
| Escalate quality findings directly to the sponsor before investigating | Sponsors want to know about problems; proactive communication is valued | PMI expects the PM to investigate, perform root cause analysis, and propose solutions before escalating; raw findings without analysis are not actionable |
| Accept the vendor's self-assessment in lieu of an audit | Trust-based vendor management is efficient and relationship-preserving | Contracts typically require independent verification; accepting self-assessment without review abdicates the PM's quality oversight responsibility |
| Treat the quality report as an internal document only | Protecting team morale and avoiding negative perceptions is a real-world concern | Quality reports are formal project documents distributed per the communications management plan; withholding them from stakeholders who should receive them is a transparency violation |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A PM on a core banking system replacement project at a large commercial bank has just completed a quality audit. The audit reveals that the development team has been bypassing the mandatory peer code review process to meet sprint velocity targets set by the sponsor. The sponsor is a senior executive who championed this project and is under board-level pressure to deliver on time. The quality manager recommends issuing a formal audit finding and raising a change request to enforce the peer review process, which will reduce velocity. The PM knows the sponsor will push back hard. The development lead privately tells the PM that the bypassed reviews have not caused any known defects yet. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Inform the sponsor of the audit finding verbally and ask for informal guidance before documenting anything.
B. Document the audit finding in the quality report and raise a change request to enforce the peer review process.
C. Allow the bypass to continue since no defects have resulted and document the risk in the risk register instead.
D. Escalate immediately to the PMO without informing the sponsor, to avoid political interference with the audit finding.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A software delivery team using Scrum is three sprints into a six-month digital transformation project for a securities brokerage regulated by BSEC. During the sprint retrospective, four team members independently raise the same issue: the definition of done does not include a security vulnerability scan, and as a result, code with known low-severity vulnerabilities has been accepted as "done" in two prior sprints. The Scrum Master notes that adding the scan will add two days to each sprint cycle. The Product Owner says the backlog is already stretched. What is the BEST course of action?

A. Add the security scan to the definition of done immediately, since BSEC-regulated environments cannot accept known vulnerabilities regardless of sprint impact.
B. Log the concern as a team impediment and escalate to the sponsor to decide whether to add the scan.
C. Create a separate security backlog and schedule scans at the end of each release rather than each sprint.
D. Accept the current definition of done and add a compensating control — a manual security review at project close.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project to implement a new trade settlement platform is in its third phase. A formal quality audit conducted at the phase gate finds that the automated test coverage for the payment reconciliation module is only 34%, against a planned threshold of 80%. The audit report is due to the governance board in 48 hours. The test lead says bringing coverage to 80% will require three additional weeks. The sponsor tells the PM to submit the phase gate report showing the project is on track and address the test coverage gap "quietly" in the next phase. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Report the actual test coverage finding to the governance board and propose a formal remediation plan with timeline impact.
B. Submit the phase gate report as the sponsor directed, since the sponsor has authority over project reporting decisions.
C. Delay submitting the phase gate report until test coverage reaches 80% to avoid governance board concern.
D. Submit the report with a note that "quality activities are in progress" without specifying the coverage gap.

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is reviewing the cost of quality report for a large infrastructure project. The report shows that the total COQ has increased 18% over the last two months. Digging deeper, the PM finds that prevention costs have remained flat, appraisal costs have risen slightly, and internal failure costs (rework, scrap) have more than doubled. Which of the following is the BEST single action?

A. Increase the appraisal budget to catch more defects before they become external failures.
B. Conduct a process analysis to identify root causes of the increased internal failure costs.
C. Issue a team communication reinforcing quality standards and expectations.
D. Request additional budget from the sponsor to absorb the increased COQ.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile team is implementing a new customer onboarding module for a bank. During a retrospective, the team identifies that the process for integrating third-party KYC (Know Your Customer) APIs is poorly documented, leading to repeated integration failures. Place the following actions in the correct PMI-aligned sequence:

A. Update the definition of done to require API integration documentation before a story is accepted.
B. Conduct a root cause analysis to understand why documentation has been consistently skipped.
C. Raise the finding in the retrospective and log it in the team's improvement backlog.
D. Implement a documentation template and validate it with the team in the next sprint.

What is the correct order?

A. C → B → D → A
B. B → C → A → D
C. A → B → C → D
D. C → A → B → D

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT an output of the Manage Quality process?

A. Quality reports
B. Change requests
C. Quality control measurements
D. Test and evaluation documents

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A PM at an investment bank discovers during a quality audit that the data migration validation process has a gap: records with null values in a mandatory field were migrated without error-flagging, meaning the production database may contain corrupted records. The PM's manager tells the PM to "close the audit finding quietly" and fix it in a patch next quarter, so the project can be formally closed before the fiscal year end. Regulatory reporting based on this data is scheduled to begin next month. What must the PM do?

A. Close the audit finding as directed by the manager and log the patch work as a future project.
B. Document the finding in the quality report, escalate through appropriate channels including compliance/legal if necessary, and refuse to close the finding until it is resolved.
C. Raise the issue with the data governance team only, without formal documentation, to preserve the relationship with the manager.
D. Complete the project close as directed and personally monitor the production data until the patch is applied.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project team is delivering a regulatory reporting system for a capital markets firm. The project uses two-week sprints for development and formal monthly governance reviews. After the latest sprint review, the PM receives quality metrics showing that error rates in automated report generation have increased for the third consecutive sprint. The trend has not yet been reported to the governance board, whose next meeting is in two weeks. Three stakeholders have asked the PM directly about data quality. What is the BEST communication approach?

A. Wait for the monthly governance meeting to present the full quality trend data in context.
B. Send an immediate written update to all stakeholders disclosing the quality trend and the investigation underway.
C. Respond to the three stakeholders individually with verbal updates and prepare a formal written quality report for the governance board meeting.
D. Escalate the trend to the sponsor only and let the sponsor decide how to communicate to the governance board.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM on a hybrid project to upgrade a bank's anti-money laundering (AML) system receives a quality audit finding that a third-party vendor's testing process does not meet the bank's documented quality standards. The vendor's contract includes a clause requiring vendor processes to conform to the bank's quality management plan. The vendor's project manager disputes the finding and claims their internal testing process is "equivalent" to the bank's standard, though no equivalence documentation exists. The next governance review is in one week. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Accept the vendor's assertion of equivalence and close the audit finding to maintain the vendor relationship.
B. Escalate directly to the governance board at the next review without first attempting resolution with the vendor.
C. Request that the vendor provide documented evidence of process equivalence and engage procurement to review the contractual obligation.
D. Issue a contract penalty notice to the vendor immediately, since the audit finding constitutes a contract breach.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A PM is designing the quality management activities for a new phase of a predictive financial systems integration project. The PM wants to ensure that Manage Quality activities are correctly scoped. Which THREE of the following activities belong to Manage Quality (as opposed to Plan Quality Management or Control Quality)?

A. Conducting a quality audit to verify the development process follows the documented coding standards.
B. Measuring the defect density of delivered software modules against the defined quality metric thresholds.
C. Performing a process analysis to identify non-value-added steps in the change management workflow.
D. Defining the quality metrics that will be used to evaluate deliverable conformance.
E. Reviewing lessons learned from prior phases to identify process improvements for the current phase.
F. Calculating control chart limits for the software build process.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Delivery domain / Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility). Manage Quality requires quality findings to be documented in quality reports and process non-conformances to be addressed via change requests. Verbal informal guidance (A) bypasses the formal quality management system and sets a precedent for undocumented process exceptions. Allowing the bypass to continue (C) prioritizes schedule over quality and ignores the PM's responsibility to maintain project standards. Escalating over the sponsor's head without informing them (D) violates Respect and is premature — the PM must first follow the documented process.
**Mindset Lesson:** Document and formalize first; political sensitivity never justifies suppressing a quality audit finding.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide / PMBOK 7 Delivery domain / Code of Ethics (Responsibility). In BSEC-regulated environments, security vulnerabilities in delivered software are not a backlog prioritization issue — they are a compliance obligation. The definition of done is the agile team's built-in quality gate; a known gap in it must be corrected immediately. Logging as an impediment (B) delays a required fix. Scheduling scans at release level (C) creates a known quality gap across multiple sprints. A manual compensating control at close (D) is neither timely nor reliable.
**Mindset Lesson:** In regulated environments, the definition of done is non-negotiable on compliance dimensions — velocity is subordinate to regulatory quality requirements.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Delivery domain / Code of Ethics (Honesty, Fairness). Phase gate governance reports must accurately reflect project status. Submitting a report that misrepresents or omits a material quality finding violates PMI's honesty principle and exposes the organization to governance and regulatory risk. The sponsor's authority does not extend to directing the PM to misrepresent findings (B). Delaying the report (C) is unauthorized and creates its own governance problem. Vague language (D) is a form of deceptive communication.
**Mindset Lesson:** Governance board reports must be truthful; the PM's integrity is not subordinate to the sponsor's preferences.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Delivery domain / COQ principles. Doubled internal failure costs signal a process breakdown — rework is the consequence, not the cause. Process analysis is the correct Manage Quality response to identify root causes. Increasing appraisal (A) catches more defects but does not fix the process; it is a reactive, non-conformance-cost action. Team communication (C) is insufficient without diagnosing the problem. Budget escalation (D) addresses financial impact, not root cause.
**Mindset Lesson:** Rising internal failure costs always demand process analysis, not more detection capacity.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A (C → B → D → A)
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide / Manage Quality process flow. The retrospective is the discovery mechanism (C). Root cause analysis precedes solution design (B). The solution — a documentation template — is implemented and validated (D). Finally, the systemic fix is institutionalized by updating the definition of done (A), which ensures the improvement persists across future sprints.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, quality improvements follow the cycle: surface → diagnose → solve → institutionalize.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Process Groups: A Practice Guide. Quality control measurements are an output of Control Quality, not Manage Quality. Quality reports (A), change requests (B), and test and evaluation documents (D) are all outputs of Manage Quality. The exam frequently tests the boundary between Manage Quality outputs (process-focused) and Control Quality outputs (product-focused).
**Mindset Lesson:** Quality control measurements belong to Control Quality; Manage Quality produces quality reports and change requests.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility) / PMBOK 7 Delivery domain. A data integrity issue in a system used for regulatory reporting constitutes a material quality and compliance risk. Closing the finding quietly (A) violates honesty and responsibility. Informal undocumented escalation (C) fails the transparency test and does not protect stakeholders. Proceeding to close and personally monitoring (D) is not a sanctioned PM action and does not address the structural risk. PMI requires the PM to document, escalate through proper channels including compliance and legal, and refuse to close a finding that represents unresolved risk to stakeholders.
**Mindset Lesson:** Regulatory data integrity findings cannot be closed quietly — honesty and responsibility require formal escalation regardless of fiscal or political pressure.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder / Delivery domains / Communications Management. A three-sprint quality trend is a material project condition that stakeholders who have asked about it are entitled to know. Waiting two weeks (A) withholds information stakeholders have explicitly requested. Individual verbal updates (C) are inconsistent and not documented. Routing through the sponsor only (D) filters information in a way that may not serve all legitimate stakeholders. PMI's communications principle requires timely, accurate, and appropriately broad distribution of material project information.
**Mindset Lesson:** A documented recurring quality trend requires proactive written communication to all appropriate stakeholders — not selective verbal briefings.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Delivery / Procurement domains. The correct first step is to request documented evidence and engage procurement — this respects the contractual process and gives the vendor a formal opportunity to substantiate their claim before escalation. Accepting undocumented equivalence (A) abdicates oversight. Escalating to governance without attempting resolution (B) is premature. Issuing a penalty notice (D) is a legal action that requires procurement and legal review before the PM can initiate it unilaterally.
**Mindset Lesson:** Vendor quality disputes require documented evidence requests and procurement engagement before escalation or contractual action.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, C, E
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Process Groups: A Practice Guide. Quality audits (A) and process analysis (C) are core Manage Quality tools. Reviewing lessons learned to identify process improvements (E) is an input-to-action cycle that is explicitly part of Manage Quality. Measuring deliverable defect density (B) is Control Quality. Defining quality metrics (D) is Plan Quality Management. Calculating control chart limits (F) is Plan Quality Management.
**Mindset Lesson:** Manage Quality = process-level activities (audits, analysis, improvement); Plan and Control Quality handle metrics definition and deliverable measurement respectively.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Manage Quality is process-focused (Executing): are project processes capable of delivering quality outcomes?
- Control Quality is product-focused (Monitoring & Controlling): did deliverables actually meet standards?
- Core tools: quality audits, process analysis, root cause analysis, Design for X, FMEA.
- Primary outputs: quality reports, change requests, test and evaluation documents.
- COQ principle: prevention and appraisal costs are always preferred over internal and external failure costs.
- PMI reflex: when defects appear, analyze the process — never just add more inspectors.
- Quality audits are learning events, not punishments; findings always generate change requests, not informal verbal corrections.
- In agile, retrospectives and definition of done serve as the quality audit and improvement mechanism.
- In regulated environments (BSEC, Bangladesh Bank, SOX), Manage Quality activities are compliance obligations — they cannot be waived by the sponsor.
- Lessons learned are a living input to Manage Quality throughout the project, not just at close.
- **When defect rates rise, your first move is always process analysis — not more inspection, not more budget, not team lectures.**
