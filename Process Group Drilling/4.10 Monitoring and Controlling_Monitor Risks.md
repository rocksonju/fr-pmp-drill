# PMP Exam Master Brief: Monitor Risks

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Monitor Risks is the process of implementing risk response plans, tracking identified risks, identifying and analyzing new risks, and evaluating risk process effectiveness throughout the project. It belongs to the Risk Management knowledge area within the Monitoring and Controlling process group, and it runs continuously — not just at phase gates or when problems arise. On the PMP exam, Risk Management carries approximately 11% of questions, but Monitor Risks bleeds into Integration (change control), Schedule Control (float consumption as a risk indicator), and Cost Control (reserve burn as a risk signal). The process consumes work performance data and outputs work performance information, change requests, and updates to risk-related documents and the risk register. Practitioners often treat risk review as a periodic meeting formality; PMI treats it as a continuous analytical discipline requiring documented reassessment and audit. Reserve analysis — checking whether contingency and management reserves remain adequate against remaining risk exposure — separates passive monitoring from active control. Workarounds, the unplanned responses to risks that materialize without prior response plans, must be documented and may trigger the Perform Integrated Change Control process. Residual risks (remnants accepted after a primary response) and secondary risks (new risks introduced by the response itself) must both remain on the register and receive ongoing monitoring. **In one sentence:** Monitor Risks is the disciplined, continuous examination of whether the project's risk environment has changed and whether existing responses are working — feeding every deviation back into the change control system.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal risk register reviews on a scheduled cadence (monthly, at phase gates); risk audits conducted by an independent reviewer; reserve analysis tied to earned value metrics; change requests routed through CCB for any response adjustment |
| Agile | Risk identification embedded in retrospectives and daily standups; risk-adjusted backlog prioritization; risk burndown charts tracked sprint-over-sprint; team self-organizes response without formal CCB in most cases |
| Hybrid | Formal risk register maintained at program/portfolio level; sprint-level risk boards used for near-term tactical risks; escalation path to CCB defined for risks breaching thresholds established in the risk management plan |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Risk audits evaluate the effectiveness of the entire risk management process — not just whether a single response worked; real-world PMs audit only after a crisis, PMI audits proactively and regularly as a scheduled activity.
2. Workarounds are unplanned but must be documented immediately and treated as change requests — experienced PMs often implement workarounds informally and move on without updating the register or submitting a change request.
3. New risks discovered during monitoring must be analyzed and added to the register through the same process as original identification — practitioners frequently log them informally and skip the qualitative and quantitative analysis steps.
4. Reserve analysis is a monitoring tool, not just a planning tool — PMs routinely check budget balance but fail to explicitly compare remaining contingency reserve against remaining risk exposure.
5. Risk reassessment is scheduled and systematic — it is not triggered only by bad news; PMI expects reassessments to occur even when the project appears to be going well, because the risk environment changes regardless of current performance indicators.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Responsibility:** Accurately reporting risk status to stakeholders even when the news is unfavorable; not suppressing escalating risk indicators to protect project image or sponsor confidence.
- **Honesty:** Documenting workarounds and secondary risks transparently rather than concealing improvised responses in the interest of appearing in control.
- **Fairness:** Ensuring risk audit findings are applied consistently across all project workstreams, not selectively to politically exposed areas.
- **Respect:** Engaging team members who surface new risks with serious analytical follow-through rather than dismissing concerns to protect the schedule baseline.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Risk Reassessment | Scheduled re-examination of existing risks for changes in probability, impact, or status, and identification of new risks | Confusing reassessment (scheduled, systematic) with risk audits (which evaluate process effectiveness) — they are distinct tools with distinct purposes |
| Risk Audit | Independent review of the effectiveness of the risk management process, including whether risk responses are working as planned | Treating risk audits and risk reviews interchangeably; audits assess process effectiveness, reviews assess individual risk status |
| Workaround | An unplanned response to a risk event that has occurred without a prior response plan | Assuming workarounds are optional to document; PMI requires them to be documented and potentially routed through change control |
| Residual Risk | Risk remaining after a primary response has been implemented and accepted | Confusing residual risk with secondary risk; residual is what remains after a response, secondary is newly created by the response itself |
| Secondary Risk | New risk introduced as a direct result of implementing a risk response | Forgetting that secondary risks must be fully analyzed and placed on the register — a common omission in practice |
| Reserve Analysis | Comparison of remaining contingency reserves against remaining risk exposure to determine adequacy | Treating reserve analysis as a cost process only; it is explicitly a Monitor Risks tool in PMBOK |
| Risk Register (updated) | Living document capturing current status of all risks, triggers, owners, responses, and residual/secondary risks | Assuming the risk register is only updated during planning — it is a key output of Monitor Risks |
| Risk Report (updated) | Summary of overall risk exposure, key risks, and aggregate risk status communicated to stakeholders | Confusing risk register (individual risk detail) with risk report (aggregate portfolio-level summary for stakeholders) |
| Technical Performance Measurement | Comparison of technical accomplishments during execution against planned values to assess whether technical risk is materializing | Treating technical performance metrics as a scope or quality tool only; it also feeds Monitor Risks as a risk signal |
| Work Performance Information | Processed work performance data analyzed in context; output of monitoring processes including Monitor Risks | Confusing work performance data (raw inputs) with work performance information (analyzed, contextualized output) |
| Risk Threshold | The level of risk exposure above which the risk owner or sponsor must be engaged per the risk management plan | Forgetting that thresholds are pre-defined; ad hoc escalation decisions based on personal judgment are a PMI red flag |
| Contingency Reserve | Budget set aside to address identified risks if they materialize; owned and controlled by the PM | Confusing contingency reserve (PM controls, for identified risks) with management reserve (sponsor controls, for unknown unknowns) |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| A risk event occurs that has no response plan | Implement a workaround AND document it; create a change request | Workarounds are legitimate but must enter the formal change system — silent improvisation violates process integrity |
| Risk review meeting reveals a risk whose probability has increased significantly | Update the risk register and re-analyze using qualitative and quantitative tools | A changed risk profile requires reassessment through the same analytical steps as original identification |
| Risk audit finds that a response strategy is not working | Issue a change request to modify the response plan | Audits produce findings; findings require formal change requests — direct modification without CCB approval bypasses integrated change control |
| Contingency reserve is nearly depleted and project has significant remaining duration | Conduct reserve analysis and request management reserve access via change request | Reserve analysis must be done first to quantify the gap; then escalate formally — do not quietly draw from other line items |
| A new risk is identified mid-execution by a team member | Capture it in the risk register, analyze it, assign an owner, and plan a response | New mid-project risks receive full analysis treatment — logging without analysis is insufficient per PMI |
| Stakeholder asks for the current overall risk posture of the project | Provide the updated risk report | The risk report (not the register) is the aggregate stakeholder-facing artifact for overall exposure |
| A secondary risk materializes from a response that was just implemented | Treat it as a new risk: analyze, register, assign owner, plan response | Secondary risks get no special shortcut treatment — they enter the full risk management cycle |
| The project appears to be performing well and the sponsor suggests skipping the monthly risk review | Conduct the risk review on schedule per the risk management plan | The environment changes regardless of current performance; skipping scheduled reviews violates the risk management plan |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Risk exposure has breached the pre-defined threshold in the risk management plan | Escalate to the risk owner and sponsor immediately per threshold protocol | Thresholds are pre-defined escalation triggers — waiting to "see what happens" is a PMI wrong answer |
| A risk response is consuming more contingency than planned and the schedule buffer is also eroding | Perform integrated reserve analysis; issue a change request if reserves are insufficient | Dual-erosion signals require integrated analysis before escalation — jumping to the sponsor without data is premature |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Formal risk register maintained with quantitative thresholds; risk audits scheduled as separate events distinct from risk review meetings.
- Reserve analysis tied explicitly to EVM metrics (CPI, SPI) to detect reserve burn rate versus remaining exposure.
- All response modifications routed through the Change Control Board with formal change request documentation and CCB sign-off.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Risk burndown chart replaces formal risk register as the primary visualization tool at the team level; tracked sprint-over-sprint.
- Retrospectives serve as informal risk reassessment events; significant findings escalate to the formal risk register at program level.
- In hybrid environments, define the escalation threshold that converts a sprint-board risk item into a formal register entry requiring CCB review.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC / Bangladesh Bank context:** Regulatory risks (capital adequacy ratios, Bangladesh Bank circular compliance, BSEC reporting deadlines) require dedicated risk owners at the compliance function level; risk thresholds must align with regulatory breach triggers, not just project performance triggers. Reserve analysis must account for regulatory penalty exposure, not only direct project cost risk. Risk audits in regulated environments may require third-party auditors per Bangladesh Bank IT risk management guidelines. Monitor Risks outputs (risk reports) may be mandated disclosures to the board risk committee, making them formal regulatory artifacts rather than optional project documents.
- For construction and engineering: technical performance measurement carries disproportionate weight; physical progress versus planned is a leading indicator of risk materialization.
- For pharmaceutical and healthcare: regulatory submission risks are often near-zero-tolerance; risk thresholds are set extremely tight and escalation is mandatory at first signal regardless of overall project health.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Implement the workaround and move on without documenting it | Workarounds are emergency responses — experienced PMs act fast and document later (or never); it keeps the project moving | PMI requires workarounds to be documented immediately and may require a change request; undocumented workarounds violate process integrity and leave the register inaccurate |
| Skip the scheduled risk review because the project is on track | Seems efficient; experienced PMs conserve meeting time when things are going well and the team is confident | The risk management plan defines the review cadence; the environment changes regardless of current performance; skipping is a process violation |
| Update the risk register verbally during a meeting without formal documentation | Agile culture normalizes verbal decisions; meeting minutes feel sufficient to most practitioners | The risk register is a formal project document; updates must be captured as written artifacts, not only in meeting notes |
| Escalate immediately to the sponsor when a new risk is identified | Sponsors expect to be kept informed; escalating shows diligence and transparency | PMI expects the PM to analyze the risk first (qualitative, and if needed quantitative) before escalating — unanalyzed escalation wastes executive time and bypasses process |
| Close a risk from the register once its trigger date has passed | Common practice: if the risk window is gone, the risk is gone — no point maintaining dead entries | Risks with passed triggers must be formally closed with documented rationale — silent removal corrupts register integrity and historical accuracy |
| Use contingency reserve for an unknown-unknown risk event | Contingency is available and the PM controls it; feels pragmatic in the moment | Unknown unknowns draw from management reserve, not contingency; misusing contingency misreports risk exposure and violates reserve policy |
| Treat a secondary risk informally because it emerged from our own response | It feels like an internal technical detail, not a "real" risk requiring full treatment | Secondary risks receive full risk management treatment: analysis, owner assignment, response planning, and formal register entry |
| Conduct the risk audit yourself as PM to save time and maintain continuity | PMs know the project best; self-audit seems most efficient and comprehensive | Risk audits should be conducted by an independent reviewer to ensure objectivity — self-audit creates a conflict of interest and undermines audit validity |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1**

You are the PM for a core banking system replacement project at a major commercial bank regulated by Bangladesh Bank. During a monthly risk review meeting, a team lead discloses that a critical database migration risk — originally rated low probability — has now materialized as a full system outage during a test cycle. The risk response plan called for a fallback to the legacy system, but the legacy system has already been decommissioned ahead of schedule by the infrastructure team as a cost-saving measure. The sponsor is in a board meeting and unreachable. Bangladesh Bank has a regulatory window requiring system availability restoration within 4 hours or a mandatory incident report is triggered. What should you do FIRST?

A. Immediately notify the Bangladesh Bank regulator that an incident has occurred and provide a restoration timeline.
B. Implement an unplanned workaround to restore system functionality, document the workaround, and prepare a change request while simultaneously alerting the sponsor's delegate.
C. Convene an emergency CCB meeting to formally approve a workaround before any technical action is taken.
D. Escalate to the portfolio manager and wait for authorization before taking any technical action.

**Q2**

Your agile team is three sprints into a payment gateway integration project for a capital markets firm. During the sprint retrospective, three team members independently raise a new risk: the third-party API vendor has begun showing signs of financial instability based on publicly available news. No risk response plan exists for vendor insolvency. The product owner wants to add vendor replacement research as a backlog item in the next sprint. What is the BEST approach?

A. Accept the team's suggestion, add vendor replacement research to the backlog, and monitor the situation through the risk burndown chart.
B. Analyze the risk through qualitative assessment, assign a risk owner, create a response plan including a contingency option for an alternative vendor, and reflect the risk in the risk burndown chart with a formal register entry at the program level.
C. Escalate immediately to the steering committee because vendor insolvency is outside the team's authority to manage.
D. Wait until there is confirmed news of vendor insolvency before allocating any response effort, as acting on rumors wastes sprint capacity.

**Q3**

You are managing a hybrid project — a regulatory reporting module for a BSEC-listed company — with a formal risk register at program level and agile sprint boards at the delivery team level. A developer identifies that a recently implemented encryption response (added to address a data-at-rest risk) has introduced a new performance risk: query response times now exceed the regulatory SLA threshold in load testing. The sprint board shows this as a low-priority item because the current sprint's velocity looks healthy. You have 10 days before the next formal risk register review. What should you FIRST do?

A. Elevate the secondary risk to the formal risk register immediately, analyze it, assign an owner, and create a response plan — do not wait for the scheduled review cycle.
B. Document the performance issue on the sprint board and allow the team to address it in the next sprint given the healthy velocity.
C. Notify the BSEC compliance officer immediately, as any SLA breach is a mandatory disclosure.
D. Convene an emergency CCB meeting to halt the encryption implementation until the performance risk is resolved.

**Q4**

During a risk audit of a large infrastructure project, the auditor finds that three risk responses were implemented without documented change requests and that the contingency reserve has been drawn down 40% faster than the risk exposure reduction rate suggests it should have been. Which answer BEST describes what the PM should do?

A. Issue retroactive change requests for the three undocumented responses and conduct a reserve analysis to quantify the gap between reserve burn rate and remaining exposure.
B. Issue retroactive change requests and immediately request management reserve access to cover the shortfall.
C. Conduct a reserve analysis, brief the sponsor on the findings, and request management reserve if the analysis confirms inadequacy — without retroactive change requests, since the responses were effective and the work is already done.
D. Accept the audit findings as lessons learned and improve the process going forward without retroactive documentation, since the responses were effective.

**Q5**

An agile team is managing a regulatory compliance sprint. Rank the following Monitor Risks activities in the correct PMI-preferred sequence for an agile project during a sprint cycle: (1) Update the risk burndown chart, (2) Identify new risks during the daily standup, (3) Add newly identified risks to the team's risk board, (4) Discuss risk status and process effectiveness in the retrospective, (5) Escalate risks breaching the program threshold to the formal register.

A. 2, 3, 1, 5, 4
B. 2, 3, 5, 1, 4
C. 4, 2, 3, 1, 5
D. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5

**Q6**

Which of the following is NOT a tool or technique of the Monitor Risks process?

A. Risk audits
B. Reserve analysis
C. Risk categorization using a Risk Breakdown Structure
D. Technical performance measurement

**Q7**

You are the PM for a government procurement digitization project. During a risk review, you discover that a team member has been reporting risks as "closed" in the register without documented closure rationale, and in two cases the risks were closed because the team member believed disclosing them would delay the project and upset the sponsor. The project is currently under a regulatory audit. What should you FIRST do?

A. Counsel the team member privately and correct the register entries before the auditor reviews them, to protect the team member from formal consequences.
B. Reopen the affected risk entries with accurate status, document the issue in the lessons learned register, and disclose the register discrepancies to the auditor proactively.
C. Ask the sponsor whether to disclose the discrepancies, since the sponsor is the one most affected by the political implications.

**Q8**

You are managing an agile squad delivering a mobile banking app for a commercial bank. The product owner has proposed that risk reviews be replaced with a biweekly "risk radar" discussion embedded in the sprint review, attended only by the squad. Senior stakeholders and the compliance team are not invited. Sprint velocity is high and the team is confident. What is the BEST response?

A. Accept the product owner's proposal because the squad is best positioned to identify and respond to risks given their velocity and confidence.
B. Retain the scheduled risk review meeting structure as defined in the risk management plan; ensure compliance and senior stakeholder representation is maintained, and use the sprint review as a supplementary forum only.

**Q9**

During a sprint review, a technical risk (API latency under peak load) is reassessed and its score moves from 0.30 to 0.45 due to new load test data. The sprint ends in 6 days. The next formal risk committee meeting is in 9 days. What should the PM do?

A. Document the updated score and present it at the next formal risk committee meeting in 9 days, since the sprint review is the appropriate escalation venue.
B. Escalate to the program risk committee within 48 hours per the risk management plan threshold definition, regardless of the sprint cycle timing.
C. Discuss the risk with the product owner and development lead; if they agree the risk is manageable, defer escalation until the next formal meeting.
D. Issue a change request to reduce the sprint scope so the API latency risk can be resolved before escalation is necessary.

**Q10**

C. A risk was reassessed and found to have decreased in probability — it now falls below the monitoring threshold.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Monitor Risks — workarounds are unplanned responses that must be implemented and documented; PMBOK 7 Delivery Performance Domain emphasizes maintaining system stability while initiating formal change processes. Option A (notifying the regulator first) skips the response step — regulatory notification may eventually be required but restoration of function is the immediate priority given the 4-hour window. Option C (waiting for CCB approval before acting) is wrong because a 4-hour regulatory window cannot accommodate a formal CCB convening before technical action. Option D (waiting for portfolio manager authorization) creates dangerous delay and abdication of PM authority in a time-sensitive regulatory environment.
**Mindset Lesson:** Workarounds are legitimate emergency responses — act first, document simultaneously, then escalate formally through change control.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** New risks identified during execution must receive full qualitative analysis, owner assignment, and response planning per PMBOK Risk Management and Agile Practice Guide. Option A is insufficient — adding to the backlog without formal analysis and a program-level register entry fails the Monitor Risks standard. Option C (immediate escalation without analysis) is premature; analysis must precede escalation. Option D (waiting for confirmation) violates the PMI principle of proactive risk management based on emerging signals.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile, new risks still require analysis and formal register entry at the program level — the sprint backlog is not a substitute for the risk register.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Secondary risks receive full risk management treatment regardless of when they are discovered (PMBOK 11.6); a sprint board item that breaches a regulatory SLA threshold is an immediate risk event, not a routine backlog item. Option B (leaving it on the sprint board at low priority) ignores the regulatory SLA breach signal entirely. Option C (notifying BSEC immediately) is premature — the breach is in load testing, not in production or regulatory reporting. Option D (emergency CCB to halt implementation) is disproportionate at the diagnostic finding stage before analysis is complete.
**Mindset Lesson:** Secondary risks that breach defined thresholds bypass the scheduled review cycle — they escalate immediately regardless of sprint timing.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Undocumented responses require retroactive change requests to restore process integrity per PMBOK Integrated Change Control; reserve analysis is the correct tool to quantify the adequacy gap explicitly (Monitor Risks tools and techniques). Option B incorrectly jumps to management reserve access without first conducting the analysis to confirm the gap magnitude. Option C skips the retroactive documentation, leaving the change control record permanently incomplete. Option D treats a process violation as a lessons-learned item rather than a corrective action requirement — a fundamental PMI mindset error.
**Mindset Lesson:** Process integrity is not optional — retroactive documentation is a corrective action requirement, not bureaucratic excess.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A (2, 3, 1, 5, 4)
**PMI Justification:** Per Agile Practice Guide and PMBOK: risks are identified as they surface (standup) → captured on the board → risk burndown updated to reflect new risk → threshold breach triggers formal escalation → retrospective reviews the risk management process effectiveness last. Option B escalates before updating the burndown chart, skipping the quantification step that informs the escalation. Option C starts with retrospective before identification, inverting the cycle.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile Monitor Risks, identification and capture precede escalation; retrospective evaluates process effectiveness at the end of the cycle.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) categorization is a tool of Plan Risk Management and Identify Risks — not Monitor Risks. Risk audits (A), reserve analysis (B), and technical performance measurement (D) are all explicitly listed Monitor Risks tools and techniques in PMBOK 6 and referenced in PMBOK 7.
**Mindset Lesson:** RBS is a planning and identification tool — it does not appear in the Monitor Risks toolset on the exam.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility require transparent disclosure of project status to authorized stakeholders, including external auditors. Option A (correcting before auditors see it) constitutes active concealment, violating the Honesty standard regardless of intent to protect the team member. Option C (asking the sponsor) inappropriately delegates an ethics decision that the PM has a direct professional obligation to make. Option D (excluding the external auditor) violates the Fairness and Honesty standards of the PMI Code.
**Mindset Lesson:** Ethics violations require transparent correction — concealing a process failure to manage consequences is itself a second, compounding ethics violation.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** The risk management plan defines stakeholder involvement in risk reviews; compliance and regulatory stakeholders in a banking context have a legitimate stake that the squad cannot self-govern away. Option A incorrectly allows velocity and team confidence to substitute for process compliance. Option C delegates a process decision to the sponsor unnecessarily when the PM has the authority and obligation to enforce the risk management plan. Option D (written summaries instead of participation) reduces compliance stakeholder engagement effectiveness, particularly for functions with legal and regulatory accountability.
**Mindset Lesson:** Agile velocity does not override stakeholder engagement requirements defined in the risk management plan — particularly for regulated industries.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** The risk management plan threshold is a binding escalation trigger — 48-hour escalation for scores above 0.40 is not subject to override by sprint cycle timing, team confidence, or scheduling convenience (PMBOK Monitor Risks / Risk Management Plan). Option A waits 9 days, directly violating the 48-hour threshold. Option C allows team consensus to override a documented plan threshold — never a correct PMI answer. Option D misuses scope management to circumvent risk process obligations.
**Mindset Lesson:** Risk thresholds are pre-defined escalation mandates — no meeting schedule, sprint cycle, or team consensus overrides them.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** A, D, F
**PMI Justification:** A — response implemented differently from the plan requires a change request to align the approved plan with actuals (PMBOK Integrated Change Control). D — reserve adequacy gap is a formal finding that requires a change request to access management reserve or adjust the reserve baseline. F — replacing an ineffective response requires a change request to authorize the new approach through CCB. B (new secondary risk) requires register updates and analysis but not a change request unless a new response is being formally authorized as a plan modification. C (risk decreasing below threshold) requires a register status update only. E (register update for verbal agreement) requires documentation but is an administrative update, not a change request.
**Mindset Lesson:** Change requests are triggered by deviations from the approved plan — response gaps, reserve gaps, and strategy replacements all qualify; register updates alone do not.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Monitor Risks runs continuously throughout the project — not only at phase gates or when problems arise.
- Risk Reassessment is scheduled and systematic; it evaluates changes in existing risks and identifies new ones on a defined cadence.
- Risk Audits evaluate process effectiveness — they are distinct from risk review meetings and should be conducted by an independent reviewer.
- Workarounds are unplanned responses to materialized risks — they must be documented immediately and may require a formal change request.
- Secondary risks (created by responses) and residual risks (accepted remainders) both stay on the register and receive ongoing monitoring.
- Reserve Analysis compares remaining contingency reserve against remaining risk exposure — it is a Monitor Risks tool, not only a cost management tool.
- All risk response changes, workarounds, and reserve adjustments route through Perform Integrated Change Control.
- New risks identified mid-project receive the same qualitative (and if warranted, quantitative) analysis as originally identified risks — no shortcuts.
- Risk thresholds defined in the risk management plan are mandatory escalation triggers — they cannot be overridden by team consensus or scheduling convenience.
- **When a risk materializes without a plan: implement a workaround, document it immediately, issue a change request — never improvise silently.**
