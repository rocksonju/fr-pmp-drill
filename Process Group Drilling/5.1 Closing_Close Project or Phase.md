# PMP Exam Master Brief: Close Project or Phase

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Close Project or Phase is the single Integration Management process in the Closing Process Group. It finalizes all activities across all Process Groups to formally complete the project, phase, or contract. Outputs include final product/service/result transition, lessons learned repository updates, final reports, and formal sign-off from the sponsor or customer. On the exam, this process appears in roughly 7–10% of questions but bleeds heavily into Scope (validate scope), Procurement (close procurements), Stakeholder (final engagement), and Communications (archive and distribute). The process is not merely administrative paperwork — it is the mechanism by which organizational process assets are enriched, the project's value is confirmed, and the team is released with integrity. Hybrid and agile contexts surface this topic through sprint retrospectives, release retrospectives, and iteration reviews that serve as mini-close cycles. Regulated industries such as Bangladesh Bank-supervised institutions or BSEC-regulated capital market firms have mandatory post-project audit, regulatory sign-off, and document retention requirements that layer on top of PMI's closure framework. Premature closure, incomplete handover, and skipping lessons learned are the three most-tested failure modes.

**In one sentence:** Close Project or Phase is the formal, deliberate act of confirming all work is done, transferring the product, capturing knowledge, releasing resources, and archiving records — with sponsor/customer sign-off as the non-negotiable gate.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Formal closure document signed by sponsor; final lessons learned session; procurements formally closed; all scope validated and accepted; resources released per resource management plan |
| **Agile** | Each iteration/sprint ends with a mini-close (retrospective + review); release retrospective captures team learning; product owner confirms increment accepted; no formal "closure document" but working software is the acceptance artifact |
| **Hybrid** | Phase gates act as closure checkpoints; agile delivery teams hold retrospectives while predictive governance layer requires signed phase-exit reports; dual documentation burden is common in regulated banking projects |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Lessons learned must be captured and stored — not just discussed.** Real-world instinct: hold the meeting, move on. PMI instinct: document, store in organizational process assets (OPA), make searchable for future projects. The *repository* matters, not the conversation.

2. **Formal acceptance must come from the sponsor or authorized customer, not the project manager.** Real-world instinct: if the PM is satisfied the work is done, mark it closed. PMI instinct: the PM cannot self-authorize closure. Documented sign-off from the customer/sponsor is a required output.

3. **Close Project or Phase happens even when the project is terminated early.** Real-world instinct: if cancelled, just stop. PMI instinct: a terminated project still requires a formal closure process — document what was done, what was not done, why it was cancelled, and what was learned.

4. **Administrative closure ≠ product acceptance.** Real-world instinct: if stakeholders are using the product, closure is complete. PMI instinct: usage does not equal formal acceptance. Written confirmation of acceptance must exist before administrative closure proceeds.

5. **Resource release is a closure output, not a side effect.** Real-world instinct: let resources go when they're done. PMI instinct: resources are formally released per the resource management plan as part of Close Project or Phase — unplanned release creates contract and morale risks.

### Ethics Angle

- **Responsibility:** Archiving lessons learned so future project teams benefit is an ethical obligation, not optional documentation hygiene.
- **Honesty:** Final project reports must accurately reflect performance — including failures, cost overruns, and risks that materialized — even when the sponsor prefers a sanitized picture.
- **Fairness:** Team performance assessments during closure must be objective; favoritism in final evaluations violates PMI's fairness value.
- **Respect:** Formal team release recognizes team members' contributions; ghosting resources after project end without recognition violates the respect value.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Close Project or Phase** | Single Integration Management closing process that formally completes all project/phase activities | Do not confuse with "Close Procurements" — procurement closure is an input TO this process, not the same process |
| **Final Lessons Learned Register** | Consolidated, searchable record of what worked, what did not, and recommended improvements; stored in OPA | Trap: thinking lessons learned only happens at project end — they are collected throughout and finalized at closure |
| **Formal Acceptance** | Written sign-off from the sponsor, customer, or authorized representative confirming the deliverable meets requirements | Trap: verbal acceptance or product usage does not constitute formal acceptance under PMI framework |
| **Organizational Process Assets (OPA) Update** | Closure updates project documents, templates, and historical records for reuse by future projects | Trap: OPAs are updated BY this process — they are not an input in the same way EEFs are; direction of flow matters |
| **Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEF)** | External or organizational conditions that influence (but are not controlled by) the PM — regulatory requirements, market conditions, organizational culture | Trap: EEFs are inputs to this process; confusing EEFs with OPAs on update direction |
| **Product/Service/Result Transition** | Formal handover of the final deliverable to operations, the customer, or a maintenance team | Trap: handover is not closure — handover must be followed by documentation of the transition and sign-off |
| **Procurement Audit** | Structured review of all procurement processes to identify successes and failures for future contracts | Trap: in Bangladesh Bank/BSEC-regulated projects, procurement audits are legally mandated and cannot be deferred post-closure |
| **Final Project Report** | Summary of project performance including scope, schedule, cost, quality, risk, and stakeholder outcomes | Trap: a final report that omits negative outcomes (cost overruns, deferred risks) is an ethics violation under PMI Code |
| **Benefits Realization** | Confirmation that the business value/benefits described in the business case are on track or have been realized | Trap: benefits realization may extend beyond project closure — the PM's responsibility is to document handover to the benefits owner, not to measure all benefits personally |
| **Premature Closure** | Termination of a project before full scope delivery — still requires a formal Close Project or Phase execution | Trap: believing cancelled projects skip closure — PMI requires closure regardless of why the project ended |
| **Administrative Closure** | The procedural side of closure: archiving documents, releasing resources, updating records, notifying stakeholders | Trap: confusing administrative closure with product acceptance — these are sequential, not simultaneous |
| **Release of Resources** | Formal notification to functional managers, HR, and procurement that team members and physical resources are available | Trap: releasing resources without updating the resource management plan creates downstream conflicts in portfolio resource allocation |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Project is complete and deliverables have been submitted" | Obtain formal written acceptance from the sponsor/customer | Submission is not acceptance — documented sign-off must precede any closure activity |
| "Project is being cancelled midway" | Initiate Close Project or Phase immediately | PMI requires closure even for terminated projects — document completed work, outstanding work, and reason for cancellation |
| "Team members are asking when they will be released" | Refer to the resource management plan and release per the plan | Release decisions follow the approved plan, not ad hoc requests or PM discretion |
| "Sponsor wants to skip lessons learned to save time" | Conduct the lessons learned session and update OPA | Lessons learned is a mandatory closure output — the sponsor cannot waive it; escalate if pressed |
| "Vendor has delivered final goods but contract payment is outstanding" | Close the procurement formally before completing administrative closure | Procurement closure is an input to Close Project or Phase — outstanding contracts block administrative closure |
| "Stakeholders are using the product but no formal sign-off exists" | Request written acceptance documentation retroactively | Usage without sign-off is not formal acceptance; the PM must close the documentation gap |
| "A newly discovered defect is found during closure activities" | Evaluate against the change control process — log as a defect or post-project issue | Defects found at closure must be formally dispositioned, not silently fixed or ignored |
| "The project final report reflects only successes and omits a cost overrun" | Revise the report to accurately reflect all performance data | Omitting negative outcomes violates PMI's honesty value and the integrity of organizational records |
| "Phase gate review has been passed but phase closure document is unsigned" | Escalate to sponsor for signature before proceeding to next phase | Phase closure requires documented sign-off — proceeding without it creates governance and audit risk |
| "Regulatory audit by Bangladesh Bank requires project documentation 3 years post-closure" | Ensure archive plan meets the 3-year retention requirement before closing records | Regulatory EEFs must be embedded in the closure plan; failure to comply is both a compliance and ethics failure |

### Topic-Specific Weak-Spot Scenarios — Escalation Edge Cases

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Sponsor insists on closing the project before outstanding change requests are resolved" | Document the unresolved change requests in the final project report and escalate to the sponsor in writing | Unresolved change requests at closure create post-project claims risk; the PM's duty is to document, not unilaterally decide |
| "Operations team refuses to sign the handover document due to incomplete user training" | Work to resolve the training gap first; do not force sign-off | Forced acceptance is not valid acceptance — the PM must address the legitimate concern before closure can proceed |

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Closure document is a formal deliverable requiring sponsor signature — store in project records
- Validate Scope (process) must precede Close Project or Phase — accepted deliverables feed into closure
- Procurement audit is conducted for each vendor contract before final administrative closure
- Final cost reconciliation and budget release must occur with finance team sign-off
- Lessons learned repository update follows organizational template — not free-form narrative

### Agile / Hybrid

- Each sprint/iteration ends with a mini-close: retrospective (lessons) + review (acceptance)
- Release retrospective at the end of a release cycle serves as phase closure equivalent
- In hybrid projects, agile teams may have continuous delivery but governance layer still requires a signed phase-exit report at major milestones
- Definition of Done (DoD) is the agile equivalent of formal acceptance criteria — satisfied DoD = implicit acceptance for that increment
- Backlog refinement at closure confirms no unaddressed items remain before project end

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Bangladesh Bank supervised institutions:** Bangladesh Bank's ICT Security guidelines (DOS/ICT Policy 2011 and revisions) require post-project documentation and audit trails; project closure must include an IT security compliance sign-off for technology projects
- **BSEC-regulated capital market firms:** Securities legislation may require preservation of project records related to system changes affecting investor data — 5–7 year retention norms apply; closure archive plan must reference these obligations explicitly
- **Banking sector procurement:** Large vendor contracts in Bangladeshi banking are subject to Public Procurement Act (government entities) or internal board-level approval; procurement closure must verify all approvals are in the project file
- **Regulated industries generally:** Closure without proper audit trails creates regulatory risk — the PM's duty is to ensure closure documentation satisfies both PMI's OPA update requirement and the external regulator's record-keeping requirement
- **Multi-phase financial system projects:** Phase closure gates in core banking implementations (e.g., T24/Temenos, Oracle FLEXCUBE rollouts) require IT steering committee sign-off in addition to sponsor sign-off

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Since the team is already working on the next project, skip the formal lessons learned session" | In practice, teams scatter fast and lessons learned feel like low-value admin when everyone is already busy | PMI requires lessons learned as a mandatory OPA update — skipping it denies future projects organizational knowledge; it is never optional regardless of team availability |
| "The project can be closed once all deliverables are submitted to the customer" | Delivery feels like the natural endpoint — if the work is done, the project is done | Submission is not acceptance. Formal written acceptance from the authorized party must be obtained; PMI explicitly distinguishes delivery from closure |
| "Release team members as they complete their tasks throughout the project" | This is efficient resource management and frees people for other assignments | Resources are released as part of Close Project or Phase per the resource management plan — uncoordinated early release can breach contracts, strand work, and violate the plan |
| "If the project was cancelled, no closure is needed — just notify the team and stop" | A cancelled project feels like there is nothing to close; the instinct is to minimize wasted effort | Terminated projects require the same formal closure: document completed work, outstanding scope, reason for cancellation, and lessons learned — PMI is explicit on this |
| "Verbal acceptance from the sponsor during the final demo is sufficient to close the project" | Senior PMs often rely on relationship and verbal confirmation, especially with trusted sponsors | PMI requires documented, written acceptance — verbal confirmation is not an auditable record and creates post-closure disputes about scope completeness |
| "The benefits are visible in operations, so benefits realization is complete and closure is done" | If users are benefiting from the product, the intent of the project is achieved | Benefits realization may extend years beyond project closure — the PM's duty at closure is to document handover to a named benefits owner, not to declare benefits achieved |
| "Update lessons learned at the end only — no need to capture throughout the project" | End-of-project consolidation feels cleaner and more efficient than ongoing documentation | PMI directs ongoing lessons learned capture throughout the project life cycle; waiting until closure risks losing detail and creates a compressed, low-quality record |
| "The PM can sign the final acceptance document if the sponsor is unavailable" | A pragmatic solution to keep the project moving and avoid administrative delay | The PM cannot self-authorize acceptance — the sponsor or designated customer representative must sign; the PM should escalate unavailability, not substitute their own signature |

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager at a BSEC-regulated brokerage firm has just completed a core trading platform upgrade. The sponsor, who is also the CTO, verbally accepted the platform during the final demonstration and has asked the PM to close the project immediately so the IT department's budget can be released for the next fiscal cycle. The sponsor explicitly says the formal closure document is "just paperwork" and not worth delaying the budget release. The PM knows the project ran 15% over budget due to a scope change the sponsor approved mid-project. The final project report drafted by the PM currently shows the project as "on budget" because the sponsor's change approval was informal and not logged in the change register.

What should the project manager do?

A. Close the project as requested by the sponsor — the sponsor's verbal acceptance during the demo and direct instruction are sufficient authority to proceed.
B. Update the final project report to accurately reflect the budget overrun with reference to the approved scope change, obtain written acceptance from the sponsor, and then close the project.
C. Close the project but add a private note in the PM's personal files about the actual cost, in case an audit occurs later.
D. Escalate the budget discrepancy to the CFO before taking any closure action, since the sponsor may be attempting to conceal financial data.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Delivery Performance Domain + Stakeholder Performance Domain) and PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty value. The final project report must accurately reflect all project performance data, including budget variances and their causes. The PM cannot sanitize a report at a sponsor's request without violating the Honesty value of the PMI Code of Ethics. Formal written acceptance is required regardless of verbal confirmation (PMBOK 7 and standard closure practice). The budget overrun must be traced to the approved scope change — if the change was verbally approved, the PM should reconstruct documentation of that approval and reflect the true cost picture.

**Why A fails:** Verbal acceptance is not formal acceptance under PMI standards; sponsor instruction does not override ethical documentation requirements.

**Why C fails:** A private note is not an OPA update and constitutes deliberate concealment — a Honesty violation.

**Why D fails:** The CFO is not the correct escalation target at this stage; the PM's immediate duty is accurate documentation, not immediate escalation above the sponsor without first attempting correct action.

**Mindset Lesson:** The sponsor's authority does not extend to authorizing false project records — accurate documentation is an ethical obligation the PM cannot delegate or waive.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide (Chapter 6 — Implementing Agile) and PMBOK 7 (Stakeholder Performance Domain). The sprint review is not complete when legitimate acceptance-blocking concerns are raised. Compliance concerns — especially those tied to regulatory requirements like Bangladesh Bank AML circulars — are acceptance criteria issues, not post-acceptance backlog items. The review must be kept open until the concern is dispositioned. Proceeding to retrospective without resolving an acceptance-level concern would mean the increment is not truly done.

**Why A fails:** Proceeding ignores a legitimate acceptance blocker — the review is not complete when acceptance-level concerns are unresolved.

**Why B fails:** Cancelling the retrospective is an overreaction; the issue should be resolved within the review first.

**Why D fails:** Accepting the increment and logging the compliance gap as a future backlog item bypasses regulatory obligation — AML compliance is not discretionary scope to be deferred.

**Mindset Lesson:** In regulated environments, the sprint review is not closed until all acceptance-level concerns — including regulatory compliance — are resolved, not deferred.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Delivery Performance Domain) and quality management principles. Intermittent transaction reconciliation failures in a payments system are potential defects affecting acceptance criteria. The PM's FIRST action must be to assess whether the reported failures are genuine acceptance-blocking defects — not to escalate over the objector's head or proceed with known failures. Forcing sign-off on a system with unvalidated defects creates operational, regulatory, and ethical risk.

**Why A fails:** Escalating over the operations lead without investigating the substance of the concern is a governance shortcut — the concern may be valid.

**Why B fails:** Proceeding to closure with a documented defect in a payments system is irresponsible and bypasses the PM's quality obligations.

**Why D fails:** A caveat signature is not genuine acceptance — it is a mechanism to formally acknowledge a defect without resolving it, which undermines closure integrity.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a legitimate technical concern blocks closure, investigate the concern before escalating the person raising it.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Team Performance Domain + OPA updates) and close project or phase process logic. Lessons learned must be captured while the team is still assembled and knowledge is fresh. Once resources are released and the team disperses, the opportunity to capture tacit knowledge is lost permanently. Resource release is important but can occur days later without losing organizational value — the reverse is not true.

**Why A fails:** Releasing resources first risks losing knowledge capture capacity — functional managers can wait a day; institutional knowledge cannot be recovered once the team disperses.

**Why C fails:** Budget reconciliation is a legitimate closure activity but is not a gate that must precede lessons learned capture — sequencing these together is an unnecessary delay.

**Why D fails:** PMI's standard logic implies lessons learned capture precedes resource release precisely to prevent knowledge loss — "no specified order" is incorrect; context and PM judgment align with capturing knowledge while the team is present.

**Mindset Lesson:** Lessons learned capture has a closing window — the team's assembly is the constraint; release resources after knowledge is preserved, not before.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide (sprint review → retrospective → administrative closure sequence) and OPA update logic. The correct sequence: product owner acceptance (2) confirms the work is done → release retrospective (1) captures what was learned from the completed release → team velocity/capacity data is updated (3) based on the now-closed release → artifacts archived (5) → stakeholders notified of transition (4) after all internal closure activities are complete.

**Why B fails:** Holding the retrospective before obtaining acceptance means reflecting on work that has not yet been formally confirmed as complete.

**Why C fails:** Notifying stakeholders before completing internal closure activities (retrospective, records update, archive) is premature.

**Why D fails:** Updating velocity data before acceptance and retrospective is out of sequence — the retrospective may surface data that affects how performance is recorded.

**Mindset Lesson:** In agile closure, acceptance gates all subsequent closure activities — the product owner's sign-off is the starting gun, not the finish line.

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 and ECO (Delivery Domain). Identifying and logging new risks for ongoing monitoring after project closure is NOT part of the Close Project or Phase process. Once a project is closed, the risk register is archived — it is not actively expanded with new risks. If new risks are identified that affect operations, they are handed to the operations team as part of the product transition, not logged as project risks in a closed project's register.

**Why A is legitimate:** Lessons learned update to OPA is a required closure output.

**Why B is legitimate:** Formal written acceptance is a required closure input/output gate.

**Why D is legitimate:** Resource release per the resource management plan is a required closure activity.

**Mindset Lesson:** A closed project does not generate new project risks — post-closure issues belong to operations, not the project register.

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct — Honesty value (mandatory standard 5.2: "We do not engage in or condone behavior that is designed to deceive others"). A final project report is an official organizational record. Framing a PM estimation failure as a regulatory change impact is a deliberate misrepresentation. The sponsor's instruction does not create ethical cover for the PM. The PM's professional obligation is to accurate reporting regardless of personal career consequence.

**Why A fails:** The sponsor's authority does not extend to directing the PM to misrepresent facts in an official document — following this instruction makes the PM complicit in fraud.

**Why C fails:** PMO guidance cannot override the ethical obligation to accurate reporting — this is not a policy ambiguity question, it is a honesty obligation.

**Why D fails:** Noting the sponsor's approval of the framing does not absolve the PM — the PM's name is on the report and the PM's professional obligation is to accuracy.

**Mindset Lesson:** A project manager's ethical obligation to accurate reporting is non-negotiable and survives even direct sponsor instruction to misrepresent.

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Stakeholder Performance Domain) and communication management principles. Stakeholder communication must be tailored to information needs and influence. The executive sponsor needs a full closure briefing. Compliance/regulatory officers require a compliance-specific closure communication given their high interest in regulatory conformance. Relationship managers need a transition notice (operational impact, not project performance detail). Vendors need contract closure confirmation. A one-size-fits-all communication to 47 stakeholders fails to meet individual information needs.

**Why A fails:** A single report to all stakeholders ignores different information needs and creates noise for stakeholders who do not need full project performance data.

**Why C fails:** An all-hands closure meeting is logistically impractical for 47 cross-divisional stakeholders and does not serve stakeholders with low project engagement equally.

**Why D fails:** Low-interest stakeholders still require formal closure notification — especially operations staff who will now support the product; omitting them creates post-project support confusion.

**Mindset Lesson:** Communication at closure follows the stakeholder register — tailor the message to what each audience needs to act on, not what is easiest to distribute.

**Q9 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 (Team Performance Domain + Governance) and PMI Code of Ethics — Responsibility value. The PM has discovered a compliance gap at phase closure. The PM does not have the authority to decide unilaterally to proceed or delay — that decision belongs to the steering committee. **Why D fails:** Logging an audit finding as a "next phase risk" without disclosure is deliberate concealment — a Responsibility and Honesty violation.

**Mindset Lesson:** When a compliance gap surfaces at closure, the PM's job is immediate transparent escalation to the appropriate authority — not decision-making beyond the PM's governance remit.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Close Project or Phase is the only Integration Management closing process — it finalizes all work across all domains and is mandatory even for cancelled projects.
- Formal written acceptance from the sponsor or authorized customer is the non-negotiable gate that must occur before administrative closure proceeds.
- Verbal acceptance, product usage, and stakeholder satisfaction are not substitutes for documented sign-off.
- Lessons learned must be documented throughout the project and formally archived in the OPA repository at closure — not discussed and forgotten.
- All procurements must be formally closed before administrative closure is complete.
- Resources are released per the resource management plan, not ad hoc — unplanned release creates contract and morale risk.
- The final project report must be accurate and complete, including failures, overruns, and deferred risks — omitting negative outcomes is a PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty) violation.
- Benefits realization may extend beyond project closure — the PM's closure obligation is to document handover to a named benefits owner, not to measure post-project value.
- In Bangladesh Bank / BSEC-regulated projects, document retention, procurement audit trails, and compliance sign-offs are mandatory closure inputs, not optional enhancements.
- Closure escalation rule: when a compliance gap, unsigned document, or unresolved defect blocks closure, investigate and escalate — do not override, bypass, or defer.

**Reflex burn-in: No formal written acceptance + no archived lessons learned = no closed project — delivery is not closure.**
