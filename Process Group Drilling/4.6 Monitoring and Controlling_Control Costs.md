# PMP Exam Master Brief: Control Costs

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Control Costs is the process of monitoring the status of the project to update the project costs and managing changes to the cost baseline. It lives in the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group under Cost Management, and on the exam it carries significant weight because virtually every scenario-based question touches earned value in some form. The process feeds directly into the Perform Integrated Change Control process — any cost variance that exceeds thresholds must flow through a change request before the baseline is modified. Control Costs is not merely about tracking spend; it is about understanding why variances exist, forecasting final cost outcomes, and taking corrective action early enough to matter. The outputs include work performance information (not raw data), cost forecasts, change requests, and updates to the cost management plan. EVM is the quantitative backbone: without understanding the four EAC formulas and when each applies, 20–30% of cost questions become guesswork. In Agile contexts, velocity and burn-down replace EVM numerically but the same control philosophy — inspect, adapt, forecast, act — applies. Cross-domain bleed is heavy: Control Costs touches Schedule (SPI), Quality (cost of quality), Risk (contingency reserve drawdown), and Procurement (contract cost performance).

**In one sentence:** Control Costs uses Earned Value Management and forecasting to detect cost variances early, trigger the right change control responses, and protect the cost baseline from unauthorized scope creep and gold-plating.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Formal cost baseline (S-curve), EVM thresholds trigger change requests, variance analysis documented in cost forecasts, management reserve accessed only via integrated change control |
| Agile | No formal cost baseline per iteration; team velocity and burn-rate serve as leading indicators; product owner reprioritizes backlog when budget burn exceeds value delivered |
| Hybrid | EVM applied at phase/milestone level; iteration-level burn tracked internally; governance reviews at phase gates decide whether to proceed, re-baseline, or cancel |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. A cost variance does not authorize you to change the baseline — it requires a formal change request through Perform Integrated Change Control. Real-world PMs often quietly adjust the baseline to hide overruns; PMI treats this as an integrity violation.
2. Management reserve is NOT part of the cost baseline and cannot be touched without a scope change; contingency reserve IS in the baseline and is managed by the PM. Real-world PMs treat all reserves as the same bucket.
3. The first response to a negative CPI is root-cause analysis and a corrective action plan — not immediately escalating to the sponsor. Real-world PMs escalate as the first move to protect themselves.
4. EAC must be recalculated when the original estimate is no longer valid; using a single EAC formula without justification is a planning failure, not a legitimate choice. Real-world PMs stick with one formula out of habit.
5. Earned Value is based on the value of work completed against the plan — it is not the same as percent of budget spent. Real-world PMs confuse AC with EV constantly.

**Ethics Angle**

- **Honesty:** Reporting actual costs and variances accurately even when the numbers reflect poorly on the PM. Adjusting cost data to satisfy stakeholder expectations violates honesty norms.
- **Responsibility:** The PM owns the cost forecast. Delegating forecast accuracy entirely to the finance team without owning the numbers is an abdication of PM responsibility.
- **Fairness:** Allocating contingency reserve transparently and consistently across work packages; not diverting reserve to favored workstreams without documented justification.
- **Respect:** Communicating cost performance data to stakeholders in a way that is complete and timely — withholding bad news until it becomes a crisis disrespects stakeholder autonomy.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| PV (Planned Value) | Budgeted cost of work scheduled to be done by a given date | Do not confuse with BAC; PV is a time-sliced portion of the budget |
| EV (Earned Value) | Budgeted cost of work actually completed | EV is NOT what was spent — it is the budget value of what was done |
| AC (Actual Cost) | Actual money spent to complete the work to date | AC says nothing about whether work was completed on schedule |
| CV (Cost Variance) | EV − AC. Negative = over budget | Negative CV is bad; negative SV is also bad — do not flip the sign rule |
| SV (Schedule Variance) | EV − PV. Negative = behind schedule | SV is expressed in dollars, not time — it can mislead if used alone |
| CPI (Cost Performance Index) | EV / AC. <1 = over budget | CPI < 1 means every dollar spent is buying less than a dollar of planned value |
| SPI (Schedule Performance Index) | EV / PV. <1 = behind schedule | SPI crosses into Control Schedule territory; both KPIs appear in cost questions |
| EAC — original estimate valid | BAC / CPI | Use when original estimate was sound but execution has drifted proportionally |
| EAC — original estimate flawed | AC + ETC (new estimate) | Use when the original estimate was fundamentally wrong and must be re-estimated |
| EAC — remaining work at planned rate | AC + (BAC − EV) | Use when you believe future work will proceed at the originally planned rate |
| EAC — remaining work at blended rate | AC + [(BAC − EV) / (CPI × SPI)] | Use when both cost and schedule underperformance will affect remaining work |
| ETC (Estimate to Complete) | EAC − AC | What you still expect to spend from this point forward |
| VAC (Variance at Completion) | BAC − EAC. Negative = projected overrun | VAC is the projected final variance, not the current variance |
| TCPI (To-Complete Performance Index) | (BAC − EV) / (BAC − AC) or (BAC − EV) / (EAC − AC) | TCPI > 1 means remaining work must outperform past efficiency — a warning signal |
| Cost Baseline | Time-phased budget approved by stakeholders, excluding management reserve | Management reserve is separate; do not include it in EVM calculations |
| Management Reserve | Budget held for unknown unknowns; outside cost baseline | Requires scope change to access; PM cannot release it unilaterally |
| Contingency Reserve | Budget for identified risks (known unknowns); inside cost baseline | PM can authorize use; no change request needed within approved thresholds |
| Work Performance Information | Analyzed cost data (CV, CPI, trends) — output of Control Costs | Contrast with Work Performance Data (raw actuals) — input from Direct and Manage Project Work |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| CPI drops below 0.8 mid-project | Perform root-cause analysis on the variance | Diagnosis precedes action; a change request without knowing why the overrun occurred is premature |
| Sponsor asks PM to adjust the cost baseline without a formal change request | Refuse and initiate the Perform Integrated Change Control process | Baselines cannot be changed informally; this is an integrity issue |
| Actual costs exceed contingency reserve | Submit a change request to access management reserve | Management reserve requires scope change authorization — PM cannot self-approve |
| Project is over budget and behind schedule (CPI < 1, SPI < 1) | Calculate EAC using the blended formula (CPI × SPI) and report forecast to stakeholders | The blended EAC is most realistic when both dimensions are underperforming |
| Stakeholder asks "will we finish within budget?" | Provide EAC and VAC based on current CPI trend | EAC is the definitive forecast; VAC quantifies the projected over/underrun |
| Team member reports actual costs are being underreported by another team | Document and escalate per the ethics complaint process | This is a honesty and integrity issue — PMI expects the PM to act, not ignore |
| PM is asked to use remaining contingency for gold-plating a deliverable | Decline; contingency is reserved for identified risks only | Repurposing contingency for unapproved scope changes violates cost control discipline |
| CPI has been stable at 0.9 for 8 of 10 weeks | Use EAC = BAC/CPI as the primary forecast | A stable CPI trend is the strongest indicator that proportional completion is appropriate |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-table**

| Escalation Scenario | Correct PM Action |
|---|---|
| Finance department insists on changing EAC calculation method mid-project without change request | Engage CCB and document the methodology change through integrated change control; do not simply comply |
| SPI is improving but CPI continues to decline — sponsor celebrates the schedule recovery | Correct the sponsor's interpretation; improving SPI does not offset cost overrun; reforecast EAC transparently |

**Mnemonic: PEACE-V**
P — PV (what was planned)
E — EV (what was earned)
A — AC (what was actually spent)

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Formal EVM thresholds (e.g., CV > ±10%) trigger mandatory variance analysis reports
- Cost baseline is locked after planning; changes require CCB approval
- Management reserve release requires documented scope change
- Cost audits may be required for regulated contracts (fixed-price, cost-reimbursable)

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Budget is often fixed at the release level; teams optimize value delivery within the budget envelope
- Burn-down and burn-up charts replace S-curves as the primary cost visualization
- Velocity-based forecasting replaces formal EAC formulas at iteration level
- In hybrid: apply EVM at phase-gate milestones; track iteration burn internally

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- **Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC (Bangladesh):** Regulatory capital projects must maintain audit trails of all cost variances; cost baseline changes require Board/Regulatory approval in many cases; EVM reporting may be contractually mandated by foreign donors or multilateral partners (ADB, World Bank)
- **Construction / Infrastructure:** Cost control is tied to payment milestones; CPI trending is scrutinized by lenders and surety companies
- **IT / Software:** Time-and-materials contracts make AC tracking continuous; scope creep is the primary driver of cost overrun — Control Costs must be paired with rigorous scope control

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Update the cost baseline when CPI drops below 0.85 | Experienced PMs re-baseline to "reset expectations" and avoid showing overrun; feels pragmatic | Baselines can only change via formal change control; unilateral re-baselining conceals variance and violates honesty |
| Use management reserve to cover cost overruns without a change request | PMs know reserves exist for exactly this kind of situation and want to avoid sponsor conversations | Management reserve requires scope change authorization; accessing it without CCB approval bypasses governance |
| Report EAC using the most optimistic formula to keep stakeholders calm | PMs want to maintain stakeholder confidence and avoid panic during a rough patch | PMI requires EAC to reflect realistic conditions; optimistic reporting is a form of dishonest communication |
| Escalate immediately to the sponsor when CPI < 1 | Escalating upward feels responsible and transparent; sponsors should know about problems | The PM should first analyze root cause and develop a corrective action plan before escalating — escalating without a plan adds burden without value |
| Calculate EAC as AC + (BAC − EV) in all situations | This formula is clean and easy to remember; many PMs use it as a default | This formula assumes future work will proceed at the planned rate — only valid when that assumption holds; using it blindly ignores CPI trends |
| Treat SV improvement as evidence that cost problems are resolving | If the schedule is recovering, the project seems to be back on track | SV and CV are independent; schedule recovery often requires acceleration spending that worsens CPI |
| Ask the team to work overtime to recover cost variance | Overtime has fixed people costs — adding hours seems like it would improve CPI | Overtime increases AC without proportionally increasing EV unless productivity is maintained; it often worsens CPI |
| Cancel contingency reserve once the risk it covered did not materialize | The risk has passed so the reserve is no longer needed — it seems fiscally responsible to return it | Unused contingency reserve remains available until project close; returning it prematurely removes a buffer for residual or emergent risks |

### 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "CPI is 0.78 and trending downward" | Calculate EAC = BAC/CPI; issue variance analysis; prepare change request if baseline breach is projected |
| "The cost baseline needs to be updated" | Only via formal change request and CCB approval — never informally |
| "Management reserve is needed" | Requires scope change and CCB authorization — PM cannot self-approve |
| "The team is ahead of schedule but over budget" | Positive SPI does not offset negative CPI; cost problem must be addressed independently |
| "Actual costs are lower than planned" | Favorable variance — but investigate: scope may have been skipped, not truly saved |
| "Stakeholder asks for a cost forecast" | Provide EAC and VAC using current CPI trend |
| "TCPI > 1.1" | Project is in serious trouble — future work must significantly outperform past performance |
| "Variance is within the control threshold" | Document, monitor, no change request required — thresholds exist for a reason |
| "The original estimate was fundamentally flawed" | Use EAC = AC + new ETC (bottom-up re-estimate) |
| "Cost variance is caused by scope creep" | Issue change request; scope change is the root cause, not a cost management failure alone |
| "Finance is asking PM to reallocate contingency" | Contingency is for identified risks; reallocation for non-risk purposes requires a change request |
| "Project is 50% complete but has spent 70% of the budget" | CPI = EV/AC ≈ 0.71; calculate EAC and report projected overrun to stakeholders immediately |
| "Sponsor wants to know if we'll be over budget at completion" | Calculate VAC = BAC − EAC; if negative, report the projected overrun with supporting analysis |
| "EVM data shows SV is positive but CPI is still negative" | Schedule has recovered but cost problem persists — do not conflate the two |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager on a large infrastructure project discovers during a routine cost audit that the project accountant has been systematically underreporting actual costs over the past three months to maintain a favorable CPI. The project is now 65% complete. The true CPI, when corrected, is 0.74. The accountant explains that the sponsor threatened to cancel the project if the CPI dropped below 0.80. The PM has a scheduled status meeting with the sponsor in two days. The project management plan includes a threshold that requires variance analysis and corrective action when CPI drops below 0.85. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Report the corrected cost data to the sponsor at the status meeting and present a revised EAC
B. Confront the accountant and issue a formal warning before taking any other action
C. Report the cost misrepresentation to the PM's management and the relevant oversight body, then correct the cost records
D. Quietly correct the cost records and implement corrective actions before the sponsor meeting so as not to trigger the cancellation threat

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A Scrum team is midway through a six-sprint release. The product owner reports that the team has completed 60% of the planned story points but has consumed 75% of the release budget. The Scrum master suggests the team simply increase velocity in the next sprint. The development team says the remaining backlog items are more complex than the completed ones. The product owner wants to proceed without any backlog reprioritization. The release sponsor asks for a forecast of whether the release will complete within budget. What should the PM/Scrum master recommend FIRST?

A. Agree with the Scrum master that increased velocity in the next sprint will resolve the budget gap
B. Facilitate a backlog refinement session to re-estimate remaining items and recalculate the burn-rate forecast
C. Recommend the sponsor approve additional budget before the next sprint
D. Proceed as planned since the team is still within the sprint and variances should be assessed only at sprint review

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project uses EVM at the phase level and Agile iterations internally. At the phase-2 gate review, the cumulative CPI is 0.82 and the SPI is 1.05. The phase-2 budget was $500,000; actual spend is $420,000 with EV of $344,400. The governance board is deciding whether to proceed to phase 3. The PM prepared an EAC using the formula AC + (BAC − EV) and concluded the project would finish $18,000 over the phase-2 budget. A board member challenges the EAC formula choice, arguing that the blended CPI × SPI formula should be used instead. What should the PM do?

A. Defend the original EAC formula since the PM is the cost authority on the project
B. Recalculate EAC using the blended formula and present both EACs with the rationale for each formula's assumptions
C. Accept the board member's formula and revise the EAC without comment
D. Defer the EAC discussion to the next phase planning meeting to avoid delaying the gate decision

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is reviewing weekly cost performance data. The CPI is 0.88, which has been stable for the past six weeks. The project is 40% complete. The BAC is $2,000,000. The cost management plan specifies that a variance analysis memo is required when CPI falls below 0.90, and a formal change request is required when CPI falls below 0.85. The PM has not yet submitted a variance analysis memo. What are the PM's BEST next two actions? (This question has four options; select the single best answer.)

A. Submit the variance analysis memo only — the CPI has not crossed the change request threshold
B. Submit the variance analysis memo AND initiate a change request, because the trend suggests the threshold will be breached
C. Recalculate the CPI using a different formula to see if the threshold is actually crossed
D. Escalate directly to the sponsor since the variance is ongoing and may indicate a systemic issue

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A release team using Kanban realizes at the weekly flow review that the cost of the last three features delivered was 40% above estimate, the cycle time has increased significantly, and the team is consuming the release contingency faster than planned. The product owner wants to understand what the final release cost will be. Place the following actions in the correct order: (i) Facilitate a retrospective to identify root causes of cost increase; (ii) Recalculate the release burn-rate forecast using updated actuals; (iii) Present the updated forecast to the product owner; (iv) Identify and prioritize backlog items with the highest value-to-cost ratio.

A. (ii) → (i) → (iv) → (iii)
B. (i) → (iv) → (ii) → (iii)
C. (i) → (ii) → (iv) → (iii)
D. (ii) → (iii) → (i) → (iv)

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT a valid input to the Control Costs process?

A. Work performance data
B. Project funding requirements
C. Cost management plan
D. Approved change requests

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A project manager discovers that the EAC submitted to the client last month overstated the remaining budget by 15% because a team lead used an incorrect formula. The client made a procurement decision based on that forecast. The project management plan was not followed in preparing the forecast. Correcting the EAC will show the project is projected to exceed the client's budget ceiling. The sponsor says, "The procurement is already done — there is no benefit to correcting the forecast now." What should the PM do?

A. Agree with the sponsor; since the procurement is complete, correcting the forecast serves no practical purpose
B. Correct the EAC, disclose the error to the client, and present a revised cost forecast with corrective options
C. Flag the issue internally in the lessons learned register but maintain the current forecast for client reporting
D. Rerun the formula using assumptions that produce a result closer to the original forecast to minimize disruption

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project has just completed a phase gate. The EVM data shows CPI = 0.91 and SPI = 0.96. The executive steering committee receives a summary dashboard. The development team receives sprint burn-down charts. The project sponsor receives the formal variance analysis memo. A new steering committee member notices the dashboard does not include EAC or VAC figures and asks why. The PM explains that the dashboard was tailored to avoid overwhelming executives with financial details. The steering committee member insists on seeing EAC and VAC. What should the PM do?

A. Explain that the dashboard format was approved in the communications management plan and cannot be changed mid-project
B. Add EAC and VAC to the dashboard and update the communications management plan through change control
C. Provide EAC and VAC verbally at this meeting but do not change the dashboard format
D. Escalate the request to the project sponsor to decide whether the dashboard should be changed

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM on a hybrid capital markets technology project has been managing a CPI of 0.87 for the past four months. The cost management plan requires a corrective action plan when CPI drops below 0.90 and a CCB review when CPI drops below 0.85. The PM submitted a corrective action plan in month two but has not yet triggered a CCB review. In month five, the CPI drops to 0.84. The project is 70% complete. The sponsor is aware of the corrective action plan but has not been formally notified of the CPI threshold breach. What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Update the corrective action plan and continue monitoring since the project is 70% complete and near done
B. Immediately convene the CCB and notify the sponsor of the threshold breach, presenting the updated EAC and corrective options
C. Escalate directly to the portfolio steering committee since the sponsor has already been aware of the trend
D. Re-baseline the project to a CPI of 0.84 to reflect current performance and reset the threshold clock

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

A project manager is performing Control Costs on a large predictive project. The CPI has been 0.83 for the past eight weeks. The BAC is $3,000,000. AC to date is $1,200,000. EV to date is $996,000. Which THREE of the following actions are appropriate at this stage?

A. Calculate EAC = BAC / CPI = $3,000,000 / 0.83 ≈ $3,614,458 as the primary forecast
B. Submit a change request to access management reserve without CCB approval since the situation is urgent
C. Perform a root-cause analysis to identify why cost performance is consistently below plan
D. Calculate TCPI = (BAC − EV) / (BAC − AC) to understand the efficiency required to complete within budget
E. Re-baseline the project budget to $3,614,458 without a formal change request to align budget with forecast
F. Document the variance analysis and forecast in work performance information for distribution

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility. The PM has an obligation to report accurate cost data regardless of political consequences. PMBOK 7 Stewardship principle: PMs are stewards of the project and the organization's resources, which includes ensuring financial reporting integrity. Correcting records and reporting the misrepresentation to management and oversight protects all stakeholders. Option A is incomplete — it corrects the symptom but does not address the ethics violation. Option B addresses the interpersonal issue but not the broader accountability obligation. Option D is a cover-up and violates both Honesty and Responsibility.
**Mindset Lesson:** When cost data is falsified, the PM's first obligation is integrity, not project survival.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — inspect and adapt principle. Before forecasting or requesting more budget, the team must understand the actual remaining work and its complexity. Backlog refinement with re-estimation produces the data needed for an accurate burn-rate forecast. Option A ignores the complexity data provided by the team. Option C seeks more budget before understanding the problem. Option D defers needed action irresponsibly.
**Mindset Lesson:** In Agile cost control, re-estimate before you escalate or commit to additional resources.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — Models, Methods, and Artifacts; Cost forecasting. When a formula assumption is challenged, the PM must be transparent and analytical. Presenting both EACs with their underlying assumptions demonstrates competence and honesty, and enables the board to make an informed gate decision. Option A is defensive and closes off legitimate governance input. Option C capitulates without analysis. Option D avoids a critical governance conversation at the wrong time.
**Mindset Lesson:** When formula assumptions are questioned, present multiple scenarios with explicit rationale — governance bodies deserve the full picture.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** Cost management plan defines thresholds for a reason. CPI = 0.88 crosses the variance analysis memo threshold (< 0.90) but NOT the change request threshold (< 0.85). Following the cost management plan precisely is the correct action. Option B triggers a change request prematurely — the plan does not authorize one at this CPI level. Option C manipulates data. Option D bypasses established escalation protocols.
**Mindset Lesson:** Always act according to the thresholds defined in the cost management plan — no more, no less.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — retrospectives (root-cause analysis) drive improvement before forecasting. Understanding why costs increased (i) allows the team to prioritize high-value items (iv), then reforecast accurately (ii), then communicate to the product owner (iii). Option A forecasts before understanding root cause. Option C is close but skips value-based reprioritization before forecasting. Option D reports before the team has acted.
**Mindset Lesson:** In Agile cost control, retrospect before you forecast and forecast before you report.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** D
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7 — Approved change requests are an input to Direct and Manage Project Work and Monitor and Control Project Work, not specifically to Control Costs. The inputs to Control Costs include: project management plan (cost management plan, cost baseline, performance measurement baseline), project documents (lessons learned register, project funding requirements), work performance data, and organizational process assets. Approved change requests are outputs of the Perform Integrated Change Control process and flow into Direct and Manage Project Work. All of A, B, and C are legitimate inputs.
**Mindset Lesson:** Know the precise ITTOs — approved change requests are inputs to execution, not monitoring and controlling of costs.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty. The obligation to provide accurate information to stakeholders does not expire once a decision has been made based on that information. The client deserves the corrected forecast and the opportunity to respond. PMBOK 7 Stewardship: protecting stakeholders from decisions made on false data is part of the PM's stewardship role. Option A rationalizes concealment. Option C records the issue without correcting the harm to the client. Option D is data manipulation.
**Mindset Lesson:** Disclosure of reporting errors is mandatory regardless of the inconvenience — the PM cannot withhold corrections that affect stakeholder decisions.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Communications Management Plan — tailoring is appropriate but must be responsive to legitimate stakeholder needs. A steering committee member requesting EAC and VAC has a valid information need. The correct response is to update the dashboard and formally amend the communications management plan through the change control process. Option A hides behind process to avoid addressing a legitimate need. Option C addresses the immediate request but leaves the communications plan misaligned. Option D escalates a routine communications update unnecessarily.
**Mindset Lesson:** Stakeholder information needs override pre-approved dashboard formats — update the plan through proper change control.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Cost management plan — the CCB review threshold has been crossed (CPI < 0.85). The PM must convene the CCB immediately and formally notify the sponsor. Waiting because the project is near completion is not a valid exception to the threshold protocol. Option A ignores a mandatory governance trigger. Option C bypasses the sponsor and the CCB in favor of the portfolio level — this is an escalation order violation. Option D is unilateral re-baselining without authorization.
**Mindset Lesson:** Once a documented threshold is crossed, the PM must trigger the defined governance response — proximity to completion does not create exemptions.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, F
**PMI Justification:** (A) EAC = BAC/CPI is the correct formula when CPI has been stable — a consistent 0.83 over eight weeks is a strong trend. (C) Root-cause analysis is required to understand the variance and develop corrective actions — this is a core Control Costs activity. (F) Work performance information (analyzed variances, forecasts, trends) is the primary output of Control Costs and must be documented and distributed. (B) is wrong — management reserve requires CCB authorization, and urgency does not waive governance. (D) TCPI calculation is valid analytically but computing it is secondary to the three primary obligations at this stage. (E) Re-baselining without a change request is an integrity and governance violation.
**Mindset Lesson:** In cost control, the three non-negotiables are: forecast accurately, investigate root causes, and document performance information — governance shortcuts are never acceptable.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Control Costs monitors actuals vs. the baseline and uses EVM to quantify variances and forecast completion
- EV is the budget value of work done — never confuse it with AC (money spent) or PV (money planned)
- CV = EV − AC; CPI = EV/AC — negative/below 1.0 means over budget
- Four EAC formulas exist; choose based on whether the original estimate is still valid and whether CPI/SPI trends apply
- EAC = BAC/CPI when CPI is stable; EAC = AC + new ETC when original estimate is flawed
- TCPI > 1.0 means remaining work must beat past performance — a red flag requiring action
- Contingency reserve is inside the baseline; management reserve is outside and requires scope change to access
- Cost baseline changes require formal change requests through Perform Integrated Change Control — no exceptions
- Root-cause analysis precedes corrective action; corrective action precedes escalation
- In Agile: burn-rate and velocity replace EVM numerically but the same inspect-adapt-forecast logic applies
- **When CPI is bad, the PMI reflex is: analyze root cause → forecast EAC → submit change request if needed → report honestly, every time.**
