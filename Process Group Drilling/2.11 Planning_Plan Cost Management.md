# PMP Exam Master Brief: Plan Cost Management

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Plan Cost Management is the process of defining how project costs will be estimated, budgeted, managed, monitored, and controlled throughout the project lifecycle. It produces the Cost Management Plan, a component of the overall Project Management Plan that sets the rules of engagement for every cost-related decision on the project. This process sits entirely in the Planning Process Group under the Cost Management Knowledge Area, yet its outputs ripple into Estimate Costs, Determine Budget, Control Costs, and indirectly into risk and procurement planning. On the PMP exam, Cost Management as a domain carries roughly 8–10% of questions, but cost-related judgment appears embedded in Integration, Risk, and Stakeholder scenarios, making cross-domain bleed significant. The process is deceptively simple in its inputs and outputs — its power lies in the decisions it forces the team to make before a single estimate is produced: unit of measure, precision level, variance thresholds, performance measurement baseline rules, and funding limit reconciliation approaches. Practitioners routinely skip this process and jump straight to estimating, which is the exact anti-pattern PMI tests. Expert judgment and data analysis (alternatives analysis) are the primary tools, reflecting that governance decisions, not math, are the substance here. In one sentence: Plan Cost Management establishes the governance framework — thresholds, methodologies, and rules — that every subsequent cost process must follow.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | A formal Cost Management Plan is produced early in planning, establishing WBS-linked estimating methods, control accounts, variance thresholds, and EVM rules before any estimates begin. |
| Agile | Cost governance is lighter; teams use burn rate, velocity-based forecasting, and rolling-wave budgets. The "plan" may be a team agreement on how cost will be tracked each iteration rather than a formal document. |
| Hybrid | Predictive governance structures (thresholds, reporting formats) are established upfront, but estimating methods and reserve usage rules flex sprint-by-sprint as scope is elaborated. |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. The Cost Management Plan must be created before estimation begins — real-world PMs routinely estimate first and document governance after, but PMI holds that undocumented rules produce inconsistent estimates and unmanageable variances.
2. Units of measure and precision levels are governance decisions, not administrative formalities — experienced PMs treat rounding rules as trivial, but PMI tests them because inconsistency cascades into flawed EVM calculations.
3. Variance and change thresholds must be pre-approved, not set reactively — practitioners often wait until the first overrun to decide what triggers a change request, but PMI requires thresholds established in the plan so stakeholders agree before pressure exists.
4. The choice of performance measurement methodology (EVM vs. milestone vs. percent complete) is a planning decision, not a monitoring one — experienced PMs pick measurement rules mid-project based on convenience, which PMI marks as a planning failure.
5. Funding limit reconciliation is a planning output concern, not a schedule concern — practitioners defer reconciliation to finance teams, but PMI requires the PM to plan how cost expenditures align with fund availability before work begins.

**Ethics Angle**

- Responsibility: Committing to a cost governance framework before estimates are produced removes post-hoc rationalization; the PM is accountable to documented rules.
- Honesty: Setting realistic variance thresholds, rather than politically comfortable ones, requires disclosing true uncertainty to sponsors — a direct application of PMI's honesty value.
- Fairness: Establishing uniform estimating rules across all work packages ensures no team or vendor receives preferential measurement treatment.
- Respect: Involving relevant stakeholders in defining cost reporting formats and thresholds honors their legitimate interest in how performance will be communicated to them.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Cost Management Plan | Component of the Project Management Plan defining how costs are estimated, budgeted, managed, monitored, and controlled. | It is a subsidiary plan, not a standalone document; it cannot conflict with the overall Project Management Plan. |
| Units of Measure | Agreed units for each resource type (staff hours, days, meters, lumps sums). | Changing units mid-project without a change request invalidates comparability of EVM data. |
| Level of Precision | The degree to which cost estimates are rounded (e.g., to nearest $100). | Precision is not accuracy; a precise estimate can still be far off target value. |
| Level of Accuracy | The acceptable range of cost estimates (e.g., ±10%). | Accuracy range is set in planning — it is not adjusted because an estimate came in outside it. |
| Control Thresholds | Pre-approved variance levels (SV, CV, CPI) that trigger corrective action or change requests. | Thresholds are set in the plan, not when a variance is first observed. |
| EVM — Earned Value (EV) | EV = % Complete × BAC | Confusing EV with AC (what was actually spent) is the single most common EVM error. |
| EAC (Estimate at Completion) | EAC = BAC / CPI (typical); or EAC = AC + ETC; or EAC = AC + (BAC − EV) | PMI uses multiple formulas; the stem will signal which: "future work will proceed at planned rate" = AC + (BAC − EV). |
| ETC (Estimate to Complete) | ETC = EAC − AC | ETC is forward-looking remaining cost, not the remaining budget (BAC − EV). |
| VAC (Variance at Completion) | VAC = BAC − EAC | Negative VAC means over budget at completion; do not confuse sign convention with CV. |
| TCPI (To-Complete Performance Index) | TCPI = (BAC − EV) / (BAC − AC) for baseline; or (BAC − EV) / (EAC − AC) for revised | TCPI > 1.0 means the remaining work must be done more efficiently than planned — a bad sign, not a target to celebrate. |
| Analogous Estimating | Uses historical data from similar projects; top-down, fast, less accurate. | Appropriate early in planning or when detail is unavailable — not as a substitute for bottom-up when WBS exists. |
| Parametric Estimating | Statistical relationship between historical data and project variables (cost per unit × quantity). | Only valid when the historical model is statistically reliable and scalable to the current project. |
| Bottom-Up Estimating | Estimating individual work packages and aggregating upward; most accurate, most time-intensive. | Requires a defined WBS; do not apply before scope is sufficiently decomposed. |
| Three-Point Estimating (PERT) | E = (O + 4M + P) / 6; Std Dev = (P − O) / 6 | Triangular distribution: E = (O + M + P) / 3 — distinguish which formula the stem requires. |
| Funding Limit Reconciliation | Aligning planned expenditures against authorized funding tranches; may require schedule adjustments. | This is a planning activity (Determine Budget output), but the rules for it are set in Plan Cost Management. |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Project has just been authorized; cost estimating is about to begin" | Develop the Cost Management Plan before generating any estimates | Governance rules must precede estimating; estimates without a plan lack traceability and comparability. |
| "Sponsor asks for a cost estimate at project initiation, Cost Management Plan not yet developed" | Provide a rough order of magnitude range and inform the sponsor a formal plan will set precision rules | A ROM is appropriate before the plan; promising precision that the plan has not yet authorized misrepresents accuracy. |
| "Team members are using different units of measure for labor costs" | Refer to and enforce the Cost Management Plan's units-of-measure standard | The plan is the authority; ad hoc unit changes introduce EVM data inconsistency. |
| "Change request submitted that would affect cost variance thresholds" | Evaluate the change through the integrated change control process | Threshold changes are a plan amendment; unilateral adjustments by the PM violate governance. |
| "Stakeholder demands weekly cost reports; Cost Management Plan specifies monthly" | Follow the Cost Management Plan reporting cadence and seek a change request if the stakeholder's need is legitimate | The plan governs; changing reporting frequency outside of change control undermines governance integrity. |
| "New PM joining project asks how cost performance will be measured" | Direct them to the Cost Management Plan | The plan documents measurement methodology; verbal briefings introduce inconsistency. |
| "Lessons learned from a previous project suggest the current estimating method is flawed" | Evaluate whether a change request to the Cost Management Plan is warranted | Lessons learned inform plan updates via change control, not informal mid-project method switches. |
| "Funding tranche is smaller than the planned expenditure in Q2" | Address through funding limit reconciliation, likely adjusting the schedule baseline | Expenditure-to-funding alignment is managed in the plan; work cannot proceed beyond authorized funding without a change. |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Scenarios**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Finance department insists on a different cost tracking methodology than what is in the Cost Management Plan" | Escalate through integrated change control; do not unilaterally adopt the finance method | The Cost Management Plan is an approved subsidiary plan; overriding it informally compromises governance and audit trails. |
| "Sponsor verbally approves a change to variance thresholds during a status meeting" | Document the verbal approval and process a formal change request before updating the plan | Verbal approvals are not plan changes; only approved change requests modify the Cost Management Plan. |

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- Formal Cost Management Plan produced as a standalone document with detailed governance sections.
- EVM rules, control account definitions, and variance thresholds are specified to contract or organizational standards.
- Funding limit reconciliation rules are explicit, especially in multi-year or phased projects.
- Level of accuracy is defined per phase gate (rougher at initiation, tighter at baseline).

**Agile/Hybrid**
- Cost governance may be embedded in a team working agreement or iteration planning norms rather than a formal document.
- Burn rate and velocity-based forecasting replace traditional EVM in pure agile contexts.
- Rolling-wave approach means estimating rules are set per release or epic rather than for the entire project at once.
- Hybrid: predictive thresholds apply to the overall program budget; agile teams operate within fixed sprint budgets (cost as a constraint, scope as the variable).

**Industry/Regulatory Factors**
- Banking/Capital Markets/BSEC context: regulatory reporting requirements (e.g., BSEC or central bank audit standards) may mandate specific cost tracking formats, variance documentation intervals, and independent cost verification — these constraints must be captured in the Cost Management Plan before estimation begins.
- Government/Defense: earned value management systems (EVMS) must comply with ANSI/EIA-748; the Cost Management Plan must reference the applicable standard.
- Construction/Infrastructure: unit cost databases and quantity surveyor norms may dictate parametric model selection in the plan.
- Healthcare/Pharma: regulatory submission costs require separate cost tracking structures that must be planned upfront, not added retroactively.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Begin estimating costs immediately after project charter approval" | Experienced PMs feel schedule pressure and want to show progress; estimation feels like productive cost planning. | Without the Cost Management Plan, estimates lack agreed precision, units, and methodology — they cannot be formally baselined. |
| "Set variance thresholds after the first variance is observed" | Real-world PMs calibrate thresholds to actual project volatility; setting them before any data feels arbitrary. | PMI requires pre-agreed thresholds so stakeholders commit before pressure exists; reactive thresholds enable political manipulation. |
| "Use the same estimating approach as the last similar project without documenting the choice" | Experienced PMs rely on organizational memory and precedent; documentation feels bureaucratic. | The Cost Management Plan must document the chosen methodology so all team members and auditors understand the basis of estimates. |
| "Allow the finance department to define cost tracking methodology unilaterally" | Finance owns the ledger; PMs often defer to finance to avoid conflict and leverage their systems. | The PM owns the Cost Management Plan; finance inputs are incorporated through stakeholder engagement and integration, not ceded authority. |
| "Update variance thresholds verbally with the sponsor during a status meeting" | Status meetings are where sponsors make decisions; capturing an immediate verbal agreement feels efficient. | Plan updates require formal change requests through integrated change control; verbal agreements are not plan amendments. |
| "Skip Plan Cost Management for a small, fast-moving project" | Small projects seem to warrant less overhead; experienced PMs apply proportionality and cut documentation for speed. | Tailoring reduces formality, not the process itself; even minimal cost governance (a brief team agreement) must exist. The exam does not reward skipping processes. |
| "Treat the Cost Management Plan as a finance team deliverable, not a PM deliverable" | Finance teams often have cost tracking systems and experience; PM involvement feels redundant. | The Cost Management Plan is a PM deliverable within the Project Management Plan; the PM is accountable for its content regardless of who provides input. |

### 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "before any cost estimates are developed" | The action required is completing or referencing the Cost Management Plan. |
| "team is using inconsistent estimating approaches" | Root cause is absence or non-adherence to the Cost Management Plan; solution is enforce or update the plan. |
| "sponsor asks what triggers a change request for cost" | Answer: the variance threshold defined in the Cost Management Plan. |
| "how will cost performance be measured" | Answer: per the performance measurement methodology in the Cost Management Plan. |
| "what unit of measure should be used for labor" | Answer: the unit specified in the Cost Management Plan. |
| "funding is not available in the planned period" | Answer: funding limit reconciliation, likely requiring a schedule change, per Cost Management Plan rules. |
| "project has no documented cost governance" | Answer: develop or update the Cost Management Plan before proceeding with cost work. |
| "variance threshold was informally changed" | Answer: process a formal change request; informal changes to the plan are not valid. |
| "first step in cost management for a new project" | Answer: Plan Cost Management — always the first process, before estimating or budgeting. |
| "how accurate must estimates be at this stage" | Answer: defined by the level of accuracy in the Cost Management Plan for that phase or stage. |
| "finance wants weekly cost reports; plan says monthly" | Answer: follow the plan; address the discrepancy through change control if justified. |
| "agile team asks how to handle cost tracking" | Answer: establish cost governance rules (burn rate, budget per sprint) in a team agreement or lightweight Cost Management Plan equivalent. |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are a PM for a regulatory compliance system upgrade at a mid-sized bank. The project sponsor, who controls your performance review, insists that variance thresholds be set at ±20% to avoid triggering change requests that would require board-level approval. Your risk register shows that similar projects at this institution have experienced cost variances of ±35%. The organizational governance policy requires board approval for cost changes exceeding 15%. You have not yet finalized the Cost Management Plan. What should you do?

A. Accept the sponsor's ±20% threshold to maintain the relationship and avoid delays to plan finalization.
B. Set the threshold at ±15% to align with organizational governance policy, document the sponsor's preference in the assumption log, and seek sponsor sign-off.
C. Set the threshold at ±20% as requested but include a footnote that historical variance suggests this may be insufficient.
D. Escalate immediately to the board before the Cost Management Plan is approved.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

Your agile team is three sprints into a digital transformation project for a financial services firm. The product owner reports that the team has no shared understanding of how cost will be tracked — some members track actual hours, others track story points, and one uses a blended approach. Sprint reviews have produced inconsistent cost reporting to stakeholders. No formal Cost Management Plan exists. What is the BEST first action?

A. Standardize on story-point-based burn rate tracking immediately and update the sprint backlog.
B. Facilitate a team session to define lightweight cost governance rules — tracking unit, reporting cadence, and variance signal — and document them as a team agreement.
C. Escalate to the PMO to assign a cost controller to the team.
D. Continue current practice until the end of the release, then establish governance for the next release.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid infrastructure modernization project. The predictive portion has an approved Cost Management Plan specifying parametric estimating and monthly reporting. The agile workstreams are about to begin their first sprint. The agile team lead argues that the Cost Management Plan does not apply to sprint work and proposes tracking costs using velocity alone. The project sponsor expects a unified cost report covering both workstreams. What should you FIRST do?

A. Allow the agile team to use velocity tracking; prepare two separate cost reports and reconcile them manually for the sponsor.
B. Amend the Cost Management Plan through integrated change control to address agile workstream cost governance before sprint work begins.
C. Override the agile team lead and require parametric estimating for sprint work to maintain plan consistency.
D. Defer the governance question until after the first sprint retrospective to gather data before deciding.

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A new project manager has taken over a construction project mid-planning. The Cost Management Plan is partially complete — units of measure and precision levels are defined, but control thresholds, EVM rules, and reporting formats are blank. Estimating is scheduled to begin next week. Which TWO actions are most important before estimating begins?

A. Complete the control thresholds section of the Cost Management Plan.
B. Begin estimating immediately to avoid schedule delays and complete the plan in parallel.
C. Define the performance measurement methodology (EVM rules) in the Cost Management Plan.
D. Conduct a risk assessment to determine whether estimating can proceed without a complete plan.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile team is setting up cost governance for a new product development project. Place the following activities in the correct order: (1) Define burn rate tracking unit, (2) Establish sprint budget cap, (3) Agree on variance signal that triggers stakeholder notification, (4) Identify funding tranches available per quarter.

A. 4, 1, 2, 3
B. 1, 2, 3, 4
C. 4, 2, 1, 3
D. 1, 4, 2, 3

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

All of the following are legitimate inputs to Plan Cost Management EXCEPT:

A. Project Charter
B. Organizational Process Assets
C. Cost Baseline
D. Enterprise Environmental Factors

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are finalizing the Cost Management Plan for a large government contract. Your supervisor instructs you to set the level of accuracy at ±5% even though your analysis of available estimating data indicates ±15% is the honest representation of uncertainty at this stage. The supervisor argues that a tighter accuracy range will make the plan more competitive and increase the chance of contract renewal. What do you do?

A. Set the accuracy at ±5% as instructed; the supervisor bears responsibility for the decision.
B. Document ±15% as the technically justified accuracy level, explain the basis to your supervisor, and if overruled, escalate per your organization's ethics policy.
C. Compromise at ±10% to balance honesty with the supervisor's concern.
D. Set ±5% in the plan but add a risk register entry noting the true uncertainty range.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project serves both a predictive program management office (PMO) requiring monthly earned value reports and agile product teams expecting sprint-level burn rate dashboards. The Cost Management Plan currently only addresses predictive reporting. Stakeholders in both groups are dissatisfied with cost visibility. What is the BEST communication strategy to incorporate into the Cost Management Plan?

A. Define two separate reporting formats — monthly EVM for the PMO and sprint burn rate for agile teams — with a reconciliation mechanism to produce a unified cost view.
B. Require all teams to adopt EVM reporting to standardize across the project.
C. Allow each team to self-select their reporting format since they are autonomous.
D. Report only at the program level to the PMO and leave agile teams to manage their own internal cost visibility.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are the PM of a hybrid regulatory project at a securities firm. Midway through planning, the compliance department requests that cost variance thresholds be reduced from ±15% (already in the approved Cost Management Plan) to ±5% to comply with a new BSEC regulatory directive. The change would significantly increase the frequency of board-level change requests. The project sponsor prefers to keep the original thresholds to avoid governance overhead. What is the correct course of action?

A. Side with the sponsor; regulatory directives are compliance's problem, not the project's.
B. Evaluate the regulatory directive's applicability to the project, then initiate a change request to update the Cost Management Plan if the directive legally applies, escalating to the sponsor and compliance leadership jointly.
C. Update the Cost Management Plan immediately to ±5% per the regulatory directive without a change request, since regulatory compliance supersedes the plan.
D. Leave the Cost Management Plan unchanged and address compliance through a separate compliance register.

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility. The PM must set thresholds that reflect genuine governance needs, not political convenience. Organizational governance policy (±15%) is the binding constraint; the Cost Management Plan cannot be designed to circumvent it. Option A sacrifices honesty for relationship management — a direct ethics violation. Option C documents disagreement but still approves a non-compliant plan. Option D bypasses the sponsor prematurely; the correct path is to set the compliant threshold and document the sponsor's position, then seek sign-off.
**Mindset Lesson:** When governance thresholds are politically contested, the PM's obligation is to the documented organizational policy, not to stakeholder preference.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — cost governance in agile contexts is documented as team agreements, not skipped. Option A standardizes one element but skips the full governance structure needed. Option C over-escalates a team-solvable problem. Option D defers governance, which is the anti-pattern PMI tests most heavily in agile cost questions.
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile contexts, cost governance lives in team agreements; lightweight does not mean absent.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Integration / Plan Cost Management. The Cost Management Plan is a living document; hybrid projects require it to address both workstreams. Proceeding without updating the plan (Option A or D) creates uncontrolled governance gaps. Option C imposes an inappropriate method on agile work without going through change control.
**Mindset Lesson:** Governance gaps at the boundary of predictive and agile workstreams must be resolved through change control, not workarounds.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** A and C
**PMI Justification:** Control thresholds and EVM rules are foundational to the Cost Management Plan's ability to govern estimating and subsequent cost control. Estimating without these defined (Option B) produces estimates that cannot be properly evaluated against performance criteria. Option D adds analysis overhead without resolving the core gap.
**Mindset Lesson:** Estimating without complete cost governance produces data that cannot be meaningfully controlled.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** A — 4, 1, 2, 3
**PMI Justification:** Funding availability is the external constraint that shapes all subsequent cost decisions. Once funding tranches are known (4), the tracking unit is selected to align with funding granularity (1), sprint budgets are set within those constraints (2), and variance signals are calibrated to the established budget (3).
**Mindset Lesson:** Cost governance in agile always starts with funding constraints, not internal team preferences.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** The Cost Baseline is an output of the Determine Budget process, not an input to Plan Cost Management. It does not yet exist when Plan Cost Management is being performed. The Project Charter (A), OPAs (B), and EEFs (D) are all valid inputs per PMBOK 6/7.
**Mindset Lesson:** The Cost Baseline comes after Plan Cost Management — it cannot be an input to the process that sets the rules for its own creation.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty (mandatory standard): "We do not engage in or condone behavior that is designed to deceive others." Setting a technically unjustified accuracy level to appear competitive is deception. Option A violates personal accountability. Option C is a compromise of an integrity issue — honesty is not negotiable. Option D documents the truth privately while presenting falsehood publicly, which is a more sophisticated but equally clear ethics violation.
**Mindset Lesson:** Accuracy levels in the Cost Management Plan must reflect genuine estimating capability; misrepresenting uncertainty to win favor is an ethics violation regardless of who directs it.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** A
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 / Tailoring. The Cost Management Plan must address the communication needs of all key stakeholders; a hybrid project's plan must accommodate both predictive and agile reporting formats with reconciliation. Option B imposes an inappropriate method. Option C abandons governance. Option D denies agile teams the cost visibility they need to self-manage.
**Mindset Lesson:** Hybrid project cost governance must explicitly bridge both reporting paradigms with a reconciliation mechanism.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Integration / Plan Cost Management / Code of Ethics — Responsibility. Regulatory directives that legally apply to the project must be incorporated through proper change control, not ignored or unilaterally implemented. Joint escalation respects both the sponsor's authority and the compliance obligation. Option A ignores a legal constraint. Option C bypasses change control — even regulatory changes require a change request. Option D creates a parallel compliance record that contradicts the approved plan.
**Mindset Lesson:** Regulatory constraints override sponsor preferences but must still flow through change control to maintain plan integrity.

**Q10**
**Correct Answer:** B, C, E
**PMI Justification:** Units of measure (B), control thresholds (C), and performance measurement methodology rules (E) are all direct decisions/outputs of Plan Cost Management per PMBOK 6 Section 7.1. The Cost Baseline (A) is an output of Determine Budget. The WBS (D) is an output of Create WBS. Funding limit reconciliation (F) is a technique used in Determine Budget, not a decision made in Plan Cost Management.
**Mindset Lesson:** Plan Cost Management produces governance rules and definitions — it does not produce numbers, baselines, or funding decisions.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Plan Cost Management is always the FIRST cost process — no estimates before the plan.
- Output: Cost Management Plan (subsidiary plan within the overall Project Management Plan).
- Key decisions: units of measure, precision, accuracy, estimating method, EVM rules, variance thresholds, reporting formats.
- Inputs: Project Charter, PM Plan, EEFs, OPAs — the Cost Baseline is NOT an input (it does not yet exist).
- Tools: expert judgment, data analysis (alternatives analysis), meetings.
- Variance thresholds must be pre-approved; reactive threshold-setting is a planning failure.
- Agile context: lightweight team agreement replaces formal document; burn rate replaces EVM.
- Hybrid context: plan must address both predictive and agile workstreams with reconciliation.
- Regulatory constraints (BSEC, ANSI-748, audit standards) must be captured in the plan before estimating begins.
- **Reflex burn-in: If the stem says "before estimating begins" or "team has no cost governance" — the answer is always Plan Cost Management first.**
