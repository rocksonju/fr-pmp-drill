# PMP Exam Master Brief: Estimate Activity Durations

---

### 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Estimate Activity Durations is the process of approximating the number of work periods needed to complete individual activities with the estimated resources. It transforms the activity list and resource requirements into quantitative duration estimates that feed directly into the schedule model. This process depends on the activity list, resource calendars, and the Schedule Management Plan — it cannot produce reliable estimates without knowing who is doing the work and under what constraints. Estimating techniques range from expert-based analogous estimates (fast, less accurate) to parametric models (formula-driven, accurate when historical data is rich) to three-point PERT estimates (probabilistic, preferred when uncertainty is high). In predictive projects, estimates are made for all activities before the schedule is developed; in agile, story points and velocity replace duration estimates as the forecasting currency. Cross-domain bleed is significant: cost estimates, resource assignments, and risk reserves all depend on the duration estimates produced here. In regulated industries such as banking and capital markets, duration estimates for compliance deliverables often carry mandatory buffers that must align with BSEC or audit calendar constraints. In one sentence: Estimate Activity Durations converts resource-loaded activities into time forecasts using the best available data, techniques, and expert judgment to feed a credible schedule model.

### 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

| Lens | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Predictive | Duration estimates are produced for every activity in the WBS decomposition using analogous, parametric, or three-point techniques; basis of estimates documents assumptions; contingency reserves are calculated from risk data. |
| Agile | Teams estimate in relative units (story points, T-shirt sizes) and use velocity from prior iterations to forecast how many points can be completed per sprint; no individual activity duration is estimated. |
| Hybrid | Fixed regulatory or contractual milestones are estimated using predictive techniques; iterative delivery work within milestones is estimated via velocity and relative sizing. |

**Top 5 PMI-isms**

1. Duration estimates are NOT the same as effort estimates — most real-world PMs use them interchangeably, but PMI requires awareness that a 40-hour effort task may take 2 calendar weeks if the resource is only 25% allocated.
2. The basis of estimates is a mandatory companion to the estimate — experienced PMs often deliver a number without documenting assumptions, but PMI expects every estimate to be traceable.
3. Contingency reserves belong in the estimate at the activity level, not invented during schedule development — practitioners typically bolt on buffer at the end of the schedule.
4. Expert judgment must be supplemented by data-driven techniques when available — real-world PMs often rely solely on a senior team member's gut feel, which PMI considers insufficient alone.
5. Three-point estimates are preferable to single-point estimates wherever uncertainty exists — experienced PMs resist three-point estimates because they feel like admitting uncertainty, but PMI rewards quantified uncertainty over false precision.

**Ethics Angle**

- Honesty: Documenting the basis of estimates ensures stakeholders receive transparent, traceable forecasts rather than black-box numbers.
- Responsibility: Padding estimates covertly (student syndrome protection) without disclosing the padding violates the PM's duty to provide accurate information.
- Fairness: Applying consistent estimation techniques across all teams and vendors prevents bias toward politically favored workstreams.
- Respect: Involving the people doing the work in duration estimation respects their expertise and produces more reliable estimates than top-down imposition.

### 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Duration Estimate | Number of work periods (not effort hours) needed to complete an activity. | Duration includes elapsed calendar time considering resource availability; it is not the same as effort. |
| Effort | Total person-hours of work required, regardless of calendar time. | 80 effort-hours at 50% allocation = 4 weeks duration; confusing effort with duration is the #1 trap. |
| Analogous Estimating | Using historical data from similar past activities as the basis for the current estimate. | Analogous is fast and cheap but less accurate; PMI considers it appropriate when limited detail is available, not as a final answer when detail exists. |
| Parametric Estimating | Using a statistical relationship between historical data and other variables (e.g., cost per unit * units = total). | Parametric is only as reliable as the historical data; applying it to novel work with no precedent is an exam trap. |
| Three-Point Estimate | Uses Optimistic (O), Most Likely (M), and Pessimistic (P) to calculate expected duration. | The PERT formula tE = (O + 4M + P) / 6 is weighted; a simple average (O + M + P) / 3 is the beta distribution variant (triangular), not PERT. |
| PERT Formula | tE = (O + 4M + P) / 6 | Denominator is always 6. Standard deviation σ = (P − O) / 6. Variance = σ². Getting σ wrong on confidence interval questions costs points. |
| Standard Deviation (σ) | σ = (P − O) / 6 | Used to calculate confidence intervals: tE ± σ = 68%, tE ± 2σ = 95%, tE ± 3σ = 99.7%. |
| Bottom-Up Estimating | Estimating each component of work and aggregating to get the total duration for the activity or work package. | Bottom-up is the most accurate but most time-consuming technique; it requires the WBS to be fully decomposed. |
| Contingency Reserve | Time added to the activity or schedule to account for identified risks (known unknowns). | Contingency reserves are for identified risks; management reserves are for unidentified risks and are NOT part of the schedule baseline. |
| Reserve Analysis | Process of determining appropriate contingency and management reserves by analyzing risk probability and impact. | Contingency comes from identified risks (risk register); management reserve is a percentage or judgment-based buffer for unknowns. |
| Resource Calendars | Documents showing availability, capability, and skills of resources by time period. | Ignoring resource calendars in duration estimation is a common real-world shortcut that PMI explicitly penalizes. |
| Basis of Estimates | Supporting documentation explaining the approach, assumptions, constraints, confidence level, and identified risks of each estimate. | The basis of estimates is a required output; delivering a number without it is incomplete. |
| Velocity | In agile, the average number of story points completed per iteration; used to forecast release dates. | Velocity is a team-level metric; applying one team's velocity to another team's backlog estimate is a common wrong answer. |

### 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "The team gave duration estimates without considering resource availability" | Review resource calendars and request revised estimates accounting for actual availability | Duration = effort / allocation; ignoring calendars produces unreliable estimates. |
| "The sponsor asks for estimates before the activity list is complete" | Explain that activity definition must precede duration estimation and provide a range estimate based on available scope information | Estimation without a defined activity list produces guesses; a documented range with assumptions is the honest interim answer. |
| "Two team members give very different duration estimates for the same activity" | Facilitate a three-point estimation session to capture optimistic, most likely, and pessimistic values | Divergent estimates signal uncertainty; three-point technique quantifies the range rather than forcing false consensus. |
| "Historical data from a similar past project exists but the current project has significant differences" | Use analogous estimating as a starting point, document the differences as adjustment assumptions in the basis of estimates | Analogous data is usable with disclosed adjustments; ignoring it wastes available information. |
| "An activity estimate was based on the assumption of a senior resource but a junior resource has been assigned" | Revise the duration estimate and update the basis of estimates to reflect the resource change | Resource skill level directly affects duration; estimates must reflect actual, not assumed, resources. |
| "The team is in the middle of sprint planning and needs to commit to stories for the iteration" | Use the team's established velocity to determine how many story points can be accepted in the sprint | In agile, velocity-based commitment replaces duration estimation for iteration planning. |
| "The PM realizes no contingency reserves were added to any activity estimates" | Perform reserve analysis using the risk register to calculate appropriate contingency reserves | Contingency must be tied to identified risks; adding it without analysis is padding, not risk management. |
| "A vendor provides a fixed-price bid without a basis of estimates" | Request the basis of estimates from the vendor to validate assumptions and identify hidden risks | Without a basis, the PM cannot assess reasonableness or identify assumption misalignments. |

**Escalation / Weak-Spot Sub-Table**

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| "Management insists estimates be cut by 20% to meet the target date before analysis is done" | Document the requested reduction as an assumption in the basis of estimates and perform schedule compression analysis to quantify impact | PMI requires the PM to surface risk of compressed estimates through analysis, not to comply silently or refuse categorically. |
| "The technical lead refuses to provide three-point estimates, insisting single-point is 'accurate enough'" | Educate the technical lead on the value of quantified uncertainty and request three-point estimates, citing the Schedule Management Plan's prescribed technique | The Schedule Management Plan defines the estimation approach; adherence is a governance matter. |

               |

               |

               |

**Mnemonic: APBV-R**
A — Analogous (historical similarity)
P — Parametric (formula-driven)
B — Bottom-Up (decomposed aggregation)
V — Velocity (agile forecasting)
R — Reserve (contingency from risk analysis)

Memory hook: "A Proper Budget Validates Reality" — every technique produces an estimate that must be validated against risk and resource reality.

### 7. Tailoring Considerations

**Predictive Project**
- All activities require documented duration estimates before the schedule model is assembled.
- Bottom-up estimating is preferred for high-complexity, high-risk activities.
- Parametric models are appropriate where historical unit rates exist (e.g., lines of code per day, cubic meters per crew per day).
- Basis of estimates must document the technique used, assumptions, confidence level, and identified risks.
- Contingency reserves are calculated from the risk register and attached at activity or work package level.

**Agile / Hybrid**
- Story points and planning poker replace duration estimates for iterative work; velocity is the forecasting engine.
- In hybrid: duration estimates for milestone-defining activities (regulatory submissions, contractual deliverables) use predictive techniques; sprint-level work uses velocity.
- The team's velocity should be measured over at least three iterations before using it for release-level forecasting.
- Re-estimation at each sprint planning replaces upfront comprehensive estimation.

**Industry / Regulatory Factors**
- Banking / Capital Markets / BSEC: Compliance deliverables often have regulatory-imposed deadlines; duration estimates for these activities must include review cycles, approval lead times, and regulatory response periods as part of the estimate.
- Construction: Parametric models (crew productivity rates, weather contingency) are industry-standard; ignoring them produces systematically optimistic estimates.
- IT / Telecom: Technical uncertainty is high; three-point estimates and sensitivity analysis are expected, not optional.
- Pharmaceutical: Regulatory submission review periods (FDA, EMA) are fixed external durations that must be included as activity durations, not risks.

### 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Use the expert team member's single-point estimate since they have done this before" | Experienced PMs trust domain experts and find three-point estimation bureaucratic on familiar work. | PMI requires quantified uncertainty for activities with any variability; single-point estimates from even the best expert embed hidden assumptions. |
| "Cut estimates by a fixed percentage to fit the target date" | Management pressure to compress schedules is a universal real-world experience; compliance feels efficient. | Compression without analysis conceals risk; PMI requires the PM to analyze and document the impact of any compression rather than simply comply. |
| "Add a 20% buffer to all estimates as a standard contingency" | Uniform buffers feel pragmatic and easy to defend. | Contingency must be risk-based (tied to the risk register); a blanket percentage is padding, not reserve management. |
| "Use the previous project's estimates directly since the projects are similar" | Analogous reuse feels efficient and data-driven. | Analogous estimates require expert adjustment for known differences; direct reuse without adjustment ignores the current project's unique conditions. |
| "Estimate durations before confirming resource assignments" | Getting estimates done quickly appears to accelerate planning. | Duration without resource calendar data is effort-only; calendar-based availability transforms effort into actual duration. |
| "Provide estimates without a basis of estimates document to save time" | Documentation feels like overhead on time-pressured projects. | The basis of estimates is a required output; its absence makes estimates unverifiable and untrustworthy for future projects. |
| "Use velocity from a different team to estimate the current team's sprint capacity" | Cross-team benchmarking seems efficient when historical data is available. | Velocity is team-specific; applying another team's velocity ignores the current team's skill mix, tooling, and working environment. |

### 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

A project manager on a regulatory compliance project for a capital markets firm has received duration estimates from the development team lead. Upon review, the PM suspects the estimates have been inflated by 40–50% to protect against management pressure to compress the schedule — a pattern observed on the previous project. The PM also knows that if the inflated estimates are accepted, the project will exceed the approved budget and trigger a portfolio review that could cancel the project. The technical lead insists the estimates are accurate. The sponsor is pressuring for schedule commitment by end of week.

What should the PM do?

A. Accept the estimates as submitted since the technical lead has the domain expertise.
B. Reduce the estimates by 40% to fit the budget and proceed with schedule development.
C. Facilitate an estimation review session using three-point techniques and the basis of estimates to objectively assess the estimates and surface any hidden assumptions.
D. Escalate the suspected padding to the sponsor without first discussing with the technical lead.

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

An agile team is in their second sprint of a six-month product release. The product owner asks the PM how many sprints will be needed to complete the remaining 240 story points in the backlog. The team completed 38 story points in Sprint 1 and 42 points in Sprint 2. No other velocity data exists yet.

What is the BEST response to the product owner?

A. 240 / 40 = 6 sprints, so the release will complete in 6 more sprints.
B. Velocity is not yet stable with only 2 sprints of data; provide a range forecast (e.g., 5–8 sprints) and commit to refining the forecast after Sprint 3–5 data is available.
C. Defer the forecast until at least 10 sprints of velocity data are available.
D. Ask the team to re-estimate all backlog items using PERT before making a forecast.

**Q3 — FIRST Action Conflict (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project PM is developing estimates for a regulatory submission milestone. The agile delivery team provides story point estimates for the software deliverables, but the compliance officer notes that the regulatory review period for their jurisdiction is 45 business days after submission — a fixed external duration that is not in any team member's estimate. The project schedule model does not include this period. The PM is about to finalize estimates for the Develop Schedule process.

What should the PM do FIRST?

A. Add a 45-business-day placeholder in the schedule as a risk reserve.
B. Add the 45-business-day regulatory review period as a defined activity duration with the compliance officer as a subject-matter expert input.
C. Exclude the regulatory review period from the project schedule since it is an external dependency.
D. Ask the agile team to convert the regulatory review period into story points for backlog tracking.

**Q4 — Two Correct Pick Best (Predictive)**

A PM is estimating durations for a large construction project. The project has 800 activities. Two estimation approaches are being considered: (1) analogous estimation using data from 12 completed similar projects, adjusted by the project engineer for known differences; and (2) bottom-up estimation where every activity is estimated individually by crew leads. The schedule must be submitted in 10 days. The project is in the early planning phase with high-level scope defined but not fully decomposed.

Which approach should the PM recommend and why?

A. Bottom-up, because it is always more accurate than analogous estimating.
B. Analogous, adjusted for known differences, because the scope is not fully decomposed and the timeline does not permit bottom-up estimation at this stage.
C. Parametric, using industry productivity rates, because construction projects always have reliable historical unit rates.
D. Three-point PERT for every activity, because uncertainty is inherent in construction.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

An agile team is about to begin sprint planning for Sprint 3. Place the following activities in the correct sequence for estimating work during sprint planning:

1. Confirm team availability and capacity for the sprint
2. Review the product backlog and prioritize the top items with the product owner
3. Break selected backlog items into tasks and estimate task effort
4. Confirm velocity from Sprints 1 and 2 to set sprint capacity
5. Commit to the sprint goal and sprint backlog

Which sequence is CORRECT?

A. 2, 4, 1, 3, 5
B. 4, 1, 2, 3, 5
C. 1, 4, 2, 3, 5
D. 2, 1, 4, 3, 5

**Q6 — EXCEPT / NOT**

Which of the following is NOT an input to the Estimate Activity Durations process?

A. Activity List
B. Resource Calendars
C. Schedule Baseline
D. Risk Register

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

A PM is under significant pressure from the program director to finalize duration estimates that support a pre-approved project end date. The PM's basis-of-estimates analysis shows that achieving the end date requires every activity to complete at its optimistic estimate with zero contingency. The PM knows that in three of five similar past projects, activities routinely exceeded the optimistic estimate by 30–40%. The program director says: "Just put in the numbers that get us to the deadline — we can manage the risk as it comes up."

What is the PM's ethical obligation?

A. Comply with the program director's request since they hold authority over the project.
B. Submit the forced estimates but add a private note to the project file documenting the concern.
C. Present the data from past projects to the program director, document the risk of the compressed estimates in the risk register, and formally escalate if the director insists on suppressing the risk documentation.
D. Resign from the project rather than submit estimates the PM believes are unrealistic.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A hybrid project is 4 months into a 12-month delivery. The steering committee asks the PM for an estimate of when the remaining agile backlog items will be completed. The agile team has 5 sprints of velocity data (average 35 story points per sprint) and 350 story points remain. The predictive regulatory milestone track is on schedule. The steering committee members are not familiar with story points.

What is the BEST way to communicate the duration estimate?

A. Share the raw velocity data and backlog size and ask the committee to calculate the forecast.
B. Convert the velocity-based forecast to a calendar date range (approximately 10 sprints = 20 weeks from now = target completion range of Month 9–10) and present it alongside the milestone track status in terms the committee can act on.
C. Tell the committee that agile projects cannot provide reliable completion date forecasts.
D. Provide only the milestone track status since that is the predictive component the committee understands.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A PM discovers that a subcontractor's duration estimates for a critical path activity were based on a crew of 8 workers, but the subcontract agreement only funds a crew of 5. The schedule model was already approved by the sponsor. The discrepancy, if corrected, would add 3 weeks to the critical path and push the project past a contractual penalty date. The subcontractor insists the estimates are correct and the contract language is ambiguous.

What should the PM do?

A. Leave the schedule as approved since changing it requires rework and stakeholder re-engagement.
B. Immediately update the schedule with the 3-week extension and notify the sponsor.
C. Initiate an integrated change request, perform impact analysis on the contractual penalty exposure, document the subcontractor discrepancy, and present options to the sponsor for decision.
D. Direct the subcontractor to honor the 8-worker assumption by adding unpaid overtime.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Select 3)**

B. The project's overall budget
C. Resource availability (percentage of time allocated to the activity)
D. The number of identified risks associated with the activity (informing contingency reserve)

### 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

**Q1**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Planning Domain and Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility require the PM to surface and investigate suspected estimation problems rather than accepting or arbitrarily cutting them. Facilitated three-point estimation with basis of estimates review is the structured, evidence-based approach. Option A abdicates PM judgment. Option B is arbitrary compression without analysis. Option D escalates before the PM has gathered their own data — premature escalation that may damage the relationship unnecessarily.
**Mindset Lesson:** Suspected padding is investigated through structured technique, not accepted or cut unilaterally.

**Q2**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide: velocity stabilizes after approximately 3–5 iterations; using 2 data points produces a forecast with wide uncertainty. Providing a range with a commitment to refine is honest and stakeholder-appropriate. Option A presents a false precision from unstable data. Option C is overly conservative and unhelpful. Option D applies the wrong estimation paradigm to an agile planning question.
**Mindset Lesson:** Agile forecasts from immature velocity data must be presented as ranges with explicit uncertainty acknowledgment.

**Q3**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6/7: external regulatory review periods are defined durations — they are activities, not risks. Treating them as contingency (A) misclassifies a known, fixed duration. Excluding them (C) creates a schedule that does not reflect reality. Converting them to story points (D) is a category error. The PM must include the regulatory period as an explicit activity duration in the schedule model.
**Mindset Lesson:** Fixed external durations (regulatory reviews, permit periods) are activities in the schedule, not risk reserves.

**Q4**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 6 — Analogous estimating is appropriate and PMI-endorsed when the WBS is not fully decomposed and time constraints prevent bottom-up work. Bottom-up (A) requires full decomposition, which does not exist. Parametric (C) may apply to some activities but not all; it is not universally applicable. Three-point PERT for every activity (D) is theoretically sound but impractical for 800 activities at early planning without a complete activity list.
**Mindset Lesson:** Technique selection is driven by available data and time; analogous estimating with documented adjustments is a valid, PMI-endorsed choice at early planning stages.

**Q5**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** Agile Practice Guide — sprint planning begins with historical velocity and capacity confirmation to establish the sprint's work limit, then the team reviews and selects backlog items, breaks them down, and commits. Sequence: 4 (velocity), 1 (capacity), 2 (backlog review), 3 (task breakdown), 5 (commitment).
**Mindset Lesson:** In agile sprint planning, capacity and velocity establish the container before backlog items are selected to fill it.

**Q6**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** The Schedule Baseline is an output of Develop Schedule, a later process. It does not exist yet during Estimate Activity Durations. The Activity List (A), Resource Calendars (B), and Risk Register (D) are all valid inputs: the activity list defines what needs estimating; resource calendars translate effort to duration; the risk register informs contingency reserves.
**Mindset Lesson:** The Schedule Baseline is created after estimates are made, not before — it cannot be an input to estimation.

**Q7**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics — Honesty and Responsibility: the PM must present factual data, document the risk, and escalate if the data is suppressed. Option A is a direct ethics violation. Option B documents privately but fails to act on the obligation to escalate. Option D is disproportionate — the PM's first duty is to act within the system before considering exit.
**Mindset Lesson:** Presenting inconvenient data, documenting risk, and escalating when suppressed is the PMI-defined ethical reflex — not compliance with authority, not silent documentation.

**Q8**
**Correct Answer:** B
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Stakeholder Performance Domain: communication must be tailored to the audience's decision-making needs. Steering committees need actionable calendar-based information, not raw agile metrics. Converting velocity to a date range bridges the agile/predictive communication gap. Option A transfers analysis burden to the audience. Option C is false — velocity-based forecasting is a valid technique. Option D withholds relevant information.
**Mindset Lesson:** Always translate agile metrics into the language of the audience; calendar dates serve decision-makers better than story point ratios.

**Q9**
**Correct Answer:** C
**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 — integrated change management and uncertainty domains: a discovered schedule discrepancy affecting the critical path and contractual penalties requires formal change control, impact analysis, and sponsor decision — not unilateral PM action. Option A suppresses a known problem. Option B updates without formal process or sponsor engagement on penalty exposure. Option D is an unauthorized directive that may also violate the subcontract.
**Mindset Lesson:** Discovered schedule integrity issues always trigger formal change control with documented impact analysis and stakeholder decision-making authority.

**Q10**
**Correct Answers:** A, C, D
**PMI Justification:** Resource skill and experience (A) directly affects how long an activity takes — a senior analyst completes a task faster than a junior one. Resource availability/allocation percentage (C) converts effort into calendar duration — the fundamental duration equation. Identified risks (D) determine contingency reserves added to the base estimate. Project budget (B) is a constraint, not a driver of duration. Organizational risk appetite (E) shapes risk response strategy, not activity durations directly. Sponsor preference (F) is a constraint that may trigger compression analysis, but it does not determine the estimate — it may challenge it.
**Mindset Lesson:** Duration estimates are driven by who does the work, how much of their time is available, and what risks are known — not by budget or stakeholder preferences.

### 12. 60-Second Recap Card

- Estimate Activity Durations converts activities into time forecasts using the assigned resources and their availability.
- Duration ≠ Effort: duration accounts for resource allocation percentage; always distinguish the two.
- Four main techniques: Analogous (fast, less accurate), Parametric (formula-driven, data-dependent), Bottom-Up (most accurate, most effort), Three-Point/PERT (quantifies uncertainty).
- PERT formula: tE = (O + 4M + P) / 6. Standard deviation: σ = (P − O) / 6. Denominator is always 6.
- Basis of estimates is a required output — numbers without documented assumptions are incomplete.
- Contingency reserves come from the risk register (identified risks); management reserves cover unknowns and are outside the schedule baseline.
- Resource calendars are mandatory inputs; estimates without them are effort-only guesses.
- In agile: story points + velocity replace duration estimates; velocity requires 3+ iterations to stabilize before use in release forecasting.
- In hybrid: predictive techniques for fixed milestones; velocity for iterative components.
- When management pressures compression, analyze the impact and document the risk — never silently comply.
- **Reflex burn-in: When a stem mentions a single-point estimate on an uncertain activity, the correct PMI answer always moves toward three-point PERT with a documented basis of estimates — never false precision, never arbitrary buffer.**
