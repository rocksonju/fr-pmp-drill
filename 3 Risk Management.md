# PMP Exam Master Brief: Risk Management
### Weak Spots Targeted: Escalation · Communication

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Risk management is the disciplined practice of identifying uncertainty that could harm or help your project, analyzing its probability and impact, planning responses, and adapting those responses continuously as conditions change. It treats both threats and opportunities — PMI does not let you ignore upside. It is not a one-time risk workshop at planning; it is a recurring loop that runs from charter through closeout. On the exam, ~12–18% of questions touch risk directly, and the topic bleeds heavily into stakeholder engagement (escalation), procurement (transfer), schedule (reserves), and cost (contingency funding). In real projects, weak risk management produces "surprise" issues, blown reserves, and reactive sponsor escalations; on the exam, misreading "when to escalate a risk to the sponsor" and "when to use contingency vs management reserve" kills your score. The PMI examiner expects you to be proactive, transparent, and proportionate — not a worrier, not a cowboy.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about systematically anticipating, owning, and communicating uncertainty so that nothing material ever surprises the sponsor.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Risk Management Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Formal risk register and risk report, qualitative + quantitative analysis, contingency reserves baked into schedule/budget, defined risk owners, periodic risk reviews |
| **Agile** | Risk-adjusted backlog (high-risk items prioritized early), spikes for unknowns, retrospectives surface emerging risks, fast feedback shortens exposure window, no formal register required |
| **Hybrid** | Risk register exists but reviewed each iteration; high-risk PBIs pulled forward; reserves managed at release level; escalation paths formal but identification is continuous |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Opportunities are risks too.** Real world ignores upside. PMI expects exploit/share/enhance strategies on every risk register.
2. **Risk owner is not always the PM.** The PM facilitates; the person with authority to act on the response owns it. Assigning yourself to every risk is a red flag.
3. **Contingency reserve is the PM's to spend. Management reserve is not.** Crossing that line without authorization is an integrity failure, not a paperwork miss.
4. **A risk that materializes is no longer a risk — it is an issue.** Different log, different response. PMI tests whether you migrate it correctly.
5. **Escalate a risk when it exceeds your threshold or authority, not when it scares you.** Threshold-driven escalation is PMI; gut-driven escalation is real world.

### Ethics Angle
- **Honesty:** Never suppress, downgrade, or delay reporting a known risk to protect a status color. Withholding material risk = ethics violation.
- **Responsibility:** PM owns the risk management process even when individual risk owners are elsewhere. You cannot delegate accountability for the system.
- **Respect:** Risks identified by team members must be logged even if you disagree — disqualifying input is disrespectful and weakens the register.
- **Fairness:** Reserve allocation must follow documented criteria, not stakeholder politics. Pet projects do not get priority on contingency draw-down.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk Register** | Living document of identified risks: ID, description, category, probability, impact, owner, response, status | Trap: treating it as a planning artifact. Must be updated at every review, not at phase gates only |
| **Risk Report** | Summary of overall project risk exposure and top individual risks for sponsor/stakeholders | Trap: confusing with risk register. Report = communication output; register = working document |
| **Probability × Impact Matrix** | Qualitative ranking grid (e.g., 1–5 × 1–5) to prioritize risks | Trap: treating high-probability/low-impact same as low-probability/high-impact. Score, then rank by combined score |
| **EMV (Expected Monetary Value)** | `EMV = Probability × Impact ($)`. Sum across branches in decision tree | Trap: forgetting threats are negative EMV, opportunities positive. Net EMV drives decision |
| **Decision Tree Analysis** | Branches with probabilities and payoffs; pick path with best EMV | Trap: ignoring sunk-cost choices; only forward-looking EMV matters |
| **Monte Carlo Simulation** | Probabilistic schedule/cost analysis using thousands of iterations to produce confidence ranges (e.g., P50/P80) | Trap: assuming P50 = "the plan." Sponsor commitments usually require P80 or higher |
| **Sensitivity Analysis / Tornado Diagram** | Shows which variables most affect project outcome; longest bars = biggest drivers | Trap: confusing sensitivity (range) with criticality (path). Different concepts |
| **Contingency Reserve** | Time/cost set aside for KNOWN-UNKNOWN risks (identified, response planned). Part of cost baseline. **PM controls.** | Trap: confusing with management reserve. Contingency is INSIDE baseline |
| **Management Reserve** | Time/cost for UNKNOWN-UNKNOWN risks. **Outside** cost baseline. Requires sponsor/management approval to use | Trap: dipping into it without authorization. Always escalate first |
| **Risk Appetite / Threshold / Tolerance** | Appetite = stomach for risk overall; Threshold = measurable trigger; Tolerance = acceptable variance | Trap: using interchangeably. Threshold is the number that triggers action |
| **Risk Response Strategies — Threats** | Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept, **Escalate** | Trap: forgetting Escalate is a valid PMI strategy for risks outside PM authority |
| **Risk Response Strategies — Opportunities** | Exploit, Share, Enhance, Accept, **Escalate** | Trap: only listing four. Five strategies per side including Escalate |
| **Secondary Risk** | A new risk created by implementing a risk response | Trap: ignoring it. PMI expects you to register secondary risks |
| **Residual Risk** | The leftover risk after the response is applied | Trap: assuming response = elimination. Almost always residual remains |
| **Workaround** | Unplanned response to a risk that has occurred (now an issue) | Trap: calling it a contingency. Contingency is pre-planned; workaround is reactive |
| **Risk-Adjusted Backlog (Agile)** | Backlog ordering that pulls high-risk/high-value items earlier to reduce exposure | Trap: ordering purely by business value. PMI agile expects risk-weighted prioritization |
| **Spike (Agile)** | Time-boxed investigation to reduce uncertainty before committing to delivery | Trap: treating a spike as failed work if no shippable output — its output is learning |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| A **new risk is identified** mid-execution | Log it in the risk register, assess probability and impact, assign an owner | Identification without analysis and ownership is not management |
| A risk has **occurred** (probability now 100%) | Move it from risk register to issue log; execute planned response or workaround | A materialized risk is no longer a risk — wrong log = wrong process |
| A risk response **creates a new risk** | Register the secondary risk with its own response plan | PMI expects explicit secondary-risk tracking, not "we'll watch it" |
| **Contingency reserve** is exhausted but identified risks remain | Reassess reserves, document, request management reserve through governance | Quietly absorbing into baseline = unauthorized scope/cost change |
| Sponsor asks why **risk exposure is rising** | Provide updated risk report with top risks, trends, and proposed responses | Risk report is the sponsor communication tool — register is internal |
| A team member raises a **risk you disagree with** | Log it anyway, then analyze. Disagreement is not a filter | Suppressing input weakens the register and violates Respect |
| **Quantitative analysis** shows P80 schedule date is later than committed date | Update the risk report, communicate to sponsor, propose reserve or scope action | Hiding the gap is dishonest; committing to P50 as if it were P80 is reckless |
| An **opportunity** emerges (e.g., vendor offers early discount) | Apply exploit/share/enhance/accept analysis the same as for a threat | PMI does not let you skip opportunity management |
| Risk owner is **unable or unwilling** to act on their assigned response | Re-engage the owner; if unresolved, reassign or escalate to sponsor | Ownership gaps = process failure, not personal failure |
| **Regulatory risk** is identified late (e.g., BSEC requirement missed) | Log, assess immediately, escalate per governance — regulatory threshold usually exceeds PM authority | Regulatory exposure almost always exceeds PM tolerance threshold |

**Two Escalation-Specific Scenarios (your weak spot):**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| A risk's impact exceeds the PM's authority/threshold (e.g., requires management reserve or strategic decision) | Escalate to sponsor with the risk report, recommended response, and decision required. Do not act unilaterally. |
| A risk identified by the team belongs to a parent program or portfolio | Use the **Escalate** response strategy explicitly — transfer ownership to program level and document. PM is no longer the owner. |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

### The Risk Response Decision Tree (PMI Style)

```
RISK IDENTIFIED
        │
        ▼
Is it a THREAT or OPPORTUNITY?
        │
   ┌────┴────┐
   ▼         ▼
 THREAT   OPPORTUNITY
   │         │
   ▼         ▼
Outside PM authority / threshold?
   YES → ESCALATE (both threats and opportunities)
   NO  ↓
   ▼
Can you eliminate the cause?
   YES → AVOID (threat) / EXPLOIT (opportunity)
   NO  ↓
   ▼
Can a third party handle it better?
   YES → TRANSFER (threat) / SHARE (opportunity)
   NO  ↓
   ▼
Can you reduce probability/impact (threat) or increase it (opportunity)?
   YES → MITIGATE (threat) / ENHANCE (opportunity)
   NO  ↓
   ▼
ACCEPT (active = contingency plan; passive = monitor)
        │
        ▼
Log response, assign owner, set trigger, update risk report
        │
        ▼
If risk occurs → move to issue log → execute response
```

### Response Strategy Mnemonic: **A-T-M-A-E** (both sides)

> **Threats:** **A**void → **T**ransfer → **M**itigate → **A**ccept → **E**scalate
> **Opportunities:** **E**xploit → **S**hare → **E**nhance → **A**ccept → **E**scalate

Memory hook: **"ATM cash always escalates."**
- The first four are the working strategies; **Escalate** is the gate when the risk leaves your zone of control.
- If the answer choice says "escalate" and the stem shows the risk is within PM authority, it is the wrong answer.

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Risk register and risk report are formal, version-controlled documents
- Quantitative analysis (Monte Carlo, decision tree, EMV) more common on large/complex projects
- Contingency reserves built into cost and schedule baselines at planning
- Risk reviews held on a fixed cadence (e.g., weekly risk meeting) and at phase gates
- Reserve drawdown follows change control; management reserve requires sponsor approval

### Agile / Hybrid
- High-risk items pulled to the front of the backlog (risk-adjusted backlog)
- Spikes used to reduce technical uncertainty before commitment
- Retrospectives are the structured risk identification mechanism each sprint
- Information radiators (impediment board, risk burndown) make risk exposure visible
- No formal register required — but hybrid projects keep one alongside iteration ceremonies
- Escalation in agile: impediment → Scrum Master / PM → sponsor; the team never escalates straight to the sponsor

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** BSEC and Bangladesh Bank exposure usually exceeds PM threshold by default — treat regulatory risks as escalation-bound on identification
- Operational-risk frameworks (Basel-style RCSA) often pre-exist; align project register to them, do not duplicate
- AML/KYC failures, data-residency breaches, and cybersecurity incidents are reputational risks — reserve allocation must reflect that, not just direct cost
- Audit trail of risk decisions is mandatory; verbal acceptance of a risk does not satisfy regulators
- **Government / state-owned projects:** political risks dominate; salience-driven stakeholders may override technical risk priorities

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Escalate the risk to the sponsor immediately" | Sponsors should know about risks early; better safe than sorry | PMI: escalation is for risks beyond PM authority/threshold. Default escalation = abdication |
| "Add the risk to the issue log so it is tracked" | An issue log is for tracking problems — risks are problems | Risks ≠ issues. A risk in the issue log signals the PM does not understand the distinction |
| "Use management reserve to cover the unplanned cost" | Reserves exist to be used; project is over-budget anyway | PM has no authority over management reserve. Always requires sponsor/governance approval first |
| "Mark the risk as accepted because the team has it handled" | Acceptance is a valid strategy; the team is competent | Passive acceptance with no documented response and no monitoring fails PMI's traceability bar |
| "Run a Monte Carlo simulation before responding to any new risk" | Quantitative rigor is best practice | Monte Carlo is for overall project exposure, not individual risk response. Wrong tool, wrong stage |
| "Remove the risk from the register once the response is implemented" | The risk is handled; deleting keeps the register tidy | Residual risk remains; risks stay on the register with updated status until they expire or occur |
| "Inform the sponsor only when the risk's probability exceeds 50%" | Communication should be material, not noise | PMI requires proactive communication of all risks above the agreed threshold, regardless of probability stage |
| "Reassign the risk to the functional manager and consider it transferred" | Transfer to someone with authority feels right | Internal reassignment ≠ transfer. Transfer means a third party (insurer, vendor, contract) bears the consequence |
| "Add a buffer to the schedule personally to absorb the risk" | Senior PMs know how to protect a plan | Padding outside the formal contingency process is hidden risk management and violates transparency |

## 11. Exam-Day Trigger Phrases

| Trigger Phrase in Stem | Your Reflex Answer Pattern |
|---|---|
| "A **new risk** has been identified" | Log → analyze (P × I) → assign owner → plan response → communicate |
| "The risk has **occurred**" | It is now an issue. Move to issue log. Execute planned response or workaround |
| "Use of **management reserve**" as an option | Almost always wrong unless governance/sponsor approval is explicitly stated |
| "Contingency is **exhausted**" | Reassess, document, escalate for management reserve — do not silently absorb |
| "Run a **Monte Carlo simulation**" | Right answer when question asks about overall schedule/cost confidence range, not individual risk |
| "The team **disagrees** with a flagged risk" | Log it anyway. Analyze. Do not suppress input. |
| "**Sponsor was not informed** of a risk that materialized" | Communication failure, not risk failure. Examine your reporting cadence and risk report |
| "An **opportunity** has emerged" | Apply Exploit / Share / Enhance / Accept — never ignore upside |
| "Risk owner is the **PM** for every risk" | Red flag — reassign by authority; PM cannot own everything |
| "Risk is **outside the project's scope**" | Use the Escalate response strategy explicitly — program/portfolio takes it |
| "**Regulatory** risk identified" | Escalate; regulatory exposure almost always exceeds PM threshold |
| "**Spike**" mentioned in agile context | Time-boxed learning. Output is reduced uncertainty, not deliverable |
| "**Risk-adjusted backlog**" | Agile prioritization that pulls high-risk items earlier — correct PMI agile answer |
| "Sponsor asks for **risk update**" | Provide risk report (summary), not raw risk register |
| "Risk **threshold** exceeded" | Trigger for escalation or response activation, not for monitoring continuation |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are managing a USD 14M core-banking modernization for a private commercial bank. During quantitative risk analysis, your lead architect identifies a critical data-migration risk with potential to cause a regulatory breach with Bangladesh Bank. Your sponsor, who is also the COO, asks you to "downgrade the rating in the next steering committee report so we do not alarm the board ahead of the annual review." The current rating is supported by documented analysis. What should you do FIRST?

A. Downgrade the rating as requested since the sponsor has final authority over project communications.
B. Refuse to alter the rating and present the analysis-supported rating in the steering committee report, while privately discussing the COO's concerns with them.
C. Escalate the COO's request to the board chair as an ethics violation.
D. Keep the rating but exclude the risk detail from the report so the rating "speaks for itself."

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

You are the project manager on an agile capital-markets trading platform team. Over the last three sprints, the team has consistently identified new technical risks during sprint planning but rarely revisits or closes them in retrospectives. The product owner has begun raising the same risks again in stakeholder demos, surprising business stakeholders. What is the BEST action?

A. Replace the retrospective with a dedicated weekly risk review meeting.
B. Make risk review a standing agenda item in the retrospective and update the team agreement to include risk status updates in each sprint review.
C. Move all risk tracking to the PM's risk register and remove it from team ceremonies.
D. Escalate the recurring risks to the sponsor for visibility.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are leading a hybrid digital-onboarding project for a bank. Two senior stakeholders — the Chief Risk Officer and the Head of Retail — disagree on the response to a recently identified fraud risk. The CRO wants to mitigate aggressively (delays launch by 6 weeks). The Head of Retail wants to accept with monitoring (preserves launch date). Your sponsor has asked you to "make a call." What should you do FIRST?

A. Decide in favor of the CRO since risk authority outranks delivery preference.
B. Facilitate a joint working session with both stakeholders and the risk owner to quantify the residual risk under each option and align on a recommendation.
C. Make the call to accept with monitoring to preserve the committed launch date and document the decision.
D. Escalate the conflict back to the sponsor since both stakeholders report to them.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

You are managing a large infrastructure rollout. During execution, you discover that the contingency reserve has been depleted by 70% and three identified risks with planned mitigations have not yet been actioned. The sponsor's next status review is in five business days. Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Reassess remaining contingency against outstanding risk exposure and prepare a reserve forecast for the sponsor review.
B. Apply for additional management reserve through governance immediately.
C. Activate the planned mitigations for the three unactioned risks and confirm risk owner status.
D. Reclassify the unactioned risks as accepted to bring the register into alignment with reality.
E. Suspend further mitigation spend until after the sponsor review.

---

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

During a sprint review for a payments product, a stakeholder raises a previously unidentified compliance risk that could block release. The product owner is uncertain whether the team should continue building or pause for a deeper investigation. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Add a time-boxed spike to the next sprint to investigate the compliance question.
2. Log the risk in the risk register (or risk-adjusted backlog) with probability and impact.
3. Update the release plan based on spike outcome.
4. Facilitate a conversation between the product owner, compliance stakeholder, and team to clarify the risk.

A. 2 → 4 → 1 → 3
B. 4 → 2 → 1 → 3
C. 1 → 2 → 4 → 3
D. 2 → 1 → 4 → 3

**Q6 — EXCEPT Question (Predictive)**

A project manager is performing the Plan Risk Responses process. Which of the following is NOT a valid output of this process?

A. Updates to the risk register with response strategies and owners
B. Change requests (e.g., to the schedule or cost baseline)
C. Project documents updates (assumption log, lessons learned register)
D. Risk audit findings
E. Updates to the project management plan

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are six months into an 18-month regulatory-reporting platform delivery. A senior architect privately tells you that a known design risk — flagged in the register but rated "low" — is actually materially under-rated and could cause a reporting failure with BSEC. The project sponsor is presenting to the board next week and the current rating supports a green status. Reassessing would change the status to amber. What do you do?

A. Wait until after the board presentation, then formally reassess to avoid undermining the sponsor.
B. Reassess the risk immediately, update the risk report, and inform the sponsor before the board presentation so they have accurate information.
C. Take the architect's input as one perspective and continue with the current rating until you have a second technical opinion.
D. Reassess only the residual risk in a footnote so the headline rating does not change.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid capital-markets platform delivery. The risk register has grown to 142 active risks. The sponsor complains that weekly risk emails are "too noisy" and skim past them. Regulatory stakeholders simultaneously demand granular detail in their monthly reports. The team uses a real-time risk dashboard accessible to all parties. What is the BEST communication approach?

A. Send the full risk register to all stakeholders weekly so nobody is uninformed.
B. Stop sending risk emails entirely and rely on the dashboard for everyone.
C. Send the sponsor a risk report covering only top risks and trend changes; produce a detailed monthly risk report for regulatory stakeholders; keep the dashboard live for all — tailor to each audience.
D. Reduce the risk register to top ten risks only to keep communication simple.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project. A newly identified cybersecurity risk has a potential impact of USD 4M and a probability of 30%. The project's contingency reserve is USD 1.2M. The PM's documented financial authority on this project is USD 500K. Your two facilitation sessions with the security stakeholder and the vendor have not produced a workable mitigation. What is the BEST next step?

A. Continue facilitating until a mitigation emerges since escalation is premature.
B. Brief the sponsor with the risk report, the EMV calculation, the two facilitation attempts, and a recommended response requiring sponsor decision.
C. Accept the risk and add it to the risk register as residual.
D. Use the contingency reserve to fund a partial mitigation now and escalate only if it fails.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

A project manager on an agile project notices that risk identification has dropped sharply over the past four sprints. Stakeholders have stopped raising concerns in sprint reviews, and the team's risk-adjusted backlog has not been updated in two iterations. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Investigate why risk identification has dropped before taking corrective action.
B. Mandate that each team member submit at least two risks per sprint.
C. Add a standing risk-review item to the retrospective and surface risk status in each sprint review.
D. Replace agile ceremonies with a formal monthly risk workshop.
E. Work with the product owner to revisit the risk-adjusted backlog and pull high-risk items earlier.
F. Escalate the decline in risk identification to the sponsor and request mandatory risk reporting.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty) prohibits altering analysis-supported risk ratings for political comfort. The risk report must reflect the documented assessment. The PM's first move is to hold the line on the rating AND understand the sponsor's underlying concern through direct dialogue — this respects the relationship without compromising integrity. Option A is dishonest. Option C jumps to ethics escalation without first engaging the sponsor. Option D is selective disclosure — also dishonest.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor authority does not override accurate risk reporting; engage the sponsor on their concern, not their request.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Per the Agile Practice Guide, retrospectives are the team's continuous-improvement engine and the appropriate ceremony for risk-process adjustment. Sprint reviews are where stakeholders see progress and surface concerns — adding risk status there closes the loop. Option A removes risk work from the team and rebuilds a parallel process. Option C centralizes work that agile expects the team to own. Option D escalates a process problem the team should fix itself.

**Mindset Lesson:** Risk in agile lives in team ceremonies — fix the ceremony, not the org chart.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Uncertainty and Stakeholder Performance Domains expect the PM to facilitate fact-based decisions before deciding or escalating. Quantifying residual risk under each option turns a values clash into a data-supported choice. Option A picks a side without analysis. Option C picks the opposite side without analysis. Option D bounces an unanalyzed conflict back to the sponsor — the sponsor asked the PM to make the call after preparation, not before.

**Mindset Lesson:** "Make a call" from the sponsor = facilitate, analyze, recommend — not flip a coin or bounce it back.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** The immediate priority is to stop ongoing risk accumulation (C: activate the planned mitigations) and to give the sponsor a fact-based reserve picture (A: reassess and forecast). Option B (request management reserve) is premature without a current reassessment. Option D (reclassify as accepted) hides exposure — dishonest and a planning failure. Option E (suspend mitigation spend) increases risk while reserves are stressed, which is the opposite of risk management.

**Mindset Lesson:** When reserves are stressed, PMI expects corrective action + transparent forecast — never hiding exposure to balance the books.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: B (4 → 2 → 1 → 3)**

**PMI Justification:** Per the Agile Practice Guide, when a new risk emerges in a sprint review, the first move is alignment — facilitate a conversation between PO, stakeholder, and team to clarify the risk (4). Then log it with P × I (2). A spike is then time-boxed to investigate (1), and the release plan is updated based on spike output (3). Spiking before clarifying wastes capacity on the wrong question. Logging before clarifying produces a vague register entry.

**Mindset Lesson:** Alignment before logging, logging before spiking, spike output before plan change — sequence is the whole answer.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: D**

**PMI Justification:** Risk audit findings are an output of the Monitor Risks process, not Plan Risk Responses. Plan Risk Responses produces updated risk register, change requests, project documents updates, and PM plan updates. Knowing which process generates which artifact is a recurring PMI input/output trap.

**Mindset Lesson:** "Audit" language belongs to Monitor Risks — never to Plan Risk Responses.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Honesty, Responsibility) requires the PM to provide accurate information to the sponsor even when timing is inconvenient. The risk must be reassessed on the merits, the risk report updated, and the sponsor informed in time to act before the board presentation. Option A delays disclosure of material information. Option C uses "need a second opinion" as a stall tactic on a clear technical input. Option D is selective disclosure — dishonest.

**Mindset Lesson:** Material risk reassessment cannot wait for a calendar that benefits anyone — the sponsor decides what to do with the information; the PM decides only whether to surface it.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 explicitly endorses tailoring communication to audience. The sponsor needs decision-grade summary; regulators need compliance-grade detail; the team needs real-time visibility. All three can coexist. Option A is push-spam. Option B abandons regulatory obligation. Option D hides risk to simplify communication — dishonest and dangerous.

**Mindset Lesson:** Communication tailoring is not compromise — it is matching the right artifact to the right audience simultaneously.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** EMV here is 0.30 × USD 4M = USD 1.2M, which exceeds the PM's USD 500K authority and equals the entire contingency reserve. Facilitation has been attempted twice without success. PMBOK 7 Uncertainty Domain treats this as the textbook **Escalate** strategy — risk exceeds PM authority and threshold. Option A is continued facilitation past the point of return. Option C silently accepts a risk beyond PM authority — an integrity failure. Option D commits the entire contingency reserve to a partial response without sponsor approval.

**Mindset Lesson:** Escalate is a response strategy — pick it when the risk exceeds your authority/threshold, not when it scares you.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:**
- **A:** Diagnose before intervening — PMBOK 7 Uncertainty Domain and Agile Practice Guide both require understanding cause before corrective action.
- **C:** Embeds risk identification into the team's existing rhythm rather than building a parallel process.
- **E:** Risk-adjusted backlog is the agile mechanism for converting risk visibility into prioritization action; updating it closes the loop with delivery.
- B is wrong: a quota produces noise, not signal.
- D is wrong: replacing agile ceremonies with a waterfall workshop contradicts the delivery approach.
- F is wrong: mandatory reporting policies are coercive and do not address why identification has dropped.

**Mindset Lesson:** Falling risk identification = process problem; fix the ceremony, not the headcount or the org chart.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**RISK MANAGEMENT — Exam Morning Card**

- Risk = uncertain event affecting objectives. Threats AND opportunities. Both get strategies.
- **Threats:** Avoid · Transfer · Mitigate · Accept · **Escalate**. **Opportunities:** Exploit · Share · Enhance · Accept · **Escalate**.
- Risk → Issue when it occurs. Different log, different process.
- **Contingency reserve = PM controls (known-unknowns).** **Management reserve = sponsor controls (unknown-unknowns).** Crossing the line without approval = ethics failure.
- Risk owner ≠ always PM. Assign by authority to act.
- Risk register = working doc. Risk report = sponsor communication.
- Quantitative tools: EMV for choices, Monte Carlo for overall exposure, sensitivity/tornado for drivers.
- Agile: risk-adjusted backlog + spikes + retrospective risk review. Information radiators visible to all.
- "Escalate" the response strategy is right when risk exceeds PM authority/threshold. Otherwise it is abdication.
- Honesty overrides political timing. Reassess and report when the data says so.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *PMI escalates a risk when it crosses authority or threshold — never to feel safe, never to delay a decision, always with the risk report.*
