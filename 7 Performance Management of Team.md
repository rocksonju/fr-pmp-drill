# PMP Exam Master Brief: Performance Management of Team
### Weak Spots Targeted: Escalation · Communication

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Team performance management is the active practice of building, developing, and sustaining a high-performing project team — through clear expectations, training, coaching, recognition, conflict resolution, and continuous monitoring of team health and output. It runs in parallel with delivery; it is not a separate phase. PMI treats team performance as a Team Performance Domain in PMBOK 7 and an explicit responsibility under the People domain on the current exam (which alone is ~42% of questions). Topics include team charter, team development activities, recognition and rewards, training, conflict management within the team, virtual team management, burnout, psychological safety, and performance assessment. In real projects, weak team performance management produces silent disengagement, hidden burnout, and surprise resignations; on the exam, missing the difference between team-level intervention (process, ceremonies, environment) and individual-level intervention (coaching, discipline) kills your score. PMI expects the PM to invest in the team continuously, not reactively.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about building and sustaining a high-performing team through clear expectations, deliberate development, fair recognition, and continuous attention to team health.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Team Performance Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Resource plan, formal training plans, scheduled performance reviews, recognition tied to milestones, team charter signed at kickoff |
| **Agile** | Team charter / working agreement, retrospectives as continuous performance loop, peer recognition, velocity as planning input only (never as performance metric), team-owned DoD |
| **Hybrid** | Formal resource plan + agile retrospectives; performance assessment at iteration and milestone level; recognition mixes formal and peer |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Team-level problems get team-level solutions; individual problems get individual conversations.** Mixing levels is a recurring PMI trap.
2. **Velocity is never a performance metric.** It is a planning tool. Using velocity to evaluate the team produces gaming, not productivity.
3. **Psychological safety is the PM's responsibility.** A team that does not speak up about risk, defects, or burnout is a leadership failure.
4. **Recognition is a leadership tool, not a budget line.** Public, timely, contribution-based recognition often outperforms monetary rewards.
5. **Burnout is a process problem, not a personal weakness.** PMI never tells you to ask people to "push through."

### Ethics Angle
- **Respect:** Honest feedback delivered with care is required; sugar-coating is disrespect dressed up as kindness.
- **Fairness:** Performance assessments and recognition must be based on documented criteria, not impressions or proximity.
- **Honesty:** Team performance data shared with stakeholders must be accurate; window-dressing is a Honesty breach.
- **Responsibility:** Burnout in the team is the PM's responsibility to surface and address, even when the team appears to "want" to push through.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Team Charter / Working Agreement** | Document of team norms, ceremonies, conflict resolution, decision-making | Trap: PM imposing — must be co-created to be effective |
| **Ground Rules** | Specific behavioral expectations agreed by the team | Trap: ground rules without enforcement; team holds itself accountable |
| **Resource Calendar** | Availability and capacity of resources over time | Trap: confusing with project schedule; resource calendar covers people, not tasks |
| **RAM (Responsibility Assignment Matrix)** | Maps roles to work; RACI is a common form | Trap: confusing R and A; multiple R, one A |
| **Team Performance Assessment** | Periodic evaluation of team health and output | Trap: confusing with individual performance review |
| **Team Building Activities** | Deliberate activities to improve cohesion, trust, capability | Trap: assuming social events alone build teams; structured activities are stronger |
| **Recognition and Rewards System** | Documented basis for recognition; mix of formal and informal | Trap: relying on recognition by visibility |
| **Training Plan** | Documented plan for team skill development | Trap: assuming training is a closure activity; it is continuous |
| **Psychological Safety** | Team-level belief that interpersonal risk-taking is safe | Trap: confusing with comfort; safety enables disagreement, not silence |
| **Burnout** | Sustained exhaustion + cynicism + reduced efficacy | Trap: dismissing as individual weakness; PMI treats as process problem |
| **Velocity (Agile)** | Story points completed per iteration | Trap: using as performance metric; it is for planning only |
| **Cycle Time (Agile/Kanban)** | Time from start to completion of a single work item | Trap: confusing with lead time |
| **Lead Time (Agile/Kanban)** | Time from request to delivery of a single work item | Trap: confusing with cycle time |
| **Throughput (Agile/Kanban)** | Number of items completed per time unit | Trap: confusing with velocity (velocity uses points, throughput uses items) |
| **Co-located Team** | Team in same physical location | Trap: assuming co-location is mandatory for high performance; tailoring matters |
| **Virtual / Distributed Team** | Team across locations / time zones | Trap: managing as if co-located; requires deliberate communication design |
| **Team Agreement / Definition of Done** | Shared criteria for "done" | Trap: PM-defined DoD; team-owned |
| **One-on-Ones** | Regular private conversations between PM/manager and team member | Trap: skipping one-on-ones when busy; this is when issues should surface |
| **360-Degree Feedback** | Performance feedback collected from peers, subordinates, and supervisors | Trap: assuming applicability everywhere; cultural and organizational fit varies |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Team **morale is dropping** | Investigate cause (workload, conflict, scope change, leadership) before intervening | Diagnose before treatment |
| Team member shows **burnout signs** | Hold a private conversation; assess workload + scope + sustainable pace | Burnout is process problem first, individual second |
| Sprint velocity is **declining** | Investigate cause in retrospective; do not punish team for the metric | Velocity is planning input, not performance KPI |
| **Public conflict** within team | Pause, name dynamic, facilitate problem-solve conversation | Conflict resolution in the room |
| Team is **silent in ceremonies** | Investigate psychological safety; ask one-on-ones what is unsaid | Silence ≠ agreement; often signals fear |
| A team member asks for **training** | Evaluate against project needs; include in training plan; do not refuse without analysis | Training is investment, not cost |
| Recognition system feels **inconsistent** | Audit criteria; reset with team input | Fairness in recognition is structural, not gut-driven |
| Virtual team member is **disengaging** | Increase synchronous touchpoints + one-on-ones; check belonging | Virtual disengagement signals connection design failure |
| A team member is **outperforming** but resented by peers | Hold conversations; check whether work allocation is fair | High performance with team friction often signals allocation imbalance |
| Sponsor demands **faster delivery** without scope reduction | Surface sustainable pace risk; renegotiate scope/timeline/quality | Pushing the team without renegotiation breaks sustainability |

**Two Escalation-Specific Scenarios (your weak spot):**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| Team-wide burnout indicators persist after PM-level workload action | Escalate to sponsor with quantified pace data, retro evidence, recommended renegotiation — never absorb |
| A functional manager pulls a team member off-project for other work without coordination | Engage the functional manager first; escalate to sponsor if unresolved, with the resource plan and project impact |

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Team charter signed at kickoff; resource plan formal
- Performance reviews at milestones; recognition tied to deliverables
- Training plans budgeted at planning; risk of being cut under cost pressure
- Co-located teams more common; virtual coordination structured

### Agile / Hybrid
- Working agreement co-created; retrospectives are the continuous performance loop
- Peer recognition more common; team-owned DoD; velocity for planning only
- Training is continuous and team-driven; "learning sprints" or spikes for skill build
- Hybrid: formal resource plan + agile ceremonies; performance assessment at both levels

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** hierarchical culture often discourages psychological safety; PM must build it deliberately
- Recognition culture may be sparse; even small public acknowledgment outperforms silence
- Regulatory deadlines (BSEC, Bangladesh Bank) tempt sustained overtime — surface as sustainable-pace risk, do not normalize
- **Outsourced / vendor-heavy teams:** team-charter approach must include vendor team members; otherwise two-class system emerges
- **Multinational / cross-cultural teams:** recognition norms vary by culture (public praise welcomed in some, embarrassing in others); check before assuming

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Use velocity as a team performance metric" | Velocity is measurable and trendable | Velocity is a planning tool; using it to evaluate produces story-point inflation, not productivity |
| "Add overtime to recover the schedule" | Senior PMs deliver | Sustainable pace is the principle; overtime is short-term debt with team-health interest |
| "Bring in extra team members to absorb the new scope" | More hands = more output | Brooks' law: adding people to a late project makes it later in the short term |
| "Have the PM author the team charter and share with the team" | PM-authored charter is consistent | Team agreements require co-creation to produce ownership |
| "Replace the underperforming team member quickly" | Quick removal protects delivery | Coaching and joint action with FM/HR come first; replacement is last resort |
| "Recognize only the highest-output contributor publicly" | Reward visible excellence | Recognition by visibility violates Fairness; audit actual contribution |
| "Discourage open disagreement to keep ceremonies productive" | Reduces friction | Psychological safety requires disagreement to be safe; suppression hides real signal |
| "Skip one-on-ones during a delivery crunch" | Crunch demands focus | One-on-ones are exactly when issues should surface; skipping them creates surprise failures |
| "Treat burnout as a personal resilience issue for the team member" | Adults manage their own well-being | PMI treats burnout as a process problem first; pace, scope, and clarity are the PM's controls |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are PM of a USD 5M project at a bank. Your sponsor asks you to "lean on" a particular team member who has been performing slightly below the team average, by giving the team member the most difficult tasks until they "leave on their own." Performance issues are minor and have not been addressed through coaching. What should you do FIRST?

A. Carry out the sponsor's instruction since the sponsor has authority.
B. Refuse the request and explain that performance management requires coaching and fair process; propose a documented coaching plan with the team member.
C. Escalate the sponsor's request to HR immediately as an ethics violation.
D. Give the team member harder tasks but document the rationale as professional development.

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

You are a Scrum Master on an agile capital-markets product. Sprint velocity has been declining for four sprints. The product owner is pressuring the team to "bring velocity back up." Team members have begun privately inflating story-point estimates so velocity numbers look healthier. What is the BEST action?

A. Allow the inflation since it satisfies the product owner and maintains team morale.
B. Confront the team about the inflation publicly and reset estimation rules.
C. Facilitate a retrospective on the velocity pressure, coach the PO on the planning-vs-performance distinction, and reset estimation norms with team agreement.
D. Replace story-point estimation with hours-based estimation.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are leading a hybrid platform project. A senior developer has been mentoring a junior developer for two months. The junior has improved noticeably. Now the senior tells you privately they no longer want to mentor because it is "slowing down their own delivery." The junior still needs support. What should you do FIRST?

A. Reassign the mentoring to another senior developer immediately.
B. Have a conversation with the senior developer to understand the workload pressure and explore options including reducing their delivery commitment to protect the mentoring.
C. Escalate to functional management to mandate continued mentoring.
D. End the mentoring and accept that the junior must learn independently.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

You are managing a team that has been performing well but is now showing signs of burnout: team members declining vacation, working evenings, and reporting reduced engagement in sprint reviews. The sponsor's milestone is in 5 weeks. Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Hold one-on-one conversations to assess individual workload and stress.
B. Push through the milestone and address burnout after.
C. Convene a focused retrospective on pace and workload patterns.
D. Mandate that all team members take vacation immediately.
E. Replace team members showing burnout with fresh staff.

---

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A team member's performance has been declining over three sprints. As Scrum Master, you want to address this responsibly. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Hold a joint conversation with the functional manager / HR if performance does not improve.
2. Hold a private one-on-one with the team member to understand the cause.
3. Document coaching attempts and performance pattern.
4. Coach the team member with specific, time-bound improvement actions.

A. 1 → 2 → 4 → 3
B. 2 → 4 → 3 → 1
C. 4 → 2 → 3 → 1
D. 3 → 2 → 4 → 1

---

**Q6 — EXCEPT Question (Predictive)**

Which of the following is NOT a typical input to creating a team charter?

A. Team member preferences for working hours and ceremonies
B. Organizational culture and norms
C. Project management plan
D. Stakeholder communication plan
E. Final cost performance index (CPI)

---

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are managing a team where one team member has consistently been the strongest performer for nine months. The team member's spouse has been ill for two weeks; output has dropped substantially. The sponsor is unaware and is reviewing performance trends. The sponsor asks you for honest assessment of team performance. What do you do?

A. Highlight the team member's drop in output to ensure accurate assessment.
B. Provide an honest aggregated team-performance picture, acknowledge the dip is recent and being addressed privately, and decline to share the team member's personal circumstance without their consent.
C. Hide the performance drop entirely to protect the team member.
D. Share the team member's personal circumstance to explain the drop.

---

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project with three team members co-located, four remote in another country, and two contractors in a third country. Ceremonies are held in person for the co-located group; remote and contractors join by video. Engagement of non-co-located members is dropping. They report feeling left out of side conversations and decision-making. What is the BEST approach?

A. Move all ceremonies to fully virtual format where every participant joins individually.
B. Continue current format and ask non-co-located members to be more vocal.
C. Send written summaries of ceremonies to non-co-located members.
D. Require all team members to be physically co-located for ceremonies.

---

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project. A functional manager has assigned 30% of one of your team members' time to a separate priority project without coordinating with you. This is the third time this has happened. The team member is now overcommitted; sprint commitments have slipped. You have already discussed this with the functional manager once. What is the BEST next step?

A. Continue to absorb the impact and adjust sprint commitments.
B. Brief the sponsor with the resource plan, the pattern of reallocation, the impact on commitments, and request governance-level resolution.
C. Refuse to release the team member regardless of functional-manager request.
D. Reduce team commitments silently to absorb the reallocation.

---

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

A project manager notices that team engagement and quality have been declining over several iterations. Defects in production have risen and team members appear disengaged in ceremonies. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Investigate root causes through one-on-ones and a focused retrospective.
B. Increase the number of ceremonies to reinforce team discipline.
C. Surface pace, scope, and psychological-safety conditions to the team and adjust agreements.
D. Replace team members showing the lowest output.
E. Renegotiate scope or timeline with the product owner and sponsor based on team-health data.
F. Add monetary bonuses tied to defect-free delivery.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Fairness, Respect, Responsibility) requires fair process before any adverse personnel action. The PM refuses the sponsor's request and proposes a documented coaching plan — this is professional pushback without escalation. Option A complies with an unethical instruction. Option C escalates to HR before engaging the sponsor on the principle. Option D dresses up coercion as development.

**Mindset Lesson:** PM pushback to a sponsor on fairness is the right move — refusal + alternative, not silence or escalation-first.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Velocity inflation under PO pressure is a system problem, not an individual integrity problem. The Scrum Master retrospects the pressure with the team, coaches the PO on the planning-vs-performance distinction, and resets estimation norms collaboratively. Option A normalizes dishonest metrics. Option B publicly shames the team. Option D changes the unit without addressing the cause.

**Mindset Lesson:** Metric gaming is a system signal — fix the pressure, not the metric.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team Performance Domain expects the PM to diagnose before reassigning. The senior's workload pressure may be addressable by reducing their delivery commitment to protect the mentoring. Option A reassigns without diagnosis. Option C coerces. Option D abandons a working development arrangement.

**Mindset Lesson:** Mentoring is a team asset — protect it by adjusting workload, not by reassigning the mentoring relationship.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** One-on-ones (A) surface individual workload and personal factors privately; focused retrospective (C) surfaces team-level pace and workload patterns. Together they cover individual + team levels of the same signal. Option B accepts burnout to hit a milestone. Option D mandates vacation without diagnosis. Option E punishes burned-out team members.

**Mindset Lesson:** Burnout signal needs individual + team diagnosis simultaneously — not push-through, not mandate, not replacement.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: B (2 → 4 → 3 → 1)**

**PMI Justification:** Private one-on-one to understand cause first (2). Coach with specific, time-bound improvement actions (4). Document the coaching and performance pattern (3). If improvement does not occur, joint conversation with functional manager / HR (1). Documenting before coaching is premature; escalating before coaching skips the diagnostic step.

**Mindset Lesson:** Performance management sequence: diagnose → coach → document → escalate jointly — never reverse the order.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: E**

**PMI Justification:** CPI is a project performance index, not a team charter input. Team charter inputs include team preferences, organizational culture, PM plan, and stakeholder context. Financial performance indicators are not relevant to working agreements.

**Mindset Lesson:** Team charter is about how the team works together — not about project financial state.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics requires honesty (accurate aggregated picture) AND respect for individual privacy (not disclosing personal circumstances without consent). The PM provides honest team-level data, acknowledges the dip is recent and being handled, and protects personal information. Option A risks unfair individual exposure. Option C is dishonest. Option D breaches the team member's privacy.

**Mindset Lesson:** Honesty does not require disclosing private circumstances — aggregate truthfully and protect the individual.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** Asymmetric participation (co-located + remote) creates structural disadvantage for remote members. Fully virtual format equalizes participation. Option B blames the disadvantaged participants. Option C accepts the asymmetry. Option D is impractical and ignores remote work realities.

**Mindset Lesson:** Equal-disadvantage ceremonies often outperform partial co-location for hybrid team engagement.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Resource and Stakeholder domains: resource conflicts that recur after PM-level engagement require sponsor-level governance. The PM briefs with data — resource plan, pattern, impact — and a recommendation. Option A absorbs structural reallocation. Option C refuses without governance. Option D hides the impact.

**Mindset Lesson:** Recurring resource reallocation is a governance issue — escalate with data, not as complaint.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:**
- **A:** Diagnose engagement and quality decline through one-on-ones + retrospective.
- **C:** Surface and adjust pace, scope, and safety conditions with the team.
- **E:** Renegotiate scope or timeline with PO/sponsor based on team-health data.
- B is wrong: more ceremonies do not fix engagement.
- D is wrong: replacement is punitive and disregards root cause.
- F is wrong: bonuses for defect-free delivery often produce hiding of defects.

**Mindset Lesson:** Team-health decline is diagnosed + adjusted + renegotiated — never solved by ceremony inflation, replacement, or risk-distorting bonuses.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**TEAM PERFORMANCE MANAGEMENT — Exam Morning Card**

- Team-level problems → team-level solutions (process, ceremonies, agreement). Individual problems → 1:1 conversations.
- Velocity is a planning tool. Never a performance metric. Gaming follows pressure.
- Burnout is a process problem first. Renegotiate scope/timeline; never push through.
- Psychological safety = PM responsibility. Silent teams are leadership failures.
- Recognition: fair, timely, contribution-based, often public. Audit by criteria, not visibility.
- Team charter and ground rules co-created. Imposition does not produce ownership.
- One-on-ones are non-negotiable during crunch — that is when issues should surface.
- Performance management sequence: diagnose → coach → document → escalate jointly with FM/HR.
- Virtual teams: deliberate connection design; equal-disadvantage ceremonies.
- Brooks' law: adding people to a late project makes it later in the short term.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *PMI escalates a team performance issue with documented diagnosis, coaching, and pattern — never as surprise, never as unilateral removal, never to absorb structural workload.*
