# PMP Exam Master Brief: Leadership
### Weak Spots Targeted: Escalation · Communication

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Leadership on the PMP exam is the deliberate practice of influencing people, building team capability, resolving conflict, and shaping culture so the team delivers value. It is distinct from management: management is about systems, processes, and authority; leadership is about influence, motivation, and behavior. PMI treats the PM (or Scrum Master / project lead) as the primary leader of the project — even when formal authority is limited. The People domain accounts for ~42% of the current exam, and leadership is the largest single thread inside it. Topics include leadership styles, emotional intelligence, motivation theories, conflict resolution, team development (Tuckman), negotiation, and servant leadership. In real projects, weak leadership produces compliance without commitment; on the exam, missing the difference between servant leadership and passive leadership, or between collaboration and compromise, kills your score.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about influencing people, developing teams, and resolving conflict — without relying on formal authority.

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Leadership Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Directive at start, increasingly delegative as team matures; formal authority more prominent; conflict often resolved through structured negotiation |
| **Agile** | Servant leadership default; team self-organizes; PM/Scrum Master removes impediments and protects the team; coaching > directing |
| **Hybrid** | Servant leadership at team level + directive leadership at program/governance level; situational style shift across audiences |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Leaders serve, not direct.** Servant leadership is PMI's default. The exam will choose the servant option almost every time, unless safety/ethics demand direction.
2. **Collaborate (problem-solve) is the highest-value conflict mode in almost every PMP scenario.** Real world uses compromise often; PMI rewards collaborate.
3. **Coaching is not directing.** A coach asks questions and develops the person; a director gives answers. PMI tests this distinction.
4. **EI is a skill, not a personality trait.** PMI expects you to actively practice self-awareness, empathy, and relationship management.
5. **Storming is healthy.** A team that never storms has not formed properly. PMI rejects "avoid conflict at all costs."

### Ethics Angle
- **Respect:** All team voices deserve to be heard; the loudest is not automatically the right one.
- **Honesty:** Leaders give candid feedback even when uncomfortable; sugar-coating is a Honesty failure.
- **Responsibility:** A leader's job is to develop the team — abdication ("they should figure it out") is irresponsibility, not empowerment.
- **Fairness:** Recognition and rewards must be distributed on contribution, not visibility or proximity to leadership.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Servant Leadership** | Leader serves the team by removing impediments, developing people, and protecting from distractions | Trap: confusing with passive leadership — servant leaders still hold accountability and give feedback |
| **Transformational Leadership** | Inspires through vision, intellectual stimulation, individualized consideration | Trap: assuming it is only about charisma; it is structured behavior |
| **Transactional Leadership** | Reward/punishment-based; performance-for-recognition | Trap: assuming it is always bad; appropriate for routine, repeatable work |
| **Situational Leadership** | Adapts style (directing → coaching → supporting → delegating) to follower readiness | Trap: assuming style is fixed; PMI expects you to adapt |
| **Charismatic Leadership** | Influence through personal magnetism and conviction | Trap: confusing with transformational; charismatic is more personality-driven |
| **Laissez-faire Leadership** | Hands-off; team makes all decisions | Trap: equating with servant leadership; laissez-faire is absence, servant is active service |
| **Autocratic / Directive** | Leader decides, team executes | Trap: assuming always wrong; appropriate in crisis or with novice teams |
| **Democratic / Participative** | Decisions made with team input | Trap: confusing with consensus; democratic uses input, consensus requires agreement |
| **Emotional Intelligence (EI)** | Self-awareness + self-management + social awareness + relationship management | Trap: assuming it is innate; PMI tests as a developable skill |
| **Tuckman Model** | Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning | Trap: assuming linear; teams can regress under stress |
| **Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Modes** | Collaborate, Compromise, Smooth/Accommodate, Force/Direct, Withdraw/Avoid | Trap: defaulting to Compromise; PMI prefers Collaborate where possible |
| **Maslow's Hierarchy** | Physiological → Safety → Belonging → Esteem → Self-actualization | Trap: assuming everyone is at the same level; check before assuming |
| **Herzberg Two-Factor** | Hygiene factors (prevent dissatisfaction) vs Motivators (produce satisfaction) | Trap: assuming better hygiene = motivation; only motivators motivate |
| **McGregor X / Y** | Theory X = people dislike work, need control; Theory Y = people want to contribute, need support | Trap: assuming Y is always right; context determines |
| **McClelland's Acquired Needs** | Achievement, Affiliation, Power | Trap: confusing with motivators; these are personal need patterns |
| **Vroom's Expectancy Theory** | Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence | Trap: ignoring any of three factors; all must be present for motivation |
| **Team Charter / Working Agreement** | Document of team norms, ceremonies, conflict resolution | Trap: assuming PM owns it; team co-creates |
| **Ground Rules** | Behavioral expectations agreed by team | Trap: PM imposing; ground rules must be team-owned to be effective |
| **Coaching vs Mentoring** | Coaching = developing skills/performance via questions; Mentoring = sharing experience for career growth | Trap: using interchangeably; PMI tests the distinction |
| **Negotiation** | Process to reach mutually acceptable agreement | Trap: confusing with conflict resolution; negotiation is broader |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Two team members are in **open conflict** over a technical decision | Facilitate a collaborate/problem-solve conversation; do not impose a decision | Collaborate produces a win-win and develops the team |
| Team is in **storming** phase and morale is dipping | Coach through it; reinforce ground rules; do not dissolve the team | Storming is necessary for high performance |
| A team member is **underperforming** | Hold a private coaching conversation to understand cause | Diagnose before discipline |
| Team is **forming** and waiting for direction | Provide more directive leadership; share vision and define roles | Situational leadership: new teams need direction |
| A team that was **performing** suddenly struggles | Investigate cause (membership change, scope change, stress); style shift may be needed | Tuckman regression is real; adapt style |
| Sponsor demands you **fire** a team member without prior coaching | Push back; coach first; document; escalate only with cause | Firing without coaching is unjust and weakens team trust |
| Team disagrees with a **PM decision** | Listen, explain reasoning, adjust if input warrants | Authority used badly destroys team trust |
| A **virtual / distributed team** is disengaging | Increase synchronous touchpoints, build relationships, check belonging | Virtual teams need deliberate connection design |
| Recognition is being awarded **based on visibility** not contribution | Audit recognition criteria; redistribute fairly | Fairness in recognition is a Code of Ethics requirement |
| Team member raises an **ethical concern** | Acknowledge openly, investigate, document, act within PMI Code | Suppressing the concern compounds the ethics breach |

**Two Escalation-Specific Scenarios (your weak spot):**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| A conflict persists after genuine collaboration attempts and impacts delivery | Escalate to the appropriate authority (sponsor, functional manager) with the issue, attempts made, and recommendation — never with surprise |
| A team member's behavior breaches the PMI Code of Ethics (harassment, discrimination, fraud) | Escalate immediately to HR or appropriate authority; coaching is not the right tool for ethics breaches |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

### Conflict Resolution Decision Tree (Thomas-Kilmann + PMI Lens)

```
CONFLICT IDENTIFIED
        │
        ▼
Time available + relationship value high?
    YES → COLLABORATE (problem-solve) — preferred PMI default
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Both parties willing to give something up + outcome shared?
    YES → COMPROMISE — both win partially
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Issue trivial / relationship preservation matters most right now?
    YES → SMOOTH / ACCOMMODATE — temporary
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Crisis / safety / ethics / authority required?
    YES → FORCE / DIRECT — accept the cost to relationship
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Cooling-off needed / not worth it / no resolution possible now?
    YES → WITHDRAW / AVOID — temporary, must return to the issue
```

### Tuckman Mnemonic: **F-S-N-P-A**

> **F**orming → **S**torming → **N**orming → **P**erforming → **A**djourning

Memory hook: **"Fresh Storms Need Patient Adjustment."**
- Forming: directive style; provide vision and roles.
- Storming: coach through conflict; reinforce ground rules.
- Norming: facilitate, less directive.
- Performing: delegate; servant-leadership mode.
- Adjourning: celebrate, capture lessons, transition.

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project
- Larger teams; formal hierarchy more present
- Directive leadership common in execution under deadline pressure
- Team charter and ground rules typically formalized in kickoff
- Conflict often surfaces through structured channels (HR, functional managers)

### Agile / Hybrid
- Servant leadership default; PM/Scrum Master removes impediments
- Team co-creates working agreement; ground rules emerge from retrospectives
- Conflict surfaces and resolves within ceremonies (retrospective especially)
- Coaching > directing as default mode; team self-organizes
- Hybrid: situational shift — servant at team level, directive at governance level when needed

### Industry / Regulatory Factors
- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** hierarchical culture is strong; servant leadership requires deliberate counter-cultural effort
- High-pressure regulatory deadlines (BSEC, Bangladesh Bank) tempt directive style — use it judiciously, do not default to it
- **Multinational / cross-cultural teams:** EI matters more; recognition norms differ by culture; check before assuming
- **Public-sector / state-owned:** formal authority deeply embedded; leadership style must navigate without bypassing

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Force the decision since the PM has authority" | Authority resolves conflict fastest | Forcing damages relationship and trust; appropriate only in crisis/safety/ethics scenarios |
| "Compromise so both parties get something" | Compromise feels fair | Compromise is lose-lose; PMI prefers collaborate where possible |
| "Avoid the conflict to keep the team focused" | Reduces friction | Withdrawal without return suppresses the issue; it resurfaces later |
| "Smooth over the disagreement to maintain morale" | Smoothing protects relationships | Smoothing is temporary; persistent smoothing hides real issues |
| "Fire the underperforming team member to protect the team" | Quick removal protects delivery | Coaching and documented improvement plan come first |
| "Direct the team in detail to ensure quality" | Directive leadership shows ownership | Over-directing breaks self-organization and limits team capability growth |
| "Allow the team to resolve all conflicts on their own without involvement" | Servant leaders stay hands-off | Servant ≠ absent; servant leaders facilitate when teams cannot resolve internally |
| "Recognize the most visible contributor publicly" | Visible work deserves visible reward | Recognition by visibility violates Fairness; audit actual contribution |
| "Have the team agreement created by the PM and shared with the team" | PM-authored agreement is consistent | Team agreements must be co-created; imposition breaks ownership |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You are the PM on a USD 7M predictive infrastructure project at a bank. A senior team member tells you privately that a peer has been claiming credit for the team member's work in steering committee meetings, and has done so for two months. The accused peer is the sponsor's personal favorite. The team member is considering leaving the project. What should you do FIRST?

A. Recommend the team member raise it directly with the sponsor.
B. Acknowledge the concern, conduct a confidential fact-finding conversation with the accused peer, and prepare a documented plan to redirect credit accurately going forward.
C. Escalate to HR immediately as an ethics breach.
D. Defer action until the next performance review cycle.

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

You are a Scrum Master on an agile platform team. During a sprint retrospective, two senior developers disagree publicly about the technical direction for the next epic. The disagreement turns personal. Other team members stop participating. What is the BEST action?

A. End the retrospective and reschedule it for the next day.
B. Pause the disagreement, name the dynamic in the room, and facilitate a problem-solve conversation focused on the technical merits.
C. Decide the technical direction yourself to restore order.
D. Ask the product owner to make the call.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

You are leading a hybrid capital-markets project. A team member from a vendor partner has consistently missed sprint commitments for three iterations. The vendor's account manager defends the team member. Your team is frustrated. The product owner wants the team member removed. What should you do FIRST?

A. Demand the vendor remove the team member immediately.
B. Meet with the team member to understand the cause of missed commitments before any action.
C. Escalate to the vendor's senior management.
D. Reassign the team member's tasks to other developers and tolerate the situation.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

You are a new PM on a large project team. The team has been together for nine months and was producing well under the prior PM. Since your arrival, productivity has dropped. The team avoids eye contact in meetings. Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Hold one-on-one conversations with each team member to understand their concerns.
B. Introduce new processes to assert your leadership.
C. Acknowledge the transition openly and invite feedback in a team meeting.
D. Replace key team members to send a signal.
E. Report the productivity drop to the sponsor immediately.

---

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A new agile team has just been formed for a digital-wallet product. The PM/Scrum Master wants to set the team up for high performance. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Facilitate the creation of the team working agreement and ground rules.
2. Hold one-on-one introductions and learn each team member's strengths and goals.
3. Run the first sprint planning and commit to a sprint goal.
4. Hold a team kickoff covering vision, principles, and roles.

A. 4 → 2 → 1 → 3
B. 2 → 4 → 1 → 3
C. 4 → 1 → 2 → 3
D. 1 → 4 → 2 → 3

**Q6 — EXCEPT Question (Predictive)**

Which of the following is NOT a stage of the Tuckman model of team development?

A. Forming
B. Storming
C. Negotiating
D. Performing
E. Adjourning

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You are leading a team where one team member is consistently the highest contributor by output. The sponsor wants to give this team member a public award. You know that two other team members have been doing equally critical but less visible work. Recognizing only the visible contributor would mislead the team and stakeholders about who delivered. What do you do?

A. Recommend the sponsor recognize the visible contributor since they have the strongest output.
B. Recommend the sponsor recognize the team as a whole and individually acknowledge the less-visible contributors with equally meaningful recognition.
C. Take no action and let the sponsor decide alone.
D. Object to all recognition until the contribution analysis is settled.

---

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

You are leading a hybrid project with a co-located core team and three remote team members across two time zones. Sprint reviews and retrospectives are held in person; remote members join by video. Engagement of remote members is dropping. They report feeling like "second-class team members." What is the BEST approach?

A. Require all team members including remote ones to be co-located for ceremonies.
B. Move ceremonies to a fully virtual format where everyone joins remotely so participation is equal.
C. Send detailed written summaries to remote members so they stay informed.
D. Keep the in-person format and ask the remote members to be more proactive.

---

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

You are managing a hybrid project. A team member has missed multiple sprint commitments. You have held two private coaching conversations. The team member acknowledges the gap but has not improved. The team is frustrated and morale is dropping. You have documentation of the coaching attempts. What is the BEST next step?

A. Continue private coaching and avoid escalation to protect the team member.
B. Brief the functional manager (or HR) with the coaching record, the impact on the team, and request joint action.
C. Remove the team member from the team immediately.
D. Publicly call out the team member in retrospective to apply peer pressure.

---

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

You are coaching a team that has been performing well for six months. Recently, a major scope change has caused team morale to drop and a few team members have privately expressed burnout. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Investigate the root causes of the morale drop and burnout indicators.
B. Continue the current pace since the team has been performing and the scope change is unavoidable.
C. Renegotiate the scope or timeline with the product owner and sponsor based on sustainable-pace data.
D. Add additional team members to absorb the new scope.
E. Hold a focused retrospective on the morale and pace issues with the full team.
F. Replace the team members showing burnout to restore team energy.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Respect, Fairness) requires the PM to act when contribution is misattributed. The first move is private fact-finding with the accused peer before any escalation — this respects both parties' dignity and the principle of "understand before you act." Option A pushes the affected person to take the political risk alone. Option C escalates to HR without investigation. Option D delays action on a documented concern.

**Mindset Lesson:** Recognition fairness is a leadership responsibility; investigate privately first, escalate only when fact-finding warrants it.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMBOK 7 Team Performance Domain and the Agile Practice Guide both expect the Scrum Master to name the dynamic and facilitate problem-solving, not deflect or override. Option A delays the dynamic to another day. Option C overrides self-organization. Option D pushes a leadership task to the PO.

**Mindset Lesson:** Facilitate the dynamic in the room — naming + redirecting is the servant-leadership move, not deferring or deciding.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Diagnose before discipline — PMBOK 7 Leadership Domain. Understanding why commitments are missing may reveal training gaps, scope clarity issues, or personal factors. Option A demands removal without diagnosis. Option C escalates to vendor senior leadership without engaging the person. Option D tolerates and reassigns without addressing the issue.

**Mindset Lesson:** Underperformance is diagnosed first — even with a vendor team member, even with PO pressure.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** A new PM walking into a performing team must learn the team before changing anything. One-on-ones (A) build relationship and surface concerns; an open team conversation acknowledging the transition (C) creates psychological safety. Option B asserts authority before earning it. Option D punishes the team for the transition. Option E externalizes a leadership problem.

**Mindset Lesson:** Transition leadership starts with listening — new PMs lose performing teams by asserting before understanding.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A (4 → 2 → 1 → 3)**

**PMI Justification:** Team kickoff establishes shared vision and principles (4). One-on-ones build personal relationships and surface strengths (2). Working agreement and ground rules are then co-created from a base of shared understanding (1). Sprint planning (3) happens last, against an actual team agreement. Reversing the order weakens commitment.

**Mindset Lesson:** Vision → personal relationships → team agreement → commitment — each step depends on the previous.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: C**

**PMI Justification:** Tuckman's stages are Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, Adjourning. "Negotiating" is not a Tuckman stage.

**Mindset Lesson:** Tuckman is a recurring recognition question — memorize the five stages exactly.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Fairness, Honesty) requires recognition to reflect actual contribution. Recognizing the team as a whole plus individually acknowledging the less-visible contributors maintains fairness and honesty without removing the original contributor's recognition. Option A misrepresents contribution. Option C abdicates. Option D blocks recognition while contribution analysis runs.

**Mindset Lesson:** Recognition fairness can be additive — protect the visible contributor and surface the less-visible ones.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Asymmetric ceremonies create class systems on hybrid teams. Moving to a fully virtual format equalizes participation. Option A is impractical and ignores remote roles. Option C accepts the asymmetry. Option D shifts blame to the disadvantaged participants.

**Mindset Lesson:** Equal participation in ceremonies often requires equal disadvantage — make everyone remote rather than partially co-located.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Two coaching attempts with no improvement and team-level impact crosses the threshold for joint action with the functional manager / HR. The PM brings the documentation and the impact. Option A continues a failed approach. Option C removes without joint governance. Option D is public shaming — an ethics breach.

**Mindset Lesson:** Escalation in people management is a partnership with the functional manager / HR, not a unilateral removal.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:**
- **A:** Diagnose before intervening — the morale signal needs cause analysis.
- **C:** Sustainable pace is a principle; renegotiation with PO and sponsor is the PM's tool when scope outpaces capacity.
- **E:** A focused retrospective gives the team voice in the response.
- B is wrong: continuing pace under burnout indicators violates sustainable pace.
- D is wrong: adding members mid-stream typically slows delivery (Brooks' law).
- F is wrong: removing burned-out members is punitive rather than restorative.

**Mindset Lesson:** Morale + burnout = diagnose, renegotiate, give the team voice — never pace through it or replace people.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**LEADERSHIP — Exam Morning Card**

- Servant leadership is default for agile/hybrid. Remove impediments, coach, develop. Not passive.
- Situational leadership: directive for forming/novice; coach for storming; support for norming; delegate for performing.
- Tuckman: Forming · Storming · Norming · Performing · Adjourning. Storming is healthy.
- Conflict modes: **Collaborate** > Compromise > Smooth > Force > Withdraw. Collaborate is PMI default.
- EI: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship management. Developable skill.
- Motivation: Maslow (hierarchy), Herzberg (hygiene vs motivators), McGregor (X/Y), McClelland (achievement/affiliation/power).
- Coaching = ask questions to develop. Mentoring = share experience for career.
- Recognition by contribution, never visibility. Fairness principle.
- Underperformance → diagnose privately → coach → document → escalate jointly with functional manager.
- Virtual teams: deliberate connection design; equal disadvantage > partial co-location.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *PMI escalates leadership issues with documented coaching attempts and joint action — never as surprise, never as unilateral removal, never as public shaming.*
