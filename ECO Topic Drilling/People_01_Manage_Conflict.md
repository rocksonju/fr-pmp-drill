# PMP Exam Master Brief: Manage Conflict (People · Task 1)

---

## 1. Topic Snapshot — Plain English

Managing conflict is the deliberate practice of surfacing, diagnosing, and resolving disagreement at the lowest effective level — between team members, between stakeholders, and between project and sponsor — without suppressing the conflict or escalating reflexively. PMI treats conflict as healthy when handled openly and corrosive when ignored. The People domain accounts for 42% of the exam; conflict-handling questions appear in roughly 20% of People-domain questions and bleed into stakeholder, leadership, and ethics scenarios. Topics include the Thomas-Kilmann conflict modes, conflict sources (resource, schedule, priority, technical, personality), de-escalation, and ethics-driven escalation thresholds. In real teams, conflict is avoided to preserve comfort; on the exam, PMI rejects avoidance as default and rewards collaborate/problem-solve.

**In one sentence:** This topic is about resolving conflict at the lowest level through collaboration and de-escalation, escalating only when authority or ethics demand it.

---

## 2. The PMI Mindset on This Topic

### Predictive vs Agile vs Hybrid Lens

| Lens | Conflict Management Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Predictive** | Documented escalation paths, HR involvement for personnel conflicts, formal negotiation for inter-team conflict |
| **Agile** | Retrospective is the standing conflict-surfacing mechanism; team agreement defines conflict-resolution rules; conflict resolved in the room |
| **Hybrid** | Team-level conflict handled agile-style; cross-team or governance conflict via predictive escalation paths |

### Top 5 PMI-isms on This Topic

1. **Collaborate / problem-solve is the highest-value mode in almost every scenario.** Real world uses Compromise; PMI rewards Collaborate.
2. **Storming is healthy.** Conflict-free teams have not formed properly.
3. **Escalate only when authority or ethics demand it.** Reflexive escalation is abdication.
4. **Diagnose source before choosing mode.** Resource conflict and personality conflict need different approaches.
5. **Address the conflict, not the person.** Behavior is debatable; identity is not.

### Ethics Angle

- **Respect:** Honest engagement with the dissenting view, not dismissal.
- **Fairness:** Apply the same standard to powerful and junior stakeholders.
- **Honesty:** Name the conflict — do not paper over it.
- **Responsibility:** Surface conflict early; suppression compounds blast radius.

## 4. Key Terms, Formulas & Acronyms

| Term / Formula | Definition / Calculation | Exam Trap to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Thomas-Kilmann Modes** | Collaborate, Compromise, Smooth/Accommodate, Force/Direct, Withdraw/Avoid | Trap: defaulting to Compromise; PMI prefers Collaborate |
| **Collaborate / Problem-Solve** | Win-win; integrates concerns; requires time and trust | Trap: confusing with Compromise (lose-lose) |
| **Compromise** | Both parties give something; mid-value | Trap: thinking it is the best general mode |
| **Smooth / Accommodate** | Emphasize agreement; concede ground; temporary | Trap: relying on smoothing to suppress issue |
| **Force / Direct** | One party imposes; fast; damages relationship | Trap: confusing with leadership; only crisis/safety/ethics |
| **Withdraw / Avoid** | Step away from conflict; temporary | Trap: avoidance becoming permanent suppression |
| **Conflict Sources** | Schedule, priorities, resources, technical, admin, cost, personality | Trap: assuming personality is most common; resources/schedule dominate |
| **Storming** | Tuckman stage where conflict surfaces | Trap: trying to skip; storming is necessary |
| **De-escalation** | Lower emotional intensity to enable rational dialogue | Trap: jumping to solutions while emotion is high |
| **Active Listening** | Paraphrase and confirm understanding | Trap: assuming listening = waiting to speak |
| **BATNA** | Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement | Trap: ignoring BATNA; it determines walk-away power |

## 5. PM Behavior Cheat Sheet — FIRST / NEXT / BEST / EXCEPT

| Stem Signal | PM Should FIRST Do | Because |
|---|---|---|
| Two team members in **open conflict** | Facilitate collaborate/problem-solve in the room | Collaborate produces win-win |
| **Personality conflict** between key members | Hold private one-on-ones to diagnose; reconvene jointly | Personality conflict often masks deeper issue |
| Resource conflict between teams | Engage functional managers; renegotiate calendar | Resource conflict is structural |
| Two stakeholders with **conflicting requirements** | Facilitate joint session; quantify trade-offs | Picking a side is premature |
| Team avoids conflict; **silent** in ceremonies | Investigate psychological safety; coach team agreement | Silence is suppression |
| Conflict escalates because **PM was bypassed** | Re-engage; restore communication path; understand the bypass | Bypass signals communication failure |
| Conflict involves an **ethics breach** | Escalate to HR / appropriate authority immediately | Coaching is wrong tool for ethics |
| **Crisis** requires immediate action | Force / Direct is appropriate; accept relationship cost | Crisis ≠ general operating mode |
| Both parties **withdraw** from a real issue | Bring back; withdrawal is temporary, not resolution | Issue resurfaces if avoided |
| Sponsor is the **source** of conflict | Engage sponsor first; escalate to governance only if unresolved | Going around sponsor breaks trust |

**Escalation-Specific Scenarios:**

| Stem Signal | Correct Escalation Logic |
|---|---|
| Conflict persists after genuine PM-level resolution attempts | Escalate to appropriate authority (sponsor, FM, HR) with the issue, attempts, and recommendation |
| Conflict involves Code of Ethics breach (harassment, fraud, discrimination) | Escalate immediately to HR; do not coach an ethics breach |

## 6. Visual Memory Aid

### Conflict Resolution Decision Tree

```text
CONFLICT IDENTIFIED
        │
        ▼
Is it an ethics breach?
    YES → Escalate immediately to HR / appropriate authority
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Time available + relationship matters?
    YES → COLLABORATE — preferred PMI default
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Both give something + outcome shared?
    YES → COMPROMISE
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Issue minor / relationship priority?
    YES → SMOOTH / ACCOMMODATE — temporary
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Crisis / safety required?
    YES → FORCE / DIRECT — accept cost
    NO  ↓
        ▼
Cooling-off needed?
    YES → WITHDRAW — temporary, must return
```

### Conflict Mode Mnemonic: **C-C-S-F-W**

> **C**ollaborate (win-win) → **C**ompromise (lose-lose) → **S**mooth (temporary) → **F**orce (crisis) → **W**ithdraw (temporary)

Memory hook: **"Choose Collaborate, settle for Compromise, smooth then storm, withdraw last."**

## 7. Tailoring Considerations

### Predictive Project

- Documented escalation paths in governance
- HR involvement for personnel conflicts
- Inter-team conflict resolved through formal negotiation

### Agile / Hybrid

- Team agreement defines conflict-resolution rules
- Retrospective is the standing conflict-surfacing forum
- Scrum Master facilitates; team owns resolution

### Industry / Regulatory Factors

- **Banking / Capital Markets (your context):** hierarchical culture suppresses junior voice; build psychological safety deliberately
- BSEC / Bangladesh Bank conflicts often go straight to senior management — proactively engage to retain PM channel
- **Cross-cultural teams:** conflict-style varies (direct vs indirect cultures); calibrate before facilitating
- **Vendor relationships:** conflict resolved through contract escalation clauses, not personal escalation

## 8. Common Wrong Answers & Why PMI Rejects Them

| Tempting Answer | Why Experienced PMs Pick It | Why PMI Marks It Wrong |
|---|---|---|
| "Compromise so both get something" | Fair and fast | Compromise is lose-lose; Collaborate is win-win |
| "Force the decision since PM has authority" | Fast resolution | Force damages relationship; appropriate only in crisis |
| "Avoid the conflict to keep team focused" | Reduces friction | Avoidance suppresses; issue resurfaces |
| "Escalate to sponsor immediately" | Sponsors solve hard problems | Reflexive escalation is abdication |
| "Smooth over the disagreement" | Protects relationships | Temporary; persistent smoothing hides real issue |
| "Separate the conflicting members onto different work" | Reduces friction | Treats symptom; structural conflict remains |
| "Document and move on" | Risk management | Documenting ≠ resolving |

## 9. 10 Hardest Scenario-Based Questions

---

**Q1 — Ethics Dilemma (Predictive)**

You manage a USD 6M banking compliance project. A senior team member privately tells you another team member has been making demeaning comments about a junior team member's accent during ad-hoc calls. The accused is the sponsor's nephew. The junior team member has not complained but appears withdrawn. What should you do FIRST?

A. Ignore the report since the junior has not complained.
B. Document the report, hold a private fact-finding conversation with both parties, and escalate to HR if confirmed regardless of family relationship.
C. Speak to the accused privately and ask them to stop.
D. Move the junior to a different team to reduce friction.

---

**Q2 — Agile Ceremony / Engagement**

A retrospective surfaces that two senior developers have been escalating disagreements directly to the sponsor instead of resolving them in the team. The Scrum Master was unaware. What is the BEST action?

A. Reprimand the developers in the next standup.
B. Facilitate a conversation in the next retrospective on the team agreement, restore the impediment-resolution path, and engage the sponsor on the bypass with the developers present.
C. Escalate the bypass to the sponsor privately.
D. Allow direct sponsor escalation since it has been working.

---

**Q3 — FIRST Action, Conflict (Hybrid)**

Two business stakeholders dispute the priority of a new compliance feature. One wants it now; the other wants it deferred. Both report to the same VP, who is your sponsor. What should you do FIRST?

A. Decide based on regulatory risk.
B. Facilitate a joint session with both stakeholders to quantify the trade-off and reach a recommendation.
C. Escalate to the VP/sponsor immediately.
D. Implement both in parallel.

---

**Q4 — Two Correct, Pick Best (Predictive)**

A team has experienced ongoing conflict between two senior members for three sprints. Productivity has dropped. Coaching attempts have not resolved the issue. Which TWO actions should you take FIRST?

A. Hold a structured collaborate session with both members and a neutral facilitator.
B. Reassign one member to a different project.
C. Engage with each member privately to understand underlying concerns.
D. Escalate to the functional manager without further attempt.
E. Document the conflict and continue.

**Q5 — Ordered Sequence (Agile)**

A team conflict has just been raised by a developer in retrospective. Arrange the following actions in the correct order:

1. Update the team agreement to prevent recurrence if a pattern.
2. Facilitate de-escalation: name the dynamic, lower emotion, confirm understanding.
3. Identify the source of conflict (schedule, priority, technical, personality).
4. Choose conflict mode (collaborate, compromise, etc.) based on source.

A. 2 → 3 → 4 → 1
B. 3 → 4 → 2 → 1
C. 4 → 3 → 2 → 1
D. 1 → 2 → 3 → 4

**Q7 — Ethics / Honesty Hardline**

You witness your sponsor publicly humiliate a team member during a steering meeting over a minor mistake. The team member is visibly distressed. The sponsor moves on. What do you do?

A. Take no action since the sponsor has authority.
B. Privately check on the team member, then meet with the sponsor to raise the impact and request behavior change going forward.
C. Publicly defend the team member in the same meeting.
D. File an HR complaint immediately.

**Q8 — Communication Strategy (Hybrid)**

A team and a vendor are in repeated conflict over interface specifications. Conflict surfaces every sprint review. Both sides have written documentation that supports their position. What is the BEST approach?

A. Hold a joint working session to create a single shared interface specification owned by both parties.
B. Escalate the dispute to procurement.
C. Have the PM unilaterally pick the better specification.
D. Continue tolerating the conflict.

**Q9 — Escalation Judgment (Hybrid)**

A conflict between two senior business unit leaders has persisted through two facilitated sessions you led. Both report to your sponsor. One leader has now threatened to escalate to the sponsor directly. What is the BEST next step?

A. Allow the leader to escalate; let the sponsor handle direct reports.
B. Proactively brief the sponsor with the conflict, your facilitation attempts, the impact, and request their involvement.

**Q10 — Multiple Response (Agile/Hybrid)**

A team has been highly productive but conflict has emerged after a recent scope change. Two members are openly disagreeing on direction; others are withdrawing. Which THREE actions are MOST appropriate? (Select three.)

A. Investigate the root cause of the recent shift in team dynamic.

C. Facilitate a structured collaborate session focused on the technical merits of the disagreement.
D. Separate the two openly disagreeing members onto different work.
E. Address the withdrawal pattern by re-engaging individually with affected members.

## 10. Answer Key with Full Justification

---

**Q1 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** PMI Code of Ethics (Respect, Fairness) requires investigation regardless of family relationship. Private fact-finding precedes HR escalation; confirmation triggers HR. Option A ignores a credible report. Option C handles informally without documentation. Option D punishes the affected party.

**Mindset Lesson:** Reports of disrespect get investigation, not informal correction or relocation of the affected party.

---

**Q2 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Retrospective is the right forum to address bypass. Restoring the impediment-resolution path requires team-agreement adjustment + sponsor engagement with developers present. Option A is public shaming. Option C escalates privately without involving developers. Option D normalizes bypass.

**Mindset Lesson:** Bypass patterns are restored openly with all parties — not privately, not through compliance with the bypass.

---

**Q3 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Joint session quantifies trade-off and produces a recommendation. Option A decides without dialogue. Option C escalates without analysis. Option D may exceed capacity.

**Mindset Lesson:** Conflicting priorities are reconciled through facilitated trade-off analysis — not picked by gut, not bounced to sponsor.

---

**Q4 — Correct Answers: A and C**

**PMI Justification:** Private 1:1 diagnosis (C) precedes joint facilitation (A) — together they cover diagnosis and structured resolution. Option B treats symptom. Option D escalates without further attempt. Option E documents without resolving.

**Mindset Lesson:** Persistent conflict resolution is private diagnosis + structured joint session — not reassignment, not silent escalation.

---

**Q5 — Correct Answer: A (2 → 3 → 4 → 1)**

**PMI Justification:** De-escalate first (2) to enable rational dialogue. Identify source (3) to choose mode. Choose mode (4). Update team agreement (1) if pattern. Reversing order applies mode to wrong source.

**Mindset Lesson:** De-escalate → diagnose → mode → agreement update — sequence matters.

---

**Q6 — Correct Answer: E**

**PMI Justification:** The five Thomas-Kilmann modes are Collaborate, Compromise, Smooth/Accommodate, Force/Direct, Withdraw/Avoid. Negotiate is a broader skill, not a Thomas-Kilmann mode.

**Mindset Lesson:** Memorize the five modes exactly — Negotiate is a common distractor.

---

**Q7 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Respect and care for the affected member first; engage sponsor on the behavior privately. Option A condones. Option C creates a second public scene. Option D bypasses sponsor engagement.

**Mindset Lesson:** Sponsor misbehavior is addressed privately with care for the affected — care first, behavior conversation second.

---

**Q8 — Correct Answer: A**

**PMI Justification:** Joint working session creates a shared specification owned by both parties — addresses the structural cause. Option B escalates without resolution attempt. Option C imposes. Option D tolerates.

**Mindset Lesson:** Interface conflicts are solved by joint ownership of the artifact — not by escalation or imposition.

---

**Q9 — Correct Answer: B**

**PMI Justification:** Two facilitation sessions without resolution warrants proactive sponsor engagement with documentation. Option A is reactive. Option C bypasses correct authority. Option D adds an attempt without new input.

**Mindset Lesson:** Escalation after exhausted PM-level attempts is proactive briefing with attempts and recommendation.

---

**Q10 — Correct Answers: A, C, E**

**PMI Justification:**

- **A:** Diagnose the dynamic shift.
- **C:** Structured collaborate on technical merits.
- **E:** Address withdrawal individually.
- B is wrong: passive observation lets conflict harden.
- D is wrong: separation treats symptom.
- F is wrong: routing all disagreement through PM breaks self-organization.

**Mindset Lesson:** Team conflict response = diagnose + structured facilitation + individual re-engagement — never passive, never separation, never PM-routed.

## 12. 60-Second Recap Card

**MANAGE CONFLICT — Exam Morning Card**

- Thomas-Kilmann: Collaborate · Compromise · Smooth · Force · Withdraw.
- Collaborate is PMI default. Compromise is lose-lose.
- Storming is healthy. Conflict-free team has not formed.
- Diagnose source (schedule, priority, resource, technical, personality) before choosing mode.
- De-escalate emotion before applying logic.
- Force only in crisis / safety / ethics.
- Withdraw is temporary — must return to the issue.
- Ethics breach → escalate to HR immediately. Not coaching territory.
- Persistent conflict → private 1:1 diagnosis + structured joint session.
- Personality conflict often masks deeper structural issue.

**Escalation one-liner to burn in:** *PMI escalates conflict after exhausted facilitation, with documentation and recommendation — never reflexively, never to bypass the sponsor, never to suppress an ethics breach.*
