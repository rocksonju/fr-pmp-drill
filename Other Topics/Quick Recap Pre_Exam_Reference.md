# PMP Pre-Exam Study Reference

> A topic-segmented, exam-mindset reference covering the three PMP domains — **People**, **Process**, and **Business Environment** — plus a keyword decision map, conflict/power reference, and miscellaneous definitions.

---

## Quick Reference — Keyword → Answer Logic

When the exam scenario contains a trigger word or pattern, this is the answer direction PMI usually rewards.

| When you see… | The expected answer direction is… |
| --- | --- |
| **Value** | Show benefits / benefits plan |
| **Quick wins** | Look for options that provide quick wins (especially in Agile / showing value) |
| **Uncertainty or risk around requirements** | Hybrid or iterative development approach is best |
| **Transitioning from predictive to hybrid** | Deliver iterations to show early benefits to leadership |
| **Missing feature** | Work with the Product Owner to resolve |
| **Issue with a deliverable** | Work with the whole team to resolve |
| **Product not meeting scope requirements** | Initiate change control |
| **Virtual team issues** | Answer is usually about communication |
| **Dependency on multiple departments** | Meet with the functional managers |
| **Adding new resources to projects** | Integrate skills development activities into the execution phase |
| **Risks** | Identify → Quantify → Response strategy and/or schedule risk audit |
| **Costs (identified risk)** | Use **contingency reserve** |
| **Costs (unidentified risk)** | Use **management reserve** |
| **New PM taking over a project** | Review history and status with the sponsor |
| **Switching from Kanban to story points** | **FIRST** convert all work in progress to story points |
| **Uninformed stakeholders** | Review the communications plan |
| **New stakeholder or new information** | Update the stakeholder register / engagement plan |
| **Requirements** | Used to measure **completeness** |
| **Quality Control** | Checks the **correctness** |
| **Checklist** | Ensures the work is done the same way each time |
| **Check sheets (tally sheets)** | Organise data about a quality issue |
| **Process and criteria to test** | Not quality — most likely a flowchart / process-defining option |
| **SPI and CPI** | Above **1** is good |
| **CV and SV** | Above **0** is good |
| **"Do first"** | Usually **review a document** |
| **Team member problems** | Team Charter |
| **Missed scope** | Change request |
| **Parametric value** | A parameter exists for estimating; used for repetitive work |
| **Disengaged stakeholders** | Review the stakeholder engagement plan |
| **"A better understanding…"** | Often correct — builds a shared vision and enhances teamwork |

## Exam Mindset (Cross-Cutting)

- **Always look for the root cause before acting** — think before actioning.
- **Be direct and honest with stakeholders.**
- **Collaboration and negotiation** are recurring winning themes.
- **Do not hand off your problems** — the PM works to find solutions, not escalate away from them.
- **Compliance always takes priority** — assess impact *first*, then look at options and communications.
- **Interactive, face-to-face communication** is almost always preferred (unless geography makes it unrealistic).
- **Emotional Intelligence (EI)** is a mindset to apply across *all* answers, especially in the People section.
- **Eat your dessert first** — deliver the most valuable items / MVP early.
- **Work the highest-risk items first.**

# Domain 1 — People

## Task 1 — Manage Conflict

**Conflict resolution methods** (look for these, especially in People questions):

- Withdraw / Avoid
- Smooth / Accommodate
- Compromise / Reconcile
- Force / Direct
- **Collaborate / Problem-solve** ← usually the favoured response

**Common conflict types and preferred handling:**

| Conflict | Preferred resolution |
| --- | --- |
| **Between team members** | Team members should resolve it themselves (especially in Agile); PM encourages collaboration |
| **Between client and project team** | Balance team concerns with client concerns — negotiation and problem-solving |
| **Between stakeholders** | Collaboration, always aligning to project goals |
| **Between sponsor and a team member** | Considering power dynamic and urgency, PM may direct the team member to adopt an accommodating response |
| **With Functional Managers** | Often over shared resources — check the **Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM)** and work *with* the FM toward resolution |

> **Exam tip:** Collaboration and negotiation are popular winning themes.

## Task 2 — Lead a Team

- **Management** is about getting things done; **Leadership** is about getting people to do what needs to be done.
- The PM should always be a **Servant Leader** — focused on the needs of the project teams.
- **Team success is more important than individual success.**
- Allow team members to take charge; motivate them; show recognition of individuals and teams.
- **Avoid zero-sum rewards** (e.g., only one person can win — "employee of the month").

**Servant Leader / Scrum Master core principles:**

- **Focus on team needs** — foster growth and development.
- **Empowerment and collaboration** — delegate, provide resources, remove blockers/impediments.
- **Act as coach and mentor** — help the team learn, grow, and take ownership.

**Roles of a Project Manager:** motivate, align and direct people; communicate (key); guide decisions; negotiate internally and externally; problem-solve; be honest, direct, and approachable; apply EI.

### Types of Power

| Power | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **Positional** | Authority from your formal role; can force a viewpoint |
| **Informational** | Influence from access to / control over valuable information |
| **Referent** | Influence from admiration, respect, and trust (often from prior work together) |
| **Situational** | Power granted by circumstances (e.g., leadership/team change) |
| **Personal / Charismatic** | Warm personality others like |
| **Reward** | Team pleases the PM to receive rewards |
| **Ingratiating** | Favour gained through flattery — *false power* |
| **Pressure-based** | Restricts choices via deadlines/consequences |
| **Guilt-based** | Puts people on a guilt trip |
| **Persuasive** | Influence via compelling arguments, logic, appealing to needs |
| **Avoiding** | Refuses to act or decide |

### Matrix Structures (PM authority level)

- **Weak matrix:** Team blends departmental + project duties. PM has **little authority**; functional manager usually owns the budget.
- **Balanced matrix:** PM has **low-to-moderate** authority.
- **Strong matrix:** PM has **moderate-to-high** power (often part-time teams).
- **Project-oriented:** PM has **high power and autonomy** (usually a full-time project team).

### Organisational Structure Types

- **Organic / Simple:** People wear many hats; PM has little to no authority over resources.
- **Functional (Centralised):** Clear business divisions (Sales, Marketing, Legal); PM has little autonomy; functional manager owns the budget.
- **Multidivisional:** Same functions duplicated per business unit; functional manager owns the budget.

### PMO Types

- **Supporting:** Coach/advisory role; provides templates, training, information; doesn't control.
- **Controlling:** Defines governance — compliance, frameworks, specific forms/templates.
- **Directive:** PMO **owns** the project.

> Your PMO type changes how you manage the project.

### Project & Strategic Planning Roles

- **Executives** want to know **why** the project should be approved and how it aligns with vision, values, and goals.
- **Functional Managers (middle management)** want the **mechanics** — how it supports strategy and achieves the executive vision.
- **Operations Managers** want to know **how** to implement the project to deliver the *what* and *why* from above.

## Task 3 — Support Team Performance

- Complete **team assessments:** measure team velocity, use a task matrix for individuals, create individual goals, compare goals achieved per iteration; encourage **team self-assessment**.
- Use **active listening** — be present, summarise what the speaker said.
- Provide **feedback as often as possible** — acknowledge good work, correct unsatisfactory work.
- **New members struggling:** scheduled training per the Resource Management Plan; unplanned training (e.g., job shadowing) may follow performance appraisals.
- **Team member leaves (Agile):** remove items from the current Sprint with Product Owner approval so the team focuses on the most critical, valuable tasks.

> **Exam tip:** You can set up a benefits or reward system to drive performance/motivation.

## Task 4 — Empower Team Members and Stakeholders

In Agile, the team **self-organises** tasks rather than the PM prioritising/scheduling them.

**Watch for these answer keywords:** *Empower…*, *Collaboration…*, *Self-organise…*, *Negotiate…*

### Tuckman's Team Ladder

- **Forming:** team first comes together.
- **Storming:** members position for leader/follower roles — possible hostility as ideas are challenged.
- **Norming:** people accept roles and begin leading/following.
- **Performing:** team is cohesive and performing well.
- **Adjourning:** team disbands.

## Task 5 — Ensure Teams / Stakeholders Are Adequately Trained

- **Adding new resources:** integrate skills development activities into the execution phase.
- **Prepare training:** assess team members' preferred learning methods — different members need different training.
- **Knowledge sharing when a member leaves:** ensure a **knowledge transfer document** is created.
- **Multinational project:** be culturally sensitive and aware; cultural sensitivity training helps.

**Understand stakeholder roles** — know each one's contribution and ensure they have the right knowledge/training:

- **Interests:** affected by the project
- **Rights:** legal or moral rights
- **Ownership:** own an asset or equipment
- **Knowledge:** knowledge that can help
- **Contribution:** funds, resources, support

## Task 6 — Build a Team

- When getting a new resource, **integrate skills development activities** into the project.
- A **resource histogram** helps define resource requirements over time.

**PM interpersonal & team skills:** active listening, emotional intelligence, cultural/social awareness, leadership, networking, political/organisational awareness.

**Estimating-duration considerations:**

- **Law of diminishing returns** — more resources eventually yield diminishing returns.
- **Number of resources** — adding resources doesn't necessarily reduce duration.
- **Advances in technology** — faster equipment / learning curve.
- **Motivation of staff** — student syndrome / Parkinson's law.

## Task 7 — Address & Remove Impediments, Obstacles, Blockers

- In Agile, make work visible using a **Kanban board** to help take action and remove impediments.
- For poorly documented work or a new team, use **Agile sprints and Kanban** to accelerate learning and fault-finding; teams share knowledge to remove impediments.

## Task 8 — Negotiate Project Agreements

**Renegotiating prices with suppliers mid-project:**

1. **First, review the contract.**
2. Work *with* the supplier to agree new prices, minimising cost impact and avoiding delivery delays.
3. Maintain a good relationship with suppliers responsible for critical components.

## Task 9 — Collaborate with Stakeholders

> **Exam tip:** For disengagement, misalignment, or disputes, **review the stakeholder engagement plan** to determine strategies to improve communication.

- The **stakeholder management plan** identifies strategies/actions to promote productive involvement; an **assessment matrix** provides ready reference.
- **Always be direct and honest with stakeholders.**

## Task 10 — Build Shared Understanding

**Always look for the root cause — think before actioning.**

- **Root Cause Analysis**
- **Five Whys** — ask "why?" five times to reach the root cause.
- **Fishbone / Ishikawa diagram** — diagrams the causes of a problem.
- **Pareto chart** — identifies the most important causes.
- **SWOT analysis** — understand problems and gaps before acting.

**Low team morale:** first run a **satisfaction survey** (or similar) to gather data and identify the root cause *before* addressing it.

## Task 11 — Engage & Support Virtual Teams

**Virtual teams (not collocated):**

- **Advantages:** remote expertise; include people with disabilities.
- **Disadvantages:** communication, isolation, gaps in knowledge sharing, no face-to-face.

**Best practice:** work *with* virtual members to determine the most appropriate communications for their location/participation; let them self-organise and evolve roles; support trust and collaboration.

> **Exam tip:** For virtual-team issues/disputes, choose the answer about **improving communication**.

## Task 12 — Define Team Ground Rules

- Ground rules live in the **Project Team Charter** — review it for rules or disputes.
- Self-organised teams should all participate in creating and abiding by agreed rules.

**Team Charter elements:** vision statement, team rules, code of conduct, communication rules, definition of done / success factors, success criteria.

> **Exam tip:** For team-related issues, look for options that mention the **Team Charter**.

## Task 13 — Mentor Relevant Stakeholders

- Always educate stakeholders on the **benefits of the methodology** you're using.
- **Agile:** if stakeholders are unfamiliar/concerned, facilitate a meeting with the **Product Owner** to explain the Backlog and align on deliverables.
- **Expectations:** when unrealistic, gather proof to present and set correct expectations.

> Product Owners and Functional Managers can also be stakeholders that can be mentored.

## Task 14 — Promote Performance Through Emotional Intelligence

- **90% of project management is communication.**
- Understand emotion and how it drives behaviour; control your own responses; influence others' emotions.
- **Inbound:** control your own emotions (self-awareness). **Outbound:** relationship management — don't react emotionally.
- Reduces staff turnover; emotionally competent teams are more effective.

**Quadrants of EI:** self-management, self-awareness, social skills, social awareness.

> **Exam tip:** EI is a *mindset* to apply across all answers, especially in People questions.

### Performing Stakeholder Analysis (cross-cutting)

- **Stakeholder mapping** — diagram of relationships.
- **Stakeholder analysis matrix** — table of needs, interests, expectations.
- **Stakeholder engagement plan** — plan for managing relationships.
- **Power / Interest Grid** — prioritise stakeholders.

# Domain 2 — Process

## Task 1 — Execute Project with Urgency for Business Value

- **Benefits Management Plan** captures project outcomes that provide value (goodwill, reputation, brand recognition, public benefit). Track progress against it.
- Measure **actual vs predicted** performance in business documents and the benefits plan.
- In Agile, **deliver value incrementally** — most valuable items first.

> **Exam tip:** **MVP** and prioritising the most valuable items early are common trends.

- **Minimum Viable Product (MVP):** the most basic functional version with just the core features to be usable and deliver value.

## Task 2 — Manage Communications

**Communication model:** Sender → Encoder → Medium → Decoder → Receiver, with **Noise**, **Barriers**, **Acknowledgements** (received ≠ agreed), and **Feedback/Response**.

| Type | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| **Push** | Sent to specific recipients; doesn't ensure reception/understanding |
| **Pull** | For large, complex info sets; recipient accesses content (web portal, intranet, lessons-learned DB, knowledge repos) |
| **Public** | Single speaker to a group |
| **Interpersonal** | Face-to-face |
| **Small group** | 3–6 people |

> **Exam tip:** Know push vs pull pros/cons. **Interactive face-to-face is almost always preferred** unless geography makes it unrealistic.

## Task 3 — Assess & Manage Risks

**Risk response order:** Identify → Quantify (qualitative/quantitative) → Implement response strategy and/or schedule risk audit → Control/track/monitor.

- **Risk = uncertainty** — effect can be good or bad.
- In Agile, do risk analysis **before each iteration**.
- **Pure risk:** no upside (e.g., injury during project work).

**Qualitative vs Quantitative:**

- **Qualitative** — *qualify* the risk (probability & impact); Probability-Impact Matrix with Low/Moderate/High.
- **Quantitative** — *quantify* the financial impact; **Risk Exposure = Probability × Impact.**

**Secondary effects:**

- **Residual risk** — smaller risks remaining after a response that you live with.
- **Secondary risk** — a new risk caused *by* your response (domino effect).

**Results of implementing responses:** updates to Issue Log, Lessons Learned, project team assignments, Risk Register, and Risk Report.

> **Exam tip:** Prioritise risky requirements **early** — work high-risk items first.

## Task 4 — Engage Stakeholders

> **Exam tip:** Identification, prioritisation, and engagement should be reviewed **regularly**. New stakeholders → **update the stakeholder management plan**.

- For stakeholders with opposing expectations, **FIRST review influence and interest** (Influence/Interest Grid), then align needs with objectives.
- **Power/Interest Grid:** High influence + High interest = **Manage Closely**; High influence + Low interest = **Keep Satisfied**; Low influence + High interest = **Keep Informed**; Low + Low = **Monitor**.

**Stakeholder identification (initiating):** use the **Stakeholder Register**.

1. Identify interests, influence, contribution.
2. Prioritise by power, influence, impact.
3. Anticipate responses and plan accordingly.

**Engagement Matrix groups:** Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Leading (move them toward Supportive/Leading).

## Task 5 — Plan & Manage Budget and Resources

> **Exam tip:** For a new project with no experience/data, use **Triangular (Three-Point)** estimation.

**Three baselines** (changes only via change control):

- **Scope baseline** — Scope Statement, WBS, WBS Dictionary
- **Schedule baseline** — schedule model, flow of work
- **Cost baseline** — time-phased budget

**Estimation techniques:**

| Technique | Use / Formula |
| --- | --- |
| **Analogous** | Similar past projects as a basis; low cost, less reliable |
| **Parametric** | A parameter exists; repetitive work |
| **Three-Point (Triangular)** | `(O + ML + P) / 3` |
| **PERT** | Weighted to most likely: `(O + 4ML + P) / 6` |
| **Bottom-up** | Longest but most reliable; start at work packages |
| **Monte Carlo (simulation)** | Repeated simulation with random values; S-curve output; can support qualitative risk analysis |
| **Affinity** | Group items into similar categories (popular in Agile) |

**Managing resources:**

- **Crashing** — add cost/resources to reduce schedule.
- **Fast Tracking** — overlap phases/tasks in parallel; introduces risk.
- **Resource Levelling** — keep demand within supply by delaying/advancing activities.
- **Resource Smoothing** — move activities within existing float to avoid demand peaks.

> **Exam tip:** Expect "which estimation technique?" scenarios; the one calculation was usually **PERT**.

## Task 6 — Plan & Manage Schedule

**Measuring progress:**

- **Work Performance Data** — raw facts (costs, change requests, defects, durations).
- **Work Performance Information** — analysed data (ahead/behind schedule).
- **Work Performance Report** — packaged for stakeholders (dashboards, status reports).

**Lags and leads:**

- **Lead** — accelerated time (move start earlier); shown as negative days (e.g., -3); reduces duration.
- **Lag** — waiting time (push start later); shown as plus days (e.g., +3); increases duration.

**Earned Value Management:**

| Metric | Formula | "Good" |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **EV** | %Complete × BAC | — |
| **PV** | %Planned × BAC | — |
| **Cost Variance (CV)** | EV − AC | Above **0** |
| **Schedule Variance (SV)** | EV − PV | Above **0** |
| **Cost Performance Index (CPI)** | EV / AC | Above **1** |
| **Schedule Performance Index (SPI)** | EV / PV | Above **1** |

**Agile measures:**

- **User story** — small chunk of functionality (1–3 days, sized not hours).
- **Story points** — assigned by the whole team; reflect effort/complexity.
- **Burndown chart** — remaining work over time (tracks progress).
- **Burn-up chart** — completed work over time (good for checking scope).

**Using historical data:** benchmarking; consult historical information and lessons learned when starting a new project.

## Task 7 — Plan & Manage Quality

- **Quality Control happens first** (project team checks the product meets standards); **Scope Validation** is the customer accepting deliverables.

**Quality vs Grade:**

- **Quality** — fulfilling requirements; **low quality is always a problem**.
- **Grade** — category/ranking; low grade isn't always a problem (may suit budget).

**Quality is prevention-driven** — build it in to do the work right the first time.

| Tool | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| **Checklist** | Ensures work is done the same way each time |
| **Check sheet (tally sheet)** | Organises data about a quality issue |
| **Control chart** | Measures batch results — **Rule of 7**: 7 in a row = a trend |
| **Pareto diagram** | Bar + line; distribution of problems and frequencies |
| **Scatter diagram** | Relationship between variables |
| **Run chart** | Like a control chart; dots show time taken to accumulate the measure |

## Task 8 — Plan & Manage Scope

> **Exam tip:** Track deliverables with the **Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM)**. Missing key deliverable? **Check the RTM.**

**Requirement types:** Business, Stakeholder, Solution (Functional / Non-functional), Transition, Project, Quality — all managed via the RTM, each linked to a WBS component.

**Requirement delivery by life cycle:**

| Life cycle | Requirements | Delivery |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Predictive** | Fixed | Single delivery |
| **Iterative** | Dynamic | Single delivery |
| **Agile** | Dynamic | Frequent smaller deliveries |

> In Agile, **user stories** are usually written by the **Product Owner**, derived from customer requirements.

## Task 9 — Integrate Project Planning Activities

> **Exam tip:** With many assumptions, instruct the team to **record all assumptions** and track changes for impact/alignment.

**Dependency types:**

- **Mandatory** — hard logic (physical/logical constraint); cannot move.
- **Discretionary** — soft logic (preference/best practice); can move.
- **External** — constraint on something external (e.g., awaiting an inspector).
- **Internal** — hard logic within the project (e.g., need a specific resource).

## Task 10 — Manage Project Changes

**MACD** (Move, Add, Change, Delete) — apply to any scope change:

1. **Identify** changes (status meetings, documentation, stakeholder feedback).
2. **Evaluate** impact on schedule, budget, scope.
3. **Approve** via sponsor / authorised decision-maker.
4. **Implement** per the approved change procedure; **update the change log and notify stakeholders** per the Communications Management Plan (even if rejected).
5. **Monitor** the effect on schedule, budget, scope.

**Integrated Change Control:**

- **Configuration control** — changes to features/functions go through configuration + change management systems.
- **Change management system** — everything else.
- **Integrated Change Control** — examines impact to scope/schedule/cost.
- **Change Control Board (CCB)** — reviews/approves/rejects; some orgs bypass it under a threshold (e.g., under $1,000).

> **Exam tips:** A CCB is **less common in Agile** — know the methodology. **Any change to a baseline** goes through change management — including work executed in response to a risk.

## Task 11 — Plan & Manage Procurement

**Lifecycle:** Planning → Choosing a source → Administering the contract → Closing the contract (from buyer or seller point of view).

**Market conditions:**

- **Sole source** — only one seller can meet the need.
- **Single source** — many sellers exist, but you prefer one.
- **Oligopoly** — tight market; one seller's actions affect others' prices.

**Contract types:**

| Contract | Description |
| --- | --- |
| **Firm Fixed-Price (FFP)** | Set price; doesn't change |
| **Fixed Price Incentive Fee** | Set price + bonus for meeting performance objectives |
| **Cost Plus Fixed Fee (CPFF)** | Costs + a fixed fee |
| **Cost Plus Incentive Fee (CPIF)** | Costs + incentive for performance |
| **Time and Materials (T&M)** | Pay for time and materials |

## Task 12 — Manage Project Artifacts

- **Version control:** document how artifacts are kept current — review/approval processes, update frequency, accuracy, and access (e.g., stakeholder access needs).
- **Determine requirements:** know when artifacts need updating — Project Charter, WBS, Schedule, Baselines, Issue Log, Lessons Learned, and Agile artifacts like the Product Backlog.

> **Exam tip:** Periodically evaluate the version-control mechanism — especially stakeholder access and out-of-date versions.

## Task 13 — Determine Appropriate Methodology

| Life cycle | Characteristics |
| --- | --- |
| **Predictive** | Waterfall; plan up front; single delivery; tightly controlled scope |
| **Iterative** | Functional work in multiple iterations; feedback feeds the next round; single final delivery |
| **Incremental** | Deliverables in usable increments; good when uncertainty/risk exists |
| **Agile** | Backlog + Product Owner; changes welcome; rapid iterations; shows early value |
| **Hybrid** | Combination of Agile + Predictive; good for uncertainty/risk |

> **Exam tip:** When transitioning predictive → Agile/Hybrid, keep delivering iterations and show the **value increments** to leadership.

**Buy vs Build (break-even example):**

- Build: $80,000 upfront + $7,500/month. Buy: $68,000 upfront + $9,800/month.
- Upfront difference $12,000 ÷ monthly difference $2,300 = **5.2 months**.
- Kept **longer** than 5.2 months → **build**. Kept **less** → **buy (vendor)**.

## Task 14 — Establish Project Governance Structure

- Understand the organisation's governance and align roles, responsibilities, processes, monitoring, and reporting to it.
- Replicate org governance thoughtfully — consider project-specific needs, stay flexible, and monitor that it contributes to success.

## Task 15 — Manage Project Issues

**Risk vs Issue:**

- **Bad thing may/might/could happen = risk** → review Risk Register / Risk Management Plan.
- **Bad thing did/has/already happened = issue** → log in the Issue Log and execute the risk response.
- Unplanned risk not yet triggered → **qualify** it. Once triggered → **workaround**.

> **Exam tips:** "A problem was identified" implies the risk is **triggered** → move to using **reserves**. **Do not hand off** issues to others — the PM finds solutions.

- **Agile retrospectives** ensure issues don't recur.
- Once a risk becomes an issue, review the Risk Management Plan for the response and communicate per the Communications Management Plan.

## Task 16 — Ensure Knowledge Transfer for Continuity

**Knowledge management techniques:** storytelling; work shadowing (you follow an expert); reverse shadowing (expert follows you, coaches); discussion forums / focus groups; networking; training events.

**Lessons Learned** — a bridge between past projects and future success; a key closure artifact.

- **Holds:** key insights, successes/challenges and how addressed, areas for improvement.
- **Why:** saves time/money, shares knowledge, improves PM practice.
- **Process:** Identify → Document → Analyse → Store/Retrieve — gathered throughout, emphasised at each phase end. The **PM updates it**.

## Task 17 — Plan & Manage Closure / Transitions

**Order of closure events:**

1. Archive project knowledge and artifacts.
2. Release team members; close contracts.
3. Complete retrospectives / lessons learned for each deliverable.
4. Evaluate performance against baselines.
5. Review the **Project Charter and business case** to confirm intended benefits/value before final closeout.
6. **Administrative closure** is part of *final project* closure, not phase completion.

> **Exam tip:** If stakeholders disagree on success at closure, **review the Project Charter** (objectives and success criteria are documented there).

**Phase gates:** key milestones with a definition of done; often where scope is validated / benefits reviewed; review progress and funding (next phase often funded only after the gate); different phases introduce different risks.

**Rolling Wave Planning:** plan imminent work in more detail than distant work; phase-gate planning (finish phase 1, then plan phase 2).

**Theory of Constraints:** focus energy on the biggest constraint/bottleneck so it's no longer the most limiting factor.

> **Exam tip:** You may be shown a chart to interpret — know **burn-up vs burndown**.

## Risk Response Strategies

**Negative risks (threats):**

- **Escalate** — beyond PM authority; needs a higher-level decision.
- **Avoid** — change the plan to eliminate the risk (e.g., delay to dodge hurricane season).
- **Transfer** — shift to another party (e.g., insurance).
- **Mitigate** — reduce probability and/or impact.
- **Accept** — acknowledge and take no action unless it occurs.

**Positive risks (opportunities):**

- **Escalate** — opportunity beyond PM authority; escalate for review.
- **Exploit** — increase probability/impact (make it happen).
- **Enhance** — boost likelihood/positive impact (e.g., extra training/resources).

> **Exam tip:** If a question says "a problem was identified," the risk is **triggered** → move to reserves.
>
> If the team can't agree whether a change should be made → review the **Scope Management Plan**.

# Domain 3 — Business Environment

## Task 1 — Plan & Manage Project Compliance

**Compliance always takes priority.** If new regulations/compliance may impact the project, **FIRST review the impact**, then look at options and stakeholder communications.

> Regulators are **stakeholders** — they fall under compliance with stakeholder requirements.

**Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs):**

- **Internal:** organisational culture, structure, governance, leadership, vision; infrastructure (equipment, facilities, tools, comms, technology); resource policies (people, procurement).
- **External:** conditions and regulations; marketplace conditions; laws; government/industry standards; physical factors (location, weather).

**Organisational Process Assets (OPAs):** historical information / lessons learned; standards, policies, procedures; standardised guidelines and performance measurements; templates (e.g., Scope Management Plan template).

## Task 2 — Evaluate & Deliver Project Benefits and Value

**Types of value:**

- **Tangible:** monetary assets, stockholder equity, market share.
- **Intangible:** goodwill/reputation, brand recognition, public benefit.

- **Metric monitoring for value:** establish data collection/tracking *before* project completion to confirm benefits are achieved.
- **Demonstrate value:** use an appropriate delivery system and communication plan tailored to stakeholder needs/format/impact.

> **Exam tip:** Good benefits metrics help avoid disputes over expected vs achieved benefits.

## Task 3 — Evaluate & Address External Environment Changes for Scope Impact

> Regulators are **stakeholders** under compliance with stakeholder requirements.

**Organisational culture:** when working across countries, be culturally sensitive — cultural sensitivity training helps. Be conscious of **culture shock** and **ethnocentrism** (measuring other cultures by your own).

## Task 4 — Support Organizational Change

> **Exam tip:** For a problem from a new law/regulation, **seek guidance on the topic before committing to any action.**

**Code of Ethics order to follow:**

1. Laws of the country
2. Company policies
3. Customs and culture
4. Ethics

**Determine the correct response:** compliance first — assess impact, then options and communications.

# Miscellaneous Definitions

- **Sunk costs** — funds already spent; should **not** influence the go/no-go decision.
- **Order of magnitude** — a rough early estimate of size/scale when detail is limited.
- **Transference** — applying knowledge/skills/experience from one domain's projects to another.
- **Life Cycle Costing** — recurring cost of using, maintaining, and supporting the product/result.
- **Cost budgeting inputs** — cost estimates and the project schedule.
- **Cost of quality (three examples)** — inspection-driven, continued throughout the project, keeping mistakes out of customers' hands.
- **QA** — quality assurance for overall project performance.
- **JIT (just-in-time) scheduling** — decreases inventory investment; material mistakes can cause downtime with no buffer stock.
- **ISO 9000** — international quality-management standards; industry-agnostic; any org size.
- **Kaizen** — small, steady, continuous improvements to processes/products to cut cost and improve quality.
- **Tight matrix** — collocated team (e.g., a project war room).
- **Zero-sum reward** — only one can win (e.g., employee of the month) — **avoid**.
- **Delphi Technique** — expert panel reaching consensus, often via anonymous poll.
- **Unilateral contract** — simply a purchase order.
- **Privity** — confidential agreement between buyer and seller; a third party can't enforce terms unless named.
- **Low-fidelity prototyping** — quick sketch for fast feedback.
- **Ameliorate** — reduce the impact.
- **Convergence** — participatory decision-making for collective agreement.
- **Swarming** — the team (or several members) collectively focus on resolving a blocker.
- **Punitive** — actions/consequences intended to punish.
- **Active problem solving** — begins with **problem definition**.
- **Multicriteria decision analysis** — evaluates multiple criteria with positive/negative impact.
